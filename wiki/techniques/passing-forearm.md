# Passing — forearm

## Overview

The forearm pass is the two-arm platform contact used on most serve-receive balls below the shoulders, most first-contact defensive digs, and most free-ball transitions. It is the first-contact skill that determines whether your offense gets to run — in modern women's indoor volleyball, passing quality is one of the two or three variables that correlates most tightly with sideout percentage, and at the 14U and high-school levels it is the single biggest determinant of whether a team can compete with more physical opponents. This page covers the individual technique; [[passing-serve-receive]] covers the team/tactical layer.

## Teaching progressions

Lead with reading, not platform. The modern consensus across the AOC/Kiraly line, the USA Women's National Team, and the current-generation coach-development literature is that passing is **a reading skill executed with a platform**, not a platform skill executed after reading. The old order — "get the platform right first, then worry about reading" — produces passers who look clean against tossed balls and fall apart against real serves. Reverse it.

**Beginner — build the platform at rest, then the posture around it.**

Start without a ball. Stand in a receive-ready posture: feet slightly wider than shoulders, right foot half a step ahead of the left, knees bent so the shoulders are over the knees, weight forward on the balls of the feet. This is the posture half of Ray Bechard's **posture–pursuit–platform** frame, and it is the precondition for everything else. From that posture, form the platform: hands together (thumb-over-thumb stacked, or the fist-in-palm variant), thumbs parallel pointing at the floor, wrists pressed down, elbows locked, arms straight. The platform should be flat — no gap, no lifted thumb, no one-forearm-higher-than-the-other.

Add a tossed ball from a partner at close range. Two priorities at this stage: (1) contact on the meat of the forearms, roughly two fists above the wrists, and (2) no arm swing — the ball rebounds off a stable platform, it is not hit toward the target. Kiraly's fourth of his four keys — "simple one-way motion" — names exactly this: the platform moves toward the target in one direction, the arms do not swing like a golf club. Liskevych's ASICS fundamentals instruction converges on the same point from a different vocabulary.

Angle the platform by rotating the shoulders, not by swinging the arms. If the ball is to the target's left, the right shoulder drops slightly and the platform faces target. "Set the angle" — Kiraly's third key — is the shoulder rotation, not an arm adjustment.

Introduce the target early. From the first toss, the passer is passing *to* a spot (a coach, a cone, a shagger), not just "up." The pass target for a 14U team is typically the setter zone — about 10 feet off the net, between Zones 2 and 3 — with a ball high enough that the setter has options. You are training the shoulders and platform to orient to the target before you worry about how hard the feed comes.

**Intermediate — pursuit: move to the ball with the legs, not the arms.**

This is where modern teaching diverges most sharply from traditional teaching, and where posture-pursuit-platform delivers its biggest payoff. The traditional instinct — reach for the ball with the platform, then hope the body catches up — produces off-balance passes and moving platforms at contact. The modern correction is: **midline the ball with the legs, then form the platform late.**

Use shuffle steps for balls within a couple of steps. Crossover for longer pursuits. Drop-step for deep balls. In every case the goal is the same: arrive behind the ball early enough that you can stop, balance, and pass from a stationary base. Kiraly's first key — "stopped and balanced" — is the test: if you are still moving when you contact the ball, you were late.

Jim Stone names a specific error at this stage: **do not pre-form the platform on the way to the ball.** Passers who build the platform early cannot adjust the angle once the serve path evolves in the last few feet of flight. Relaxed arms, lower body arrives first, platform forms at the last moment.

As pursuit becomes routine, introduce the midline concept: wherever possible, play the ball at your centerline rather than off your hip. Balls played off the hip require a platform angle that's hard to control; balls played at the midline let a flat platform do the work.

**Intermediate-advanced — reading the server.**

Read before the ball is tossed, not after. Kiraly's framing of reading as a part of every skill — "What do I see? What do I know? How do I respond?" — applies to serve receive as cleanly as it does to defense. What do I see: the server's toss height, the server's approach angle, the server's contact point relative to the toss. What do I know: this server's tendencies from scout or from the last two serves. How do I respond: start moving before the ball crosses the net, not after.

Drill it with live serving from the first intermediate session. Game-like feeds — actual servers from 25–30 feet, not coach tosses at half speed — are the only way to train the perception-action coupling the game will actually demand. A tossed ball decouples the passer from the reading information a real serve carries; passing reps off tosses teach a passer to pass tossed balls well and nothing more. The research underpinning is explicit: practice tasks must preserve the information-movement coupling athletes will use in competition, or the practice transfers poorly.

**Advanced — passing inside game situations.**

Pass in serve-receive formations with full offensive consequences. Ten passes in a row for the setter is the operational standard at the club/HS level; teams that meter their drills as "X good passes to target in a row, reset on a bad one" develop the skill faster than teams that grade passes one-by-one. Kiraly's "precision passing" frame — game-like chaos and randomness as the training environment — is the practical expression of the motor-learning principle that retention and transfer improve under random practice conditions, even though acquisition-phase performance looks worse.

At this level, layer in the interaction with the setter and the offense: pass height must match the tempo the setter is running, pass location must give the setter options. A "perfect" pass in isolation that forces the setter to a 45-degree-off-center step is a worse pass than a 2-pass placed where the setter can run a quick.

Classical variant, for contrast: the traditional teaching order was platform-first, reading-last — lines of tosses to stationary passers, then tosses at passers who shuffle, then eventually live serves. It produces clean-looking early reps at the cost of transfer to game conditions. Coaches who prefer the classical order often cite the early confidence benefit; the modern counter-argument is that confidence against tosses is not the same thing as passing skill.

## Common errors

- **Reaching with the arms instead of moving the feet.** Correction: drop the arms, shuffle to the ball, form the platform late. Pursuit with the legs is the habit; reaching is the failure mode.
- **Platform not square to target.** Correction: rotate the shoulders toward the target (Kiraly's "set the angle"); do not try to angle the ball with the arms alone. Start from a posture where the target is in peripheral vision so the rotation is small.
- **Active arm swing at contact.** Correction: the platform moves in one simple one-way motion toward the target; no backswing, no hit. GMS teaches this as small-arm movement. Drill it by having passers freeze the finish — if the finish position is more than a few inches different from the start, they swung.
- **Pre-forming the platform on the way to the ball.** Correction: arms stay relaxed during pursuit; the lower body arrives behind the ball first; the platform forms at the last moment so the angle can still be adjusted to the final serve path.
- **Watching the ball instead of reading the server.** Correction: track the server's toss and armswing, not just the ball in flight. Reading cues are in the server's body before the ball is hit.
- **Passing off the hip instead of at midline.** Correction: shuffle to center the ball in front of the body whenever time allows; off-hip passes require platform angles that are hard to control and collapse under pressure.
- **Finish in a standing, reset-to-ready posture.** Correction: hold the passing posture after the pass — shoulders still over knees, ready to chase the overpass, the serve that bounced off a teammate, or the transition ball. Passers who stand up after the pass miss the second contact.
- **Passes too low, too fast, too flat for the setter.** Correction: train pass *height* deliberately. A pass at the 2-pass standard (high enough for the setter to run any tempo, roughly one-setter-height above the net antenna) gives the offense options; a laser to the setter's knees gives the offense nothing.

## Schools-perspectives

The forearm pass is the most contested teaching topic in volleyball fundamentals. Four positions are represented here; the wiki does not resolve the disagreements.

**Gold Medal Squared.** GMS teaches passing inside its broader motor-learning frame: build the movement pattern first so attention is free for reading, use random/variable practice over blocked repetition, keep the drill library small so players see the same problems and coach vocabulary is shared. The 2018 Charleston Academy clinic captured the operational detail — passing taught with emphasis on small-arm movement for control, conservative pass targets off the net, and whole-skill training in game-like conditions rather than isolated part-practice. Shea and Morgan's 1979 contextual-interference finding — random beats blocked for retention and transfer — is the empirical anchor. GMS's position is not anti-technique; it is technique built inside game-like practice, not technique built first and then inserted into games.

**Art of Coaching Volleyball (Kiraly / Bechard / Stone / Dunning / Liskevych).** The AOC line centers on reading-first teaching and the posture-pursuit-platform order. Kiraly's 4 keys to the forearm pass — stopped and balanced, thumbs/wrists together, set the angle, simple one-way motion — is the canonical individual-technique frame. Bechard's posture-pursuit-platform breaks the component order into teachable pieces. Stone's late-platform-formation teaching is the most specific individual-technique correction in the AOC corpus. Dunning's "reading is part of every skill" work places passing inside the same reading framework as defense and blocking. The AOC order is consistent: the passer's eyes, feet, and posture resolve first; the platform forms at the last moment to match what the eyes and feet have already committed to.

**Japanese training tradition.** The Japanese lineage's passing identity sits inside its defensive identity: dig up everything, recover to ready after every contact, treat each first-contact as a point-saving act. The specific drill that names the tradition is *kaiten reshibu* — the rolling dive — but the pedagogical principle travels: high-volume repetition until the response is autonomic, precision on every contact as a program standard, and midline emphasis. Contemporary Japanese youth practice is less severe than the Daimatsu-era model but retains the same technical targets. The Japanese lineage's read on American passing: too tall to dig effectively, too reliant on platform-at-rest passing, not built for the extended rally.

**Ecological dynamics / constraints-led.** The theoretical opposition to GMS and to the traditional decompose-and-recombine progression. Pinder and colleagues (2011) argue that practice tasks must preserve the information-movement coupling of competition — a passing drill off coach tosses is not representatively designed because it decouples the passer from the serve-read information a real server carries, no matter how clean the technique looks. The practical implication is to design practice constraints — server pairings, target zones, scoring systems — so that passing emerges from reading real serves, and to treat decomposed platform-first drills as a developmental dead-end for any passer past the first few weeks of exposure. The constraints-led coach does not dictate technique; the coach engineers the constraints under which effective technique self-organizes. GMS and ecological dynamics agree that practice must look like the game; they disagree, consequentially, on whether skill is a motor program the athlete acquires or an emergent relationship between athlete and environment.

Practical reconciliation for the working coach: the disagreements are theoretical, but the operational practice designs converge more than the rhetoric suggests. Everyone agrees you should train passing against live serves at game speed, early and often. Everyone agrees that tossed-ball decomposition produces clean-looking acquisition and poor transfer. The live coach-craft question is how much scaffolding a 14U passer needs before the game-like work begins — and that question is empirical per-player, not settled in the literature.
