# Defense — Platform

## Overview

The defensive platform is the two-arm contact used to dig an attacked ball. Mechanically it shares almost everything with [[passing-forearm]] — flat forearms, angled to target, legs providing cushion — but the surrounding conditions are harsher. The ball arrives harder, the contact point is lower, the time to set the angle is a fraction of what a passer has, and the platform often forms around the ball rather than in front of it. The modern teaching frame treats platform as the last of three things: **posture**, **pursuit**, **platform**, in that order. Get feet set and body low first; then let the platform form in the narrow window the attacker leaves you.

This page covers the platform mechanic on digs. Reading the hitter to get to the right spot is on [[defense-reading]]; pursuit is on [[defense-pursuit]].

## Teaching progressions

**Beginner — ready posture and quiet platform, no movement.** The defensive ready posture is lower than serve receive: knees deeply bent with hips below the knee crease, shoulders forward of the knees, heels just off the floor, weight on the balls of the feet. Hands are apart and low, not pre-formed — a defender who pre-forms the platform on the way to the ball cannot adjust the angle when the hit or tip changes shape in the last few feet. This is Jim Stone's late-platform principle, more consequential for defense than for passing because the time window is shorter.

Drill platform formation off a coach-tossed ball at dig height. Fundamentals: thumbs parallel pointed at the floor, wrists pressed down, platform flat; contact on the meat of the forearms. The platform angles to target by rotating the shoulders, not by flinging the arms. A beginner who can produce twenty quiet digs in a row from a cooperative toss is ready for the next stage.

**Beginner-intermediate — legs provide the cushion.** A hard-driven ball does not need a platform pop; it needs a platform that absorbs. Cue: "quiet hands, soft legs." Knees bend further on contact, hips drop, platform stays passive, ball rebounds high off a cushioned base. Stiff legs with a stabbing platform send the ball off the court; soft legs with a stable platform float it up to a zone the setter can read.

Drill from progressively harder feeds: coach-tossed lob, coach-struck down-ball, half-speed spike, live hitter. At each step, the target zone is the same — roughly 10-15 feet high and in the middle of the court.

**Intermediate — controlling the platform with body position.** The modern teaching detail that matters most is that platform angle is a function of where the body is relative to the ball, not just where the arms are. If the defender is behind the ball, the platform is flat and the ball digs up. Off the hip, the platform has to angle across the body and the ball tends to go where the shoulders point. Correction is almost always footwork: midline the ball before the dig.

Specific cases. On a hard-driven ball at the chest, modern coaching is to drop the hips and let the platform form low at the waist — the ball comes down onto the platform rather than spiking up into the face. On a ball at the hip, the inside arm drives down and the outside shoulder rotates; the goal is redirecting toward the center of the court with minimum platform angle change. On a cut shot or low line ball, step through and dig while moving forward — unstable platform, but the alternative (a reach) loses the ball cleanly.

**Intermediate-advanced — platform inside emergency moves.** Emergency moves — one-arm dig, overhead dig on a ball driven above the shoulders, tomahawk on a ball coming down above the forehead, dive or sprawl — are platform variants that preserve as much of the flat-forearm principle as the situation allows. Kiraly and Slabe's "Blitz the Bro" and "Neville's Pepper Drill" keep the libero under constant platform demand. Score design matters: "you should always be rewarded for a good dig," because defenders who get no credit for emergency saves stop attempting them.

Overhead digs deserve explicit teaching for back-row players. The technique is an open-handed deflect, not a set — fingers spread, contact slightly in front of the forehead, no attempt at accuracy; the job is just keeping the ball on your side. Trying to set an incoming spike is a double-contact call in most readings.

**Advanced — platform inside team defense.** Once the individual platform is reliable, the defender coordinates with the block. Liskevych and McCutcheon's defense-blocking integration teaches defenders to read the block's shape before the hit, so the platform angle is pre-committed to the options the block leaves open. Reading tells the defender where to be; the platform in the right spot gets the ball up.

## Common errors

- **Pre-forming the platform on the way to the ball.** Correction: hands apart and low during pursuit; platform forms at the last instant, built around the ball's final path.
- **Platform too high at contact.** A chest-height platform turns the ball into a floater going behind the court. Correction: drop the hips, get the platform below the ball.
- **Stabbing with stiff legs.** Arms extend, platform meets the ball actively, no cushion. Correction: "quiet hands, soft legs"; freeze the platform, let the knees absorb.
- **Over-rotating the shoulders.** Defender twists the arms toward target to fix a bad body position, producing a spinning sideways dig. Correction: midline the ball with footwork first; large shoulder rotations mean the footwork was wrong.
- **One-arm digs as a default.** Playing every ball one-arm converts a control surface into an open slap. Correction: reserve one-arm digs for balls a two-arm platform cannot reach.
- **Standing up after the dig.** Defender misses the next ball if the attacker gets a second touch. Correction: hold the dig posture; first job after the dig is to see where the ball is going, not to reset.
- **Pulling the head up at contact.** Flinching breaks down the platform. Correction: chin tucked toward the platform; the platform is only as good as the defender's willingness to stay behind the ball.
- **Trying to direct a hard dig accurately.** Ambition outruns capability; defender aims for the setter and shanks off the court. Correction: "up and in" is the standard for hard digs; accuracy is reserved for softer touches and tips.

## Related drills

- [[blitz-the-bro]] — USA WNT 6v6 in which every serve targets the libero; the platform has to survive an entire game's worth of hard-driven serves and attacks.
- [[neville-pepper]] — all-back-row-attack 6v6 drill from Kiraly's libero corpus; score design rewards good digs and penalizes non-pursuit.
- **Partner Pepper** — cooperative two-player pepper scaling hit intensity to warm up platform and legs together.
- **Three Person Defense** — three back-row defenders against a live hitter, rotating through positions; builds the platform inside team-defense spacing.
