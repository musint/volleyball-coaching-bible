# Hitting Arm Swing

## Overview

The arm swing is the sequence that converts jump height and torso rotation
into ball speed and directional control at contact. Power comes
predominantly from **torque** — the rotation of the torso with the hips and
core driving through — and the second key is **hand height at contact**:
a high contact point lets the hitter hit down through the ball, over the
block, and into the floor rather than into the top of the net or into the
block's hands.

A modern full swing is a two-arm action, not a one-arm action. Both arms
rise during the approach's plant step (the "double-arm lift"), storing
elastic energy through the shoulder girdle and orienting the torso
perpendicular to the net. The non-hitting arm pulls down and across the
body at contact to accelerate torso rotation; the hitting arm follows the
classic bow-and-arrow load, high-elbow drive, contact-over-the-hitting-
shoulder, and wrist snap for topspin. Done well, the full swing is
repeatable, protects the shoulder and lower back, and generates maximum
ball speed without muscling the ball.

## Teaching progressions

**Stage 1 — Down-ball arm swing, no jump ("puppet position").** Start in a
staggered-stance loaded position with the hitting hand above and slightly
behind the head, non-hitting hand lifted in front as a sight line. Coach
tosses the ball shoulder-high on the hitting arm side; the hitter snaps
the ball down into the floor using only the arm and a small hip rotation.
This stage builds the wrist snap and contact-over-the-shoulder feel before
any jumping is added.

**Stage 2 — Wall arm swing.** Standing a step off a wall, the hitter
loads the hitting arm into the "Sight 90" position (elbow high, hand
behind the head, eyes sighting the contact point on the wall) and snaps
the ball into the wall, catching the rebound. Cue "elbow leads, hand
finishes high, wrist snaps through." The wall provides immediate feedback
on contact angle — ball snapping down vs. drifting up tells you whether
the contact point was above the hand or behind it.

**Stage 3 — Partner throw-to-hit across the net.** Two players face
across the net. The first throws an approaching ball (using the hitting
arm in a full-swing motion) across to the partner, who catches it. As
arm mechanics stabilize, the throw becomes a hit — partners snapping
topspin balls back and forth. This progression protects the lower back
and shoulders during warm-up because the hitter builds the full-swing
motion gradually from throw to hit.

**Stage 4 — Stationary jump-and-swing.** From a balanced two-foot plant
(no approach), the hitter jumps straight up, loads the double-arm lift at
the peak, and swings on a self-toss or coach toss. Isolates the swing
from the approach so the hitter can feel torso rotation at full jump
height.

**Stage 5 — Box hit.** The hitter stands on a low box or bench near the
net, swings at coach- or setter-delivered balls without jumping. Removes
the jump variable so the coach can focus on contact point, ball spin,
and shot placement. Also a staple of early-season volume-building since
it saves the knees.

**Stage 6 — Full approach + live set.** Combine with the footwork from
[[hitting-approach]]. Coach toss to setter, setter delivers, hitter
approaches and swings. Repetition volume is the engine here — GMS-style
blocked reps build the motor program; AOC-style randomized reps (varying
sets, locations, tempos) build the decision-making layer.

**Stage 7 — Swing with shot options and against a block.** At this
stage, arm mechanics are no longer the focus — the hitter decides
between hard-driven cross, line, tip, roll, and tool based on read.
Covered on [[hitting-shot-selection]].

## Common errors

- **Early arm drop ("bicycle swing").** The hitter drops the hitting arm
  through the approach and whips it forward on takeoff rather than
  lifting it high during the plant. Result: low contact, weak ball.
  Fix: drill the double-arm lift explicitly — both arms back on the
  second-to-last step, both arms up and forward on the close step.

- **Low contact point / contact behind the hand.** The ball contacts the
  palm at or behind the top of the head instead of out in front of the
  hitting shoulder. Power goes into the net. Fix: cue "hit the top-front
  quarter of the ball"; use box-hit reps with a targeted spot on the
  floor to enforce down-angle contact.

- **Shoulder-only swing (no torque).** The hitter rotates through the
  shoulder without engaging hips and torso — arm motion isolated from
  the kinetic chain. Over time, this burns out the rotator cuff. Fix:
  cue "non-hitting arm pulls down and across"; drill partner-throws
  emphasizing the torso snap; work on hip separation with
  ground-based rotation patterns.

- **Open hand vs. full-fist-then-snap.** Young hitters often contact
  with a flat, fingers-only slap. A crisp swing contacts with the whole
  hand behind the ball, wrapping and snapping through the top so the
  palm finishes past the ball. Fix: contact drills emphasizing full-
  hand contact and finish; check for spin — a well-snapped ball has
  heavy topspin, a slapped ball knuckles.

- **Stopped non-hitting arm.** The non-hitting arm drifts or pushes up
  instead of pulling down across the chest, so the torso doesn't rotate
  through contact. Fix: explicit cue "yank the off-arm down past the
  hip at contact."

- **One-leg takeoff / off-balance landing.** Linked to [[hitting-
  approach]] errors; costs jump height and amplifies landing impact on
  the lead knee. Fix: two-foot plant and two-foot landing in the same
  square-to-net alignment; contrast with basketball lay-up pattern to
  show why it's wrong for hitting.

- **Muscling the ball (all arm strength, no whip).** Stronger athletes
  often try to power through a late swing with raw arm force. The ball
  goes hard but without accuracy or topspin, and the shoulder pays.
  Fix: slow down the approach, re-emphasize torque and whip; tempo drills
  where the hitter hits at 50% effort but full mechanics.

- **Breaking at the waist on landing.** Hitter lands with shoulders over
  the toes and hips pushed back — a forward-fold landing that stresses
  the low back. Fix: "shoulders in front of hips, chin up, soft knees"
  on landing; pair with plyometric landing drills in the warm-up.

## Related drills

- **Wall Arm Swing** — daily warm-up rep count; builds the wrist snap and
  contact-point feel.
- **Partner Throw To Hit** — two-player throw-then-hit progression
  across the net; shoulder-safe warm-up.
- **Box Hit Arm Swing** — box-hit reps isolating swing mechanics from
  the approach.
- **Stationary Jump Swing** — two-foot plant + jump + swing on self-toss
  or coach toss.
