# Transition — Out of System

## Overview

Out-of-system (OOS) is the phase where the pass or the dig does not reach the setter cleanly enough for the intended offense to run — setter off the net, setter took the first contact, ball to a non-setter, pass deep. At 14U often over 40% of total contacts happen OOS, and the gap between teams that practice it and teams that do not is one of the largest compounding edges at club and HS.

OOS play is **not** improvisation. It is a set of trained patterns: setter bails high to a pin, libero or non-setter takes the second contact when the setter took the first, hitter adjusts approach, team calls OOS so everyone knows the play has changed. Modern AOC and USA WNT consensus is that OOS needs explicit training.

This page covers transition mechanics: detection, call-out, second-contact decision, hitter adjustment, completion. Individual setting technique is on [[setting-hands]] and [[setting-out-of-system]] (forthcoming).

## Teaching progressions

**Beginner — detect and call "out of system."** Everyone on the court has to know the play has changed. If only the setter knows, the hitters keep approaching for the called offense and the second contact is surrounded by teammates running the wrong play. The fix is a loud, unambiguous call — "OOS!" or "out!" — from whoever first sees the pass is off.

Drill the call before the play. Passers and defenders call the quality of every first contact ("good," "off," "out") for the full team to hear. Over time the call becomes automatic and triggers the OOS response. A 14U team that develops reliable OOS calling has solved half of the OOS problem.

**Beginner-intermediate — setter bails to a pin.** The default OOS second-contact at nearly every level is a high ball to a pin — left outside for right-handed setters, right pin for the back set. The set is taller than in-system, slightly inside the antenna (OOS sets tight to the antenna are death), with enough hang-time that the hitter can approach from outside the court. The target is "high and inside the pin," not "on the pin."

This is a skill, not a last resort. Drill setters bailing from deep, off-net, and behind-them passes with the explicit cue that the bail is the intended play. The Kiraly and Rose corpora both treat the OOS bail as a core setting skill, not a concession.

**Beginner-intermediate — if the setter took the first ball, the libero sets.** Whenever the setter digs, passes, or chases an errant ball on the first contact, someone else takes the second. Modern consensus is that the **libero** is the de-facto emergency setter, and libero-as-setter should be practiced explicitly. Kiraly's "Make the libero set" team drill is canonical: the winner of each rally is fed a hard-driven ball so the setter has to dig, the libero is forced into the second contact, and the drill scores whether the libero converts into a runnable pin attack. The libero is not improvising — the libero runs the same offense at a degraded tempo from a more conservative target.

When the pass ends up with a non-setter who is not the libero — a DS, an outside hitter who read it first — the same principle applies with an even more conservative set target. At club and HS, every player who could plausibly be the second contact should have drilled the high-outside bail.

**Intermediate — hitter adjusts approach to the high-outside ball.** A hitter who runs their in-system approach on a bail set is early, flat, and gets stuffed. Adjustments: start later (more hang-time), start wider (set is further outside), accept a less-than-ideal plant if the set is tight, and adapt shot selection to the block's extra setup time. Train the OOS approach as a distinct pattern. Shot selection shifts too — tips, cut-shots, high-hands-off-the-block, roll shots are more sustainable than a hard line swing on a bail ball. Hitters who insist on the hard swing become predictable and the defense camps.

**Intermediate — team integration.** Once call-out, bail, libero-set, and hitter adjustment are in place, drill the integration: compromise first contacts intentionally, call OOS, respond, score on attack conversion. McCutcheon's pattern-recognition frame applies — a team that has named OOS as a pattern executes a rehearsed response; a team treating every OOS rally as a novel crisis plays from behind.

**Intermediate-advanced — back-row and deep-court alternatives.** At higher levels, the pin bail is not the only OOS option. Back-row attacks (pipe, D-ball) can run on a deep pass if the back-row hitter is in approach position — a major weapon and one of the differences between Italian/Brazilian transition games and older US patterns. At 14U, back-row OOS is rarely viable; at varsity and above, it belongs in the repertoire.

**Advanced — OOS indistinguishable from first-ball.** The endpoint is a team whose OOS offense looks like in-system to the defense. The international men's standard; the US men's transition debate under Kiraly is partly about closing this gap. At club-HS, a team approximating this on 60% of OOS rallies is top-tier.

## Common errors

- **Failing to call "out of system."** Teammates keep running the called offense; the second contact has no support. Correction: train the call on every first contact; grade team communication independently of the outcome.
- **Setter forcing a poor option instead of bailing.** Tries to run a quick on a pass 15 feet off the net, or sets tight to the antenna. Correction: when in doubt, bail to the pin — rehearse it as a decision rule.
- **Bail too tight to the antenna.** Hitter runs into the antenna or the block stacks the pin. Correction: target is inside the antenna, not on it.
- **Hitter refusing the high-outside ball.** Pulls out of the approach, leaving the setter no option. Correction: develop tip, roll, and cut-shot options so the hitter can survive OOS balls without a 100% swing.
- **Libero avoiding second-contact responsibility.** Waits for someone else. Correction: drill libero-as-setter explicitly (Kiraly's team drill).
- **First-ball approach on a bail set.** Early, flat, blocked. Correction: drill the OOS approach as a distinct pattern with later start and wider origin.
- **Back-row attack regardless of position.** Hitter out of position chases a set not for them. Correction: back-row OOS attacks require the hitter inside the 3-meter line and in approach position.
- **Treating OOS as a practice afterthought.** Team sides out in-system and loses long rallies. Correction: dedicate practice time explicitly — Kiraly, Rosen, and the AOC OOS corpus all rest on the same premise that OOS is a distinct trainable phase.
