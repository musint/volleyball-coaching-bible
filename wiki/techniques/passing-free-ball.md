# Passing — free ball

## Overview

A free ball is any ball the opponent sends over the net that is not an attack — a sloppy third contact, a desperate reach, an emergency overhand push. The free-ball pass is the back-row's first contact on that ball, handled in transition from base defense rather than from a serve-receive formation. It is a tempo-setting contact: a well-placed free-ball pass lets the setter run the full offense at full speed; a mis-played one burns the offensive opportunity a free ball is supposed to hand the receiving team. This page focuses on the individual-and-team contact; the systemic "free balls are always point-scoring opportunities" frame (Rose: *no free balls go over from my team*) lives on the offensive-systems pages.

## Teaching progressions

Free-ball passing is less contested than serve-receive or the forearm pass in isolation, and the teaching is correspondingly more uniform across modern and traditional schools. The operational principle is: treat the free ball as the *best opportunity of the rally*, because it usually is.

**Beginner — recognize and call the free ball.**

The instant the ball crosses the net on a trajectory that is not an attack, the back row calls "**free!**" loud and in unison. The call is the trigger for three parallel actions:
1. Back-row passers transition from base-defense posture into a free-ball passing posture — higher than dig base, platform already forming.
2. The setter transitions from base to target, standing tall and calling for the ball.
3. Front-row attackers release from block responsibility and begin their approach footwork so they are ready to swing on a quick or on-tempo ball.

Drill the call from day one. A quiet free ball is a wasted free ball. The 14U habit — freezing when a free ball comes, watching to see where it lands — is the single most correctable error at this level.

**Beginner — the free-ball passing posture.**

Higher than serve-receive base. Free balls arrive slower and higher than serves; the passer has time to step into the ball and deliver a clean platform pass from a stationary base. Feet slightly wider than shoulders, knees bent, shoulders over knees, platform forming early because the ball's trajectory is readable long before contact.

Contact point is midline, pass height is higher than a serve-receive pass. A free-ball pass at setter height — about eight to ten feet off the floor, arriving at a spot 10 feet off the net between Zones 2 and 3 — gives the setter full tempo options. A flat, fast free-ball pass to the setter's knees is worse than a serve-receive pass of the same quality, because the offensive expectation is that a free ball produces an *A-pass*, not a merely-playable pass.

**Intermediate — base position and free-ball zone coverage.**

Base defense for most teams in most rotations puts the libero in Zone 5 or 6, DS or outside in the other deep zone, and the back-row setter or opposite in Zone 1. The free-ball transition from base is a short shuffle, not a sprint — back row moves forward a step or two into a passing formation, front row releases the block.

The standard free-ball formation is effectively a 3-person serve-receive: libero middle-back, two outside/back-row passers splitting the deep zones. Zone assignments are the same as in serve-receive, which is part of why teams that drill both patterns share the rotation logic.

**Intermediate — communication with the setter.**

The setter calls for the free ball the moment it is identified: "setter up!" as she transitions. The pass is delivered *to the setter*, not to the target cone — if the setter has moved to an edge of the target zone for any reason, the pass follows her. This is a visible departure from serve-receive, where the target cone is fixed; free-ball passing treats the setter's live position as the target.

The setter, receiving a clean free-ball pass, has more options than off a served ball: she can set outside at full tempo, run a middle quick, set back to opposite, or even attempt a second-contact attack if the ball is tight enough and the blocker is slow off the net.

**Advanced — keeping the ball high and on-tempo.**

The difference between an elite free-ball system and a mediocre one is the height-and-location consistency of the pass. Two operational benchmarks:

- **Height.** A free-ball pass should be high enough that the setter can release from base, move to the target, set, and have the hitter swing on a full-tempo ball. For a 14U team that is an 8–10 foot pass height. For an HS-varsity team with a quicker setter and faster offense it is a 10–12 foot pass. A pass lower than that forces the setter to rush or forces the hitter to a slow, late approach.
- **Location.** At target, every time. Free-ball passes shank less than serve-receive passes because the ball is slower — if a free-ball pass is off-target, it is an execution error, not a forced error, and it costs the offense the same way a bad serve-receive pass does.

Drill the pass-height dimension explicitly. In practice, grade free-ball passes on *height* as well as location: a pass at target that's too flat is a partial-credit pass, not a 3-pass. 14U teams often treat any free ball that stays in their side as a success; the standard is higher than that at competitive club and HS level.

**Advanced — never send a free ball over.**

Russ Rose's program-level rule — *no free balls go over from my team* — is the corollary to every team's free-ball aggressiveness. A free ball sent over is a gift of an A-pass to the opponent; the receiving team usually sides out. Program-level standard: if the only play you have is to send it over clean, find a way to send it over *ugly* instead — a high arcing over-the-head set, a deep deep corner dump, anything that denies the opponent a clean pass even at the cost of your own rally continuation. Train the habit: reward teams in practice for converting "almost a free ball" situations into down-ball-style third-contact attacks rather than clean free balls over.

## Common errors

- **Taking the free ball too low.** Pass height is flat or shoulder-high rather than setter-tempo height; setter has no time to set anything but high-outside. Correction: free balls permit a higher arc than serve receive — use it. Drill height as a grading dimension.
- **Failure to call "free!"** Silent free balls end with one passer playing it while teammates watch. Correction: mandatory call as soon as the ball's trajectory is readable; no-call reps don't count in drills.
- **Front row fails to release from the block in time to attack.** Free-ball drills should include the full sequence: call → release block → approach → swing. Correction: the free-ball drill is not a passing drill; it is a transition drill.
- **Setter caught in base position.** Setter's transition from base to target is too slow; pass arrives to an empty target zone. Correction: drill the setter's "setter up!" call and footwork as part of every free-ball rep.
- **Using hands for a free ball that should be a platform.** Modern teams use hands more on free balls than on serves because the ball is slower and higher — but any ball arriving below the shoulders should still be a platform. Correction: the same hands-vs-platform decision rule as serve receive, applied to the slower free-ball tempo.
- **Treating a down ball as a free ball.** Down balls — attacked balls that the hitter could not jump on — arrive faster and flatter than free balls and deserve a serve-receive-style pass, not a free-ball-style pass. Correction: call the difference. "Down ball!" is a distinct call from "Free!"; the team's response to each is different.
- **Sending a free ball over.** Covered above. Correction: program-level commitment to the no-free-balls rule; train the alternatives.

## Related drills

- **Free Ball Transition** — coach tosses a free ball over, team executes the full transition from base to pass to set to attack
- **Six On Six Wash** — full-rotation 6v6 with scripted free-ball opportunities; rewards teams that convert free balls to kills
- **Base Defense To Free Ball** — base-defense read drill where the coach signals the attack type (hitting, down-ball, free-ball) and the team responds with the correct transition
