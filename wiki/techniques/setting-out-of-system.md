# Setting — Out-of-System

## Overview

An **out-of-system (OOS)** second contact is any set where the receiver fails to deliver a pass that lets the designated setter run the planned offense: the pass is off the net, too low, too high, moving laterally, or the setter took the first ball and someone else has to set. The rate at which a team finishes out-of-system points is one of the highest-leverage performance differentiators at the HS varsity level and above — the quality of in-system kills is converging across competitive levels, but out-of-system conversion still has a long tail.

Out-of-system setting is not one technique; it is a **decision-and-toolbox skill** in which the second-contact player reads the situation, selects the best available tool, and executes. The player making the decision is frequently *not* the setter. In modern offense the libero, outside hitter, opposite, or back-row DS all set whenever the setter takes the first ball, and the tempo and reliability of their second contact determines whether the team's best hitters ever get a swing.

This page covers the tools and the decision frame; [[setting-hands]], [[setting-jump]], and [[setting-backset]] cover the in-system mechanics the out-of-system tools build on. Because this topic is genuinely contested across schools of thought, the page includes a **Schools-perspectives** section.

## Teaching progressions

**Decision frame.** Before the mechanics, the player needs a simple in-the-rally decision frame:

1. **Can I overhead-set this?** Overhead is the first choice when rules allow and the ball's trajectory gives time.
2. **If not, where is the best attack available right now?** The pin is the default bail; a back-row attack is an option if the setter is out of position.
3. **Who is the best hitter available?** A second-choice hitter with room to swing beats a first-choice hitter trapped against the block.
4. **Call "out" loud and early.** "OOS" or "help" tells hitters to adjust their approach angle and the coach to read whether the pre-called play survives.

**Tool inventory.**

- **Bump-set to outside.** The fundamental bail. Forearm-platform set to the outside-hitter pin, height high enough that the hitter has time to get to it. The outside-hitter is the canonical bail target because she is most often already in approach.
- **Overhead emergency set from off-net.** When the pass is low but in front, the player still sets with hands — the overhead tool remains the first option until trajectory forces a platform.
- **Two-handed redirect / jumping bump-set.** An overhead or platform set delivered in the air, often with one hand or as an emergency redirect, when the ball is over the setter's head and a standing set is impossible. Higher-risk, higher-reward; trains the same hand mechanics as [[setting-hands]] but in an off-balance context.
- **Forearm set to a middle or opposite.** When the offense has time for a quick but the setter is out of position, a platform set to a middle or opposite can recover tempo. Hard to execute reliably under pressure.
- **Tilt-set / angled emergency set.** A redirected platform contact where the ball was going past the player's midline and the only option is to angle the platform to send the ball down the sideline to the pin.
- **Second-contact attack.** When the second-ball player is the opposite or a back-row threat, a down-ball attack on contact two can finish the rally entirely rather than extending it.

The toolbox expands by position. A **libero** needs reliable bump-set to outside and an overhead emergency set. An **outside-hitter** taking a second ball needs a bump-set across the net to the opposite or a two-handed redirect to the middle. An **opposite** needs a second-contact attack option. The setter herself needs the full inventory plus the ability to run the planned offense off passes that are marginal rather than genuinely emergency.

**Progression.**

1. **Isolated bump-set to pin.** Coach-tossed pass ten to fifteen feet off the net; player bump-sets to the outside antenna at a target height. Track location and height consistency. Useful as an introduction; limited transfer by itself.
2. **Second-contact decision drill.** Player receives a random coach-tossed second contact — some in front, some over the head, some low, some high — and chooses an appropriate tool. Coach grades the decision, not the outcome.
3. **Live dig-and-set.** Setter is made to dig the first ball; a named second-contact player — usually the libero — delivers the set to a pin attacker. This is the [Kiraly make-the-libero-set team drill] family. Requires two full teams, real rallies, realistic tempo.
4. **Hard-driven ball into the court.** After a rally, coach drives a hard ball into the receiving team's court; the first-contact player is not the setter; the second-contact player sets. Creates a realistic out-of-system rep embedded in live play.
5. **Out-of-system scoring games.** Games with scoring constraints that reward out-of-system finishes — e.g., a bonus point for an out-of-system kill, or a validation-style rule requiring a clean finish on a specific attack. Embeds the skill in a game context so the decision pressure is real.
6. **OOS call-and-respond.** Setter or hitter calls "out" on every out-of-system ball; hitters adjust approach angle and tempo accordingly. Practiced explicitly.

**Reading the block under pressure.** An out-of-system set gives the block time to form — the block usually wins the in-system race, but in out-of-system it has more time, not less. The second-contact player has to read block position *before* delivering: if the pin is doubled, the redirect goes to a different hitter. This is the perceptual layer modern instruction treats as part of every setting rep; out-of-system is where it is most visible.

## Common errors

- **Over-committing to the high-outside bail.** Every out-of-system ball goes to the outside antenna; the block and opposing defense read it and shut the pin. Correction: keep the bail as the default but introduce the opposite, the back-row attack, and tempo variation so the defense cannot pre-commit.
- **Failing to bail to a pin at all.** Player tries to run a quick or a set-middle-from-off-net combination, the set fails, and the team finishes the rally with a free ball. Correction: the pin is the default for a reason; high-risk recoveries only when the in-system offense is not available at all.
- **Not calling "OOS" early enough.** Hitters approach as if in-system, the set comes in late, the attack is trapped. Correction: the call has to come at the first contact, not at the second; everyone on the court has to train to hear it.
- **Jump-setting an out-of-system pass.** See [[setting-jump]] — out-of-system passes are not jump-set candidates.
- **Over-reliance on the two-handed redirect.** The redirect is flashy and sometimes the only option, but it is high-risk and players who default to it lose control of set location. Correction: teach it as a last-resort tool, not a first-choice tool.
- **Ignoring the back row.** Pipes and bic-style back-row attacks are viable when the front-row hitters are trapped; teams that train only front-row finishing narrow their options. Correction: include a back-row attacker in out-of-system drills.
- **Using the libero only for digging.** Teams rarely train the libero as a second-contact setter and then expect her to produce sets in matches. Correction: libero-as-setter needs dedicated reps. Modern AOC instruction treats libero-as-second-contact as a trained position expectation, not an emergency improvisation.
- **Coach-tossed-only OOS practice.** Decomposing the out-of-system rep from the upstream play destroys the perceptual information the second-contact player actually reads. Correction: move to live reps early; use the coach toss only as the first teaching rep, not as the whole drill library.

## Schools-perspectives

Out-of-system setting is one of the more contested topics in modern coaching because schools disagree on both *what to train* and *how to train it*. The four positions summarized below are stable in the literature and worth teaching to an assistant coach; the page does not resolve between them.

**Art of Coaching Volleyball — toolbox approach.** AOC-aligned coaches — Dunning, Fisher, Liskevych, Mattox — teach a broad second-contact inventory: overhead redirect, bump-set, jump-bump where appropriate, two-handed redirect, and second-contact attack threats. The pedagogy emphasizes breadth of tools, held follow-through and vision work on every contact, and drill progressions that move from cooperative feeds to live reps. The implicit claim is that volleyball's variability exceeds what a small tool set can handle; the second-contact player has to carry a toolbox and make tool-selection decisions in-flight.

**Japanese tradition — high-outside bail plus relentless defense.** The Japanese-lineage position, informed by Daimatsu-era defensive priority, is that the high-outside bail is the default and the offensive problem is therefore a defensive problem upstream: keep the rally alive via relentless floor-defense, and the next opportunity will present itself. Precision on the bail itself matters more than breadth of second-contact tools. This is consistent with a lineage that prizes repetition-to-autonomic-response on a small set of reliable techniques rather than improvisational breadth. The modern Japanese senior program hybridizes with imported methodology, so the lineage position is most clearly visible at the youth and historical levels.

**Gold Medal Squared — motor-learning-grounded decision training.** GMS's position is methodological rather than technical: whatever tools the team uses, they must be trained under **random and variable practice** conditions as quickly as the learner allows. GMS is skeptical of isolated blocked reps of out-of-system mechanics because the motor-learning literature (Shea & Morgan 1979; Magill & Hall 1990) finds that blocked practice produces better acquisition-phase performance but worse retention and transfer. Out-of-system performance is a retention-and-transfer problem by definition — it happens under novel, pressured conditions — so GMS pushes coaches to move quickly into game-like reps and train the decision, not just the mechanics.

**Ecological dynamics — representative-design practice.** The ecological-dynamics position sharpens the GMS point into a testable criterion: practice tasks must preserve the **information-movement coupling** of real play (Pinder et al. 2011). Applied to out-of-system setting, this means a coach-tossed ball into a decomposed out-of-system drill does not train the perceptual skill that actually discriminates expert performers, because it strips the upstream setter-and-hitter-and-block information the player would read in a real rally. The 2012 volleyball-specific work by Afonso and colleagues is suggestive here: elite defenders attend to relational information between setter, hitters, and blockers, not just the ball. The same almost certainly holds for out-of-system setters reading block geometry before they set. The prescription is constraint-led practice — small-sided games, live digs into live sets, representative-design reps — rather than decomposed drills.

The four positions share more than they disagree about: everyone agrees out-of-system performance is high-leverage, everyone agrees the default bail is the high-outside, and everyone agrees that live play is eventually required. The disagreement is about **how much tool-inventory breadth to teach** (AOC broad, Japanese narrow-and-precise) and **how quickly to move from decomposed reps to game-speed reps** (GMS and ecological dynamics push faster than a traditional technique-first progression).
