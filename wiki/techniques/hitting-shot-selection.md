# Hitting Shot Selection

## Overview

Shot selection is the decision layer on top of the swing — what the hitter
does with the ball based on the set, the block, and the defense behind the
block. A one-dimensional hitter who only swings hard cross-court can be
shut down by disciplined positional play. A complete hitter has a
**toolbox**: hard cross, hard line, high-hands/tool off the block, tip
(short to zone 2/3/4), roll shot (soft topspin landing behind the block),
off-speed drive, and deep-corner placement. The goal is enough options
that the defense cannot key on one answer.

The first shot-selection skill is **block reading**. The hitter scans the
block during the approach: single or two blockers? Hands inside the pin
(tool available off the outside hand) or sealed over the net (line
closed)? Seam open? Advanced hitters adjust swing angle and hand position
on the ball in the final quarter-second of the approach.

The second skill is **self-awareness** — knowing what kind of hitter you
are. Dunning asks every hitter to articulate her style: high-point
technician, angle hitter, placement hitter, raw power swinger. Knowing
your type tells you what to invest in and where your gaps live.

This is a genuinely contested topic across schools — see
[[#Schools-perspectives]] below.

## Teaching progressions

**Stage 1 — Cross vs. line on a single set.** Foundation shots first.
Hitter approaches a 4 ball with no block and swings cross-court to a
target (deep corner, zone 1). Then swings line to a line target (zone 5).
Build repetition of the two core power lanes before adding tools.

**Stage 2 — Add the tip.** Introduce the off-hand tip (soft finger-
placement shot) over the block or into a deep zone behind the defenders'
feet. Teach: same approach, same jump, same arm motion until the final
instant; change only the hand (open palm, fingers firm) and the target.
A well-disguised tip is one of the highest-percentage attacks at every
level.

**Stage 3 — Add the roll shot.** Soft topspin placement landing
mid-court or behind the block. Useful when the block is well-formed and
the defense is deep. Cue: "hit the top of the ball with a brushed wrist
snap, aim for a spot you can see behind the block."

**Stage 4 — High-hands / tool off the block.** Against a two-blocker
front, the hitter intentionally drives the ball into the outside hand
of the pin blocker so the ball deflects out of bounds on the block's
side. High-hands is a staple at HS varsity and above. The hitter
"shows line" — swinging body lines toward the sideline — and uses the
outside hand of the block as a target.

**Stage 5 — Read-and-adjust.** Randomized block fronts. Coach varies
block location, stance, and set location; the hitter must choose shot
mid-approach. GMS-style clinics rely heavily on this stage: whole-skill
game-like reps where decision-making is the training stimulus.

**Stage 6 — Deep-corner placement and seam work.** Advanced hitters
target specific defender-gap zones. The classic high-seam cross (over
the middle blocker's outside shoulder into the seam between the
right-side defender and libero) is a common finishing shot. "Cutting"
the ball — sharp angle to zone 5 or zone 1 short — punishes a defense
that has shaded deep for the power lane.

**Stage 7 — Out-of-system shot selection.** When the set is off the
net or far off the pin, the hitter's toolbox shrinks. Good out-of-
system hitters default to high-seam, high-line, or a controlled roll
shot instead of forcing a kill. Part of maturing as a hitter is
knowing when not to swing for a kill.

## Common errors

- **One-dimensional hitting.** Hitter only has the hard cross-court
  swing. Opposing teams key the defense behind it and the attack
  efficiency collapses. Fix: deliberate toolbox work — commit practice
  time to tips, rolls, and high-hands weekly; track shot variety in
  games.

- **Tipping into the block.** A short tip that lands in front of the
  3-meter line is often picked up by the middle blocker coming off
  the net, especially at higher levels. Fix: tip deep, not short;
  target behind the defender, not in front of the block.

- **Failure to read the block's fingers.** Hitter swings blind — same
  shot regardless of block formation. High-hands attempts go into
  sealed blocks, line shots close shut against a line-set block. Fix:
  explicitly train the block read during the approach; video review
  with the hitter identifying what she saw on each swing.

- **Forcing the kill on bad sets.** Hitter tries to swing hard cross
  off a 30-foot-off-the-net set, producing a block kill or a hit-out.
  Fix: teach out-of-system defaults (high-line, roll, high-seam); make
  "keep it in" a hitting-efficiency metric in practice.

- **Telegraphed tips.** Hitter jabs a tip so obviously the defense
  reads it. Fix: tip from the full swing motion; only the last instant
  of the hand changes from fist to palm. Film check — if the coach can
  tell a tip is coming, the back-row defender can too.

- **Using the same tool every time.** Only going high-hands on the
  outside hand eventually draws an inside-hand block that stuffs it.
  Fix: rotate line, cross, tip, roll, and tool even in drills.

- **No self-knowledge of hitting type.** The hitter can't articulate
  her strengths. She trains generically. Fix: Dunning's question —
  classify yourself, build a development plan around that.

## Schools-perspectives

Shot selection is a legitimately contested skill area. The core
disagreement is how the decision-making actually develops — through
menu instruction, through exposure, or through selection pressure —
and what shot the "default" should be.

**Art of Coaching Volleyball (toolbox / self-awareness).** AOC content —
Dunning, Liskevych, Schmidt — teaches shot selection as a **menu** the
hitter learns and expands. Dunning's "what kind of hitter are you"
question pushes deliberate self-classification and targeted development.
Liskevych's ASICS hitting series walks through each shot as a distinct
teachable skill. A hitter's ceiling is set by how many shots she
executes reliably and how well she reads the block that tells her which
to use. See [[coaches/john-dunning]] and [[schools/art-of-coaching-
volleyball]].

**Gold Medal Squared (random-practice exposure).** GMS argues decision-
making cannot be drilled as a separate skill from execution — it
emerges from game-like reps under varied conditions. Their clinic
curriculum limits drill variety to ~12 core activities, run in
randomized whole-skill formats. The hitter develops shot selection
through exposure to varied block fronts and set locations, not through
a "shot menu." See [[schools/gold-medal-squared]] and
[[schools/block-vs-random-practice]]. GMS and AOC differ in emphasis
more than substance — both use the toolbox and the randomized reps.

**Russian / Soviet tradition (pressure-hitting).** The Soviet lineage
teaches hitting as a power-first discipline. Primary shot is
hard-driven and in the court — finesse shots are specialized tools,
not defaults. The hitter's job is to pressure the defense; a hitter
who tips too often is leaking pressure. Practice emphasizes repetition
of hard swings against live block-and-defense with strict in/out
accounting. See [[schools/russian-school]].

**Italian tradition (versatility within a reliability frame).** Italian
volleyball's defensive and ball-control identity shapes hitters to
keep the ball in and trust the system to produce another swing. Shot
variety is developed but bounded by reliability — hitters who
terminate with risky tool or tip attempts that sail out are
disciplined toward "keep it in and swing again." See
[[schools/italian-school]].

**Brazilian tradition (creativity and tempo).** Brazilian hitters are
known for angle unpredictability and disguise. Attack the whole court;
use tempo variance (quick middles, pipes, slides, combos) to collapse
the block before the shot is chosen; hit off-beat angles. The school
treats shot selection as a creativity problem as much as a reading
problem — produce a shot the defense cannot predict. See
[[schools/brazilian-school]].

**Where the schools converge.** Block reading, topspin contact,
two-foot plant, and the core shots (cross, line, tip, roll, tool) are
agreed across all five traditions. The disagreement is about emphasis
and about the hitter's default when in doubt.

## Related drills

- [[hitter-vs-defender]] — cooperative-competitive 1v1 against a
  single defender; forces shot variety because a single direction is
  predictable.
- [[target-hitting-cones]] — cones placed in specific court zones
  (short line, deep cross, high seam); hitter scores by hitting the
  called target.
- [[block-read-attack]] — three-blocker random front, hitter must
  choose shot on the read; no "called" shot.
- [[shot-menu-rotation]] — each hitter cycles through a fixed menu
  (cross, line, tip, roll, tool) across a series of sets.
