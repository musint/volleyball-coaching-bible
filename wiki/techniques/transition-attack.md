# Transition — Attack

## Overview

Transition attack is the offensive contact that follows a defensive play. It is where most rallies past serve-receive are won or lost, and where the speed gap between elite programs and developing ones is most visible. A team that sides out on first-ball attack but cannot transition relies entirely on its serve receive; a team that transitions cleanly converts every dig into an attack. At the international men's level, transition speed is the dominant offensive variable; at the club and 14U level, transition is the most undertrained phase of the game.

The underlying mechanics are built on [[hitting-approach]] — same two-footed plant, same acceleration on the last two steps, same shoulder orientation — executed from wherever the hitter was at the moment the team regained the ball. A front-row hitter who just blocked is two feet off the net; an outside hitter who just passed is 15 feet off and facing the opponent; a back-row hitter who just dug is inside the 3-meter line. All three need to be in approach position faster than the block can organize. Out-of-system transition is on [[transition-out-of-system]].

## Teaching progressions

**Beginner — clear, then approach.** The foundational skill is just clearing space. A hitter who blocked, landed, and started an approach from the net is too close to jump cleanly; a hitter who stood still is not transitioning. After any defensive contact, the first move is to clear — retreat off the net (front-row) or out of the 3-meter zone (back-row) — before the approach begins. The cue is "touch and go."

Drill clearing without a ball first: simulate a block, land, retreat to a taped approach-start spot. Then add the approach, then the set. A hitter who cannot clear reliably will not transition reliably.

**Beginner-intermediate — block-to-approach recovery.** For middles and opposites, transition starts with a clean two-footed block landing. A blocker who lands leaning forward or with crossed feet has added a full beat to the clear. Teach the landing explicitly — feet shoulder-width, weight centered, eyes tracking back to the ball — then the retreat: a crossover-and-run to the approach-start spot, hands already cocked, eyes already on the setter. For middles, the retreat is shorter and the quick-set window is about a second after the pass. Middles who transition well hit a quick off their own blocked ball; middles who don't are a silent drain on every non-first-ball attack.

**Intermediate — dig-to-approach.** Back-row hitters have more time (the ball has to travel setter-set-cross-net before they hit) but start deeper. Dig, clear beyond the 3-meter line, read the setter's second contact, approach. Dig-to-approach is positional — left-back digs set up pipe approaches, middle-back digs set up line-A approaches, right-back digs set up D-ball. The ball leaving the setter's hands is still the trigger; the cadence is still "slow-slow, fast-fast"; what changes is the starting point and the distance-to-plant.

**Intermediate — attack after free ball.** A free ball is a slow controlled ball coming over because the opponent could not attack. At 14U it is the dominant non-first-ball scenario. The teaching point is that a free ball is a chance to run real offense — quicks, shoots, back-row — not to spray up a high-outside and hope. Teams that treat free balls as "just pass and swing" leave points on the court; teams that run full offense off free balls gain a compounding edge. Drill free-ball transition as its own phase and score on which offensive option was run, not just on the point outcome.

**Intermediate-advanced — transition off a scramble.** The hardest case is when the defensive play itself was unstable — deflected block, dig behind the setter, pass the setter chased. The hitter has to read the instability and adjust: the set may be a high outside instead of a called quick, a bail to the pin instead of a combo, or may not come at all. Mark Rosen's transition drills and the AOC in-and-out-of-system transition work train this read-and-adjust explicitly, pairing transition reps with degraded first contacts so the hitter builds the read as part of the approach. Every hitter's transition repertoire should include: the in-system approach on a clean ball, the bail approach on a tight set, the high-outside approach on an off-net emergency, and the hold-for-reset approach when the setter has no viable option. The fourth is most underused — hitters swing at a ball the setter is delivering as a free ball, and give points away.

**Advanced — tempo maintenance across multiple contacts.** A team's transition speed across the second, third, fourth rally contact is the dominant elite offensive metric. Italian and Brazilian traditions built identity on this speed — quick-set middles transitioning off their own block, outsides running combinations off dig-to-setter flows, pipes in the same rhythm as pin attacks. The US men's program publicly identifies transition speed as the gap against elite European/Brazilian peers. A team that runs the same options on rally ball 3 as on rally ball 1 is running a different game. Train tempo maintenance by removing the "survive the rally" default — wash drills and extended rallies should score attack quality, not just point outcomes. McCutcheon's pattern-recognition frame applies directly: strong transition patterns make rallies predictable enough to run offense inside.

## Common errors

- **Over-committing to the block (no back-to-approach).** Front-row hitter lands and stays at the net. Correction: drill the touch-and-go landing; every block is followed by a retreat.
- **Slow recovery off the net.** Blocker walks back and arrives after the set is up. Correction: cross-step-and-run, not walk; hands loaded on the way back.
- **Approach from the wrong position.** Hitter transitions toward the pin but starts inside the court, losing angle. Correction: mark approach-start spots for every transition scenario.
- **Same shot selection as first-ball.** Transition sets often arrive a half-step different. Correction: coach shot selection inside transition reps — tips and off-speed are not concessions.
- **Losing cadence when transitioning from deep.** Back-row hitter runs further and arrives in a sprint. Correction: start earlier, preserve "slow-slow, fast-fast."
- **Approach without looking at the setter.** Hitter commits regardless of what the setter does. Correction: the trigger is still the ball leaving the setter's hands.
- **Middle-outside approach collision.** Both converge on the same pin. Correction: explicit transition rules — bail sets to the pin belong to the outside.
- **Transitioning as if the set will be perfect.** Hitter commits arm-loaded for a hard line swing; set comes tight and the swing shanks. Correction: approaches are 90% commits with a last-moment shot-selection adjustment.

## Schools-perspectives

Transition attack is not fully contested, but the schools weight it differently. The Italian men's lineage (Velasco through De Giorgi) built its identity on tempo-offense including transition tempo. The Brazilian lineage under Bernardinho shares the speed-first frame, including aggressive back-row attack in transition. The Japanese tradition emphasizes transition speed under its never-give-up ethic — a dig almost always becomes an attack, and quickly. The US tradition has historically been slower than Italian/Brazilian peers, and Kiraly's 2024 move to the men's NT has been framed publicly as in part a project to close that gap. At club and HS the consensus is stronger: transition tempo is undertrained, modern practice should train it explicitly (Rosen, AOC transition corpus), and teams that do gain a compounding advantage. The disagreement is in time allocation, not technical teaching.

## Related drills

- [[block-to-attack]] — middle blocker simulates a block, retreats, and takes a set; trains the block-to-approach transition in isolation.
- [[dig-to-attack]] — back-row hitter digs a coach-hit ball, clears, and takes a set; scales from single hitter to full team.
- [[free-ball-transition]] — coach throws a free ball over, team runs full offense off the free; scored on which offensive option was run.
- [[wash-transition]] — extended-rally drill where attack quality is scored across multiple transition touches, not just on the first contact.
