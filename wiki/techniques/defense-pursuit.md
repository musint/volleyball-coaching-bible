# Defense — Pursuit

## Overview

Pursuit is the movement skill that gets a defender from the read to the ball. The platform is useless if the body is not behind the ball; the read is wasted if the feet cannot cash it in. Modern teaching treats pursuit as the middle leg of **posture → pursuit → platform**, with the caveat that defensive time windows are shorter than serve-receive and the emergency-move inventory is larger.

Good pursuit is footwork-first: shuffles for short distances, crossover for longer, run-throughs when the ball cannot be midlined. Emergency moves — dive, roll, sprawl, pancake — are the last-line inventory. Teach normal footwork until it is the default; emergency moves are earned, not shortcuts.

## Teaching progressions

**Beginner — shuffle and stay square.** The default pursuit pattern is a shuffle: low fast steps with shoulders square to the net, hips facing the likely target, covering roughly five to six feet of court in two steps. Drill without a ball first until knees stay bent and shoulders stay square, then add a tossed ball.

Key teaching point: **pursue with the legs, not the arms.** A defender who reaches with the platform on a ball to the side converts pursuit into a desperate stab; a defender who shuffles and forms the platform at the end gets a clean dig. The vocabulary is "shuffle instead of reach," and it is one of the highest-leverage early teaching points in back-row defense. Traditional teaching in the 1990s let the arms lead on shorter balls; modern teaching is feet-first even for balls within arm's reach.

**Beginner-intermediate — crossover for longer distances.** Balls more than two steps away need a crossover: far foot crosses over near for a long stride, near foot recovers. The crossover converts lateral distance into speed in a way the shuffle cannot. The body rotates in the direction of travel, so the final platform has to rotate back toward target as the defender stops — a slightly less stable platform, but the defender is actually there. Rule of thumb: shuffle for one-step balls, crossover for two-plus-step balls, run-through for balls where stopping is impossible.

**Intermediate — the run-through.** When a ball travels fast away from the defender, the defender **passes through the ball** — runs under it, redirects with the platform without stopping, recovers. The cue: "don't stop at the ball, go through it." The platform is brief, often one-armed; the target is simply "up and in." Liskevych and Rose both emphasize that stopping at the ball is the worst run-through error — the stop breaks the dig into a stab, and the stab almost always fails.

**Intermediate — drop step and turn-and-run for deep balls.** A ball going over the head is the hardest read. The drop step — rotate hips, push off the near foot, open toward the ball's travel — is the start; a back-pedal or turn-and-run is the middle; the overhead dig is the end. The correction: **turn, don't back-pedal** on balls clearly behind the defender. A back-pedal costs speed and balance.

**Intermediate-advanced — emergency moves.** When no normal pursuit reaches, the defender commits to an emergency: a dive (forward, arms extended, platform flat toward target), sprawl (lateral, body wide), roll (Japanese *kaiten* lineage — contact, tuck the shoulder, roll to feet), pancake (palm slid under a ball about to hit the floor), or extension dive. Train each as an isolated skill first (dive onto a mat from kneeling, roll from seated, pancake from a kneeling reach), then integrate by varying feed distance so some balls demand the emergency and some don't. A defender with only one emergency move (usually the pancake at 14U) leaves balls on the floor; a defender with four or five in the inventory saves more. Kiraly's "Neville's Pepper Drill" is the representative integration — all back-row attacks, points for digs, negatives for non-pursuit.

**Advanced — platform commitment on the emergency.** The most common advanced error is failing to form a platform while diving or sprawling. A dive with palms open is a muff; a dive with the platform formed to target — even a one-arm platform with the opposite arm braced for landing — is a dig. Platform commitment is non-negotiable even on desperate pursuits.

## Common errors

- **Reaching with arms instead of moving feet.** The default failure. Correction: "shuffle, don't reach" — arms stay home, feet go to the ball, platform forms late.
- **Stopping at the ball on a run-through.** Defender arrests momentum, stabs, shanks. Correction: run through the ball, not to it; platform forms while still moving.
- **Back-pedaling on balls behind the defender.** Loses speed and balance. Correction: drop-step and turn-and-run for balls clearly over the head.
- **Diving as a default rather than an emergency.** Looks dramatic, loses efficiency. Correction: show on film that a dive on a reachable ball was a run-through the defender skipped.
- **Giving up the platform on the dive.** Palms open, single-hand slap. Correction: commit to a flat surface on at least one forearm even at full extension.
- **No commitment to pursuit after the read.** Sees the cue, calls it, does not move. Correction: drill read-to-move as a single action; verbalize "go" on the read.
- **Stiff-legged, upright pursuit.** Cannot absorb hard-driven balls at the end of pursuit. Correction: stay low; hips below knees before contact.
- **Chasing a teammate's ball.** Collision in the back-court. Correction: pursuit is always called — "mine," "out," or "you" — and teammates hold their zones until released.

## Related drills

- [[neville-pepper]] — USA WNT all-back-row-attack pepper drill; score design penalizes failure to pursue and rewards a dig quality, making pursuit the dominant skill under test.
- [[blitz-the-bro]] — every serve targets the libero; the libero has to pursue far serves that other formations would cover with a teammate.
- [[run-through-pursuit]] — coach rolls balls at varying distances, defender pursues and digs through; no stopping at the ball allowed.
- [[sprawl-emergency]] — isolated sprawl work from a squat start, then integrated into a pursuit-to-sprawl sequence.
