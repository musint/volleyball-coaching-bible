# Blocking hand position

## Overview
Hand position at the block decides whether the blocker stuffs the ball,
deflects it to an easy dig, or gets tooled out of bounds. Modern coaching is
near-consensus on the target: press both hands over the net, fingers spread
and stiff, thumbs up, palms angled down into the opposing court. The fight is
in the details — how high the hands start, how narrow they sit, whether the
blocker "swats" or "presses," and how the outside blocker seals the seam
versus turns the line in.

## Teaching progressions
**Stance and starting height.**

1. **Hands at face level, on the net or just off it.** The blocker starts in
   athletic posture roughly six inches off the net, hands up at eye level,
   elbows just below shoulder height. Hands do not drop between plays on good
   blocking teams. Hands at the waist mean a slow arrival at the net and a
   late contact above it.
2. **Shoulder-width, not wider.** Hands and forearms form a shape narrower
   than the shoulders — about the width of the torso. Wider than that opens
   a seam through the blocker's body the hitter can exploit.

**Over-the-net press.**

3. **Wall drill: press and stick.** Stand at a wall, jump and press both
   hands against it as high as possible, stick for one count, land. Teaches
   the shape of the penetrating block without a ball.
4. **Partner stationary pressing.** Partner stands on a box on the other
   side of the net, holds a ball at max-attack height. Blocker jumps and
   presses both hands against the ball, squeezing it between hand and net.
   No swinging, no slapping — just the shape.
5. **Partner attack, no jump.** Coach or partner hits gentle down-balls from
   a box; blocker jumps and presses. Rep the contact quality before adding
   game speed.

**Fingers, thumbs, seam.**

6. **Fingers spread and stiff.** Fingers wide apart and tensed, thumbs up.
   This enlarges the blocking surface and stiffens the deflection so the
   ball doesn't absorb through. Weak fingers = tooled blocks.
7. **Hands together for seam coverage.** Both hands inside the hitter's
   armswing with no gap between them — GMS cues "arms together when reaching
   over to avoid split-block surfaces." A half-inch gap between the hands is
   the seam every hitter looks for.
8. **Press down, not forward.** The palms angle toward the opposing 10-foot
   line. Pressing straight forward sends the block into the antenna; pressing
   down puts the ball in the opposing court.

**Positional adjustments.**

9. **Outside blocker: turn the line in on an on-system set.** Outside hand
   (the one over the sideline) turns its palm in slightly so a hit that deflects
   off it goes into the court, not into the stands. This is the single
   adjustment that converts most "block-out" tooling into an in-system dig.
10. **Middle blocker: square up.** Middle should be square to the attacker
    with both hands equally penetrating; a twisted middle leaves a seam for
    the wing blocker to cover alone.
11. **Live jumping contest.** Final step: two-blocker tandem against a
    live hitter; emphasize maintaining shape at contact rather than winning
    the rep.

**Hand path through the jump.**

12. **Hands stay on the path up.** The hands travel in a tight vertical line
    from eye-level starting position to over-the-net pressed position. They
    do not swing out wide to gain momentum, they do not drop first to load,
    and they do not cross the midline. Any deviation from that straight line
    costs time and separates the hands into a seam.
13. **Reach, don't throw.** The hands finish in a held pressed position with
    wrists firm — not whipping through the ball and dropping. Reaching
    finishes the penetration; throwing finishes in the blocker's own court.
14. **Retract on the way down.** After the block, bring the hands down
    inside the net on the landing so the blocker is ready to transition or
    block again. Pulling them down outside the net risks a net touch.

**Seam coverage between two blockers.**

15. **Inside hand goal: no daylight.** The two blockers' inside hands should
    be close enough that no ball can travel between them on a direct line
    from the hitter's contact. Cue pin and middle during drill: "your inside
    hand is your partner's inside hand."
16. **Communicate the press.** On a clean close, the outside blocker sets
    the shape and the middle matches into them — middle is usually the
    late-arriver on an attack out of the pin. A late middle with the right
    hand shape still closes the seam; a late middle with flapping hands
    does not.

## Common errors
- **Swatting instead of pressing.** Hands break the wrists forward and slap
  at the ball, losing penetration. Fix: cue "press and stick" — imagine
  holding the ball on the other side of the net.
- **Hands too narrow or too wide.** Too narrow opens seams around the block;
  too wide opens a seam between the hands. Fix: shoulder-width, hands
  together over the net.
- **Fingers not spread or soft.** Ball absorbs through the hand or deflects
  off. Fix: tension drill — blocker keeps fingers wide and stiff for a five-count
  before every jump.
- **Absorbing instead of deflecting.** Arms give on contact, ball drops into
  the blocker's own court. Fix: stiff wrists and elbows at contact; "block
  with your hands, not your arms."
- **Hands drop between plays.** Classic between-rally or post-move habit.
  Fix: drill hands-up as a permanent posture in 6v6 segments; coach to it.
- **Pressing into the antenna.** Blocker overcommits to an outside attacker
  and lands over the sideline. Fix: the outside hand is the boundary —
  it stays inside the antenna.
- **Swinging the block on a static close.** Moving the arms through the ball
  rather than pressing a stable shape across it. Swing-blocking finishes with
  pressed hands, not moving arms; if the arms are still moving at contact the
  swing has been mistimed.
