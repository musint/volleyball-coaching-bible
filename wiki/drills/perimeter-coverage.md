# Perimeter Coverage

## Setup
Six defenders in perimeter defense — back-row players hugging the sidelines and endline, middle-back playing deep in zone 6, with a designated off-blocker and libero. Coach on a box at zone 4 or zone 2. Place a target at the setter position for dig-to-target feedback.

## Execution
1. Coach calls a zone and attacks (or tips) into that zone; defense reads and covers.
2. Perimeter defenders own the sidelines and the deep corners; middle-back chases tips in front and long balls behind.
3. Dig must arrive at the target for the rep to score.
4. If the ball lands inside the perimeter seam (between a sideline defender and middle-back), the coach pauses and diagnoses the gap before the next rep.
5. Run 8-10 reps per attack zone, then rotate the attack position.

## Coaching points
- Perimeter's strength is the sidelines and corners; its weakness is the middle seam — middle-back's first move is forward, not back.
- Off-blocker pulls hard and low; do not stand up to watch the attack.
- Sideline defenders stay wide — the block takes the middle, you have the gap.
- Read the hitter's shoulder angle: closed shoulder = cross-court; open shoulder = line. The defense rotates off that cue.
- Deep tips are the perimeter killer — someone calls "tip" loudly or the middle-back sells out forward too late.

## Variations
- **Tip-only rep:** coach tips to every zone so middle-back drills forward coverage.
- **Mix with rotation defense:** alternate perimeter and middle-back rotations so the team feels the tradeoff — see [[six-player-defense]].

## Adaptations by level
- **14U:** simplify to four back-row defenders and a designated middle-back; skip the off-blocker read.
- **HS:** full 6-player perimeter with a blocker read layer.
- **College:** live block-and-defend with counter-attack transition.
