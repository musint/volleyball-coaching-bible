# Commit Block Trigger

## Setup
Three blockers on one side. Other side: setter, middle running a 1-set,
right-side running a slide. Coach feeds passes from zone 6 at varying
tempo. The middle blocker is the primary subject; pins respond to what
the middle does.

Designate one commit cue ahead of the drill: either setter-eye-tell
(setter looks at the middle before the quick) or quick-set tempo tell
(pass in zone 2-3 on-net, middle hitter already in the air). The cue is
a live read, not a coach signal.

## Execution
Two rules for the middle:

1. **Cue fires → commit.** Move on release toward the middle's attack
   point, jump with the hitter, press hands on the quick regardless of
   where the set actually goes.
2. **Cue does not fire → read.** Hold ground, let the ball leave the
   setter's hands, react to slide or outside help.

Pins follow a secondary rule: if the middle commits, the far pin closes
one step inside to rebuild the block. If the middle reads, pins hold
their normal alignment.

Run 20 reps. Score each rep on two axes: did the middle read the cue
correctly, and did the pins react to the commit on time.

## Coaching points
Commit is not a guess — it is a response to a specific cue decided on
before the rally. A middle who commits on feeling gets baited; one who
never commits gets beat on the quick. Commit is a weapon against the
opponent's best hitter or their fast 1-set, not a default posture. Pins
see the middle's feet in peripheral vision and react.

## Variations
Rotate the cue mid-drill — switch from eye-tell to tempo-tell after 10
reps. Add a quick-set fake where the setter back-sets the right side;
the commit is now punished and the pin rebuild must be sharp.

## Adaptations by level
14U: skip this drill. Commit blocking demands reading and team
coordination that aren't in place yet. HS: run with a single cue only —
tempo-tell is easier than eye-tell. College: run both cues with setter
disguise.
