# Practice Planning

## Overview

Practice planning is where coaching philosophy meets the clock. Every decision — what to drill, how long, in what order, at what intensity, with what feedback — is a bet on how motor learning works and what the season needs next. The modern evidence base has shifted most serious volleyball programs in the same direction: more meaningful touches per hour, more random and variable practice, more training on the bounce of the whistle, less blocked repetition with a stationary coach-toss. The old "lines of forearm passes to a tossing coach" practice is a relic. The reference frame now is meaningful-touches-per-hour — how many times did the athlete make a consequential decision against a live stimulus in a game-like context.

Four modern frames anchor the methodology landscape. Gold Medal Squared pushes random-over-blocked practice built on motor-learning research (contextual-interference effect): initial in-session performance is worse, but retention and transfer are dramatically better. USA Volleyball's game-like-training frame (Rick Swan et al. in the 2009 CAP materials) makes the same point pedagogically: if you want the skill to show up in matches, the practice has to look like the match — opposing intent, real tempo, full-speed decisions. Ecological dynamics — through Pinder, Davids, Woods and the constraints-led approach — reframes the same question as representative learning design: practice is representative to the extent that the perception-action couplings on the court match the couplings in competition. Finally, Karch Kiraly's "training efficiently" and "minutes count" framing and Russ Rose's "compete every rep" culture push the same conclusion from a competitive-intent direction: every minute, every rep, something is on the line.

The Japanese tradition layers on something the random-practice camp sometimes underweights: deliberate repetition with purpose. Daimatsu and the tradition that followed him earned their "precision" reputation through sheer volume on specific cues — but always with intent and competitive context, not mindless blocked reps. Modern AOC (Art of Coaching Volleyball) sits eclectically across these traditions: competing-in-practice, reading emphasis in every skill, video-first review, and a strong bias toward team drills that earn points or lose points based on execution.

## Major subtopics

- **Session design** — the arc of a single practice: warm-up, skill-development, strategic, competition, cool-down. Modern sessions front-load live-ball contact and keep blocked segments short and targeted. See [[gold-medal-squared]], [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]], [[game-based-training]], [[usa-volleyball]].
- **Periodization** — how training load and content change across a macrocycle (season), mesocycles (weeks), and microcycles (days). Volume high and complexity moderate in preseason, intensity high and volume trimmed mid-season, taper into peaking for regionals/nationals. See [[season-planning]].
- **Microcycle planning** — the week-by-week flow. Hard-technical Monday, game-like Tuesday, video + corrective Wednesday, team-tactic Thursday, pre-tournament activation Friday is a typical US-club shape; HS and college adjust around match nights.
- **Practice ratios** — the mix of blocked vs. random, technical vs. tactical, individual vs. team. The modern default skews heavily random-and-tactical once fundamentals are stable. See [[block-vs-random-practice]].
- **Game-like training** — Swan's USAV frame: drills that replicate the opposing-intent, tempo, decision-load, and scoring pressure of competition. Random-practice, representative-learning-design, and competing-in-practice are all game-like-training under different theoretical hats.
- **Competing in practice** — Kiraly's "training efficiently" and Rose's "everything matters" both converge on the same operational rule: score every segment, celebrate winners, consequence losers, make practice the hardest thing in the athlete's week. Penn State's seven NCAA titles were built on this.
- **Practice energy & focus** — the coach's attentional economy: where your voice goes, where feedback lands, when to let a rep live and when to stop-and-teach. The 50-percent-drill and "asking questions" styles push athlete-driven focus over coach-driven instruction.

## Schools of thought

- [[gold-medal-squared]] — motor-learning orthodoxy: random/variable practice, specificity, external-focus cues, reading-reaction primacy, skill-simplification. The cleanest articulation of "maximize meaningful touches per hour."
- [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] — pedagogically eclectic; reading-centric; video-first review; strong on competing-in-practice and team-drill-earn-points framing.
- [[ecological-dynamics]] — constraints-led approach, representative learning design, perception-action coupling. Frames practice as designing information-rich environments, not teaching movements.
- [[game-based-training]] — TGfU (Bunker & Thorpe) / Play Practice (Launder) / Games Sense heritage applied to volleyball; tactical-awareness before isolated technique; modified games as the default unit.
- [[block-vs-random-practice]] — the methodology comparison itself: contextual-interference effect (Shea & Morgan 1979), Magill 1990 review, and how schools differ on where to set the blocking↔random balance.
- [[japanese-training]] — deliberate repetition with purpose, defense-first intensity, ball-up-always pursuit culture; overlays competitive context on high-volume technical work.
- [[usa-volleyball]] — the federation's game-like-training frame (2009 CAP, modern Coach Academy); pillar-based coach education (Craft/Body/Mind/Heart/Team).

## Getting started

If you are new to volleyball methodology and trying to upgrade how you plan practice, read in this order:

1. [[block-vs-random-practice]] — start with the evidence base. Understand the contextual-interference effect before you decide how to structure anything.
2. [[gold-medal-squared]] — the cleanest American application of motor-learning research to volleyball practice design.
3. AOC flagship practice pieces — Kiraly's training-efficiently and competing-in-practice posts, Rose's 10-things-learned and coach-for-life, Liskevych's controlling-feelings (see [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]]).
4. [[ecological-dynamics]] — if you want the theoretical frame underneath why random and representative practice works.
5. Your age-lens page — [[age-lens-14u]] for club 14U, [[age-lens-hs]] for HS varsity, [[age-lens-college]] for college. Developmental overlays change what random-practice can demand and what belongs in skill-blocked work.
