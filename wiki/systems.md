# Systems

## Overview

A team's "system" is usually spoken about in the singular — "we run a 5-1" — but that phrase is shorthand for a stack of four layered systems that have to mesh: an offensive alignment, a serve-receive formation, a team-blocking scheme, and a back-row defense (with a transition pattern tying all three back to the offense). Swap one layer and the others shift. Move from commit-blocking to read-blocking and your defensive landmarks have to change because blocker timing changes. Move from 4-passer receive to libero-split and the OHs now start in hitting lanes more often, which changes the transition flow. The detail pages below treat each layer independently; the skill is learning how they combine.

The modern HS-and-above default stack is a 5-1 offense paired with read-blocking, a libero-split 3-passer serve-receive, and either rotation defense or a middle-back-libero scheme. One setter all six rotations, three blockers reading the setter and hitter before committing, the libero and one or two partners absorbing most serves, and the back row rotating around where the set goes. This is what every elite college and professional program runs some variant of.

The 14U club stack is meaningfully different. Most 14U teams run a 4-2 (two setters, always front row) or 6-2 (two setters, always back row when setting), paired with commit-blocking or release-to-zone team blocking, a 4-passer serve-receive, and perimeter defense. The reasons are developmental: reading is a skill that grows slowly, so commit-blocking removes the decision; libero-split puts enormous pressure on a single passer, so 4-passer keeps coverage even; a 5-1 demands a setter who can run the offense in all six rotations, which few 14U athletes can yet. At 12U/14U entry, the 6-6 "everyone plays everywhere" offense is common as a pure developmental system. HS varsity sits between the poles — installing 5-1, introducing read-blocking as swing-blocking footwork matures, moving to libero-split once the libero is trained. The age-lens pages ([[age-lens-14u]], [[age-lens-hs]], [[age-lens-college]]) lay out what a given developmental level can actually run.

## Major subtopics

- **Offense systems** — how the six positions distribute setting, attacking, and back-row responsibility. 5-1, 6-2, 4-2, 6-6 are structural choices; quick, pipe, slide, high-ball are tempo/attack patterns layered within.
- **Defense systems** — how the back row covers the court behind the block. Perimeter, rotation, man-back, middle-back-libero.
- **Serve-receive systems** — how many passers absorb the serve and how responsibilities split. 3-player, 4-player, libero-split.
- **Team-blocking systems** — how the three front-row blockers coordinate against the opposing offense. Read, commit, swing (footwork overlay).
- **Age-appropriate selection** — which combinations match which developmental level.
- **System layering** — how the layers combine coherently, and what shifts when you change one in isolation.

## Offense systems

- [[offense-5-1]] — HS-and-above default: one setter all six rotations.
- [[offense-6-2]] — two setters rotating; always three front-row attackers; coordination-heavy.
- [[offense-4-2]] — two setters always front-row; the 14U entry system.
- [[offense-6-6]] — "everyone plays everywhere" developmental 12U/14U system.
- [[offense-quick]] — fast-tempo middle attack (1 / 31 / slide) layered over 5-1 or 6-2.
- [[offense-pipe]] — OH back-row attack by zone/rotation.
- [[offense-slide]] — middle one-leg-takeoff angled attack behind the setter.
- [[offense-high-ball]] — out-of-system bail-to-pin default.

## Defense systems

- [[defense-perimeter]] — "ring the fishbowl"; fixed-landmark coverage; 14U-friendly default.
- [[defense-rotation]] — adaptive-by-set; demands accurate reading.
- [[defense-man-back]] — deep-ball dedicated coverage; pairs with commit blocking.
- [[defense-middle-back]] — libero-in-6 middle-back; modern HS+ default.

## Serve-receive systems

- [[serve-receive-3-player]] — modern HS+ 3-passer; libero plus two OHs or one OH and a DS.
- [[serve-receive-4-player]] — 14U entry 4-passer; evenly-split responsibility.
- [[serve-receive-libero-split]] — modern HS+ default; libero gets a wide central zone.

## Team-blocking systems

- [[read-blocking]] — modern HS+ default; setter and hitter read before committing.
- [[swing-blocking]] — mandatory footwork overlay at HS+ women's; typically layered on read-blocking.
- [[commit-blocking]] — pre-read alternative; situational against a dominant middle, or used at 14U to simplify the decision.

## Schools of thought

Tradition influences system choice. The [[italian-school]] (Velasco-rooted) and [[brazilian-school]] (Bernardinho-rooted) both favor tempo-centric offense — fast varied middle attack, aggressive slide, early-rotation pipe — paired with pin-heavy read blocking. The [[russian-school]] legacy (Platonov through Karpol) is systems-heavy on defense: block-first, tight rotation defense, dig-up-everything back-court intensity. [[japanese-training]] emphasizes precision over power — 4-passer-style defensive receive, deep pursuit, emergency moves — visible in the "no free balls" and "dig up everything" frames. [[usa-volleyball]] and US college volleyball have converged on the 5-1 + read-blocking + libero-split + rotation-defense stack as the default competitive template. [[gold-medal-squared]]'s contribution is methodological: whatever system you run, practice it in representative, random, game-like form so it shows up in matches. Russ Rose's Penn State rigor across 42 seasons captures the operational rule — drill what you run, and run what you drill [aoc-2018-rose-offensive-systems].

## Getting started

Building a team system from scratch, read in this order:

1. Pick an **offense** for your level: [[offense-4-2]] for 14U or brand-new players; [[offense-6-2]] for two capable setters without one clearly better; [[offense-5-1]] for one clearly-better setter with a team that can coordinate around her.
2. Pair with a **defense**: [[defense-perimeter]] for 14U or early-season; [[defense-middle-back]] once the libero is locked in; [[defense-rotation]] once your team reads accurately.
3. Add a **serve-receive**: [[serve-receive-4-player]] for 14U or early season; [[serve-receive-libero-split]] once the libero is clearly your best passer; [[serve-receive-3-player]] as the common HS+ default.
4. Add **team blocking**: [[commit-blocking]] or landmark blocking at 14U; [[read-blocking]] with [[swing-blocking]] footwork at HS+.
5. Read [[practice-planning]] and [[aoc-2024-kiraly-in-game-tactics]] for how to drill the stack in competition-realistic form and adjust mid-match.

## Age-appropriate system stacks

The "right" stack is the one the team can actually execute under pressure. Rough guidance:

- **14U stack** — 4-2 or 6-2 / 4-passer / commit or landmark blocking / perimeter defense. Emphasis on ball control, receive repetition, basic rotations, decisions simple enough to execute at full speed. Rotations are themselves a new skill [aoc-2021-teach-rotations-beginners]. See [[age-lens-14u]].
- **HS varsity stack** — 5-1 (installing or installed) / libero-split 3-passer or transitional 4-passer / read-blocking with swing-blocking footwork / middle-back-libero or early rotation defense. See [[age-lens-hs]].
- **College stack** — 5-1 / libero-split 3-passer / read-blocking with full swing-blocking footwork / rotation defense or middle-back-libero; full back-row attack integration, in-match audibles, opponent-specific adjustments. See [[age-lens-college]].

Stacks are reference points, not prescriptions. The 5-1 exists at 14U for advanced teams; the 4-2 exists at HS for programs rebuilding depth. Each systems-detail page notes its own age-appropriateness.
