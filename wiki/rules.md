# Rules

## Overview

Rules are governance, not coaching. In US indoor volleyball the federation hierarchy is unambiguous: USA Volleyball (USAV) is the national governing body and publishes the Indoor Rulebook governing club play from youth through adult; the NCAA publishes the Women's Volleyball Rules Book governing collegiate competition and diverges from USAV/FIVB in a small number of consequential places; and the NFHS plus each state federation govern scholastic play, layering variants on top (libero exchange counting, uniform conventions, match format). Youth play drops into USAV's Simplified Youth Rules for ages 3-11 — a stripped-down framework that adapts court size, net height, ball weight, team size, and blocking norms to developmental level.

This matters to a coach whose athletes will progress across federations because what is legal, illegal, and *taught-as-illegal* shifts as they move. A 14U player learns first-contact doubles as legal (USAV since 2000), arrives in HS expecting the same ruling with slight NFHS variance, and if she plays NCAA encounters dual-libero teams and serve-receive formations that assume it. The libero rule is the cleanest example: the most mechanically strict position rule on the court (no block, no attack above net height, hand-set restriction in the front court, unique jersey, substitution math separate from the team limit, serving permitted under NCAA but not FIVB), and correct execution requires knowing which rulebook you're playing under.

This hub is navigational. Specific numerical values (net heights, set lengths, server cap, antenna specs) live on subordinate pages and should be cross-checked against the current-year rulebook before game-day use — the USAV and NCAA rulebooks are both annually revised.

## Major subtopics

- **USAV indoor rules essentials** — three hits per possession with first-contact double exception, 8-second serve clock, net and centerline contact rules, ball-handling (carries, lifts, prolonged contact), rotation and positional faults, and match format. What most US club, HS, and adult indoor play rests on.
- **NCAA rule differences** — divergences from USAV/FIVB most relevant for college-bound tracking: libero-serving in one rotation (since 2004), dual-libero designation (fall 2024), video-challenge/replay, and media-timeout structure. Match format is best-of-5 to 25 with a 15-point fifth set.
- **Scholastic variants (NFHS + state federations)** — the layer 14U coaches encounter via their athletes' HS seasons. Match format is typically best-of-5 to 25 (fifth set to 15) but libero substitution-exchange counting, uniform rules, and officiating protocols shift by state. Read the state book every preseason.
- **Libero rules** — the highest rule-density position: no block or attempt-to-block, no attack of a ball contacted entirely above net height, hand-set must come from behind the 3-meter line to generate a legal front-court attack, contrasting jersey required, substitution into/out of the back row does not count against the team limit, and serving privileges differ by federation (banned in FIVB, one rotation in NCAA, permitted in most USAV competition since 2020). See [[libero]].
- **Tournament formats: pool-to-bracket and best-of-N** — US club tournaments run pool play (3-5 teams in a pool, round-robin) feeding bracket play (single or double elimination). Pool matches are typically best-of-3 to 25 (third set to 15); championship brackets move to best-of-5. NCAA regular-season and postseason is best-of-5 throughout. AAU and USAV Junior Nationals publish tournament-specific formats in the event manual.
- **Rally scoring** — every rally ends in a point (side-out scoring ended FIVB 1999-2000, NCAA 2001). Sets to 25 with a two-point margin; deciding fifth to 15. The scoring environment every modern tactical and statistical system is built on.
- **Youth rule adaptations** — USAV's Simplified Youth Rules for ages 3-11 (Kessel) reduce court size, net height (5-6 ft youngest, up to 7 ft for 12U), ball weight, and team size (2v2, 3v3, or 4v4) to match developmental capacity. First-contact doubles are always legal, single-player blocking is the norm, coach-issued server caps (3-5 per rotation) prevent dominance, and unlimited substitution is encouraged. See [[age-lens-14u]] for the bridge into full-court 14U play.

## Schools of thought

Rules are federation-issued, so "schools of thought" apply loosely — schools disagree about coaching method and tactical philosophy, not about what the rulebook says. The relevant school here is [[usa-volleyball]], the US governing body that issues the rulebook and the Simplified Youth Rules coaching document. Coaches who track international play should know that FIVB and the NCAA sometimes diverge — the dual-libero rule was FIVB-first and NCAA-adopted later. On disputed applications of the same rulebook (e.g., what counts as a "held" contact), consensus moves through officiating clinics and AVCA / USAV officials-committee interpretations, not coaching schools.

## Getting started

For a coach new to US indoor rules, or preparing to coach across levels, read in this order:

1. Current USAV Indoor Rulebook (annual, usavolleyball.org/rules). Every USAV club tournament plays under it.
2. USAV Simplified Youth Rules ([[usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]]) — the adapted framework for ages 3-11 and the conceptual basis for developmentally appropriate 14U play.
3. Your state HS federation's volleyball rulebook (NFHS-aligned, state-specific). Read the state book every preseason; revisions happen yearly.
4. Current-year NCAA Women's Volleyball Rules Book (NCAA.org). Essential for college-bound tracking; 14U/HS coaches get asked about college rules.
5. [[libero]] — the rule-density deserves dedicated study; rotation and substitution errors trace to shallow libero-rule understanding.
6. [[usav-2026-coach-academy]] and [[usav-2026-resources-for-coaches]] — USAV coach education frames rules inside the broader coach-development curriculum.
