# 14U 60-Minute Tryout Practice

## Context

You are running a 60-minute 14U club tryout session — one slot of a multi-slot evaluation day. The goal is not to teach; it is to see. You need a clean read on each athlete's current pass, set, attack, and serve under moderate pressure, plus a live-play sample that shows how she competes with rotating teammates. Every block is evaluator-oriented: rotating stations for per-skill signal, a queen's-court competition for adaptability. Written tryout plans specifying skills, hustle, and team situations are the baseline for any thoughtful 14U evaluation. See [[age-lens-14u]] for the age-appropriate lens on what you are looking for.

## Learning objectives

- Gather a comparable per-skill evaluation (pass, set, attack, serve) on every athlete within 30 minutes.
- See each athlete compete with at least two different teammate combinations.
- Identify clear libero, setter, and front-row candidate pools for post-tryout roster discussion.
- Assess hustle, body language, and coachability — the tryout signals that don't show up in skill grids.
- Finish on time with evaluator notes captured per station, so the selection meeting is not a memory test.

## Time blocks

**Note on structure:** the usual strategic block is omitted at a 60-minute tryout. You are evaluating, not installing. The 30-minute skill block is structured as rotating evaluation stations rather than a single drill, which is why the time weight lives there.

### Warm-up (10 min)
- `[[dynamic-warmup-volleyball]]` — full-team ball-in-hand movement prep. Watch movement quality, shoulder range, and how athletes carry themselves when not yet being watched for skill. First impressions start here.

### Skill development — rotating evaluation stations (30 min)
Split the floor into four stations. Groups of ~4 athletes rotate every 7 minutes. Evaluators stay at stations; athletes move. Keep evaluators out of coaching mode — observe and score.

- `[[two-line-passing]]` (7 min) — pass read. Coach or assistant tosses/serves short, tracking platform angle, first-step direction, and target accuracy. Score each athlete on a 1–5 rubric.
- `[[target-setting]]` (7 min) — hand-hinge and contact point. Athletes set to a high-outside target from a tossed ball; rotate in a jump-set rep for setter candidates.
- `[[approach-and-swing]]` (7 min) — 3-step approach mechanics and arm swing from a tossed ball or front-row set. Watch timing, high contact, and how the athlete recovers into a second attempt.
- `[[serve-targets]]` (7 min) — standing float serves to zone 1 and zone 5 targets. Score in/out/target; flag obvious toss inconsistency separately from outcome.

Rotation cost is ~2 minutes total across four moves. Blow the whistle hard on transitions; tryout-day chaos is the biggest time thief.

### Competition (15 min)
- `[[queen-of-the-court]]` (15 min) — teams of 3, free-ball entries. The specific value here for evaluators is that teammate pairings shift every rally, so you see each athlete with multiple partners under live pressure. Hofer's tryout variant is explicitly designed to expose adaptability, not just skill. Run teams of 3 at 14U and keep entries as free balls; live serve entries compress the eval window by producing too many shanks.

### Cool-down (5 min)
Team down in a circle. Thank each athlete by name. Clear communication on the notification timeline (when they'll hear, how, from whom) — parents appreciate specifics over vagueness. Do not announce roster decisions on court.

## Coaching cues

- Evaluators observe, do not correct. Correction contaminates the signal.
- Score each athlete per station on a written rubric. End-of-session memory is unreliable after 4 groups.
- Rotate evaluators between stations systematically, not randomly — each evaluator should see every athlete at least once.
- Call athletes by jersey number in notes, by name to their faces.
- Flag athletes who sustain effort between reps, not just during them. Effort during the rep is table stakes; effort between reps separates.
- Jump-test quickly if roster-size signal warrants; do it during warm-up rotation so it doesn't eat competition time.
- Keep the athletic layer visible — setup hustle, shag hustle, teammate encouragement — all of which show up most clearly in queen's court.

## Variations

- **Smaller pool (≤12 athletes):** drop one station, extend others to 10 minutes each; add a 5-minute server-vs-passer 1v1 to sharpen head-to-head signal on your toughest libero/OH cuts.
- **Intensity up:** substitute live serve entries for queen-of-the-court and add a 3-minute pressure-serving round at the end of the serve station.
- **Duration up (90 min):** add a 20-minute skill segment and a 10-minute 4v4 wash block; the underlying evaluation structure stays identical.

## Adaptations by level

- **One level down (12U):** cut the approach station; replace with a live-toss hitting rep from a high ball. Shorten queen's court to 10 minutes and extend warm-up. Expect less signal per station and plan more tryout slots.
- **One level up (HS tryout):** longer skill block (45 min), add a blocking station, upgrade queen-of-the-court to serve entries, and insert a 10-minute strategic 6v6 rotation-recognition block. Evaluators scout defensive transition as a separate lens.
