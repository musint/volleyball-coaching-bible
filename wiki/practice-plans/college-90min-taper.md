# College 90-minute Taper Practice

## Context

This is the day-before practice for a college team heading into a conference weekend or a postseason bracket match. The planning frame is simple: the work is already done. A taper session is not a teaching session, not a conditioning session, and not a dress rehearsal of new material. You are sharpening reps, confirming signals, and protecting legs. See [[age-lens-college]] for the broader practice-design frame at this level — 2-to-3 hour practices are the in-season baseline, but on match-minus-one you compress hard. The companion page [[match-prep]] carries the scouting-and-film layer that feeds this session; by the time athletes step on the floor, the scouting report has already been delivered in the film room, and this practice operationalizes two or three key lines — a serving target, a rotation tell, a defensive adjustment — rather than installing anything new. Keep reps crisp, volume low, intensity moderate. Dunning's frame from Stanford applies: you want to be the most relaxed, most confident team on the floor tomorrow, not the most tired one.

## Learning objectives

- Execute every out-of-system and in-system signal without hesitation across all six rotations.
- Hit 80%-plus serve-in rate in [[zone-serving]] with scouted targets.
- Pass at a 2.3+ team average in [[serve-receive-competition]] against scout-mirroring servers.
- Finish the session with zero high-intensity flush reps, zero new installations, and full touch volume under a 90-minute cap.
- Leave the gym with the scouting report's two or three highest-leverage points confirmed, not re-debated.

## Time blocks

### Warm-up (15 min)

Run [[dynamic-warmup-volleyball]] for 8 minutes — mobility, activation, and ball-in-hand progressions through the progressions the team already owns. No ladder drills, no maximal jumps, no novel movements. Follow with 7 minutes of [[partner-pepper-warmup]] at controlled tempo — three-contact pepper with setter-target emphasis so touch and timing line up before any structured block. Keep servers off the line during warm-up to avoid early shoulder load.

### Skill development (15 min)

One block only: [[target-setting]] for 15 minutes with the setters working front, back, and out-of-system windows from live passes off coach-controlled tosses. Hitters attack at roughly 70 percent of game swing and hold the approach, not the finish — the point is to confirm timing, not to generate peak-load hitting volume. This is the lightest skill block of any college practice in the microcycle; resist the instinct to extend it.

### Strategic (20 min)

Walk and run rotation/signal rehearsal tied to the scouting report. Ten minutes of dry rotation run-through — each rotation, calling the serve target, the blocking scheme (bunch-read vs pin-pressed, MB commit vs read), and the defensive look against the opponent's primary pin attacker. Then 10 minutes of 6v0 signal rehearsal: setters call plays off simulated passes, the front row calls blocks, the back row calls defensive rotations. No rallies, no termination — just the first 2 contacts of every rep, at signal speed. This is where Rose's Penn State rotation-by-rotation scouting discipline cashes in — the team has to know the plan cold before tomorrow's first serve.

### Competition (30 min)

Split into two 15-minute blocks. First, [[zone-serving]] — each server gets a scouted target zone (typically short-6, deep-1, or the opposing weakest passer's seam per the scout). Track serve-in rate on a whiteboard; target 80 percent or better with aggressive (not max) serve selection. Second, [[serve-receive-competition]] with the bench-side feeding serves that mirror the scouted opponent's delivery mix. Play to 5 with the 0-3 grade scale; rotate passers through all three receiving lanes so no one hides. Keep both blocks controlled — no flush, no conditioning punishment, no run-the-lines. The competition is about rehearsing decisions under mild pressure, not generating fatigue.

### Cool-down (10 min)

Finish with [[cool-down-mobility]] — 10 minutes of guided mobility, breathing, and shoulder-girdle decompression. End with a 3-minute team huddle: the head coach names the two or three scouting points the team owns tomorrow, nothing more. No film, no new notes, no long speech. The session ends on time and the team leaves the gym with legs under them.

## Coaching cues

- "Crisp, not heavy" — every touch is deliberate; nothing is maximal.
- "Know the call before the whistle" — signals out of the mouth before the serve toss.
- "Target, not safe" — serves hunt the scouted zone, not a neutral middle.
- "Walk it before you run it" — rotation rehearsal at signal speed, not rally speed.
- "Trust the work" — the taper is not where you catch up; it is where you protect what you have.
- "One more rep is not free" — every extra swing costs tomorrow.
- "Pass to the window" — the passer's job tomorrow is the same as today; hit the setter.
- "Finish on time" — the clock runs the session; the coach does not extend.

## Variations

- **Team size knob:** with travel-roster cuts (12 athletes on a bracket trip), collapse the 6v0 signal-rehearsal block to 3v3 half-court signal walk and keep the competition at 4v4.
- **Intensity knob:** if legs are heavy coming off a Friday match with a Saturday turnaround, shrink competition to 20 minutes, replace [[zone-serving]] with cooperative serving for percentage, and extend cool-down to 15.
- **Duration knob:** compress to 60 minutes by trimming warm-up to 10, cutting the second competition block, and keeping only the first 10-minute zone-serving segment.

## Adaptations by level

- **Down to HS:** fewer signals to rehearse; replace the rotation-by-rotation scouting references with a simpler "serve the weakest passer, block the best attacker" frame. Competition stays controlled but the 0-3 grade scale can be simplified to good/okay/shank. See [[age-lens-hs]].
- **Up to professional / national team:** similar shape but warm-up extends with position-specific prehab programmed by the S&C staff, and the strategic block often adds a 10-minute opponent-film review on court-side screens between rotation reps. Volume stays compressed; the taper principle is universal [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors].
