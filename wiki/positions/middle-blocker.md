# Middle Blocker

## Overview

The middle blocker (MB, middle, "bigs") is the team's primary front-row blocker and primary quick-tempo attacker. Her job is bimodal: she runs quick attacks from her approach and blocks the opposing middle at the net, then closes to a pin to form a two-block against the opposing pin attacker in transition. When the middle is the best blocker on the floor, team block-touch rate rises; when she is also a reliable quick-attack threat, the opposing middle cannot release to the pin and the team's outsides face 1v1 matchups.

Middles are typically subbed out of the back row (a libero or DS replaces them in position 1 or 6), which concentrates reps at the net. Middle-training is a specialized sub-discipline — the reps a middle actually plays differ from what an outside plays, and general practice structures under-serve the position unless middle-specific work is built into the week.

## Role and responsibilities

**Primary blocker.** Blocks the opposing middle on every quick and closes to a pin to form the two-block. She is the inside blocker taking the cross-court angle; the outside or opposite takes the line. Bunch-read systems have her blocking from a centered base near position 3 and reading the setter before committing.

**Quick-tempo attacker.** Runs 1, 31, shoot (2), slide (back-quick behind the setter), and back-quick options. 14U: one quick. HS: quick plus slide. College: full vocabulary with audibles.

**Slide attack.** Back-quick on a one-foot plant behind the setter after a lateral run. Times with jump-set delivery and opens the 2–3 seam for attackers.

**Transition middle.** After blocking, retreat to approach start, read the setter, run a quick or slide on the transition set. Setter-release to middle-plant is under a second — the tightest timing sequence on the court.

**Serving and back-row defense.** Serves in her serving rotation. Back-row defense is typically zero reps per match because the libero/DS takes her back-row slots.

**Communication.** Pre-serve play calls with the setter, block-coverage calls with pins ("mine/help"), real-time opposing tendencies.

## Physical profile

The middle is the tallest player on the floor. Collegiate starters cluster between 6'1" and 6'4"; HS between 6'0" and 6'3"; 14U middles run 5'9" upward. Height is a hard requirement — a 5'9" middle at the collegiate level is a developmental project, not a starter. Vertical jump is the second requirement; lateral quickness from a centered base is the third, because middle blockers cover a wider lateral range (pin-to-pin) than any other front-row player.

Arm speed matters more than swing length because the ball arrives above the net in under a second after setter contact. Approach footwork starts deliberate and accelerates on the last two steps, compressed into a shorter runway than an outside's 4-step.

- **[[hitting-approach]]** — compressed 3-step or shortened 4-step adjusted for quick tempo; slide approach (left-right plant for a right-handed hitter after a lateral run) as a distinct sub-skill.
- **[[hitting-arm-swing]]** — arm speed over swing length; clean contact on a ball delivered high and fast.
- **[[blocking-footwork]]** — lateral shuffle, crossover, and swing-block entries from a centered base.
- **[[blocking-read]]** — bunch-read system reading the setter and then the hitter.
- **[[blocking-swing]]** — closing to the pin with a swing-block entry when the system calls for it.
- **[[blocking-hand-position]]** — sealing the net, penetrating into the opponent's side, angling hands to take the cross-court.
- **[[defense-platform]]** — situational, usually only in the rotation where the libero/DS sub doesn't cover her.

## Position-specific coaching points

**Eye progression: setter's hands, ball, hitter.** The practical progression is *setter's hands → ball for a beat → hitter's approach angle*, with the middle committing her lateral step (or swing-block entry) as early as the setter's hands reveal the set. A middle who tracks the ball from pass to contact is late on every decision.

**Bunch-read vs. commit-block.** Youngest levels: commit-block the quick — jump with the opposing middle every rep, accept risk on pin sets. Bunch-read systems, which wait in a centered base and read the set before committing, install when the player can reliably read the setter — usually by HS. Tradeoff: commit-blocking protects the quick at the pins' expense; bunch-read protects pins at occasional quick cost. Sources disagree on install timing — AOC and GMS recommend bunch-read by HS; Japanese tradition and some US clubs hold commit-blocking longer.

**Shorter approach, faster tempo.** Modern middle-hitter teaching favors shorter approaches. A compressed 2- or 3-step starts closer to the net, matches jump-set tempo, and reduces the broad-jump problem common in young middles running outside-length approaches on quick balls. Short with intent beats long and late.

**Setter–middle conversation every point.** The quick depends on setter delivery and middle commit-on-release. Conversation is pre-serve (tempo, set number), pre-pass (commit or hold on pass quality), and mid-rally (transition adjustments). Without explicit practice time, teams run under-tempo middle attacks by default.

**Middle-to-pin transition.** After a blocked quick or a blocked pin where she was the inside blocker, the middle retreats, resets feet behind the 3-meter line, and runs a transition quick or slide. The sequence 14U and JV skip; the one college teams drill most.

**Block coverage of tips and rebounds.** A middle who blocks without getting off the net hands tips and tools to the attacker. Clean block mechanics end with hands pulling off the top of the net, body landing ready to move backward.

## Progression by level

See [[age-lens-14u]], [[age-lens-hs]], and [[age-lens-college]] for the broader level-specific framing.

**14U.** Commit-block the opposing middle every rep. Run one quick attack (1 or higher 2) with a compressed approach; slide is a stretch goal. Close-to-pin is introduced but not enforced — prioritize contact and hand position over lateral speed. Serve like any rotation player. Most 14U programs sub the middle out of the back row entirely, concentrating practice reps on quick attack and block.

**HS.** Bunch-read installed if read-speed permits. Quick and slide both live; back quick introduced. Swing-block footwork onset — swing-blocking by HS is a durability advantage. Block-to-transition is a drilled sequence.

**College.** Full tempo vocabulary including back quicks, slides with multiple entry angles, and quick-hitter combinations. Full read-block with advanced cues — setter's torso and feet, not just hands. Swing-blocking is default. Zone-accurate, rhythm-breaking serve. Weekly durability programming (shoulder, knees, posterior chain) because match volume on the middle block is the floor's highest.
