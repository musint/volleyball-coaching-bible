# Opposite

## Overview

The opposite (OPP, right-side, RS, "two") is the specialized right-side attacker who plays opposite the setter in the rotation — setter front-row means opposite back-row, and vice versa. In European and Brazilian traditions the opposite is often the team's best and hardest-hitting attacker, responsible for out-of-system high balls and back-row attacks equally; US women's indoor has trended toward that model across the last decade, moving the opposite from a defensive liability hidden on the right to a true offensive weapon.

The opposite is the only non-libero position where passing is not a primary responsibility — in 2-passer and 3-passer formations she is the non-passing front-row player. That offload frees her to specialize in right-side attacking, right-side blocking, and back-row attack (D/bic).

## Role and responsibilities

**Right-side attack.** Red set (high right), 51 or A-quick out of the right pin, situational back-row sets. Her sets are often tighter than outside sets because she attacks over the opposing outside blocker — typically the opponent's tallest pin blocker.

**Back-row attack (D, bic).** The D (back-row right) is the opposite's primary back-row attack and a defining feature of modern offensive systems. Bic (back-row pipe adjusted right) and D are both live at HS and above, called pre-serve or audibled in-rotation.

**Blocking.** Blocks the opposing outside — usually the opponent's highest-volume attacker. She is the outside blocker in the two-block; middle takes the inside angle. Right-side blocking against a left-side power attacker demands across-body reach to seal the line.

**Defense.** When in the back row, defends position 1 (right back). Programs that sub a DS for the opposite's back-row rotations keep her front-row only; programs that keep her on the floor add D attacks from back-row.

**Serving.** Full responsibility in her serving rotation. HS and college opposites frequently serve jump-top-spin — more common here than in any other rotation because the opposite is often the floor's most athletic hitter.

**No primary passing.** Standard offload in 2- and 3-passer formations. Some 14U and lower HS programs include her in a 3-passer receive; rare at varsity and above.

## Physical profile

Height, vertical, and arm speed are the defining attributes. Collegiate opposites cluster between 6'1" and 6'4"; HS varsity between 6'0" and 6'3"; 14U opposites commonly 5'8" and taller. Because she is not a primary passer, lateral mobility and platform control matter less than for outsides — but she still plays back-row defense when on the floor. Durability programming for shoulder and knee is non-negotiable at HS and above because she swings hard on tight sets into tall blocks.

Left-handed opposites have a mechanical edge on right-side attacks — the arm-swing hits across the body toward the cross-court angle that right-handed outside blockers struggle to seal. Programs with a capable left-hander often default her to opposite. At 14U the rule is looser; the position is often the best athletic right-hander available.

## Position-specific coaching points

**Right-side attack positioning.** Right-handed opposites hit across their body — mechanically harder than the outside's straight-in swing. Shoulders stay open long enough to contact cross-court, rather than closing early as the hips rotate toward line. Cue: "chest open, hit through the ball to the power angle." Shoulders closing early forfeits cross-court and tips line shots to the block.

**Using the block as a right-side hitter.** Against tall blocks, the reliable shot is often a high-hands tool off the opposing outside blocker to the antenna. Swing with intent, angle contact outward so the ball deflects off the outside hand, land ready to pursue. Separates opposites who hit into the block from opposites who use it.

**Back-row attack as a primary option.** A reliable D or bic forces the opposing middle to honor a back-row threat, which frees space for the middle quick. Coaches who do not train this leave a free lane for opposing defense.

**Right-side blocking against the opposing outside.** The opposite blocks the opponent's highest-volume attacker with across-body reach that is harder than open-side reach. Swing-blocking is, for most HS-and-above opposites, a durability requirement — the straight shuffle works but concedes range.

**No passing does not mean no defense.** Back-row rotations demand real defense. Where the opposite stays on the floor all six rotations, right-back defense is practiced on the same schedule as the outside's left-back defense.

**14U adjustment.** Most 14U clubs run 4-2 or 6-2 without a specialized opposite — the "opposite" is a second-outside on the right, running an outside-style attack from the right antenna. Developmentally appropriate; a full European opposite role at 14U usually costs more in reps than it gains.

## Progression by level

See [[age-lens-14u]], [[age-lens-hs]], and [[age-lens-college]] for the broader level-specific framing.

**14U.** Most often an "outside 2" in a 4-2 or 6-2 — an outside-style attacker on the right. Attacks high ball to the right antenna. Blocks the opposing outside in the front row, learns across-body reach. May pass in 3-passer formations depending on program; plays back-row defense unless subbed out. Serving is a full responsibility.

**HS.** Emerges as a specialized right-side. Runs red, 51 (quick right), and D. Swing-block footwork introduced. Back-row D is a primary offensive option in back-row rotations. Out-of-system high-ball hitting from the right antenna is core — emergency sets often go here rather than to the outside.

**College.** Back-row D and bic at full tempo with pre-serve calls. High-efficiency right-side attacking against tall blocks — above .250 attack percentage is the typical starter standard. Full swing-block commitment on opposing outsides. Scouting-report-driven shot selection: per-match adjustment of where to hit based on opposing defense and block tendencies.
