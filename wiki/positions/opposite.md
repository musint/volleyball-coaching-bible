# Opposite

## Overview

The opposite (OPP, right-side, RS, "two") is the specialized right-side terminal attacker who plays opposite the setter in the rotation. [[karch-kiraly]] frames the position for the U.S. Women's National Team as the "right-side terminal attacker" — the player whose primary measurable contribution is putting the ball away from the right pin and from the back-row D, not passing or running an offense. European and Brazilian traditions arrived at the same definition first; U.S. women's indoor has trended toward that model across the last decade, moving the opposite from a defensive liability hidden on the right to a true offensive weapon. Annie Drews's role on the Tokyo 2020 gold-medal squad is the canonical recent example.

In 2-passer and 3-passer formations the opposite is the non-passing front-row player by design. That offload frees her to specialize in right-side attacking, right-side blocking, and back-row attack (D and bic).

## Role and responsibilities

**Right-side terminal attack.** Red set (high right), 51 or A-quick out of the right pin, situational back-row sets. Her sets are often tighter than outside sets because she attacks over the opposing outside blocker — typically the opponent's tallest pin blocker. The modern term in many programs is "right side."

**Back-row attack as a designed first option.** D (back-row right) is the opposite's primary back-row attack; bic is the second. Both are live at HS and above, called pre-serve or audibled. Top women's programs run D in two or three rotations per match by 2024-25 — no longer situational, a designed first option.

**Block-tool specialist — closing the right pin.** Blocks the opponent's outside hitter (the floor's highest-volume attacker). She is the outside blocker in the two-block; middle takes the inside angle. Across-body reach to seal the line is the technical signature.

**Defense.** Position 1 (right back). The traditional NCAA pattern subs a DS for her back-row rotations; top programs increasingly keep her on the floor for back-row D-attack and sub the middle instead.

**Serving.** Often the team's strongest jump-topspin server. The opposite's serving line is a tactical asset.

**No primary passing.** Standard offload at HS and above.

**Emotional-engine role.** For many programs the opposite is the floor's most demonstrative competitor. The right-pin terminal-attacker tradition in Brazil under [[bernardinho]] and on the Karch-era USA Women shares this character — recruiting boards at top programs evaluate it explicitly.

## Physical profile

Height, vertical, and arm speed define the position. Collegiate opposites cluster 6'1"-6'4"; HS varsity 6'0"-6'3"; 14U opposites commonly 5'8"+. Often the tallest non-middle on the roster. Because she is not a primary passer, lateral mobility and platform control matter less than for outsides — but she still plays back-row defense. Durability programming for shoulder and knee is non-negotiable at HS+; see [[age-lens-hs]].

**Arm-swing velocity.** The defining technical attribute. The opposite's set is tighter than the OH's, the block in front is the opposing outside blocker (the floor's highest-volume blocker), and the cross-body angle a right-handed opposite hits is mechanically harder. Without high arm-swing velocity the right-side attack collapses.

**Left-handed advantage.** Left-handed opposites hit a clean cross-court angle right-handed outside blockers struggle to seal. Programs with a capable left-hander often default her to opposite. Not required — Annie Drews is right-handed.

**Jump approach reach.** Because D is a designed first option, the opposite needs the same approach reach back-row that an OH needs front-row — 9'10"+ jump-touch is the Power-5 entry standard.

## Position-specific coaching points

**Right-side attack positioning.** Right-handed opposites hit across the body — mechanically harder than the OH's straight-in swing. Shoulders stay open long enough to contact cross-court, rather than closing early. Cue: "chest open, hit through the ball to the power angle."

**Out-of-rotation management.** Traditional NCAA subs a libero or DS for the opposite's back-row rotations. Programs that keep her on the floor for back-row D-attack now sub the middle instead. Decide which model and train accordingly — three-rotation and six-rotation opposites train for different positions.

**When to take the high-ball outside vs. the right pin.** Out-of-system bail-out is sometimes the OH's high hut, sometimes a high-red. Coaches who treat the OH as the only emergency set forfeit the right-pin option in rotations where the block is tilted to the OH. Texas under [[jerritt-elliott]] and Nebraska under [[john-cook]] call high-red as a primary out-of-system option, not a fallback.

**Reading the setter on second-ball OOS.** When the libero or back-row passer takes first ball, the opposite reads the setter and calls early — "out!" releases off-hitters into cover; "right!" cues a high-red approach. The opposite who hesitates takes a half-tempo set into a closed block.

**Back-row D-attack timing.** A reliable D or bic forces the opposing middle to honor a back-row threat, freeing the middle quick. Train the chain — defensive base → clear behind the 3-meter line → approach — explicitly. The D set is higher and longer than a front-row red; timing initiates earlier.

**Using the block as a right-side hitter.** Against tall blocks the reliable shot is a high-hands tool off the opposing outside blocker to the antenna. Swing with intent, angle contact outward, land ready to pursue.

**Right-side blocking against the opposing outside.** Across-body reach is harder than open-side reach. Swing-blocking is a durability requirement at HS+. Closing the pin under live tempo is a separate skill from open-floor footwork.

**Left-handed advantage at the right pin.** If a program has a capable left-hander, default her to opposite. If not, train the right-handed opposite's chest-open swing explicitly; the line shot becomes the high-confidence default.

## Progression by level

See [[age-lens-14u]], [[age-lens-hs]], and [[age-lens-college]] for the broader level-specific framing.

**10s-12s.** Opposite identity has not specialized — the role is not a discrete teaching identity at this age. The candidate is a tall, physical, well-coordinated all-around athlete. Specialization is itself an injury-risk multiplier in the youth literature.

**13s-14s.** First opposite-specialization conversations. The candidate is usually a tall, physical, right-handed athlete (or rare capable left-hander) who is too dynamic for middle but not the serve-receive primary. Most 14U clubs run 4-2 or 6-2 without a specialized opposite — she is a second-outside on the right. A full European opposite role at 14U costs more in reps than it gains. Front-row blocking introduced; back-row attack conceptual.

**15s-16s.** Full opposite role. Runs red, 51 (quick right), and D. Swing-block footwork introduced. Back-row D introduced as a primary offensive option. Out-of-system high-ball from the right antenna is core — emergency sets often go here rather than to the outside. Jump-topspin serving introduced.

**17s-18s.** Recruiting-cycle opposite. Hitting % from the right pin enters the recruiting conversation — sub-.250 is a recruiting-board limiter; .280+ is the Power-5 entry standard. Jump-topspin serving line is a designed lineup tool. Hitter-type drives shot-mix per matchup.

**College.** Full Power-5 opposite. Pipe + back-row attack repertoire (D and bic) at full tempo with pre-serve calls. .250+ hitting is the starter standard. Full swing-block commitment. Scouting-driven shot selection. Team leadership — often captain by senior year. Texas (back-to-back 2022/2023 NCAA champions under [[jerritt-elliott]]) and Pittsburgh (five consecutive Final Fours under [[dan-fisher]]) have built around this profile.
