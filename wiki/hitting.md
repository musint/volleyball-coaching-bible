# Hitting

## Overview

Hitting is the sport's primary terminal skill — the layer where
everything else (serve-receive, setting, tempo, scouting) either
converts into a point or it doesn't. A complete hitter stitches four
things together in under two seconds: a **repeatable approach** that
times the hitter into the ball against a moving set, a **full-swing
arm action** that converts jump height and torso rotation into ball
speed, a **shot-selection layer** that reads the block and picks a
target mid-air, and for advanced players, a **back-row attack** that
lets the offense field four or five attackers against any rotation.
None of those skills carries the hitter alone; the ceiling of an
attack is set by the weakest link in the chain.

Modern hitting teaching has converged on a handful of shared
anchors. The approach uses a **right-left last two steps** for
right-handers (left-right for left-handers), with deliberate
acceleration on those final two — start slow, explode into the
plant — producing a two-foot takeoff and a rotation that points the
non-hitting shoulder at the target. The arm swing is a **two-arm
action** — both arms lift during the plant to store elastic energy
through the shoulder girdle, the hitting arm draws back in a
"bow-and-arrow" position, and the torso uncoils through contact with
the **contact point high and slightly in front** of the hitting
shoulder. Shot selection adds a read layer: the hitter scans the
block during the approach and chooses between hard cross, hard line,
tip, roll, tool, and high-seam based on what the block and defense
give her.

This hub orients you to all four subskills plus the schools of
thought that disagree on emphasis. For the mechanics themselves, go
to the subskill pages. For how the attack lives inside the bigger
game, come back here.

## Major subtopics

- **[[hitting-approach]]** — the footwork pattern that converts
  horizontal travel into vertical jump. Right-left last two for
  right-handers, start slow and accelerate, plant wide and jump up
  not out. Pipe, bic, slide, and quick tempos reduce to adjusted
  versions of this base.
- **[[hitting-arm-swing]]** — the arm and torso sequence from plant
  through contact. Double-arm lift, bow-and-arrow draw, torso
  rotation, high-and-in-front contact, wrist snap for topspin.
  Power comes from torque, not from the arm alone.
- **[[hitting-shot-selection]]** — the decision layer on top of the
  swing. Toolbox (cross, line, tip, roll, tool, high-seam), block
  reading, hitter-type self-awareness. Genuinely contested across
  schools — see [[#Schools of thought]] below.
- **[[hitting-back-row-attack]]** — front-row-tempo attacks launched
  from behind the 3-meter line. Pipe, bic, D, A. The skill that
  turns 6-on-6 from a three-attacker game into a four- or
  five-attacker game, especially valuable in [[age-lens-hs]] and
  [[age-lens-college]] offenses.

Hitting also interacts heavily with [[setting]] (the hitter-setter
connection is a deliberate training target, not an emergent
property) and [[transition]] (attacking out of transition
differentiates elite hitters from clean-pass hitters). Drills live
under the `drills/` folder — filter `primary-skill: hitting` via
Dataview on the age-lens pages for a level-filtered list.

## Positions that rely on hitting

Hitting is the primary skill for three positions: [[outside-hitter]]
(left-side pin, the go-to out-of-system hitter in most systems),
[[opposite]] (right-side pin, often the second-highest-volume
attacker and frequently the back-row specialist), and
[[middle-blocker]] (quick tempos, slides, occasional back-row
D-balls). On a 14U roster, favor rotating every athlete through
hitting progressions regardless of likely position — the overlap
with serving mechanics means every player benefits from the arm
swing anyway.

## Schools of thought

Hitting — particularly shot selection and the role of menu
instruction vs exposure — is one of the most legitimately contested
skill areas in the coaching literature. The five-way contrast
below mirrors the `schools-perspectives` frame on
[[hitting-shot-selection]] at the whole-skill level.

- **[[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] — toolbox / self-awareness.** AOC
  content (Dunning, Liskevych, Schmidt) teaches hitting as a
  deliberately expanded menu of shots plus a read layer. Dunning's
  "what kind of hitter are you" question pushes every hitter to
  classify herself (high-point, angle hitter, placement hitter,
  power swinger) and build a development plan around that
  self-knowledge. Menu instruction and reading emphasis in every
  skill.
- **[[gold-medal-squared]] — random-practice exposure.** GMS argues
  decision-making cannot be drilled as a separate skill from
  execution; it emerges from varied game-like reps. Their clinic
  curriculum limits drill variety to ~12 core activities run in
  randomized whole-skill formats — the hitter develops shot
  selection through varied block fronts and set locations, not
  through a "shot menu." Motor-learning orthodoxy underneath.
- **[[russian-school]] — pressure-hitting.** The Soviet lineage
  teaches hitting as a power-first discipline. The primary shot
  is hard-driven and in the court; finesse shots are specialized
  tools, not defaults. A hitter who tips too often is leaking
  pressure — keep the ball in and make the defense beat you.
  Practice emphasizes repetition of hard swings against live
  block-and-defense with strict in/out accounting.
- **[[italian-school]] — versatility within a reliability frame.**
  Italian volleyball's defensive and ball-control identity shapes
  hitters to keep the ball in and trust the system to produce
  another swing. Shot variety is developed but bounded by
  reliability — hitters who terminate with risky tool attempts
  that sail out are disciplined toward "keep it in and swing
  again."
- **[[brazilian-school]] — creativity and tempo.** Brazilian
  hitters are known for angle unpredictability and disguise.
  Attack the whole court; use tempo variance (quick middles,
  pipes, slides, combos) to collapse the block before the shot
  is chosen; hit off-beat angles. Shot selection is a creativity
  problem as much as a reading problem.

**Where the schools converge.** Right-left two-foot plant, double-arm
lift, topspin contact with a high hand, block reading during the
approach, and the core toolbox (cross, line, tip, roll, tool) are
agreed across all five traditions. The disagreement is about
**emphasis** — how you spend practice minutes — and about the
hitter's **default** when in doubt.

## Getting started

If you are upgrading how you teach hitting, read in this order:

1. **[[hitting-approach]]** — the foundation; a great arm swing
   cannot rescue a late approach.
2. **[[hitting-arm-swing]]** — the whole-body action that converts
   the approach into ball speed.
3. **[[hitting-shot-selection]]** — the decision layer.
4. **[[hitting-back-row-attack]]** — the four/five-attacker
   expansion.
5. Your age-lens page — [[age-lens-14u]], [[age-lens-hs]], or
   [[age-lens-college]].

## Related areas

- [[setting]] — the hitter-setter connection is a deliberate
  training target (Dunning). Varied-toss drills that force setter
  location-awareness simultaneously train hitter reading and
  timing.
- [[transition]] — transition offense is where hitting efficiency
  collapses at lower levels; plan deliberate
  pass-transition-to-approach reps.
- [[blocking]] — the block is what the hitter reads. Training
  against live blockers (rather than against a hand-held board or
  an unblocked net) is essential for shot-selection development.
- [[practice-planning]] — competing-in-practice and random
  game-like reps are where hitting improves fastest; score
  hitting drills and log efficiency to make the stakes real.
- [[age-lens-14u]] — what a 14U hitter can absorb (timing, full
  swing on high outside, toolbox intro) vs. what waits (slide
  timing, pipe synchronization, tool execution against two
  blockers).
- [[age-lens-hs]] — HS varsity hitting (full toolbox, back-row
  D-ball, hitter-type self-awareness).
- [[age-lens-college]] — college hitting (efficiency targets,
  scouting-informed shot selection, back-row tempo integration).
