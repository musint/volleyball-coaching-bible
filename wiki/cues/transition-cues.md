# Transition cues

## Overview

Transition is the phase between rally contacts — block to approach, dig to approach, free ball to attack, OOS scramble to swing. It is where elite programs pull away from developing programs, and it is where the cue vocabulary is *least* developed across the broader coaching literature. Most cues here are stitched together from adjacent skill teachings (blocking landing cues, hitting approach cues, defensive recovery cues) plus a few transition-specific phrases that the modern AOC corpus has codified [aoc-2016-rosen-training-transition-in-out-system][aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection].

The cues lean modern AOC across Rosen's transition-in-and-out-of-system corpus, Shymansky's outside-hitter transition footwork, Dunning's hitter-setter-connection drills, Brown's libero-second-ball setting, Sagula's balanced-landing block-to-transition emphasis, and the OOS detection language used across the modern setter-instruction corpus [aoc-2016-rosen-training-transition-in-out-system][aoc-2015-shymansky-oh-transition-footwork][aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection][aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting][aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals]. Munciana's working-club layer adds explicit OOS reps inside the camp progression — Lingenfelter's transcript names OOS as "the out of system part of the game is where you spend almost all of your time in high school. Probably 80% of the play is out of system" [munciana-2022-camp-drills].

Cues are organized by transition phase: block-to-approach (front-row hitters after the block), dig-to-approach (back-row hitters after the dig), free-ball-to-attack (the easy transition), OOS detect/call/bail (when the pass is off), and the second-ball protocols (setter and libero).

## Per-subskill canonical cues

### Block-to-approach (front-row recovery)

**"Land balanced, ready to transition."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. The Sagula balanced-landing cue [aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals] applied to transition. Teaches that the block ends with a controlled landing where the front-row hitter immediately enters her transition footwork — clear off the net, drop into the ten-foot line, set the approach. A blocker who lands flat-footed or off-balance has lost the rally before the ball is dug. Does NOT teach a flat landing — the cue is balanced *and ready*, with weight distributed for the next move.

**"Off the net, then approach."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The two-step block-to-approach footwork cue. Teaches the sequence: clear off the net (one or two steps backward away from the tape), then transition into the approach pattern. A hitter who tries to approach from the net produces a forward-jumping plant and a flat trajectory. Does NOT teach a deep retreat — the clearance is one or two steps; the goal is room for the approach plant.

**"Two steps clear, then turn."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. A specific footwork cue for the middle blocker block-to-attack pattern. Teaches that the middle blocker lands, takes two steps backward off the net, then turns and starts her quick-tempo approach. Does NOT teach a fixed two-step rule — the clearance varies; the cue installs the *pattern*.

**"Sprint clear, plant fresh."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The OH/opposite block-to-approach cue stitched from the Shymansky outside-hitter transition-footwork tradition [aoc-2015-shymansky-oh-transition-footwork]. Teaches that the pin hitter sprints off the net to her approach starting position rather than backpedaling, then plants from a fresh standing start as the set arrives. Does NOT teach a slow drift — the sprint clear is fast; the plant is fresh.

### Dig-to-approach (back-row recovery)

**"Up after the dig, then approach."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. Teaches the back-row hitter's recovery after a defensive contact: stand up immediately, recognize whether the rally is staying out of system or transitioning to a back-row attack, and start the approach footwork from her base position. Does NOT teach a fixed approach pattern — back-row attack patterns vary (D, A, pipe); the cue installs the *recovery*.

**"Through the ball, into the approach."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. A continuity cue for the dig-to-approach sequence. Teaches that the defender's momentum continues into the approach — the dig is not a stopping point but a launching point. Does NOT teach a one-piece motion — the dig and approach are distinct, but the body posture flows.

**"Pipe is the default for dig-to-attack."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. The Dunning hitter-setter-connection corpus implies this for back-row OHs after a dig: the pipe attack is the highest-percentage back-row option for an outside who has just dug and needs to transition [aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection]. Does NOT teach pipe-only — D and A are tools; the pipe is the high-value default.

### Free-ball-to-attack (the easy transition)

**"Free ball, hands up, attack the seam."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+. Teaches the team-level free-ball protocol: hands up to call the free ball, all six players reset to free-ball-receive positions, the first-contact passer attacks the seam to the setter (highest-success target for setting tempo). Does NOT teach a fixed formation — the free-ball receive geometry varies; the cue installs the *priorities*.

**"Free ball is offense."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. Teaches that a free ball is an offensive opportunity, not a defensive contact — convert it into the cleanest set the team can run. Does NOT teach a kill-or-error rule — the cue is about *intent*, not risk profile.

**"Run a higher tempo on free balls than on serve receive."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches that the free ball, being a slower feed, supports a faster attack tempo than a serve receive — the setter has more time, the hitters have a clean approach, and the offense can run middles or back-row attacks that would be unsafe off a real serve. Does NOT teach a fixed tempo upgrade — the cue installs the *option*; the tactical decision is real-time.

### Out-of-system: detect/call/bail

**"OOS — call it!"** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The detection cue. Teaches that the team verbalizes when the pass is off — "OOS!" or "out of system!" — so everyone shifts mental model from in-system to OOS execution. Lingenfelter's framing in the Munciana camp library — that OOS is "almost all of your time in high school" — makes this a routine call, not an alarm [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Does NOT teach a panicked tone — the call is informational, not alarming.

**"Bail to the pin."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The setter OOS bail cue. Teaches that when the first contact is off enough that the setter cannot run her called play, the default redirect is a high outside set to the pin — typically zone-4 (left front) for the strongest hitter. Does NOT teach a fixed bail target — the pin can be left or right depending on rotation; the cue installs the *destination type*.

**"High to the antenna."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The bail-set height-and-location cue. Teaches that the OOS bail set is high (giving the hitter time to read and adjust the approach) and at the antenna (giving the hitter a single-blocker outside attack opportunity). Does NOT teach a flat outside set — the height is the entire point.

**"Hitter, adjust."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The hitter-side OOS cue. Teaches that on an OOS detect call, the hitter abandons her called approach and reads the actual set the setter is delivering, then adjusts her plant point and timing. Dunning's hitter-setter-connection corpus underwrites this — pre-scripted approaches fail against real passes [aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection]. Does NOT teach abandoning the approach — the approach happens; the *spatial target* adjusts.

**"Out of system is offense, not survival."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Rosen OOS-as-real-offense cue [aoc-2016-rosen-training-transition-in-out-system]. Teaches that OOS reps are not "survive the rally" — they are a distinct offensive layer with its own tools (high outside, libero second ball, second-contact attack). Rosen's frame is explicit: training only in-system transition produces players who freeze when the pass is off [aoc-2016-rosen-training-transition-in-out-system]. Does NOT teach swinging recklessly — the cue installs the *intent* of OOS as offense.

### Setter second-ball protocol

**"Setter takes the first, libero takes the second."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The role-handoff cue when the setter is forced into the first contact. Teaches that when the setter has dug or passed, the libero (or designated secondary setter) takes the second contact; the setter does not chase her own ball. Does NOT teach a fixed handoff — sometimes the OH or DS takes the second contact based on court position; the cue installs the *priority* of role handoff.

**"Setter first ball, hitter second-ball-attack."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. The advanced setter-took-the-first protocol. Teaches that when the setter is out of the second-contact picture, the libero or front-row OH may attack the second ball directly (a setter-attack from a non-setter) rather than setting a third ball. Does NOT teach attacking every second ball — the cue installs the *option* in the OOS toolbox.

### Libero second-ball protocol

**"Libero, behind the 3 to set."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The libero-overhead-set rule cue from the Brown corpus [aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting]. Teaches the front-row attack rule: a libero can use overhead hands to set front-row attackers only when the libero is behind the 3-meter line at contact (or when the first ball is below the libero's waist, in which case the rule is moot). The libero in front of the 3 must use forearm bump-set if the second-contact target hitter is going to attack from the front row. Does NOT teach a memorize-the-rule approach — the cue installs the *positional awareness*.

**"Libero target zone, not 'anywhere useful.'"** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Brown specific-target cue captured directly [aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting]: "Specifying a target zone for the libero's second ball (rather than 'anywhere useful') is the decisive coaching point." Teaches that the libero's second-ball set goes to a defined target — typically the antenna or the high-outside zone — not to the closest available hitter. Does NOT teach a single-target rule — the target zone is the default, with situational redirects allowed.

**"Bump-set high, hands behind the 3."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The compound libero second-ball cue. Teaches the binary decision: in front of the 3 → bump-set; behind the 3 (and ball above waist) → hands. Does NOT teach a fixed contact for the libero second ball — the position and ball height drive the choice.

### Tempo maintenance (advanced transition)

**"Tempo over efficiency."** Introduced 16s. Refined 17s+. The Italian/Brazilian-tradition tempo cue applied to transition reps. Teaches that the team maintains a fast attack tempo even on transition swings — the goal is to keep the defense reactive across multiple rally contacts, not to slow down for clean swings. Does NOT teach reckless speed — the cue installs the *priority*; clean swings are still valuable.

**"Transition like first ball."** Introduced 17s. The advanced standard cue. Teaches that elite teams' transition offense should be indistinguishable from their first-ball offense — same tempo, same play options, same hitter-setter connection. Does NOT teach a fixed tempo — the cue installs the *standard*.

## Anti-cues

- **"Block, then watch."** Encourages the blocker to spectate after landing rather than transition. Modern correction: "Land balanced, ready to transition" plus "Off the net, then approach."
- **"Free ball, just send it back."** Wastes the free-ball offensive opportunity. Modern correction: "Free ball is offense" plus "Free ball, hands up, attack the seam."
- **"OOS, just keep it alive."** Treats OOS as survival rather than offense. Modern correction: "Out of system is offense, not survival."
- **"Setter, get the second ball no matter what."** Encourages the setter to chase her own first ball, breaking offense. Modern correction: "Setter takes the first, libero takes the second."
- **"Libero, set whoever's open."** Removes target-zone discipline from the libero second ball. Modern correction: "Libero target zone, not 'anywhere useful.'"
- **"Approach to the call, not to the ball."** Pre-scripted approach cue that fails against off-target sets. Modern correction: "Hitter, adjust" plus "Read the setter's hands."
- **"Slow it down on transition."** Surrenders the tempo advantage. Modern correction: "Tempo over efficiency" or "Transition like first ball" at advanced levels.
- **"Just hit it deep."** Vague target for transition swings. Modern correction: same shot-selection cues as first-ball hitting — read the block, open shoulders to seam.

## Voice consistency notes

The transition cue vocabulary is the *least* converged of the seven skill areas, partly because transition is undertrained at the club and HS levels and the cue language has not been standardized across coaches as tightly as it has for the foundational skills.

**Modern AOC** (Rosen, Shymansky, Dunning, Brown, Sagula) leads the program's working voice on transition. Rosen's transition-in-and-out-of-system corpus is the closest the AOC literature gets to a comprehensive transition framework [aoc-2016-rosen-training-transition-in-out-system]. The cue vocabulary stitches together pieces from blocking (balanced landing), hitting (read the setter), defense (up after the dig), and setting (libero second ball, OOS bail).

**Munciana** treats OOS as the dominant rally state at HS — Lingenfelter's "almost all of your time in high school. Probably 80% of the play is out of system" framing is the strongest claim that OOS deserves first-class training [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. The Munciana drill library has dedicated OOS, libero second-ball, and team-out-of-system reps as named drills.

**GMS** argues transition cannot be drilled in isolation from full-rally practice — random/variable practice with scored transition touches is the prescription, not blocked block-to-approach lines. The cue vocabulary in this dictionary is compatible with GMS practice architecture; "OOS — call it!" and "Tempo over efficiency" both work in any practice format.

**Italian and Brazilian schools** built their identity on transition tempo, with aggressive back-row-attack-in-transition as a default option. The cue "Tempo over efficiency" preserves the Italian/Brazilian principle inside the modern AOC frame.

**Japanese tradition** treats transition inside the dig-up-everything ethic — the dig becomes an attack, with transition drilled to autonomic levels. The cue vocabulary at the technique level overlaps with the modern AOC frame; the distinctive Japanese contribution is on the *practice volume* of transition reps, which is procedural rather than lexical.

**Ecological dynamics** is the most skeptical of decomposed transition footwork drills and the most committed to representative learning design. The constraint-led coach designs information-rich drills (degraded first contacts, varied ball locations, moving blockers) and lets the transition pattern self-organize. The cue vocabulary in this dictionary is compatible — most cues are relational ("Land balanced, ready to transition," "OOS — call it!") rather than decomposed body-part movements.

When schools genuinely disagree on a transition cue — for example, "block-and-stay-down" (legacy block-recovery cue) versus "land balanced, ready to transition" (modern cue) — the dictionary keeps both with attribution. The program's working voice is the modern frame.

The phrases every coach in the program should say: land balanced ready to transition, off the net then approach, sprint clear plant fresh (15s+), up after the dig then approach, free ball hands up attack the seam, free ball is offense, OOS — call it, bail to the pin, high to the antenna, hitter adjust, out of system is offense not survival, setter takes the first libero takes the second, libero behind the 3 to set, libero target zone, tempo over efficiency (16s+), transition like first ball (17s+). Sixteen phrases. Repeat them.
