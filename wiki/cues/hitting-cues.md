# Hitting cues

## Overview

Hitting is the sport's terminal skill, and the cue vocabulary the staff uses has been substantially rewritten in the last decade. The modern frame leads with **approach tempo** (Schmidt: "start slow, accelerate the last two steps"), **arm mechanics built on torque and high hand** (MacDonald), and **shot selection driven by self-classified hitter type** (Dunning) [aoc-2024-attack-approach][aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics][aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types]. The legacy "snap your wrist" arm-and-wrist cue is preserved here only as an anti-cue.

The cues lean modern AOC across Schmidt's approach instruction, MacDonald's torque-and-arm-height arm-mechanics, Mattox's verbal-rhythm cue trio (Sight-90 / Elbow-Lead / Lift-and-Whip), and Dunning's hitter-types question [aoc-2024-attack-approach][aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics][aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types]. Munciana's working-club layer adds approach-pattern and shot-selection cues used in camp [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Liskevych's ASICS hitting instruction and Kiraly's foundational corpus supply the older vocabulary preserved as anti-cues where the modern frame replaces it [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-hitting][kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball].

Cues are organized in the order a hitter's swing fails: approach goes wrong first (late, flat-footed, no acceleration), arm goes wrong second (low elbow, late draw, no torque), shot selection goes wrong third (pre-decided shot, no block read). Order the cue language to that failure order.

## Per-subskill canonical cues

### Approach footwork

**"Start slow, accelerate the last two steps."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The Schmidt approach cue verbatim from the AOC 2024 corpus [aoc-2024-attack-approach]. Teaches the deliberate tempo gradient: the first step is a settling step that times the hitter into the set, the last two steps explode into the plant. Schmidt's pass-and-hit drill operationalizes this as a consistency cue — "when the ball leaves the setter's hands, a good hitter should always be at the same spot in their approach" [aoc-2024-attack-approach]. Does NOT teach equal-tempo steps — equal tempo is the opposite of what produces explosive plant.

**"Right-left, plant" (right-handed) or "left-right, plant" (left-handed).** Introduced 11s. Refined 13s+ into the full three-step (slow-fast-fast) and four-step approaches. Teaches the last-two-steps footwork pattern that produces a two-foot takeoff with the non-hitting shoulder pointing at the target. Does NOT teach a single-step approach — that is a beginner-only entry, replaced as soon as timing allows.

**"Close the heel."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. A plant-mechanics cue specific to the hitter's outside foot at the plant. Teaches that the trailing foot closes (pivots inward) at the plant, rotating the hips and loading the torso for the swing. A heel that stays open at the plant produces a hitter who broad-jumps forward instead of jumping up. Does NOT teach a wide stance — close-the-heel is about pivot at the plant, not stance width.

**"Big arm swing on the approach."** Introduced 12s. Refined 13s+. The Schmidt arm-exaggeration cue — "exaggerate arm movement during the approach to help gain jump height" [aoc-2024-attack-approach]. Teaches that the arms swing aggressively backward through the plant phase to load elastic energy through the shoulder girdle and contribute to vertical jump. Does NOT teach a stiff-arm approach — explicitly the opposite, and not the swing itself (which is downstream).

**"Jump up, not out."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. A plant-direction cue. Teaches that the hitter's takeoff is vertical, not forward — a hitter who jumps forward off the plant attacks while broad-jumping into the net and produces flat trajectories. Does NOT teach a stationary plant — the approach has horizontal momentum, but the takeoff converts it to vertical.

### Arm swing & contact

**"High hand, torque from the hips."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The MacDonald arm-mechanics cue [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics]. Teaches that ball speed is the product of torso rotation (torque from the hips and core) plus a high contact point — not the wrist alone. Compresses the entire modern arm-mechanics frame into one cue. Does NOT teach a wrist snap as the source of power — explicitly the opposite of the legacy "snap your wrist" cue.

**"Sight-90 / Elbow-Lead / Lift-and-Whip."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The Mattox three-cue arm-progression sequence [unsourced — Mattox AOC corpus, attributed to his teaching tradition]. Sight-90 = visual focus on the ball at the 90-degree elevation point during the approach. Elbow-Lead = the hitting elbow leads the arm-draw position, sitting high and slightly behind the ear. Lift-and-Whip = the lift phase of the bow-and-arrow draw, then the whip-through of the shoulder-elbow-wrist sequence. Does NOT teach a wrist-only swing — Lift-and-Whip is whole-arm sequencing.

**"Bow and arrow."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+. Teaches the arm-draw position at the apex of the jump: hitting elbow high and bent, non-hitting arm pointing forward at target, torso loaded backward for the unwind. The shape that loads the swing. Does NOT teach a static draw — the bow-and-arrow is a position the hitter passes through, not poses in.

**"Contact high and in front."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The contact-point cue [kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball]. Teaches that the contact happens at full reach above the hitting shoulder and slightly in front, not behind the head and not below the apex. Does NOT teach reaching forward across the shoulder line — contact stays in the shoulder's vertical plane.

**"Open the shoulder, hit cross."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. A shot-direction cue. Teaches that opening the non-hitting shoulder *more* into the cross-court line directs the swing to the deep angle. Closing the shoulder line directs the swing toward the line. Does NOT teach turning the head — shoulders rotate, head stays oriented to the ball.

### Shot selection

**"Open shoulders to seam."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The seam-attack cue. Teaches that the hitter's shoulder rotation directs the ball into the seam between the two cross-court defenders (the gap between left-back and middle-back, typically), which is the highest-success angle against a clean two-blocker block. Does NOT teach a fixed seam target — the seam moves with the defense; the cue installs the *read*.

**"What kind of hitter are you?"** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Dunning self-classification question [aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types]. Teaches that each hitter knows whether she is a high-point hitter, an angle hitter, a placement hitter, or a power swinger, and develops her training around that self-knowledge. Does NOT teach a fixed identity — the answer evolves; the question is the prompt to update it.

**"Tip when the block is up, swing when it's down."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. A simple shot-selection rule. Teaches the binary read at the apex: a clean two-block above the net is a tip target, an unset or below-net block is a swing-through target. Does NOT teach a tip-default — at every level, the swing is the primary tool; the tip is a high-value change-up.

**"Tool the block."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. A shot-selection cue for high contact against a tall block. Teaches that the hitter targets the outside hand of the outside blocker to deflect the ball out of bounds via the block. A high-skill move. Does NOT teach hitting at the block — tooling is a precise edge-of-the-block target, not a "hit it off them" gamble.

**"Hit it in."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. A reliability cue captured across multiple AOC and Russian-school traditions. Teaches that the hitter's first responsibility is to keep the ball in the court; risky tool attempts that sail out are leaks. Lingenfelter's general framing in the Munciana camp library aligns: the hitter who gambles for a kill that goes out is worse than the hitter who recycles for another rep [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Does NOT teach swinging soft — hit it in *and* hard; the cue is about court awareness, not swing speed.

### Back-row attack

**"Plant behind the line."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. The back-row attack rule cue. Teaches that the hitter's plant must be behind the 3-meter line (10-foot line) on a back-row attack, but the hitter can land in front of it. Does NOT teach a plant-and-stay rule — the broad jump forward is part of the back-row attack pattern.

**"Pipe is a swing, not a survival."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. A shot-selection cue for the back-row middle attack. Teaches that the pipe attack is a real offensive tool, not a desperation set when the front-row options collapse. Does NOT teach pipe-default — the front-row options remain primary; the pipe is the back-row addition.

**"D and A as the wide options."** Introduced 16s. Refined 17s+. Names the back-row pin attacks (D = back-right, A = back-left) as discrete tactical tools. Teaches that the back-row pin attacks open up against teams that have collapsed their block on the front-row options. Does NOT teach using them every rotation — they are situational tools.

### Hitter-setter connection

**"Read the setter's hands."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches that the hitter's approach timing and shot selection both adjust to the actual set the setter is delivering, not to the call. The Dunning hitter-setter-connection corpus emphasizes pre-scripted approaches that fail against real passes [aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection]. Does NOT teach abandoning the play call — the call sets the default, the hitter adapts within it.

**"Approach to the ball, not to the spot."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. Teaches that the hitter's plant point matches where the set actually goes, not where the call said it would go. A hitter who plants at the antenna for a set that lands at zone-3 has missed the set. Does NOT teach late approaches — the timing window is the same; the spatial target adjusts.

## Anti-cues

- **"Snap your wrist."** The legacy arm-and-wrist cue that the modern arm-mechanics frame replaces. Over-emphasizes the wrist as the source of power; ignores torque and arm height — the actual modern levers. Modern correction: "High hand, torque from the hips."
- **"Just hit it harder."** Mistakes ball speed for kill rate; produces hitters who arm at the ball without setup. Modern correction: a process cue ("Close the heel," "High hand, torque") replaces the outcome cue.
- **"Don't tip."** Removes a high-value shot from the toolbox without replacing the underlying read. Modern correction: "Tip when the block is up, swing when it's down" — the tip is a *read-driven* tool, not a forbidden choice.
- **"Hit angle / hit line" without a read prompt.** Pre-decides the shot before the apex. Modern correction: "Open shoulders to seam" or "Read the block" — the read drives the shot, not the call.
- **"Three-step approach" as a fixed rule for all hitters."** Approach length varies with hitter and set tempo; a fixed three-step is a beginner default, not the modern teaching. Modern correction: scale approach length to set tempo and player preference, with the start-slow-accelerate-the-last-two-steps gradient preserved.
- **"Aim for the block-out."** Encourages over-tooling in lieu of a clean swing. Modern correction: "Tool the block" is a high-skill move, not a default — a hitter without a reliable in-court swing should not be aiming for tools.
- **"Jump as high as you can."** Internally-focused effort cue that decouples from approach. Modern correction: "Jump up, not out" plus "Big arm swing on the approach" — the height comes from the mechanics, not the effort.
- **"Get your elbow up."** Internally-focused body-part cue, often produces a stiff-elbow hold. Modern correction: "Bow and arrow" — externalizes attention onto the loaded position rather than the body part.
- **"Don't hit it out."** Negative outcome cue. Modern correction: "Hit it in" — same principle, externally framed and process-oriented.

## Voice consistency notes

The hitting cue vocabulary is heavily contested across schools, particularly on the question of menu-instruction (AOC, Italian, Brazilian) versus exposure-only (GMS) for shot selection.

**Modern AOC** (Schmidt, MacDonald, Mattox, Dunning, Liskevych) leads the program's working voice. The cue language replaces legacy "snap your wrist" arm-mechanics teaching with torque-plus-high-hand, and replaces fixed "hit angle" instructions with read-driven shot selection [aoc-2024-attack-approach][aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics][aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types].

**GMS** argues that shot selection cannot be drilled as a separate skill from execution — it emerges from varied game-like reps, not from a "shot menu." The GMS position would be that "What kind of hitter are you?" is a useful debrief question but should not be answered through prescriptive instruction. The program's resolution: keep the Dunning question as a self-classification prompt, but train shot selection via varied block fronts and set locations rather than via menu drills. Both schools agree on the *practice architecture* (varied reps, scored hitting), so the disagreement reduces to vocabulary.

**Russian school** prefers a power-first default: hard-driven swings inside the court, finesse shots as specialized tools rather than defaults. The cue "Hit it in" preserves the Russian principle inside the modern teaching frame.

**Italian school** trains versatility within a reliability frame: the hitter develops the toolbox but the default is "keep it in and trust the system." The cue language is compatible — "Hit it in" with "Open shoulders to seam" or "Tip when the block is up" matches the Italian frame.

**Brazilian school** emphasizes creativity, angle unpredictability, and tempo variance. The cue vocabulary at the individual swing level overlaps; the distinctive Brazilian contribution is on the *approach* (slide, combo, fast tempos), which is largely a setting-and-systems question rather than a hitting-cue question.

**Ecological dynamics** prefers cues that constrain the task rather than instruct the movement. "Read the block" is a constraints-led prompt; "Open shoulders to seam" is a relational cue between the hitter and the defense. The program leans this way per the recency preference.

When schools genuinely disagree on a hitting cue — for example, "snap the wrist for topspin" (legacy) versus "torque from the hips" (modern) — the dictionary keeps both with attribution and notes that the program's working voice is the modern one.

The phrases every coach in the program should say: start slow accelerate the last two steps, close the heel, big arm swing on the approach, jump up not out, high hand torque from the hips, bow and arrow, contact high and in front, open shoulders to seam, what kind of hitter are you, tip when the block is up, hit it in, read the setter's hands. Twelve phrases. Repeat them.
