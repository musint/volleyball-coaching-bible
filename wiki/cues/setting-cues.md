# Setting cues

## Overview

Setting is the offense, and the cue vocabulary the staff uses to teach it has shifted decisively in the last decade. The modern frame leads with the **hand-hinge** release, **side-of-the-ball** contact, and **beat-the-ball** footwork. Older "push-set" vocabulary — flat hands behind the ball, forearm push to target, no wrist motion — is preserved here only as anti-cues. This dictionary establishes the canonical phrasing for setter-specific instruction and for the broader expectation that every non-libero on the court can deliver a clean second contact under the same rule constraints [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge].

The cues lean modern AOC across Dunning's hand-hinge demonstrations, Rockwell's setter-training corpus, and Liskevych's ASICS setting instruction [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge][aoc-2015-rockwell-training-setters][aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-setting]. Munciana's working-club layer adds tempo cues and the libero-second-ball expectation [munciana-2022-camp-drills][aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting]. Lingenfelter's drill library supplies the tempo-by-plant timing cues used when teaching the setter-hitter connection.

Cue order tracks the order in which a setter's contact fails. Footwork breaks down first (setter is late, square is off, base is wrong); hands break down second (contact behind the ball, asymmetric release, push instead of hinge); vision breaks down third (setter looks at the ball instead of scanning blockers). Cues are catalogued in that order.

## Per-subskill canonical cues

### Footwork & posture

**"Beat the ball."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. Teaches that the setter arrives at the contact zone *before* the ball does, with feet set, knees loaded, shoulders square to the intended target. The single most repeated setter footwork cue in the modern program. The reverse failure — meeting the ball, with the feet still moving — is the most common cause of off-target sets at every level. Does NOT teach hovering; the goal is to arrive early enough to be settled, not so early that the rep loses tempo.

**"Right foot up, square to target."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+ into "stagger toward target so the right foot is forward of the left." Teaches the asymmetric base that loads the legs and aligns the hips into the set direction. Liskevych's ASICS setting instruction lands here on body alignment and footwork sequencing [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-setting]. Does NOT teach flat-footed stance — a setter set up flat-footed cannot push off into a backset or jump-set.

**"Legs first, hands second."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Teaches that power on the set comes from the legs extending through contact, not from the wrists. Common error correction at 13s+ for setters whose ball "flutters" at the apex — the wrist-snap is happening but with no leg drive underneath. Does NOT teach a leg pump — the legs extend smoothly through the set, not in a discrete jump.

### Hand shape & contact

**"Side of the ball, not behind."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The Dunning hand-hinge cue rendered as a positional rule [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge]. Teaches that the hands wrap the curvature of the ball at contact rather than meeting it flat with the pads. A setter whose hands sit behind the ball converts the set into a push and forfeits the hand-hinge release. Does NOT teach the entire set mechanic — it is contact-position-only, but it is the gating cue Dunning identifies as the difference between elite and ordinary setters [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge].

**"Ball-shaped hands."** Introduced 11s. Refined 13s+. The shape cue paired with the side-of-the-ball positional cue. Teaches that the fingers are relaxed, curved to match the ball's surface, and ready to wrap rather than slap. Does NOT teach interlocked or splayed fingers — the hands are loose and curved.

**"Hinge."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. The single-word compression of the hand-hinge release [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge]. Teaches the short, firm wrist flexion-extension at contact that releases the ball off the pads of the fingers. Does NOT teach a long wrist-snap — hinge is *short* — and does NOT teach a forearm push, which is the legacy alternative the modern frame replaces.

**"Hold the follow-through."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. Teaches that the hands stay extended in the follow-through position long enough that the coach (and the player herself) can see whether the hands tracked through the target line. A held follow-through is also the diagnostic for double-contact violations: asymmetric release shows in the held position [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge]. Does NOT teach hand stillness throughout the set — the hands move through contact and then *hold* afterward.

**"Up the chimney."** Introduced 11s. Refined 13s+. A youth-setting cue captured in the AOC kids-setting fundamentals corpus and broadly in the Rockwell setter-training tradition [aoc-2015-rockwell-training-setters]. Teaches that the early-developmental set has a strong vertical component — exaggerate the vertical to fix flat, forward-pushed sets. Does NOT teach a strictly vertical release — sets travel forward to a target; the chimney cue is a corrective, not the final mechanic.

### Vision & decision

**"Eyes on target before the ball."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. Teaches that the setter scans the block and chooses a hitter *before* the pass arrives, then locks vision on the target line through contact. The decision is made before the hands meet the ball; the contact is delivery, not decision. Does NOT teach looking away from the ball at the moment of contact — vision finds the target, then returns to the ball at contact, then returns to follow the set.

**"What's the block giving me?"** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Rockwell setter-feedback prompt rendered as a coach-question or a self-talk cue [aoc-2015-rockwell-training-setters]. Teaches that the setter's distribution is a function of what the opposing block has uncovered — middle pinched in opens the pin, two-blocker commit on the outside opens the middle. At 16s+ this becomes a real-time read. Does NOT teach a default distribution — the cue forces the read; the answer varies.

**"Find the shoulder."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches that the setter's eyes locate the receiving hitter's hitting shoulder before contact — the hitter's plant point and arm-swing window become the target geometry, not the antenna. A hitter-shoulder set is more catchable than an antenna-targeted set [aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection]. Does NOT teach setting *to* the shoulder — the set lands in the air the hitter will swing through; the shoulder is the visual anchor.

### Tempo & connection

**"Tempo 1 / Tempo 2 / Tempo 3."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Lingenfelter / Munciana-tradition tempo language [unsourced] tied to the timing of the hitter's left-foot plant relative to the set release: Tempo 1 (quick, ball released near the plant — middles), Tempo 2 (medium, ball released as the hitter is mid-approach — pipes, slides), Tempo 3 (high outside, ball released early enough for a full approach). Teaches that the setter and the hitter share a timing language, not just a target language. Does NOT teach a fixed ball-height table — tempo is a timing relationship; the height varies with player preference.

**"Set what the pass gives you."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches that set selection follows pass quality — a perfect pass to zone-3 supports any tempo, a 2-pass off the net forces a high outside or a tilt-set bail. Dunning's hitter-setter-connection corpus emphasizes that pre-scripted sets fail against real passes [aoc-2014-dunning-hitter-setter-connection]. Does NOT teach abandoning the play call — the called play is the default; pass quality dictates whether it survives.

**"Vision on target."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Compressed form of the eyes-on-target-before-the-ball cue applied during in-system reps. Teaches that the setter's gaze finds the target line before contact, holds through the release, and tracks the set until it leaves the hitter. Reinforces neutral shoulder position and prevents telegraphing distribution.

### Out-of-system & libero second ball

**"Bump-set high to the pin."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The default OOS bail. Teaches that when the first contact is poor enough that an overhead set is illegal or impractical, the setter delivers a high forearm pass to the antenna and the offense reorganizes around an outside-pin attack. Does NOT teach giving up — the high outside is an offense option, not a free ball to the opponent.

**"Libero, behind the 3."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The cue for the libero-second-ball overhead set rule: a libero can use overhead hands to set only when the libero is *behind* the 3-meter line at contact (or when the first ball is below the libero's waist, in which case overhead is moot). Teaches the rule and reinforces the trained-not-emergency expectation that the libero is a secondary setter [aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting]. Does NOT teach defaulting the libero to the second ball — only when the setter has taken the first or otherwise cannot deliver.

**"Target zone, not 'anywhere.'"** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Brown libero-second-ball cue [aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting]. Teaches that the libero's emergency set goes to a defined target zone (typically the antenna, sometimes the right pin), not to whichever hitter is closest. Does NOT teach a single-target rule — the target zone is the default, with situational redirects allowed.

### Backset

**"Square shoulders, set the back."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches that the backset releases off the same hand-hinge as the front set, with shoulders neutral so the opposing middle blocker cannot cheat. The shoulders do not rotate to give away the set direction. Does NOT teach a backward-leaning torso — that telegraphs the back set and produces flat trajectories.

**"Hand-hinge back."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The hinge cue applied to the backset: short wrist extension that drops the ball over the head rather than pushing it forward. Same modern mechanic as the front set, applied to the back direction. Does NOT teach a chest-out arched-back release — that's the legacy backset that telegraphs and floats.

### Jump set

**"Set on the way up."** Introduced 14s. Refined 15s+. Teaches that the jump-set release happens in the upward phase of the jump, not at the apex. A setter who jumps and then sets is jumping for nothing. Does NOT teach a half-jump — the setter takes a real jump that threatens a front-row attack and releases the ball during the rise.

**"Threat first, set second."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. Teaches that the jump-set's primary purpose is to threaten the second-contact attack (and thereby freeze the opposing middle blocker), with the actual set as the secondary deliverable. Does NOT teach attacking every second ball — most jump-sets are sets, not attacks; the threat does the work.

## Anti-cues

- **"Push the ball to target."** The legacy push-set vocabulary the modern hand-hinge frame replaces. A push-set produces flat trajectories and a contact behind the ball — exactly what Dunning's hand-hinge instruction corrects [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge]. Modern correction: "Side of the ball, hinge."
- **"Snap your wrists."** Internally-focused body-part cue that produces a wrist motion without leg drive. The result is a "fluttery" set without stability. Modern correction: "Legs first, hands second" plus "Hinge."
- **"Set with your forearms when in trouble."** Bails too quickly to the forearm-bump and forfeits tempo. Modern correction: "Bump-set high to the pin" *only when* the pass is below waist or the rule forbids overhead — otherwise hands.
- **"Reach for it."** Encourages reaching with the hands instead of moving the feet. Modern correction: "Beat the ball."
- **"Don't set the ball flat."** Negative outcome cue. Modern correction: a process cue ("Up the chimney" for height, "Vision on target" for direction) replaces the outcome cue.
- **"Hands behind the ball."** Wrong contact position. Modern correction: "Side of the ball, not behind."
- **"Stand straight up."** Wrong setter posture. Modern correction: "Right foot up, square to target" (legs loaded, asymmetric base).
- **"Look at your hitter."** Telegraphs distribution by rotating the eyes early. Modern correction: "Vision on target before the ball" — the gaze finds the target before contact, then returns to the ball.
- **"Set high every time."** Removes tempo from the offense. Modern correction: "Set what the pass gives you" — a concrete pass-to-tempo decision rule.
- **"You can't set with one hand."** Untrue at the rule level (though commonly enforced in youth play) and contradicts the OOS toolbox. Modern correction: in OOS, the legal-and-effective tool is whichever the rule and the situation allow; at the modern level, two-handed redirects, tilt-sets, and one-handed bumps are all in the inventory.

## Voice consistency notes

The setting cue vocabulary is genuinely contested across schools, and the dictionary preserves the disagreement.

**Modern AOC** (Dunning's hand-hinge, Rockwell's setter-training, Liskevych's ASICS setting) leads the program's working voice [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge][aoc-2015-rockwell-training-setters][aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-setting]. Hand-hinge release, side-of-the-ball contact, hold-the-follow-through, beat-the-ball footwork, and vision-on-target are the canonical cues.

**Munciana** supplies the tempo-by-plant language (Tempo 1/2/3 by left-foot plant) and the libero-second-ball expectation as a trained skill rather than an emergency [munciana-2022-camp-drills][aoc-2022-brown-libero-second-ball-setting]. Lingenfelter's library does not contradict the AOC vocabulary; it adds the working-club tempo coordinates.

**GMS / motor learning** does not contribute distinct cue vocabulary so much as cue *form*: external-focus cues outperform internal-focus cues, so "side of the ball" beats "engage your fingertips," "vision on target" beats "look up," and "hold the follow-through" beats "extend your wrists fully." The program prefers the externally-focused option whenever both are available.

**Japanese tradition** keeps a narrower setting toolbox with stronger precision standards on each tool. The cue vocabulary is similar at the mechanical level (hand shape, contact position, follow-through) but the practice volume on each tool runs higher and the toolbox breadth is smaller. The program's resolution is to teach the AOC toolbox breadth while preserving the Japanese-tradition standard of precision on each tool.

**Ecological dynamics** prefers cues that emerge from constraint-led practice rather than instruction-led practice. The modern AOC cue vocabulary is compatible — "beat the ball," "set the angle," "find the shoulder" all describe relationships between the setter and the environment rather than internal body-part movements. The program leans this way per the recency preference.

When schools genuinely disagree on a setting cue — for example, "set the ball with your fingertips" (precision-tradition cue) versus "wrap the ball with ball-shaped hands" (modern hand-hinge cue) — the dictionary keeps both, attributes each, and notes that the program's working voice is the modern one. Coaches should understand that an experienced player who learned the "fingertips" cue is not wrong; she has learned a different generation's vocabulary for the same skill, and the modern correction is to pair her existing tools with the modern hand-hinge rather than to override her hands.

The phrases every coach in the program should say: beat the ball, side of the ball, hinge, hold the follow-through, legs first, vision on target, find the shoulder, set what the pass gives you, libero behind the 3, target zone. Ten phrases. Repeat them.
