# Passing cues

## Overview

Passing is the highest-volume teaching surface in the program. Every player passes — front-row, back-row, libero, setter on the second ball, hitter on a free ball — and every practice features serve-receive, ball-control, and free-ball passing reps. That makes the cue vocabulary unusually load-bearing: the words the staff says here repeat hundreds of times per practice, thousands of times per season. This dictionary establishes the canonical phrasing.

The cues lean modern AOC reading-first per Kiraly's four-keys frame and Bechard's posture-pursuit-platform progression [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass][aoc-2025-passing-posture-pursuit-platform]. Lingenfelter's Munciana drill-library cues — "quiet platform," "drop the inside shoulder," "drag the ball to target," "always finish cover" — supply the working-club layer in the same voice [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Where modern cues replace older phrasing, the older words are catalogued in the Anti-cues section so coaches can recognize what they are *not* saying.

The teaching order is **posture → pursuit → platform**, and the cue vocabulary is organized to land in that order. A passer who is in good posture but late on pursuit will pass off-balance no matter how good her platform is; a passer who pursued correctly but reaches with the platform from a wide stance will shank balls she should have midlined. Order the cues to the order of failure modes.

## Per-subskill canonical cues

### Posture (forearm)

**"Stopped and balanced."** Introduced 10s. Refined 13s+ into "stopped and balanced before the toss leaves the server's hand." The first of Kiraly's four keys [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]. Teaches that the passer arrives at the ball stationary, on the balls of the feet, with weight forward and shoulders over knees — *before* the contact happens. Does NOT teach reaching, lunging, or hip-rotating into the ball; those are downstream errors that become possible only when posture has failed.

**"Right foot up, left foot back" (right-handed dominant) or its mirror.** Introduced 10s. Refined at 14s+ into "stagger your stance toward target." Teaches the asymmetric receive-ready stance Bechard describes [aoc-2025-passing-posture-pursuit-platform], which biases the passer to start moving forward and laterally rather than straight up. Does NOT teach square-stance posture, which is fine for setting but flat-footed for receiving.

**"Shoulders over knees."** Introduced 10s. Refined 12s+ into "chest over toes, weight on the balls." Teaches the forward-loaded athletic posture that makes pursuit possible. Liskevych's ASICS passing instruction lands the same point under different vocabulary [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-passing]. Does NOT teach a low squat — a passer who drops her hips below her knees can no longer push off in any direction.

### Pursuit (forearm)

**"Move your feet, not your arms."** Introduced 10s. Lifelong cue. The single most repeated correction in passing teaching. Teaches that the legs move the body to the ball, not the platform. A passer who reaches with the arms surrenders platform stability the moment she misses. Does NOT teach a perfect-feet rule — sometimes the ball is past, and a one-arm or shin-dig emergency move is the right call (see [[aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense]] for the emergency inventory).

**"Midline the ball."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+ into "play the ball at your centerline." Teaches that the passer arrives behind the ball so the platform is centered between the legs at contact, not off the hip. Lingenfelter's Munciana cue "drop the inside shoulder" is a related shoulder-rotation cue for tilt passing and is taught alongside [munciana-2022-camp-drills].

**"Move low."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. A direct Munciana camp cue ("Move low. Move low. Move low.") emphasizing that pursuit footwork stays in the athletic posture, not stand-and-shuffle [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches that lateral and angled pursuit happen without rising out of the loaded stance. Does NOT teach a slow shuffle — Munciana's library uses move-low *under speed*.

**"Beat the ball" (or "be there early").** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Teaches that the passer arrives at the contact zone with time to spare, then forms the platform — the late-platform-formation correction Stone names. Does NOT teach hovering — at higher levels the passer is already starting to read the next attacker before the ball arrives.

### Platform formation (forearm)

**"Thumbs and wrists together."** Introduced 10s. Refined 13s+ into "stack your hands, thumbs parallel pointing down." The second of Kiraly's four keys [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass], converging with Nelson's youth fundamentals (flat arms, wrists/thumbs/hands together) [aoc-2024-kids-passing-fundamentals]. Teaches a flat platform where neither arm is higher than the other and there is no thumb-gap. Does NOT teach interlocked fingers (a high-school-and-below variant that still works mechanically; the wrist-stack version is preferred at the modern teaching default).

**"Set the angle with your hips."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. Kiraly's third key [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]. Teaches that the platform's angle to target is created by shoulder/hip rotation, not arm adjustment. A passer who "aims with the platform" by lifting one arm produces a gap. The modern correction is to keep the platform flat and rotate the body so the flat platform faces target. Does NOT teach arm tilt — explicitly the opposite.

**"Aim with your shoulders."** Introduced 13s. Same teaching point as "set the angle" but in shorter form for in-rep correction. Externally-focused per the GMS motor-learning rationale: the passer's attention goes to the relationship between her shoulders and the target, not to the body part doing the work.

**"Quiet platform."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. A direct Lingenfelter Munciana cue captured verbatim in the camp transcript ("Move low. Quiet platform. Go.") [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches that the platform does not swing through the ball — it presents a stationary surface and the legs/shoulders provide what little push is needed. Lingenfelter's commentary is explicit: "Your players get in trouble when they swing their platform" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Does NOT teach a stiff platform — quiet is not rigid; the elbows lock but the shoulder girdle stays relaxed.

**"Late platform."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. The Stone correction expressed as a cue [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-passing-style overlap]. Teaches that the platform forms in the last half-second before contact, not on the way to the ball. A pre-formed platform locks in an angle that can no longer adjust to a serve that drops or drifts. Does NOT teach forming the platform *at* contact — the platform should be ready before contact, just not built early.

**"Simple one-way motion."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Kiraly's fourth key [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]. Teaches that the platform's only motion is toward target — there is no swing, no follow-through arc, no second motion. The platform pushes forward to target and stops. Does NOT teach a passive platform — there is a small forward push, but it is one-directional.

### Reading the server

**"Read before the toss."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+ into "see the server's toss before she sees it." Teaches that the passer is reading the server's toss height, contact point, and approach angle to predict serve type and trajectory. Kiraly's reads-as-part-of-every-skill frame applies directly [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill]. Does NOT teach guessing — reading is hypothesis based on observable cues, not random commitment.

**"What do I see? What do I know? How do I respond?"** Introduced 15s. The Kiraly three-question reading frame applied to passing [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill]. Teaches that reading separates into perception (what cues are visible), knowledge (what this server has done before), and response (the movement choice). At advanced levels this becomes a debrief tool between reps as much as a real-time cue.

### Overhead pass

**"Hands above the eye line."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Teaches the contact point for the overhead pass — high, in front of the forehead, with the same hand shape as a set. Does NOT teach catching or setting per se — it is a contact-only cue.

**"Choose hands or platform before the serve crosses."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches the binary decision moment in serve receive: above the shoulders the passer goes to hands; below, to forearms. The decision happens at the net, not at contact. Does NOT teach a default to hands — at the modern women's club level the forearm platform is still the volume tool; the overhead pass is the upgrade.

### Free-ball pass

**"Two steps in, target the seam."** Introduced 11s. Refined 13s+ into "free ball, attack the seam." Teaches the two-step forward closure on a free ball plus the target-direction priority — the seam between zone-2 and zone-3 is the easiest set the setter can work with. Does NOT teach a tip-back-and-restart — converting a free ball into easy offense is the teaching priority at every level.

**"Always finish cover."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. Direct Munciana cue captured in the camp transcript ("They will pass and always finish cover.") [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches the immediate move after passing — into the position required for the next contact, whether covering the hitter, setting up to attack a free ball, or transitioning to defense. Does NOT teach holding the platform — the cue is about footwork *after* the pass, not arm finish.

### Pass-and-cover (Munciana micro-skill)

**"Pass-and-cover."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. A two-word compression of the post-pass responsibility: the passer's next motion is into hitter coverage. Lingenfelter teaches this as a single micro-skill in the Munciana camp library, with reps explicitly drilling pass + immediate cover footwork [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches the mental model that passing is not a finished act when the ball leaves the platform — the rep continues into the next role. Does NOT teach a specific cover position — that varies by formation; the cue installs the *habit*, not the geometry.

## Anti-cues

These phrases sound like teaching but mislead. Coaches who hear themselves saying these should swap them for the modern equivalents.

- **"Pass with your platform" / "make a perfect platform first."** Front-loads platform mechanics over reading and pursuit, producing passers who shank against real serves. Modern correction: posture-pursuit-platform in that order. The platform is the *last* thing to form, not the first.
- **"Reach for it."** Encourages arm reaching in lieu of footwork. Modern correction: "Move your feet, not your arms."
- **"Stand tall."** Wrong posture for a passer; produces flat-footed receivers who cannot push off in any direction. Modern correction: "Shoulders over knees."
- **"Bend your knees more."** Internally-focused leg-position cue that often produces a deep squat where the hips drop below the knees. Modern correction: "Chest over toes" or "weight on the balls of your feet" — externalizes attention onto a relationship rather than a body part.
- **"Swing your arms through the ball."** A power-passing legacy cue that contradicts the modern quiet-platform standard. Modern correction: "Quiet platform" — the legs and shoulders supply what little forward motion is needed.
- **"Tilt your platform up."** Produces an asymmetric platform where one arm rises and a gap opens between the forearms. Modern correction: "Set the angle with your hips" — keep the platform flat, rotate the body to face target.
- **"Pass it high."** Vague target instruction. Modern correction: a concrete target (the setter zone, a specific cone, the second-set shoulder of a labeled target) replaces the height adjective.
- **"Don't shank."** Negative cue that focuses attention on the failure outcome. Modern correction: a process cue ("Quiet platform" or "Set the angle") replaces the outcome cue.

## Voice consistency notes

The passing cue vocabulary is the most converged across modern AOC, Munciana, and motor-learning-grounded teaching — there is less genuine disagreement here than in any other skill area. The four-key Kiraly frame, posture-pursuit-platform, late-platform formation, and quiet-platform language all sit in the same modern lineage [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass][aoc-2025-passing-posture-pursuit-platform][munciana-2022-camp-drills].

Where disagreements exist, they are about *order* and *emphasis*, not vocabulary. AOC and Munciana both lead with reading-and-pursuit; some traditional/Russian-school programs still lead with platform mechanics. The program's resolution is to lead with reading-and-pursuit per the recency preference but to keep platform-mechanics cues available when a player's platform is the actual failure point on the rep.

GMS supplies the motor-learning rationale: cues that externalize attention ("aim with your shoulders," "set the angle") outperform cues that internalize ("snap your wrists," "tighten your platform") because external-focus attention produces better skill retention and transfer. When a coach has to choose between two cues that say the same thing, the externally-focused option wins.

The Japanese tradition contributes the dig-up-everything ethic and the midline-the-ball priority — both compatible with the modern AOC vocabulary and folded into the passing cues without conflict.

Ecological dynamics' contribution is procedural rather than lexical: cues land best when spoken inside representative tasks (live serve receive against a real server) rather than in decomposed drills (coach tosses to a stationary passer). The same words work; the context determines whether they transfer.

When schools genuinely disagree on a passing cue, the dictionary keeps both with attribution. Example: "swing your arms forward through contact" (legacy/Russian power-passing cue) versus "quiet platform, simple one-way motion" (modern AOC/Munciana cue). The program's working voice is the modern one; the legacy phrasing is preserved in the Anti-cues section above.

Coaches new to the staff should listen for these phrases in the working vocabulary and add them to their own coaching language: stopped and balanced, thumbs together, set the angle, simple one-way motion, move your feet, midline the ball, quiet platform, late platform, aim with your shoulders, always finish cover, pass-and-cover. Eleven phrases. Repeat them.
