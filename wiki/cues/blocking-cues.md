# Blocking cues

## Overview

Blocking is one of the higher-leverage skills to teach with cues because the moment-to-moment decisions are dense — read the setter, read the hitter, time the jump, position the hands, finish the press — and the time window is short. The cue vocabulary has converged in the last decade on a modern frame: **read drives everything**, **swing-block footwork is the default at HS and above**, **press hands over the net**, and the Munciana-specific **outside-foot-high pin penetration** rule [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill][aoc-2026-swing-blocking-footwork-beginners][aoc-2024-slabe-blocking][munciana-2022-camp-drills].

The cues lean modern AOC across Slabe's hand-press teaching, Rosen's swing-block-for-beginners progression, Mardini's swing-blocking case, Sagula's static-blocking fundamentals, and Slabe's bunch-read system [aoc-2024-slabe-blocking][aoc-2026-swing-blocking-footwork-beginners][aoc-2024-importance-teaching-swing-blocking][aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals][aoc-2026-bunch-read-blocking]. Munciana's working-club layer adds the outside-foot-high pin-penetration cue and Lingenfelter's coaching commentary that "this doesn't happen by accident, it has to be taught" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. McCutcheon's blocking-keys corpus contributes middle-blocker independence and read-drives-system framing [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys].

Cues are organized in the order a block fails: read goes wrong first (eyes on the wrong cue, late commit); footwork goes wrong second (late close, off-balance plant); hands go wrong third (low press, cross-the-net early, no penetration). Order the cue language to that failure order.

## Per-subskill canonical cues

### Read

**"Ball-setter-ball-hitter."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The canonical Kiraly eye-progression cue [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill][kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball]. Teaches the four-step eye sequence: see the ball off the pass, find the setter, return to the ball as it leaves the setter's hands, then commit eyes to the hitter at the apex. Compresses the entire reading discipline into a single phrase. Does NOT teach staring at the hitter — eyes spend most of the read on the setter and the ball.

**"Read, then jump."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Teaches that the block's timing is set by the hitter's apex, not by the ball off the setter's hands. A blocker who jumps with the set is too early. The cue installs the patience to wait for the hitter's commitment before lifting. Does NOT teach a reactive jump — at higher levels the blocker is already committed by the time the hitter swings; the cue is about *not* jumping with the set.

**"What is the setter giving me?"** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Slabe / McCutcheon read-the-setter prompt rendered as a cue [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys][aoc-2024-slabe-blocking]. Teaches that the setter's body position, hand-shape commit point, and eye-line all telegraph distribution before the ball releases. McCutcheon emphasizes middle-blocker independence: middles should read and block independently rather than always closing to the wing blocker [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys]. Does NOT teach a fixed read protocol — the cue forces the read; the answer varies.

### Footwork (static / shuffle)

**"Outside foot high."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Munciana pin-blocking cue captured verbatim from Lingenfelter's camp library: "Make sure everybody's outside foot is high. We want to make sure the outside foot is high every time. So that means the foot on the line needs to be ahead of the foot in the court so their bellies are angled back and in the court. This doesn't happen by accident, it has to be taught" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches the pin blocker's stance — the outside foot (the one closer to the antenna) sits forward of the inside foot, with the torso angled back into the court. This sealing posture closes the seam and lets the blocker rotate her body into the line of attack rather than reaching across her own body. Does NOT teach a square-stance pin block — explicitly the opposite, and Lingenfelter's commentary names this as a teaching priority that requires explicit instruction.

**"Cut the outside foot."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Munciana movement cue captured in the camp transcript ("Make sure you cut the outside foot.") [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches that the pin blocker's lateral movement leads with the outside foot — the outside foot moves first into the seal position, the inside foot trails. Does NOT teach a shuffle that leads with the inside foot, which produces a delayed seal.

**"Step-close."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+. The shuffle-block footwork cue. Teaches the basic two-step lateral pattern: outside foot steps, inside foot closes. The literacy footwork before swing-block is introduced. Does NOT teach a single-step block — at any meaningful pin distance, the blocker steps and closes.

**"Plant balanced."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. Teaches that the block's plant arrives with weight evenly distributed and feet flat, not on the toes or off-balance. A blocker who plants on the toes lifts forward into the net; a blocker who plants off-balance produces a hand position that drifts at apex. Does NOT teach a passive plant — the plant is the loading position for the jump.

### Footwork (swing block)

**"Long, aggressive steps."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Rosen swing-block-for-beginners cue [aoc-2026-swing-blocking-footwork-beginners]. Teaches that the swing-block footwork uses long aggressive strides — sprint-arm pattern, full hip rotation, and lateral coverage that exceeds the static-shuffle pattern. Does NOT teach a slow shuffle — swing block is the *acceleration* layer on top of the static literacy.

**"Footwork first, jump later."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Rosen progression cue captured directly: "Teach the footwork first without a jump; only add the jump once steps are comfortable" [aoc-2026-swing-blocking-footwork-beginners]. Teaches the staged installation of swing-block: first practice the steps from the 10-foot line without jumping, then add the jump once the footwork is reliable. Does NOT teach the entire swing-block pattern at once — the staged order is the teaching design.

**"Sprint-arm the close."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Teaches the swing-block arm action: arms swing aggressively forward and back like a sprinter through the lateral close, building momentum into the plant. Mardini's basketball-rim analogy makes the same point: "trying to touch a basketball rim, nobody stands still — they run and jump; swing blocking applies the same principle at the net" [aoc-2024-importance-teaching-swing-blocking]. Does NOT teach static arms — explicitly the opposite.

### Hands & jump

**"Hands at face level, ready before contact."** Introduced 11s. Lifelong. The Sagula static-blocking-fundamentals cue [aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals]. Teaches that the blocker starts with hands held at face level (eye level), fingers spread, never below waist. A blocker whose hands start at waist level loses the time-to-press. Does NOT teach high static hands at all moments — the start-position rule is for the ready position, not for the apex.

**"Press over."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The Slabe penetrate-the-net hand-press cue [aoc-2024-slabe-blocking]. Teaches that at apex the hands extend over the net into the opposing court — fingers spread, thumbs up, palms angled down. The block is not a vertical wall; it is a press into the opponent's space. Does NOT teach reaching across — the press is straight forward over the net, not diagonally across to seal a seam (which is a separate cue).

**"Penetrate, don't cover."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The modern correction to a long-standing error pattern. Teaches that the blocker's hands extend *over* the net (into the opposing court) rather than reaching *back* to cover an attacker behind the net. A "covering" block produces a flat block face that absorbs the ball without redirecting it. Does NOT teach a static block — the hands still match the attacker's likely angle, but they do so while penetrating, not while retreating.

**"Thumbs up, palms down."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+. The hand-shape cue at apex. Teaches the modern fingers-spread-stiff, thumbs-up, palms-angled-down configuration that maximizes block surface area and directs the ball downward. Does NOT teach flat hands or vertical palms — both produce a block that deflects rather than presses.

**"Land balanced, ready to transition."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. Sagula's landing cue captured directly: "two keys to first-line defense: (1) jump and reach over the net (penetration); (2) land balanced and ready for transition" [aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals]. Teaches that the block ends with a balanced landing that lets the blocker immediately enter transition footwork. Does NOT teach a stuck landing — the block-to-approach recovery starts at touchdown (see [[transition-cues]]).

### System cues

**"Bunch in, release on the read."** Introduced 16s. Refined 17s+. The Slabe bunch-read system cue [aoc-2026-bunch-read-blocking]. Teaches that the pin blockers start pinched in toward the middle to help on seam sets, then release to cover the pin once the set commits. Slabe is explicit that this scheme demands "dynamic, fast pin blockers who can read sets and attackers" — it is best suited to advanced levels [aoc-2026-bunch-read-blocking]. Does NOT teach a permanent pin position — the bunch is the *start*, not the destination.

**"Middle reads, doesn't follow."** Introduced 16s. Refined 17s+. The McCutcheon middle-blocker-independence cue [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys]. Teaches that the middle blocker's commit decision is hers — she reads the setter and goes where the read sends her, rather than always closing to the pin blocker. Does NOT teach a free-lance middle — the middle's reads exist within the team blocking scheme; independence is *within* the system, not outside it.

**"Stuff for points, not for tip."** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. The Slabe goal-of-blocking cue rendered tactically: "Blocking goal: stuff for points; not funnel, soft-block, or deflect" [aoc-2024-slabe-blocking]. Teaches that the block's primary objective is the terminal stuff, with deflection and tip-coverage as secondary outcomes. Does NOT teach abandoning soft-block defense — the cue is about *intent*, not about banning softer blocks situationally.

## Anti-cues

- **"Hands at the net."** Vague start-position cue; produces blockers with hands at waist or chest level. Modern correction: "Hands at face level, ready before contact" [aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals].
- **"Jump with the set."** Times the block too early. Modern correction: "Read, then jump" — wait for the hitter's commitment.
- **"Reach over."** Used loosely, this turns into "reach across the net" rather than penetration. Modern correction: "Press over" — the press is forward, not diagonal.
- **"Square block."** Fine for some pin attacks but produces a non-sealing pin block on outside attacks. Modern correction: "Outside foot high" for pin blocks; the square stance is a middle-block default, not a pin default.
- **"Block everything."** Blocking is the skill least correlated with winning, per McCutcheon's data: "Blocking is the skill least correlated with winning; prioritize serving, passing, and sideout in practice" [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys]. Modern correction: prioritize block touches as defense, not stuff-rate as the metric — the block sets up the dig, the dig sets up the transition.
- **"Soft-block when you can't get the stuff."** Encourages a soft-block default; Slabe's frame is the opposite [aoc-2024-slabe-blocking]. Modern correction: "Stuff for points, not for tip" — soft-block is situational, not the default.
- **"Always close to the pin."** The legacy middle-blocker rule. McCutcheon's modern frame says middles should be free to read independently [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys]. Modern correction: "Middle reads, doesn't follow."
- **"Time the block."** Vague timing instruction. Modern correction: "Read, then jump" — the timing is read-driven, not internal-clock-driven.
- **"Don't get tooled."** Negative cue that focuses attention on the failure outcome. Modern correction: "Press over" plus "Outside foot high" — process cues that solve the underlying mechanic.

## Voice consistency notes

The blocking cue vocabulary is genuinely contested across schools, particularly on the swing-block-versus-static-block question.

**Modern AOC at HS and above** (Slabe, Rosen, Mardini, Kiraly) leads the program's working voice on swing-block as the default footwork [aoc-2024-importance-teaching-swing-blocking][aoc-2026-swing-blocking-footwork-beginners][aoc-2024-slabe-blocking]. The cue language reflects this: "long, aggressive steps," "sprint-arm the close," "footwork first, jump later." At 14U the program uses static-block literacy; the swing-block cue vocabulary becomes primary at 15s and above.

**AOC dissent within the modern frame.** Liskevych and Murczek question whether swing-blocking actually produces the net penetration that matters; Liskevych calls swing blocking the "flavor of the month" and argues traditional technique is more durable. The program's resolution: teach static-block literacy first, layer swing-block on top once footwork is stable, and preserve static-block cues ("step-close," "plant balanced") for blockers who never make the swing-block transition.

**Munciana** supplies the pin-blocking outside-foot-high cue and the explicit teaching commentary that this is not natural — "this doesn't happen by accident, it has to be taught" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. The Munciana voice does not contradict the AOC frame; it adds working-club specificity on a teaching point AOC sources cover less directly.

**GMS** contributes the practice-architecture argument: blocking reads transfer better when trained inside random/variable practice rather than blocked progression from predetermined sets to full variability. The cue language is compatible — the dictionary's read cues ("Ball-setter-ball-hitter," "What is the setter giving me?") are external-focus prompts that work in any practice architecture.

**Japanese tradition** trains blocking as one layer of a defense-first system, with anticipation, timing, and precise feet compensating for shorter reach. The cue vocabulary at the technique level is similar; the distinctive Japanese contribution is at the *system* level — the block is expected to deflect more than stuff, with the floor defense reading off the block's shape. This matters less for cue language than for tactical framing.

**Ecological dynamics** prefers cues that emerge from constraint-led practice. "Read, then jump" and "Press over" are relational cues that work in any task; the dictionary's vocabulary is largely compatible with the eco-dynamics frame.

When schools genuinely disagree on a blocking cue — for example, "shuffle and lift" (legacy/Liskevych preference) versus "long aggressive steps with sprint arms" (modern AOC swing-block preference) — the dictionary keeps both with attribution and notes that the program's working voice at 15s+ is the modern swing-block frame.

The phrases every coach in the program should say: ball-setter-ball-hitter, read then jump, what is the setter giving me, outside foot high, cut the outside foot, hands at face level, press over, penetrate don't cover, thumbs up palms down, land balanced ready to transition, long aggressive steps (15s+), footwork first jump later (15s+ swing-block install), middle reads doesn't follow, stuff for points not for tip. Fourteen phrases. Repeat them.
