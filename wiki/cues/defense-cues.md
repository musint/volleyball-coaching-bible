# Defense cues

## Overview

Defense is a back-row reading-and-pursuit skill, and the cue vocabulary the staff uses on defensive reps determines whether players develop into elite diggers or stay average. The modern frame leads with **the Three P's of defense (get back, get low, get a touch — Munciana)**, **Stone's hips-under-the-ball platform-management cue**, **Josephson's Look/See/Decide reading frame**, and **Kiraly's read-the-hitter-not-the-ball priority** [munciana-2022-camp-drills][aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig][aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide][aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill].

The cues lean modern AOC across Stone's controlling-the-dig corpus, Josephson's look-see-decide compartmentalization, Liskevych's ASICS defense instruction, Flynn-Oldenburg's emergency-move inventory, and Kiraly's reading-as-part-of-every-skill frame [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig][aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide][aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-defense][aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense][aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill]. Munciana's working-club layer provides the Three P's vocabulary that organizes the program's defensive identity [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Japanese-tradition cues are preserved where the dig-up-everything ethic carries vocabulary worth keeping.

Cues are organized in the order a defensive rep fails: read goes wrong first (eyes on the wrong cue, no commitment), pursuit goes wrong second (late-step, reach with the arms instead of the legs), platform goes wrong third (early formation, off-angle, hips not under the ball), and recovery-after-touch goes wrong fourth (ball goes up, no one gets to the second contact).

## Per-subskill canonical cues

### The Three P's (Munciana defensive identity)

**"Get back."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The first of Munciana's Three P's of defense, captured directly from Lingenfelter's camp library: "Now, the three P's of defense, they're going to get back. Have a quick seat. They got to tap their tush on the box. That tells me they understand position" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches the first defensive priority: get back to the proper defensive starting position before the hitter contacts the ball. A defender who is still moving forward at hitter contact has ceded the dig before the swing happens. Does NOT teach a fixed depth — the back depth varies by formation; the cue installs the *priority* of being back.

**"Get low."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The second of Munciana's Three P's, captured from the same source [munciana-2022-camp-drills]: "they understand posture." Teaches the loaded athletic posture defenders hold during the read — knees bent, weight forward, hands ready in front. A defender standing tall at hitter contact cannot push off into pursuit. Does NOT teach a fixed posture height — the squat depth varies by player and situation; the cue installs the *priority* of being low.

**"Get a touch."** Introduced 12s. Lifelong. The third of the Three P's, captured directly: "it also tells me on the second ball that they understand passion because they're going to throw a seam ball" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches the third defensive priority: get a touch on every ball, even an emergency touch — the dig does not have to be perfect, it has to *exist*. Lingenfelter's framing makes this the "passion" component of the Three P's. Does NOT teach pursuing every ball at all costs — the cue installs the *intent* to touch; physical limits remain.

**"Position, posture, passion."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. Lingenfelter's full Three P's articulation captured directly: "Seam to seam is teaching posture, position, and then passion" [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Teaches the same three priorities under their longer names — the older or more-advanced version of "get back, get low, get a touch." Does NOT teach a fixed sequence — the three are simultaneous; the names organize the teaching.

### Reading

**"Look, See, Decide."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The Josephson three-step reading frame captured directly [aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide]: "'The look' is the teachable technique: where to watch — approach angle, hips, shoulder angle, hand. 'The see' is the intangible interpretation step. ... 'The decide' is the response choice that follows." Teaches the three-stage reading process: where to look (approach angle, hips, shoulder, hand), what to see (the interpretation that names hitter intent), how to decide (the movement choice). Does NOT teach a fast-cognitive process — at high levels Look-See-Decide compresses into a unified perception, but the diagnosis tool stays compartmentalized.

**"What did you see?"** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The Josephson coach-question that drives the See step [aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide]: "coaches probe it by asking 'What did you see?' and then telling players what to see." Teaches the verbal debrief that develops the player's perceptual vocabulary — between reps, the coach asks what the player saw, then names what *should* have been seen. Does NOT teach a single right answer — the question opens the discussion; the coaching follows.

**"Read the hitter, not the ball."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The classic defensive reading priority. Teaches that the defender's eyes commit to the hitter's hips, shoulder angle, and arm-swing window during the approach — the ball is a distraction at the moment of decision. The Josephson Look frame names the same cues: "approach angle, hips, shoulder angle, hand" [aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide]. Does NOT teach ignoring the ball — the eyes return to the ball at contact, but the hitter's body has already given the read.

**"Ball-setter-ball-hitter."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The Kiraly B-S-B-H eye-progression applied to defense [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill][kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball]. Teaches the same four-step eye sequence used in blocking — see the ball off the pass, find the setter, return to the ball as it leaves the setter's hands, then commit eyes to the hitter at the apex. Does NOT teach staring at the hitter — eyes track through the four steps, ending on the hitter.

**"What do I see? What do I know? How do I respond?"** Introduced 15s. Refined 17s+. The Kiraly three-question frame applied to defense [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill]. Teaches the perception-knowledge-response chain that separates reading-as-skill from reading-as-instinct. At the advanced level this is a debrief tool more than a real-time cue.

### Posture & platform

**"Hips under the ball."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The Stone digging-position cue captured directly from the AOC source [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]: "Get hips under the ball so the torso can remain upright on hard-driven attacks." Teaches that on a hard-driven dig the defender drops her hips low enough that the torso stays upright, allowing the platform to manage trajectory rather than sending the dig flat or backward. Stone's framing: "An upright torso lets the defender manage both trajectory and platform angle" [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]. Does NOT teach a permanent low position — the hips drop to meet the ball; the cue is for the moment of contact.

**"Drop to the knees if you have to."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Stone advanced-defensive-posture cue [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]: "Dropping to the knees is a legitimate advanced defensive posture, not a failure mode." Teaches that the kneel-dig position is a real defensive tool, not an emergency. Stone's example is Morgan Hentz dropping to her knees to control a hard attack [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]. Does NOT teach defaulting to kneel-digs — the cue installs the *legitimacy* of the move, not its frequency.

**"Late platform."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Teaches that the platform forms in the last half-second before contact, not on the way to the ball. Same correction as in passing — pre-formed platforms cannot adjust to a hit that drops or drifts. Does NOT teach forming the platform *at* contact — the platform should be ready before contact, just not built early.

**"Quiet platform."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Borrowed from the passing cue dictionary, applied to defense. Teaches that the platform does not swing through the ball — it presents a stationary surface, and the body provides what little redirection is needed. The dig that "controls" — Stone's framing — comes from a quiet platform that doesn't push the ball into the rafters [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig].

### Pursuit

**"Move your feet, not your arms."** Introduced 11s. Lifelong. Same cue as in passing. Teaches that the legs move the body to the ball, not the platform. The single most repeated correction in defensive teaching. Does NOT teach a perfect-feet rule — emergency moves are real (see below).

**"Arrive stopped, then platform."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. Teaches the two-step ideal: pursuit footwork first, stationary plant second, platform formation third. A defender who is still moving at contact produces an off-angle dig. Does NOT teach a stopped-only rule — at advanced levels, run-throughs and one-arm digs are part of the inventory; the cue is the *ideal*, not the only path.

**"Shuffle, don't reach."** Introduced 11s. Lifelong. The defensive-pursuit footwork cue. Teaches that lateral movement uses shuffle steps for one-step balls (and crossover for two-plus-step balls), with the platform formation deferred until arrival. A defender who reaches with the platform across her body produces a flat dig. Does NOT teach a shuffle-only rule — the inventory includes shuffle, crossover, drop-step, and run-through; the cue installs the *priority* of footwork over arm extension.

### Emergency moves

**"Center to the ball, attack the attacker."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Flynn-Oldenburg emergency-defensive cue captured directly [aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense]: "Emergency moves work from 'GO posture,' face the action, and get belly button (center) to and under the ball; 'attack the attacker.'" Teaches that emergency moves face the attack and aggressively close center-to-ball, rather than reaching from a passive stance. Does NOT teach charging at the hitter — the cue installs the *direction* of emergency movement (forward into the attack), not a literal charge.

**"Double Knee Down."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Flynn-Oldenburg specific emergency-move name [aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense]: "Double Knee Down: controlled move to block the ball with body when a hard-driven ball lands in front of defender below the waist." Teaches a named emergency posture: knees drop simultaneously, body absorbs the ball with platform high. Does NOT teach a slide — the Double Knee Down is stationary; the slide variant is the Shin Dig.

**"Shin Dig."** Introduced 14s. Refined 16s+. The Flynn-Oldenburg companion emergency move [aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense]: "Shin Dig: one shin slides, other knee touches down, finishes in Double Knee Down — used for balls aggressively attacked left or right but not far enough for a traditional floor move." Teaches the lateral emergency move that ends in the Double Knee Down position. Flynn-Oldenburg's analogy: "a catcher blocking a wild pitch, but the defender attacks the ball" [aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense]. Does NOT teach a roll — the Shin Dig stays vertical at the end.

**"One Knee Down."** Introduced 13s. Refined 15s+. The Flynn-Oldenburg passing-and-defense low-ball cue [aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense]: "Passing's One Knee Down creates a solid foundation for getting center under a low ball." Teaches a stable low platform on a low ball — one knee drops, platform stays high, dig is controlled rather than punched. Does NOT teach a kneeling default — the cue installs the move, not a frequency.

### Recovery & cover

**"Up after the dig."** Introduced 12s. Refined 14s+. Teaches the immediate post-dig motion: the defender returns to her feet ready for the next contact. A defender who stays low after the dig surrenders coverage. Does NOT teach abandoning the dig — the upright recovery is the *next* motion, not the dig itself.

**"Cover the hitter."** Introduced 13s. Lifelong. Teaches that after the team transitions to attack, the back-row defenders close in to cover the hitter's swing — defending the block-touch return. Does NOT teach a fixed cover position — coverage geometry varies by attack tempo and rotation; the cue installs the *responsibility*.

## Anti-cues

- **"Just dig it up."** Vague outcome cue. Modern correction: "Get a touch" — the touch is the unit, framed as a process win.
- **"Watch the ball."** Wrong cue priority. Modern correction: "Read the hitter, not the ball."
- **"Stand tall."** Wrong defensive posture. Modern correction: "Get low" — the loaded posture is the defender's default.
- **"Reach with your platform."** Encourages arm extension instead of footwork. Modern correction: "Move your feet, not your arms."
- **"Pre-form the platform."** The legacy passing-style cue applied incorrectly to defense. Modern correction: "Late platform" — the platform forms at the last moment to match the dig angle.
- **"Bend your knees more."** Internally-focused leg-position cue. Modern correction: "Get low" or "Hips under the ball" — externalizes attention onto the relationship.
- **"Don't let it hit the floor."** Negative outcome cue. Modern correction: "Get a touch" — same intent, framed as process.
- **"Watch the hitter's eyes."** Wrong cue target — the hitter's eyes are not a reliable read source. Modern correction: "Look at the hips and shoulders" per Josephson's Look frame [aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide].
- **"Catch it on your chest."** Wrong dig technique for hard-driven balls. Modern correction: "Hips under the ball" plus "Late platform" — the platform makes the contact, not the chest.
- **"Don't dive unless you have to."** Discourages emergency moves and produces hesitant pursuit. Modern correction: name the emergency moves ("Double Knee Down," "Shin Dig," roll) so they are real tools, not last-resort gambles.

## Voice consistency notes

The defense cue vocabulary is genuinely contested across schools, and the dictionary preserves the disagreement.

**Modern AOC** (Stone, Josephson, Liskevych, Flynn-Oldenburg, Kiraly) leads the program's working voice on reading, posture, platform management, and emergency moves [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig][aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide][aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-defense][aoc-2022-emergency-moves-passing-defense][aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill]. Hips under the ball, Look/See/Decide, Ball-Setter-Ball-Hitter, and the named emergency-move inventory are all canonical AOC vocabulary.

**Munciana** owns the Three P's of defense as the program's defensive identity cue cluster [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Get back, get low, get a touch is the most-repeated phrase on the defensive court at every age band. Lingenfelter's "position, posture, passion" articulation provides the more-advanced vocabulary for the same three priorities.

**Japanese tradition** carries the strongest claim on defensive identity through the dig-up-everything ethic, the midline-the-ball priority, and the recover-to-ready-after-every-contact culture. The Munciana Three P's converge with the Japanese frame at the *intent* level — both traditions emphasize that defense is a culture, not a technique. The cue vocabulary differs (Munciana's three-P's vs. Japanese kaiten/midline language), but the priorities align. The program leans Munciana phrasing because it's the working-club voice, but the Japanese tradition's defensive-culture argument is preserved on the [[defense]] hub.

**GMS** argues defense emerges from random/variable practice at game-like intensity rather than from decoupled reading drills. The cue language in this dictionary is compatible — Look/See/Decide, hips under the ball, and the emergency-move names all work in any practice architecture. GMS's distinct contribution is the *practice-design* argument that reading drills should be embedded in live-hitter feeds, which is procedural rather than lexical.

**Ecological dynamics** prefers cues that constrain the task rather than instruct movement. Look/See/Decide, "What did you see?", and "Read the hitter, not the ball" are all task-relational cues that work in eco-dynamics-style constraints-led practice. The dictionary's vocabulary leans this way per the recency preference.

When schools genuinely disagree on a defense cue — for example, "watch the ball off the hitter's hand" (legacy ball-tracking cue) versus "read the hitter's hips and shoulders" (modern reading cue) — the dictionary keeps both with attribution. The program's working voice is the modern reading frame.

The phrases every coach in the program should say: get back, get low, get a touch (the Three P's), Look/See/Decide, what did you see, read the hitter not the ball, hips under the ball, late platform, quiet platform, move your feet not your arms, arrive stopped then platform, shuffle don't reach, center to the ball, up after the dig, cover the hitter. Fourteen phrases. Repeat them.
