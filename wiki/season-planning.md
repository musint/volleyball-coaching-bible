# Season Planning

## Overview

Season planning is the macro-view coaches rarely have time to do well. For US club volleyball, the 14U-through-18U calendar runs fall (many athletes play HS), winter–spring (club tournaments, qualifiers, bid chasing), and summer (High Performance tryouts, national team pipeline, select teams). HS volleyball is a compressed fall season: six to eight weeks of preseason and regular play, then the state-tournament ladder. College NCAA is fall-first (preseason August, season September–December, postseason December), with a spring non-traditional window and summer conditioning. Each calendar demands a different peaking strategy; all three require the same architecture — a staged build from general fitness and fundamentals through specific capacity and into tactical sharpening, with a taper into peak competition.

The modern season-planning frame layers three timelines on top of one another. Physical periodization (strength-and-conditioning volume, plyometric load, jump volume) builds base in the early preseason, peaks through mid-season, then tapers for the postseason or regional/national qualifier. The instrumentation literature converges on a coachable monitoring stack: **daily session-RPE** for internal load, **daily jump count and intensity** for external load (with high-intensity jumps as the more meaningful axis than raw count), **weekly countermovement-jump (CMJ)** for neuromuscular fatigue, and **daily Hooper Index** for well-being [rebelo-2024-training-stress-fatigue-wellbeing][sanders-2025-early-season-jump-load]. Pre-season shows a planned progressive load → performance decrement; in-season shows wave-distribution across weekly cycles; uniform programs systematically over-load middle blockers vs. other positions, and HR is unreliable for prescription [rebelo-2024-training-stress-fatigue-wellbeing]. Technical periodization walks fundamentals forward: early-season sessions skew toward skill-blocked scaffolding and fundamentals reset (Mattox's early-season-practice frame is a good template); mid-season shifts almost entirely to game-like and random practice; late-season is refinement plus opponent-specific tactical work. Tactical periodization layers offensive and defensive systems progressively: install base systems in preseason, add variations and special situations mid-season, rehearse against scouted opponents for the postseason. **Periodization model is a smaller lever than program consistency** at the junior-male level — 8-week block vs. linear periodization produced equivalent gains on jump, agility, power, and aerobic endurance in matched RCT, with block winning narrowly only on arm/grip strength [tavakkoli-2022-block-vs-traditional-periodization].

The 14U club-season arc is the user's immediate planning context. Fall is de-facto off-season or HS season; winter–spring club is the main competitive block (state tournaments, regional bids, qualifiers for Nationals) with a tournament roughly every 2–3 weekends; summer bridges into HP tryouts and next-season tryouts. At 14U, "peaking" is less a physiological taper than a tactical-readiness peak — the team has run its offense and defense enough times that it executes without thinking when the bracket gets hard.

## Major subtopics

- **Preseason** — base physical preparation, fundamentals reset, system installation, culture-setting. Mattox's early-season-practice frame is the canonical AOC reference; Rose's Penn State preseason prep is the high-end version. The first two weeks set the tone for the season.
- **In-season** — the sustained competitive block. Load modulation across the week, match-to-practice ratio, recovery discipline. Rose's "subs in practice" and "everything matters" culture shows how to keep practice quality from drifting down once the season's energy budget gets tight. The longitudinal volleyball burnout pattern is specific: across a 10-month competitive season, **reduced sense of accomplishment** and **sport devaluation** rise progressively while physical/emotional exhaustion stays relatively stable, and coping strategies do not change while stress rises — meaning coaches should periodize **psychological load** alongside physical load, with confidence/motivation monitoring as a defensible leading indicator of burnout risk [pires-2021-burnout-coping-volleyball-season]. Recovery-modality evidence supports nutrition and sleep as the load-bearing recovery levers (definitively shown to benefit volleyball players); cold-water immersion and laser therapy show promise but lack volleyball-specific definitive evidence — adoption is reasonable but not settled best practice [closs-2020-recovery-after-volleyball]. Concurrent training carries a manageable interference effect: 2 sessions/week of 20-30-min endurance is more strength-friendly than 3+ × 50-60 min, HIIT does not produce the same penalty as steady-state, resistance-before-endurance and 6-24 hour separation reduce interference [wang-2024-concurrent-training-strength-endurance].
- **Tournament prep & peaking** — the week (or days) before the event: taper volume, sharpen tactics, scout opponents, rehearse special situations, manage physical freshness. In club volleyball, this cycle repeats every 2–3 weeks; in HS/college, it's once per season around the postseason.
- **Tapering** — reducing volume while maintaining intensity so athletes arrive fresh. More critical in college (where physical load has been systematically heavy) than in 14U club (where the limiter is usually tactical readiness, not fatigue). The intensity-weighted jump-load research is the operational anchor: high-intensity jumps drive next-day fatigue more than total jump count, with mechanical-drift (force decreases / velocity increases at the same height) detectable as accumulated fatigue, and **front-row players accumulating more high-intensity jumps than back-row** — taper plans should be position-specific [sanders-2025-early-season-jump-load]. **Heat stress** has a sport-specific evidence base: in elite acclimated beach populations, heat-stress guidelines may be too conservative — but the conclusion does not generalize to junior, club, or non-acclimated indoor populations, for whom WBGT > 31-32°C remains a meaningful flag [bahr-2012-fivb-heat-stress-monitoring].
- **Periodization by level** — what changes between 14U club, HS varsity, and NCAA college. Practice frequency, eligible hours, S&C sophistication, tournament density, and what "peak" means all shift meaningfully.
- **Club-season arc for 14U** — the user's planning context. Fall entry, winter–spring tournament density, March-through-May regional/qualifier bids, summer HP and tryouts. Planning a season means planning which tournaments are peaks and which are builds.
- **Tournament prep & match-prep handshake** — season planning sets the frame; [[match-prep]] handles the 24–72-hour detailed work. Season planning decides which opponents to prepare deeply for and where scouting effort goes.

## Schools of thought

- [[usa-volleyball]] — the governing-body frame through coach-education (2009 CAP + modern Coach Academy's pillar-based curriculum); Coach Tools provide season-length templates.
- [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] — eclectic coach-education that covers every phase of a season through the Rose, Liskevych, Kiraly, and Dunning corpora. Especially strong on early-season (Mattox) and late-season (Rose's postseason operational rigor).
- [[gold-medal-squared]] — periodizes practice content around motor-learning principles: skill-simplification in early preseason, progressively more random/variable as fundamentals stabilize, game-like dominance mid-to-late season.
- Russ Rose's program-level rigor — Penn State's seven NCAA titles were built on practice consistency across every week, with precisely modulated subs and competitive intent (see [[penn-state]]). The "club rules" concept (2023) articulates the operational standards that scale across a season.
- Terry Liskevych's program-wide frame — "players and staff" thinking, controlling-feelings through the emotional peaks and troughs of a season; AOC co-founder's season-planning contributions emphasize team-chemistry management alongside technical periodization.
- Hugh McCutcheon's mental-model frame — the season is a series of decisions about what to train, what to tolerate, and what to discard; *Championship Behaviors* is the operational articulation.
