# Lumbar Pathology in Pro Beach Volleyball Players (Külling et al. 2014)

## Summary

Külling and colleagues (2014, *Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine*) used MRI to evaluate lumbar spine pathology in 29 professional male beach volleyball players. The findings are striking. Seventy-nine percent of players had at least one disc with Pfirrmann grade ≥3 degeneration, with L4–5 (48%) and L5–S1 (52%) most affected. Twenty-one percent showed grade-4 spondylolysis — up to three times the ~5% rate seen in the general population — concentrated overwhelmingly at L5–S1 (>70% of cases). Eighty-six percent reported low back pain at some point in their career; 35% within the previous four weeks. Yet — and this is the methodologically important finding — there was *no significant correlation* between MRI abnormalities and current low back pain, meaning severe imaging pathology is common even in pain-free athletes and imaging cannot drive return-to-play decisions in isolation. Mechanistically, the authors implicate the repetitive serve motion (excessive hip-shoulder separation generating shear forces on lumbar discs), explosive jumps, and asymmetric trunk rotation. While the cohort is beach volleyball, the loading mechanics transfer to indoor — particularly for jump servers and middle blockers. For the wiki, this paper anchors any claim about lumbar load in volleyball, the prevalence of asymptomatic spinal pathology in elite athletes, and why trunk rotation symmetry, anterior-pelvic-tilt control on landing, and posterior chain conditioning are coaching/S&C levers worth prioritizing.

## Key claims / ideas

- 79% of pro beach volleyball players show disc degeneration (Pfirrmann ≥3) on MRI.
- Spondylolysis prevalence (~21%) is up to 3× the general population baseline.
- Career LBP prevalence is 86%; recent (4-week) prevalence 35%.
- MRI findings do NOT correlate with current LBP — imaging alone is not a diagnostic or RTP gate.
- Repetitive serve motion (hip-shoulder separation, shear), jumps, and asymmetric rotation are the implicated mechanisms.
- Trunk rotation symmetry, landing pelvis control, and posterior-chain conditioning are defensible coaching/S&C prevention targets.
