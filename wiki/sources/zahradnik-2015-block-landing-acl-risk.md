# Lower extremity mechanics during landing after a volleyball block as a risk factor for ACL injury

## Summary

Zahradnik and colleagues (2015) compared two real-world block-landing variants — *stick landing* (controlled vertical absorption) and *step-back landing* (the common "block then move backward to defend" pattern) — in 14 elite male volleyball players. Force plates and 3-D motion capture measured vertical ground reaction force, knee flexion, knee moments, and energy absorption at hip, knee, and ankle. The key finding: during step-back landings, the right (dominant) lower extremity showed higher first-peak vertical GRF, greater knee-valgus moment (a canonical ACL risk marker), and reduced knee energy absorption — with hip and ankle compensating by absorbing more. In coaching terms: the very common "block, then transition backward to defend" sequence concentrates impact and rotational stress on the dominant knee at the precise moment of initial impact, plausibly raising ACL-injury risk. The paper suggests stick landings are biomechanically safer; when step-back is necessary, neuromuscular training should emphasize knee tracking and hip absorption.

## Key claims / ideas

- Step-back landings after blocks elevate first-peak vertical GRF on the dominant limb compared to stick landings.
- Step-back landings produce greater knee-valgus moment (an established ACL risk marker).
- Knee energy absorption is reduced in step-back landings; hip and ankle compensate.
- Stick landings are biomechanically safer; step-back patterns require deliberate landing-mechanics training.
- The dominant leg is the likely first failure point — bilateral landing training is justified despite the asymmetry of the jump.
