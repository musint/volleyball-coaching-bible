# 10 Things Russ Rose Has Learned About Coaching Volleyball

## Summary

A first-person list from Penn State head coach Russ Rose — author of seven NCAA Division I championships and co-founder of The Art of Coaching Volleyball — distilling lessons from a career that began in 1979. The piece is framed around the 2014 title and Rose's working relationship with setter Micha Hancock: he told her as a freshman she would "hate him" because he would push her, and she became a two-time NCAA champion and 2014 AVCA Player of the Year.

The list is short, aphoristic, and covers both craft (fundamentals, matchup planning by rotation, using tough servers to neutralize bad matchups) and program stewardship (telling the truth, coaching who you have, resting late in the season, "teams win and coaches lose"). Rose closes with an unusual principle — "addition by subtraction" — the idea that losing a staff member or a good player can sometimes make a team better, and that the best team doesn't always have the best players.

## Key claims / ideas

- "Make it fun" is listed first but acknowledged as easier said than done.
- Fundamentals are the prerequisite for winning, not an alternative to tactics.
- Matchups are rotation-driven; a coach can't control the opponent's starting rotation, so must plan for being overwhelmed (e.g., best opposing hitter over weakest blocker = exploit with toughest servers in play).
- "Teams win and coaches lose" — accountability posture.
- "Rest is best" especially approaching the end of the season.
- "Addition by subtraction" — losing a staff person or a good player can unlock team success.
- "The best team doesn't always have the best players."
- Know your own strengths and weaknesses; hire to cover your blemishes.

## Topics covered

- Coaching philosophy (Rose-style aphorisms)
- Matchup planning and serving strategy against mismatches
- Season arc management (especially rest near post-season)
- Roster and staff construction / culture fit
