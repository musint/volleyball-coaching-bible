# Building a World-class Volleyball League from the Ground Up

## Summary
Nippon.com feature on the **SV.League** launch (autumn 2024), successor to the V.League as Japan's top professional tier. Sets out the SV.League's stated ambition — world-leading status by 2030 — and its structural break with the corporate-team model that has dominated Japanese volleyball since **1967** (formation of the original national league).

Most relevant to coaching methodology: the new licensing regime requires **mandatory under-15 teams** for each club, foreign-player recruitment has expanded, and teams are rebranding away from corporate identities (Osaka Bluteon, Hiroshima Thunders). Corroborates broader reporting that most SV.League men's clubs now run **foreign head coaches**, meaning contemporary Japanese top-flight methodology is hybridized rather than insular. The article frames Japan's youth-participation decline (2.79M → 1.81M, 2001–2023) as the strategic problem the community-based club model is intended to solve.

## Key claims / ideas
- SV.League = "Strong, Spread, Society" — rebrand from V.League, 2024
- U15 teams mandatory for club licensing (youth-pipeline reform)
- Most men's SV.League clubs use foreign head coaches
- Volleyball youth participation: 2.79M (2001) → 1.81M (2023)
- 2030 target: world-leading league by attendance, retail, titles, Olympics
