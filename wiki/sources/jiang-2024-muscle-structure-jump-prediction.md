# Jiang et al. (2024) — Muscle-structure predictors of vertical jump in elite male volleyball

## Summary

Cross-sectional ultrasonography study of 15 elite male volleyball players (age 22.47 years; height 195.4 cm) published in *Frontiers in Physiology*. Quantifies which muscle-architecture variables (anatomical cross-sectional area, thickness, pennation angle, fascicle length) of vastus lateralis (VL), rectus femoris (RF), medial / lateral gastrocnemius (MG / LG) predict performance across four jump types: attack jump, countermovement jump, squat jump, drop jump. **Vastus lateralis ACSA is the dominant predictor across all jump types** (R² = 0.25 to 0.52). **Lateral gastrocnemius fascicle length uniquely predicts attack-jump height** (R² = 0.18) but not other jump types. Combined VL-ACSA + LG-FL + training age explains 73% of attack-jump variance. Rectus femoris does not predict any jump type. Practical implication: **VL hypertrophy and LG-fascicle-length training are the highest-leverage architectural targets** for volleyball jump development; attack jump is biomechanically distinct from generic CMJ / SJ / DJ and requires sport-specific training.

## Key claims / ideas

- Vastus lateralis ACSA dominates jump-height prediction across attack, CMJ, SJ, and drop jumps (R² 0.25–0.52).
- Lateral gastrocnemius fascicle length uniquely predicts attack-jump height — calf architecture matters specifically for the spike approach.
- Rectus femoris does not predict any jump type — quad-dominant programming may be inefficient.
- Combined VL-ACSA + LG-FL + training age model: R² = 0.73 for attack jump.
- Attack jump is biomechanically distinct from generic vertical-jump tests; sport-specific training required.
- Long-term training-age effect supports continuous, year-round S&C over fragmented programming.
