# Kinematic analysis of volleyball spike jump

## Summary

Wagner and colleagues (2009) is one of the most-cited 3-D motion-capture studies of the volleyball spike jump. Sixteen experienced players performed maximum-effort spikes; the authors used 3-D motion capture and force plates to identify which kinematic variables most strongly predicted jump height. The headline finding — that horizontal centre-of-mass velocity during the approach (r = 0.71) and right-knee flexion-extension range (r = 0.76) are the strongest correlates of spike-jump height — established the modern coaching consensus that **the approach phase, not just the takeoff and arm action, determines maximum jump height**. The study also found a left-shoulder hyperextension angular-velocity correlate (r = 0.72), confirming the role of arm swing in vertical lift, and documented strong asymmetry between right and left limbs as a feature of the right-handed spike (not a flaw to be corrected). The paper is a foundational reference for any coaching content on approach speed, the depth of the preparatory squat, and the role of the penultimate (second-to-last) step in spike-jump performance.

## Key claims / ideas

- The approach phase is the leading determinant of spike-jump height; takeoff and arm action are secondary.
- Faster horizontal CoM velocity correlates strongly with greater jump height (r = 0.71).
- A deeper preparatory squat (lower minimum CoM height) correlates with greater jump height (r = -0.68).
- Right-knee flexion-extension range is the single strongest correlate of jump height (r = 0.76).
- Left-shoulder hyperextension angular velocity (the "load" in the arm swing) is a meaningful contributor (r = 0.72).
- The volleyball spike jump is intrinsically asymmetric — coaches should not "fix" left/right asymmetry as if it were a defect.
