# ACL Return to Play in Division I Female Volleyball (Smith & Gallo 2025)

## Summary

Smith and Gallo (2025, *The Physician and Sportsmedicine*) retrospectively analyzed NCAA Division I female volleyball athletes who sustained ACL injuries between 2008 and 2020 across seven collegiate conferences (n=99). The study delivers some of the cleanest sport-specific return-to-play numbers available for women's collegiate volleyball: 93.1% of injured athletes returned to competition, with 65.7% playing in 35+ sets post-return (i.e., re-establishing a meaningful role), 16.2% playing in fewer than 35 sets, 6.1% recording no in-game statistics, and 12.1% graduating without returning. These numbers are notably better than the cross-sport baseline of ~63% return to pre-injury level, suggesting that volleyball's specific demand profile (predominantly vertical and short-court motion vs. cutting-heavy sports) plus structured collegiate rehab pathways produce favorable outcomes. The position-specific finding is consequential: attackers comprised 54.7% of the control population but 78.8% of injuries — a striking outsized injury share that mirrors the spike-landing mechanism documented in the broader epidemiology literature. Performance trajectory increased linearly with years post-injury, indicating progressive recovery rather than plateau. For the wiki, Smith & Gallo 2025 anchors return-to-play prognosis claims for women's collegiate volleyball, the attacker-position injury concentration, and the case for sport-specific functional rehab progression over calendar-driven RTP timelines.

## Key claims / ideas

- 93.1% of NCAA D1 female volleyball ACL athletes return to competition — among the better RTP rates in women's collegiate sport.
- 65.7% return to substantial playing time (35+ sets); 12.1% never return before graduation.
- Attackers carry outsized injury share (78.8% of injuries vs. 54.7% of population) — both highest incidence AND most demanding RTP.
- Performance metrics improve linearly post-injury — recovery is progressive, not plateaued.
- Volleyball's vertical-loading profile may produce better RTP than cutting-heavy sports.
