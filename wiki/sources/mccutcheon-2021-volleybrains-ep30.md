# VolleyBrains Episode 30 — Hugh McCutcheon

## Summary

Long-form podcast conversation in which McCutcheon traces his entry into coaching — originally a way to fund graduate work at BYU in exercise science, statistics, and business — and the series of "unique experiences" that kept him in the profession. His headline framing: the work is "committing to a standard of competitive excellence," not chasing wins per se.

He offers two pieces of advice for younger coaches: patience (development does not happen on the coach's timeline) and intellectual humility (coaching lives in gray areas, not absolutes). He explicitly argues for evaluating ideas through scientific rigor rather than tradition, and for defining guiding principles that distinguish substance from opinion.

## Key claims / ideas

- "It's about committing to a standard of competitive excellence."
- Focus on winning *through* excellence, not winning itself.
- Coaching is gray, not absolute — beware of polarized, confident claims.
- Evaluate coaching ideas with scientific rigor; do not accept tradition as evidence.
- Young coaches should cultivate patience and intellectual humility.
- Athletes in the college/NT years are at a formative moment; coaching is a launching pad for their next phase.

## Topics covered

- Career origin story (BYU grad school → coaching)
- Standard-of-excellence framing
- Tradition vs. evidence in coaching
- Advice to emerging coaches
- Hall-of-Fame career waypoints (2008 gold, 2012 silver, Grand Prix three-peat)
