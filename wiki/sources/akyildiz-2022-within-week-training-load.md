# Akyildiz et al. (2022) — Within-week external training load in elite volleyball

## Summary

Peer-reviewed, open-access primary research study published in *BMC Sports Science, Medicine and Rehabilitation* analyzing within-week (microcycle) external training-load differences in elite women's volleyball players by position, across a full 2020–2021 competitive season (62 matches total: 10 friendly, 41 league, 11 CEV Champions League). Giovanni Guidetti is a listed co-author with affiliation "Vakıfbank Women's Volleyball Team, Istanbul, Turkey" — identifying the studied "world champion club team" as **VakıfBank**, the season in which they were reigning FIVB Club World Champions. Data captured via KINEXON LPS (Local Positioning System, 12-antenna, millimeter-accurate) across all training sessions and matches for 14 players spanning all six positions. Documents that Guidetti's program operates as a data-instrumented periodization environment — not as an anecdote, but as formally published methodology.

## Key claims / ideas

- **Sports-science integration is coach-sanctioned at VakıfBank.** Guidetti's co-authorship establishes the LPS/load-management program as part of his methodology, not a parallel strength-and-conditioning silo.
- **Full LPS instrumentation.** Total distance, jump count, accelerations, decelerations, high metabolic load distance (HMLD), session workload, acute and chronic loads, acute:chronic workload ratio, monotony, and strain are tracked per player per session.
- **Position-specific load differs materially.** Middle blockers show notably lower workload than outsides, opposites, and liberos — implying Guidetti's periodization respects position demands rather than prescribing uniform training.
- **Microcycle tapers toward match day.** Training intensity decreases from MD-4 to MD-1 across nearly all variables, with moderate-to-large effect sizes — a formal, planned taper.
- **Jump count ↔ fatigue relationship.** High jump count in a session associates with fatigue sensation and reduction in jump height — quantitative basis for jump-count caps in session design.
