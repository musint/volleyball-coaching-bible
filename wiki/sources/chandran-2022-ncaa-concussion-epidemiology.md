# NCAA Concussion Epidemiology 2014/15–2018/19 (Chandran et al. 2022)

## Summary

Chandran and colleagues (2022, *American Journal of Sports Medicine*) analyzed five seasons of NCAA Injury Surveillance Program data — 3,497 sport-related concussions across 23 NCAA sports drawn from over 8.4 million athlete-exposures. The overall concussion rate was 4.13 per 10,000 athlete-exposures, with men's ice hockey (7.35) and women's soccer (7.15) leading. The volleyball-relevant finding is the *trajectory*: women's volleyball concussion rates increased over the 2015/16–2018/19 window, flagged by the authors as a concerning trend requiring prevention focus. Equally important is the mechanism profile: in men's sports, 77% of concussions came from player-to-player contact, but in women's sports, 39.2% came from contact with equipment or apparatus (ball, net, post, floor). For volleyball coaches that means the prevention vector is not "tackling mechanics" but ball-to-head awareness, floor-defense technique, and net-pole padding/protocol. Most concussions were new injuries (84.3% men, 81.1% women). For the wiki, Chandran 2022 grounds claims about NCAA-level concussion incidence in women's volleyball, the rising trend, and the mechanism profile that should drive coaching attention.

## Key claims / ideas

- Overall NCAA concussion rate ≈ 4.13 per 10,000 athlete-exposures.
- Women's volleyball concussion rates rose over 2015/16–2018/19 — concussion is no longer a hockey/football-only concern.
- Mechanism in women's sports is dominated by equipment/apparatus contact (39.2%) — distinct from men's (77% player contact).
- Implication: volleyball concussion prevention is about ball-to-head contact, floor mechanics, and net-pole padding — not tackling.
- Most concussions are new injuries; recurrence is sport-specific (notable in ice hockey).
