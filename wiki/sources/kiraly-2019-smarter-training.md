# Smarter Training = Better Volleyball

## Summary
Published November 20, 2019 on The Art of Coaching Volleyball, this short article by Karch Kiraly — then USA Women's National Team head coach — is one of the cleanest public distillations of how the USWNT actually structures practice quality. Its thesis: "the benefit you get from practice is more about HOW than it is about HOW MUCH." Rather than arguing for more reps, Kiraly argues for better reps, and names two specific mechanisms he uses with the national team.

The article is significant because it grounds his philosophy in an external framework: Anders Ericsson's *Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise*. Kiraly explicitly invokes "purposeful" / deliberate practice as the target concept, and references a Japanese music study showing that perfect pitch — long assumed innate — can be trained. That move (citing outside science, not just authority) is characteristic of Kiraly's public voice and is useful as a cross-reference whenever the wiki invokes deliberate-practice theory.

## Key claims / ideas
- "The benefit you get from practice is more about HOW than it is about HOW MUCH." — core quote.
- **Repetition without the ball:** passers repeat their motion immediately after a live contact to either reinforce correct technique or correct an error. This turns every rep into a paired live-then-ghost rep.
- **Ghost passing:** the next passer in line stands behind the active passer and mirrors their footwork and platform, doubling per-player reps and practicing read / movement.
- Five practice-maximizing recommendations: (1) set process or result goals, (2) track progress with stats, (3) concentrate fully each rep, (4) embrace discomfort rather than playing safe, (5) seek coach feedback and use video / peer observation.
- Evidence grounding: Ericsson's deliberate-practice framework; perfect-pitch trainability as proof that innate-seeming traits can be developed.

## Topics covered
- Practice design and training-density philosophy
- Passing-specific drill mechanics (repeat-without-ball, ghost passing)
- Goal-setting and stat-tracking for practice
- Discomfort / zone-of-proximal-development
- Role of video and peer observation
- Integration with Ericsson's deliberate practice literature
