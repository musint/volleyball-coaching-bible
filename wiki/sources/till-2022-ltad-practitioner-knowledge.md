# Long-Term Athletic Development: Practitioner Knowledge & Adherence (Till et al. 2022)

## Summary

Till and colleagues (2022, *PLoS One*) surveyed 236 practitioners — sport coaches, PE teachers, S&C coaches, and administrators — to investigate how well long-term athletic development (LTAD) is understood and implemented. The headline finding is a meaningful gap between belief and practice. Only 2.7% of practitioners incorporated all four required components of "athleticism" (movement, physical qualities, psychological elements, multiple environments) — most operate with incomplete definitions of what they're developing. While practitioners rated their adherence high overall (especially Pillar 5, health and wellbeing), three pillars showed notably weak adherence: Pillar 1 (accommodating individualized, non-linear development), Pillar 8 (monitoring and assessment tools), and Pillar 9 (systematic progression and individualization). The Balyi LTAD model was best-known; the more current NSCA "10 Pillars" framework had limited recognition. Practitioners themselves identified eight implementation challenges — governance conflicts, resource limitations, insufficient education, early-specialization pressures, excessive training volumes, poor staff communication, parental misconceptions, youth motivation. For the wiki, Till 2022 grounds the LTAD discussion in current practitioner reality: the framework is widely known but inconsistently and incompletely implemented, with maturity-status assessment, individualized loading, and explicit progression frameworks being the underused levers. This matters for any age-lens page or 14U/HS practice-design discussion.

## Key claims / ideas

- Only 2.7% of practitioners incorporate all four components of athleticism (movement, physical, psychological, multi-environment).
- Adherence is weakest on individualization (Pillar 1), monitoring/assessment (Pillar 8), and systematic progression (Pillar 9).
- Balyi LTAD model is most-recognized; NSCA 10 Pillars (more current/research-grounded) has lower recognition.
- Practitioners identify early-specialization pressures, excessive training volumes, parental misconceptions, and resource limits as primary implementation challenges.
- Maturity-status screening, individualized loading, and explicit progression frameworks are practitioner-acknowledged but underused levers.
