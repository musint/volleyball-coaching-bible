# Specific and general transfer of perceptual-motor skills and learning between sports: A systematic review

## Summary

Oppici and Panchuk (2022) is the contemporary systematic-review anchor for **perceptual-motor skill transfer between sports**. The authors split transfer into two kinds — **specific** (rests on shared *specifying* perceptual information, like ball-flight reading across striking sports) and **general** (rests on *non-specifying* "general" information, like pattern-recognition heuristics) — and review 17 studies on whether and how each occurs short-term (skill transfer) and long-term (learning transfer).

Most evidence supports specificity of transfer when affordances overlap (e.g., basketball ↔ darts throwing, climbing ↔ climbing, striking ↔ striking) and generality when affordances do not overlap. Six of the 17 studies showed an **expertise effect** — higher transfer in skilled vs. less-skilled athletes.

The review also notes the evidence base is thin (17 studies, low-to-moderate quality) and limited to short-term transfer, with little data on mid- and long-term dynamics. Caution is warranted before either championing or dismissing multi-sport sampling for skill development on transfer grounds alone.

For volleyball: skills shared across positions (passing → backrow defense; setting → tipping) likely benefit from specific transfer, while broader athletic exposure (basketball, soccer) supports more general transfer. This grounds claims in the long-term-athlete-development (LTAD) and multi-sport-sampling literatures with proper nuance.

## Key claims / ideas

- Transfer splits into **specific** (shared specifying information) and **general** (shared non-specifying information). Both real, both empirically supported.
- **Affordance similarity** drives whether transfer is specific or general.
- **Expertise effect:** higher-level athletes transfer more than novices.
- Cross-training across sports is more useful when affordances overlap (e.g., invasion sports), less useful when they don't (e.g., dart throwing for volleyball serve).
- Evidence base is thin — only 17 studies, low-to-moderate quality, mostly short-term — caution required when arguing for/against multi-sport sampling on transfer grounds alone.
- Volleyball-specific implication: cross-position transfer within volleyball likely follows specific-transfer rules, while across-sport transfer follows general-transfer rules.
