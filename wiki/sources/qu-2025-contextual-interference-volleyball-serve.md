# Contextual interference effects on volleyball serve acquisition: a controlled trial with physical-education majors

## Summary

Qu and colleagues (2025) ran an 8-week controlled trial with 60 PE majors comparing three serve-practice schedules: **blocked (low contextual interference)**, **mixed (medium)**, and **random (high)**. The design tested whether the classic Shea-Morgan contextual interference effect — blocked-better-during-acquisition, random-better-at-transfer — replicates in a real, multi-week volleyball serve teaching context.

Three findings:
1. **Blocked won acquisition** — best performance during the skill-formation stage.
2. **Random won transfer** — significantly better at the transfer test.
3. **Mixed won long-term retention** — surprising "middle-path" result that suggests pure-random can be too disruptive for retention while pure-blocked is too narrow.

Practical implication: the optimal practice schedule **depends on the stage of learning**. Warm-up blocked → train mixed → test random/transfer. The Qu result replicates Travlos (2010) on volleyball serve but adds the long-term retention advantage of mixed practice.

For coaches building serve training across a season, this is empirical support for varying the schedule rather than committing entirely to one mode — and for valuing a *medium* contextual-interference dose for retention specifically.

## Key claims / ideas

- 8-week three-arm RCT (n = 60) of blocked vs. mixed vs. random practice on volleyball serve.
- **Acquisition advantage:** blocked (classic acquisition advantage).
- **Transfer advantage:** random (classic CI effect).
- **Retention advantage:** mixed (medium CI) — neither pure-blocked nor pure-random.
- Replicates Shea & Morgan (1979) and Travlos (2010) on a longer volleyball-specific protocol.
- Supports a stage-matched schedule design rather than blanket "go random."
