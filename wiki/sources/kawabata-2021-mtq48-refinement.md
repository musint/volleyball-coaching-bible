# Refined Versions of the Mental Toughness Questionnaire-48 (Kawabata et al. 2021)

## Summary

Kawabata and colleagues (2021) addressed long-standing factorial-validity criticism of the **Mental Toughness Questionnaire-48 (MTQ48)** — the most widely used mental-toughness instrument in sport, built on Clough's 4Cs model (Challenge, Commitment, Control, Confidence). Across two large independent samples (N = 2,186 and N = 3,209 university students), the authors used confirmatory factor analysis and exploratory structural equation modeling to show that one-, four-, and six-factor models with all 48 items fit the data poorly. They then derived two psychometrically sound refinements: an **18-item Short MTQ** preserving the global score and six subdimensions, and a **6-item Very Short MTQ** for time-constrained settings. The paper is the current methodological anchor for any mental-toughness assessment in sport contexts and is directly relevant to volleyball coaches who want to monitor MT longitudinally without the original 48-item burden.

## Key claims / ideas

- The original MTQ48 has well-documented factorial-validity issues — none of the standard 1-, 4-, 6-factor models fit acceptably.
- The 4Cs theoretical framework (Challenge, Commitment, Control, Confidence) survives; its 48-item operationalization does not.
- The Short MTQ (18 items) is the new recommended full instrument.
- The Very Short MTQ (6 items) is suitable for repeated in-season monitoring.
- Mental toughness should be operationalized as a multidimensional construct; single global scores must be defended against the multifactor structure.
- Practical: any volleyball-program MT monitoring should use Short MTQ (post-season retrospective) or Very Short MTQ (in-season weekly check-in).
