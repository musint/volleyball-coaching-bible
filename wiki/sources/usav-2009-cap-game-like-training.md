# CAP Article: Game-Like Training

## Summary

This is a CAP-era USA Volleyball article by coach Rick Swan describing how he rebuilt his program's practice design around a single principle: "If it wasn't game-like we weren't going to do it." He chronicles the conversion from traditional drills — standing lines, pepper, coach-tossed repetitions, boxed hitting — to competitive, read-and-react formats (1v1, 2v2, small-sided over-the-net play). Within weeks the program's ball control, movement, and decision-making improved; the team finished fourth at state, then won a state championship the following year.

The article's real value is the explicit taxonomy of what counts as *not* game-like (tossed balls in hitting lines, wall spiking, pepper, partner passing without a net) and the ten operating principles that replace those patterns. It is one of the most frequently referenced short pieces in USAV's coaching-philosophy canon and maps directly onto the motor-learning case for random, whole-task, specific practice that shows up throughout USAV lesson plans and Kessel's Growing the Game writing.

## Key claims / ideas
- Reading and reacting is the single most critical volleyball skill — more fundamental than any isolated technique.
- Artificial practice situations build habits that must later be un-learned under competitive stress; better to never install them.
- Practices should eliminate: tossed balls in hitting lines, box hitting, coach-directed balls at diggers, wall spiking, partner passing/setting without a net.
- Effective practice mimics live match context through 1v1, 2v2, and competitive small-sided formats.
- Ten practice principles: (1) eliminate non-game-like activities, (2) keep score, (3) state clear drill objectives, (4) create pressure via consequences, (5) vary daily structure, (6) let players manage drills, (7) eliminate standing lines, (8) practice chaotic situations, (9) let players play more than the coach talks, (10) prioritize fun.
- Empirical claim: adopting this approach produced rapid, measurable improvement and a state-championship outcome in the author's program.

## Topics covered
Game-like training philosophy; drill vs. game taxonomy; read-and-react as the core skill; competitive scorekeeping and consequence design; athlete-led practice structure; motor-learning principles as applied by a high-school practitioner; case evidence from a multi-season program rebuild.
