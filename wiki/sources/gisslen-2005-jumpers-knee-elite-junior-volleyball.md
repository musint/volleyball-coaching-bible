# Jumper's Knee in Swedish Elite Junior Volleyball Players (Gisslén et al. 2005)

## Summary

Gisslén and colleagues (2005, *British Journal of Sports Medicine*) compared the patellar tendons of 57 Swedish elite junior volleyball players (ages 15–19) to 55 matched non-athletic controls using clinical examination, grey-scale ultrasonography, and color Doppler imaging. The study is foundational in the volleyball overuse literature for two reasons. First, it documents jumper's knee prevalence in elite adolescent volleyball at meaningful levels — 12 affected tendons (10 male, 2 female) versus zero in matched controls. Second, it identifies neovascularization (visible on color Doppler) as the discriminating imaging finding: every clinically symptomatic tendon showed structural change *plus* neovascularization, while controls who exhibited structural change showed no neovascularization. Most consequentially for coaches and S&C staff, 10 *asymptomatic* volleyball-player tendons also showed structural change with neovascularization — meaning silent, sub-clinical tendon pathology is already detectable in adolescent elite athletes before pain emerges. The paper provides hard support for adolescent load monitoring, tendon-health screening, and the coaching reality that "no pain yet" does not mean "no problem yet" at the junior elite level.

## Key claims / ideas

- Jumper's knee prevalence in elite junior volleyball is meaningfully elevated vs. age-matched non-athletes (12 vs. 0 in this cohort).
- Neovascularization, not structural change alone, is the volleyball-specific imaging marker.
- Sub-clinical tendon pathology (structural change + neovascularization with no pain) is detectable in adolescents — evidence for early load-management.
- Sex skew in this cohort favored males (10 of 12 affected tendons male).
