# External focus of attention enhances arm velocities during volleyball spike in young female players

## Summary

Slovák and colleagues (2023) is the cleanest piece of volleyball-specific evidence for **Wulf's external-focus advantage** applied to spiking. Twelve youth female players (~13.6 yr, 14U-relevant) hit standing spikes under three cue conditions: **internal** ("pull back your elbow"), **external** ("imagine cracking a whip"), and **no-cue control**. Using 3D motion capture and Statistical Parametric Mapping (whole-trajectory analysis, not just peak velocity), they measured arm, forearm, and hand velocities across wind-up, cocking, acceleration, and follow-through phases.

External-focus cues produced significantly faster hand, forearm, and arm velocities than both internal-focus and control conditions, from wind-up through acceleration (all p < 0.01). Internal-focus did NOT outperform control — i.e., the speed gain is specific to external cueing, not just to having any cue.

Practical takeaway: for young female hitters working on swing speed, **image- and effect-based cues** ("crack a whip," "snap through the ball") beat **technique-internal cues** ("get your elbow up first"). This is a 14U-and-up-friendly result.

## Key claims / ideas

- External focus produced significantly higher hand, forearm, and arm velocities throughout the spike approach phases — not just at peak.
- Internal focus performed similarly to no-cue control — suggesting harm/no-help, not benefit.
- 3D motion-capture + SPM analysis methodology; whole-trajectory comparison, not just peak.
- Sample is 14U-aged female volleyball players — direct relevance for the head-coach context.
- Aligns with broader OPTIMAL-theory / external-focus literature; one of the cleanest in-sport replications for hitting.
