# Biomechanic Differences Between Anticipated and Unanticipated Volleyball Block Jump

## Summary

Zhao and colleagues (2024) tested 15 elite male volleyball players blocking in dominant and non-dominant directions under both anticipated (pre-cued) and unanticipated (random-cue) conditions. The crucial methodological move was injecting reaction-time pressure: in the anticipated condition, the blocker knew the lateral direction in advance; in the unanticipated condition, the cue arrived too late for full motor reorganization. Key findings: dominant-side blocks showed greater landing stiffness, higher posterior ground reaction force, and reduced cushioning — signals of overuse-injury risk. Non-dominant-side blocks showed better shock absorption but elevated knee-valgus moment, the canonical ACL-injury risk marker. Most importantly, **unanticipated** blocks produced higher vertical ground reaction force, greater hip moments, and reduced knee-flexion power and ankle velocity — meaning the lower limb cannot fully reorganize from automated patterns when reaction time is squeezed. The combination of unanticipation + lateral movement was the highest-risk profile. The paper provides a strong scientific basis for incorporating read-blocking and randomized-cue blocking drills into injury-prevention work, since rehearsed-block lab data systematically under-states real-game injury exposure.

## Key claims / ideas

- Dominant-side block landings are stiffer and absorb less, raising overuse-injury risk.
- Non-dominant-side block landings absorb better but show higher knee-valgus moments — the ACL risk side.
- Unanticipated blocks produce higher vertical GRF and hip moments than anticipated.
- Knee-flexion power and ankle velocity drop when reaction time is squeezed — the limb cannot fully reorganize.
- Practice that uses only pre-cued blocking under-prepares the neuromuscular system for the conditions where injuries occur.
