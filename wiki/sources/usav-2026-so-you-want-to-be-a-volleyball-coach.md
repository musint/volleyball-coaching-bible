# So You Want To Be A Volleyball Coach

## Summary

This is USAV's mentor-voice article for new and early-career coaches, written by Pat Madia, the Western Empire Region's Coaching Education Chair. It is structured as nine pieces of direct advice that, read together, constitute a compact philosophy statement: humility over ego, preparation over improvisation, development of people over pursuit of trophies. Madia's central line — "You don't coach volleyballs, you coach people" — is the north star of the piece.

For a wiki on how to coach, this article is the connective tissue between USAV's credentialing architecture (IMPACT, SafeSport, former CAP I/II) and the soft skills those credentials alone don't teach: managing parent interactions, enforcing team rules consistently, maintaining emotional control during losses, and reframing "bench warmers" as athletes whose specific skill gaps a coach is responsible for closing. It also explicitly points new coaches at continuing-education inputs outside USAV proper — John Kessel's blogs and the "Coach Your Brains Out" podcast — making it a useful cross-reference hub. The page is undated in its current form; the 2026 year reflects the fetch date rather than an original publication date.

## Key claims / ideas
- A coach's own playing résumé is irrelevant to their athletes; athletes evaluate coaches on current competence and preparation.
- Accountability asymmetry: take responsibility for losses, credit players for wins.
- Organization is a visible signal — "athletes know the difference between someone who is prepared and someone who is making it up as they go along."
- A team handbook and a start-of-season parent meeting prevent most mid-season conflicts.
- Coaches must enforce their own stated rules or lose credibility.
- Pursue certifications (CAP I, CAP II, SafeSport) and consume coaching content (Kessel blogs, Coach Your Brains Out podcast) as continuous development.
- Emotional control — in response to losses and to difficult parents — is a coaching skill, not a personality trait.
- "Bench warmer" is a coaching failure frame; the right frame is a specific-skills-needed development plan for each athlete.
- Development is gradual; celebrate incremental progress rather than expect rapid transformation.
- "You don't coach volleyballs, you coach people" — the stated organizing principle of the piece.

## Topics covered
Coaching philosophy for new coaches; ego management and the limits of playing experience; accountability framing; organizational systems (rosters, insurance, emergency protocols, practice plans); parent communication and team handbooks; continuing coach education pathways; emotional self-regulation; development-oriented framing of lesser-used players.
