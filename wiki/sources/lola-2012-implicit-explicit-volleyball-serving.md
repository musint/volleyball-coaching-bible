# The effect of implicit and explicit practice in the development of decision making in volleyball serving

## Summary

Lola, Tzetzis, and Zetou (2012) tested how three modes of instruction — pure explicit, pure implicit, and a sequential implicit-then-explicit progression — affected serve decision-making in 60 female novice volleyball players aged 10–12. All three experimental groups beat a no-instruction control on response speed and accuracy. The order of advantage was clear: **sequential > implicit > explicit**, on both speed AND accuracy at retention.

The mechanism the authors propose is that procedural (implicit) knowledge accumulates first without burdening working memory, and then declarative (explicit) verbal cues attach to an already-functional procedural base. Coaches working with under-13 girls who are learning to serve to spots get a clean signal: front-load **implicit, perception-based** practice (live receivers, real cues) before layering on the verbal "rules" of where to serve.

This sits inside the ecological-dynamics / implicit-learning literature and supplies one of the cleaner volleyball-specific data points for cue-sequencing decisions in youth coaching.

## Key claims / ideas

- Pure-explicit serve coaching for 10–12 yr novices was **slower and less accurate** at retention than implicit or sequential practice — verbal/declarative front-loading underperforms.
- Sequential **implicit-then-explicit** beat both pure modes; ordering is a real coaching variable, not just a tempo choice.
- Implicit (procedural) accumulation appears to "free" working memory so that explicit verbal coaching can land later without overloading the learner.
- Findings are specific to early-female-novice serve decision-making; generalize cautiously to older or more skilled populations.
