# Doug Beal — Wikipedia biography

## Summary

The English Wikipedia biography of Doug Beal consolidates his birth date, education, playing record, two-tenure head-coach arc with the U.S. Men's National Team, professional coaching in Italy, USAV CEO years, and awards. It is the only single-page chronology of Beal's life and career that is publicly available without paywall and is the pragmatic anchor for date-driven claims (1977 head-coach appointment, 1984 Olympic gold, 1997 return, 2005-2017 USAV CEO). Kept at trust tier 3 because Wikipedia is tertiary; specific claims should be re-anchored to primary USAV, IVHF, or FIVB sources wherever those exist.

## Key claims / ideas

- Born March 4, 1947, in Cleveland, Ohio.
- Five-time All-America at Ohio State; 1969 MIVA MVP; 1975 USVBA Open Championships MVP.
- USA Men's National Team player 1970-1976 (across two World Championship teams and four NORCECA Zone championships).
- Head coach, Bowling Green State University (1971); head coach, Ohio State University (1972-1974).
- Appointed USA Men's head coach in 1977; built a full-time training center in Dayton, Ohio (1978) and relocated it to San Diego, California (1981).
- **1984 Los Angeles Olympics: gold medal** — the first-ever Olympic gold for U.S. men's volleyball.
- Systems he installed attributed as the foundation of the subsequent 1985 FIVB World Cup gold, 1986 World Championships gold, and 1988 Seoul Olympic gold won under successor staffs.
- National Team Center Director (1985-1987); special assistant to the Executive Director (1993-1997).
- Italian Serie A coach at Mediolanum Gonzaga (1990-1992): Club World Championship title 1991, third place 1992.
- Second USA Men's head-coach tenure 1997-2005: 2000 Sydney Olympics and 2004 Athens Olympics (fourth place; 6-0 in NORCECA Qualifying). Became the second coach in USAV history to lead three Olympic teams.
- Executive Director of the National Volleyball League (1993) — the first U.S. professional men's league organized under USAV auspices.
- CEO of USA Volleyball January 2005 through January 2, 2017 (announced February 2016).
- Authored *Spike* (1985); contributor to *The Volleyball Coaching Bible* (Part 2, Chapter 4); contributor to *Volleyball: The Keys to Excellence / Coaching Tips for the 90's* (1991, 2nd ed.).
- Volleyball Hall of Fame inductee (1989); USAV first All-Time Great Coach Award (1995); FIVB Greatest Coach of the Century finalist; International Jewish Sports Hall of Fame (2000).
