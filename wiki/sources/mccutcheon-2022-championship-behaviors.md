# Championship Behaviors: A Model for Competitive Excellence in Sports (2022)

## Summary

McCutcheon's primary book, published by Triumph Books on November 15, 2022 with a foreword by Thad Levine. The book proposes a framework for competitive excellence grounded in motor learning, psychology, and decades of coaching experience across USA Men's (2008 Olympic gold), USA Women's (2012 Olympic silver, three World Grand Prix titles), and University of Minnesota (three NCAA Final Fours).

Addressed to three audiences — athletes, parents, and coaches — the book splits responsibility: athletes must work, learn, and compete; coaches must teach, coach, and mentor. Its largest section (roughly 40% of the page count per third-party review) is "The Championship Model for Skill Acquisition and Application." The book is endorsed by Angela Duckworth.

**Important note:** The cluster brief referenced "Thinking Volleyball" as McCutcheon's book. That title belongs to Mike Hebert (Human Kinetics, 2013), not McCutcheon. This source page covers *Championship Behaviors*, McCutcheon's actual book.

## Key claims / ideas

- "Championship results require championship behaviors — it's as simple as that."
- "We won't always have five-star talent, but we can often make up the difference by being five-star teachers, learners, and competitors."
- Dual responsibility frame: athletes — work, learn, compete; coaches — teach, coach, mentor.
- Holistic integration of technical, physical, mental, and team development rather than siloing them.
- Pattern recognition, not randomness: athletes should learn to reduce uncertainty by recognizing recurring competitive patterns.
- Practice-to-competition transfer is the central test of any drill: does it transfer? Tradition is not enough.
- Questions the "all-powerful coach" archetype; emphasizes empathy in handling disruptive players.
- Treats mental and physical health, and learning from failure, as first-class coaching concerns.
