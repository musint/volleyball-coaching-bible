# Choking Under Pressure: Theoretical Models and Interventions (Mesagno & Beckmann 2017)

## Summary

Mesagno and Beckmann's (2017) review in *Current Opinion in Psychology* synthesizes the choking-under-pressure literature across definitions, personality correlates, theoretical mechanisms, and intervention evidence. They define choking as a sudden and significant performance decrement in important situations *despite the athlete possessing the requisite skill* — a clean separation of choking from underperformance due to fatigue, low skill, or poor strategy.

The review identifies **two dominant theoretical families**: (1) **attention-based models** — distraction theory (pressure consumes attentional capacity with task-irrelevant cues), conscious-processing or "reinvestment" theory (Masters: under pressure, skilled performers revert to step-by-step monitoring of automated skills, disrupting fluent execution); and (2) **self-presentation models** (anxiety from being judged drives the breakdown). Personality factors increasing vulnerability include high trait anxiety, high self-consciousness, low dispositional reinvestment, perfectionism, and fear-of-negative-evaluation. Crucially, the review documents *intervention evidence* — pressure acclimatization training, pre-performance routines, attentional cueing, left-hand contraction (Beckmann's protocol), and quiet-eye training all have empirical support in different sport contexts. The two theoretical families are presented as complementary rather than competing — coaches should address both attention and self-presentation simultaneously.

## Key claims / ideas

- Choking is a real and mechanistically distinguishable phenomenon, not just under-performance.
- Two complementary theoretical families: attention-based (distraction, reinvestment) and self-presentation.
- Personality correlates of choking vulnerability are well-documented and replicable across studies.
- Multiple interventions have empirical support: acclimatization training, pre-performance routines, attentional cueing, left-hand contraction, quiet-eye training.
- Practical implication: pressure work in practice (pressure simulations, raised stakes drills) is empirically defensible, not just folk wisdom.
- Coaching takeaway: address both attention and self-presentation — reduce judgment salience AND train automaticity.
