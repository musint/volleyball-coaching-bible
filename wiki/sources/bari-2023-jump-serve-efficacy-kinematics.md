# Analysis of the kinematic variables that predict jump serve efficacy among volleyball players

## Summary

Bari et al. (2023) examined which kinematic predictors best explain jump topspin serve ball velocity at three developmental tiers: U-23 men, U-21 junior men, and U-19 youth boys (n = 30 total). Synchronized cameras and motion-analysis software captured serves, and linear models tested combinations of predictors. The headline finding is developmental: the model that best explains serve velocity is more complex at higher levels. For U-19 boys, **approach velocity alone** predicted 89% of ball-velocity variation. For U-21 juniors, the model required centre-of-gravity height + shoulder-extension angle (98% explanatory power). For U-23 men, all three (approach velocity + shoulder extension + CoG height) were necessary, achieving near-perfect fit. The implication for coaches is sequenced: develop approach-velocity capability first, then layer the cocking-position shoulder extension, then the centre-of-gravity-management refinements. Younger servers are rate-limited by approach velocity; senior servers benefit from multi-segment optimization.

## Key claims / ideas

- For U-19 boys, approach velocity alone explains 89% of jump-serve ball-velocity variation.
- For U-21 juniors, adding shoulder-extension angle and CoG height pushes explanatory power to 98%.
- For U-23 men, the full three-variable model (approach + shoulder + CoG) explains 100% of variation.
- Coaches should sequence jump-serve development: approach velocity → shoulder extension cocking → CoG management.
- Multi-segment coordination is a senior-level marker; expecting it from younger servers is a category error.
