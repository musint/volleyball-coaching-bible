# Carl McGown — Coaching Books (Composite Notes Source)

## Summary

The Task B.9 cluster brief named this source "Carl McGown + Hilary McGown + Mariv
Adamson, *Volleyball: Foundations for Coaches*" and described it as the "GMS
canonical text." Web research at the 2026-04-24 fetch surfaces no McGown book with
that exact title, and the names "Hilary McGown" and "Mariv Adamson" do not appear
as co-authors of any McGown volleyball book in Open Library's authoritative
McGown bibliography, the Gold Medal Squared website store, Amazon, Goodreads,
Barnes & Noble, AbeBooks, or Web search. The Open Library bibliography lists six
McGown works: three editions of his 1971-1978 doctoral motor-learning research
plus three coaching books — *Science of Coaching Volleyball* (1994, solo author),
*Complete Volleyball for Coaches* (1995, with Hilda Fronske), and *Coaching
Volleyball: Building a Winning Team* (2000, with Hilda Fronske and Launa Moser).

**Conclusion:** Treat the brief title as a misattribution. The slug
`notes-mcgown-foundations-for-coaches` is retained for cluster continuity. This
source page consolidates the actual three-volume McGown coaching-books canon
under one citation key, with *Science of Coaching Volleyball* (1994) as the
primary "GMS canonical text" reference and *Coaching Volleyball: Building a
Winning Team* (2000) as the comprehensive textbook companion most likely intended
by the brief's "Foundations for Coaches" framing. Both books, paired with McGown's
peer-reviewed AVCA journal article on whole-vs-part training (with Steve Bain),
are the published methodology spine of the [[gold-medal-squared]] school.

The corpus's organizing thesis is that **coaching is an empirically answerable
discipline grounded in motor-learning science** — that volleyball-specific skills
are task-specific and context-sensitive, that the transfer from general-athletic
drills to volleyball performance is weak, and that the fastest route to competitive
skill is practicing volleyball in volleyball-like conditions with intentional
feedback, external-focus cues, and a deliberate progression from blocked to random
practice as the learner's challenge point allows. McGown's 1971-1978 doctoral work
on motor set, sensory set, and reaction time is the academic foundation; his
1994-2000 books are the volleyball-specific extension; and the AVCA whole-vs-part
article is the methodology paper that operationalized the books' arguments for
the broader U.S. coaching community.

For the wiki, this source is the composite primary anchor for McGown's published
coaching writings ([[carl-mcgown]]), the methodological canon of [[gold-medal-squared]],
and the lineage-source of the practice-design positions GMS still teaches via its
public articles ([[gms-nd-structure-practice]], [[gms-nd-blocking]]).

## Key claims / ideas

(All claims are documented across the existing Carl McGown coach profile,
[[gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]], and the GMS public articles. Reproduced here
for the composite source's standalone usefulness.)

- **Motor-learning science over opinion.** Coaching has empirically answerable
  questions; the head coach is responsible for the reading. McGown's 1971-1978
  doctoral work on motor set, sensory set, and natural set on reaction time at
  different levels of auditory intensity is the academic origin of this
  commitment, and *Science of Coaching Volleyball* (1994) is its volleyball
  translation.

- **Specificity of training.** Volleyball skills are task-specific and
  context-sensitive; the transfer from general-athletic drills to volleyball
  performance is weak; therefore the fastest route to competitive skill is
  practicing volleyball in volleyball-like conditions. The [[gold-medal-squared]]
  practice-structure article descended from this stance frames it as: "the
  single most correlative activity to being a good volleyball player is having
  played a lot of volleyball."

- **Random / variable practice over blocked practice.** Move to game-speed,
  variable practice as fast as the learner's "challenge point" allows. Blocked
  practice (slow, isolated, predictable) is useful only while an athlete is in
  the cognitive stage of learning a brand-new pattern; retention and transfer
  live in random conditions.

- **Reading-and-reaction over prescriptive movement.** "First we have to teach
  them how to move. Then, we need to teach them how to see." The two-stage
  framing: efficient movement patterns exist so attention can be freed for
  reading the game.

- **External-focus cues and simplified skill language.** Movement-effect cues
  and analogies ("cracking a whip") over internal body-mechanic instructions,
  because the motor-learning literature consistently finds better retention and
  transfer from external focus. Paired with a deliberately small drill library
  (a 2018 GMS clinic observed by John Forman used roughly 12 core activities
  rather than a sprawling drill catalog).

- **Intentional feedback, not reflexive feedback.** Feedback is a scarce learning
  resource that must be deployed intentionally rather than sprayed onto every
  rep. Athletes need to develop self-evaluation for performance to survive
  outside the practice gym.

- **Whole-skill training in game-like conditions, not isolated part-practice.**
  The peer-reviewed AVCA journal article McGown co-authored with Dr. Steve Bain
  ("Motor Learning Principles and the Superiority of Whole Training in
  Volleyball") was a published rebuttal to earlier AVCA guidance favoring
  part-training methodology. The article's wholistic-training argument is the
  methodology that GMS still teaches via clinics and online coach education.

- **Training efficiency and "comfort with discomfort."** Practice hours either
  move skill forward or are misallocated; the McGown ethic, captured in the
  USAV remembrance, was a relentless focus on training efficiency paired with
  acceptance that growth requires discomfort.

## Topics covered

### *Science of Coaching Volleyball* (1994) — primary methodology text
- Sport-science principles applied to everyday coaching situations
- Motor-learning research base for skill acquisition
- McGown enlists "the aid of sport science experts" per publisher copy
- The bridge from his doctoral motor-learning work into volleyball coaching
- The volume most often cited in GMS-affiliated coach discussion as "the
  McGown book"
- Solo author; Human Kinetics; ISBN 0873225724 / 9780873225724

### *Complete Volleyball for Coaches* (1995, with Hilda Fronske)
- Co-authored precursor or companion to the expanded 2000 textbook
- Brown & Benchmark publisher (per Open Library)
- Less referenced in current coach-community discussion than the 1994 solo
  volume or the 2000 textbook

### *Coaching Volleyball: Building a Winning Team* (2000, with Hilda Fronske + Launa Moser)
- McGown's most comprehensive coaching textbook
- Allyn & Bacon (academic textbook publisher); ISBN 0205309585 / 9780205309580
- Multi-author treatment suggests broader topical coverage than the 1994 solo
  methodology text — coaching theory + skill instruction + program management +
  practice design
- Likely target of the Task B.9 brief's "*Volleyball: Foundations for Coaches*"
  framing (with comprehensive textbook scope)

### The AVCA whole-vs-part training article (with Steve Bain, ~2010)
- Peer-reviewed methodology paper
- Rebuttal to earlier AVCA part-training guidance
- Per Steve Bain's account at the GMS tribute page: "generated more interest
  and enthusiasm (and debate!) than all of his other scientific endeavors
  combined"
