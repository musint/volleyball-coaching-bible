# Ramirez-Campillo et al. (2020) — Plyometric jump training meta-analysis (volleyball)

## Summary

Systematic review with meta-analysis of 14 randomized-controlled trials (n = 322 participants in PJT arms) published in the *Journal of Sports Science & Medicine*. Pooled effect size for plyometric jump training (PJT) on volleyball vertical jump height: **Cohen's d = 0.82, p < 0.001** — moderate-to-large benefit, no publication bias (Egger's p = 0.59). Critically, moderator analyses showed **no significant differences** between higher and lower training frequencies (≤2 vs >2 sessions/week), volumes (<2,000 vs >2,000 total jumps), or program durations (≤8 vs >8 weeks). Females (ES = 1.3) responded more strongly than males (ES = 0.5) in this dataset. Zero adverse events reported across all 14 RCTs. Practical bottom line: **moderate-volume, moderate-frequency PJT (≈ 2,000 jumps over 8 weeks at 2 sessions/week) captures the bulk of available adaptation** — adding more produces little extra benefit and increases injury risk.

## Key claims / ideas

- Pooled effect size Cohen's d = 0.82 for PJT on volleyball vertical jump — moderate-to-large.
- No moderator effect for training frequency, volume, or duration — coaches have programming flexibility.
- Females showed numerically larger response (ES = 1.3) than males (ES = 0.5) in pooled samples.
- Zero adverse events across all 14 included RCTs — PJT is safe when properly supervised.
- Recommendation: individualize by position; libero may tolerate higher loads than middles.
- A short 6–8-week block at 2 sessions/week and ~2,000 total jumps is sufficient.
