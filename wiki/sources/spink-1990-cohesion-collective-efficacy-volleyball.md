# Group Cohesion and Collective Efficacy of Volleyball Teams (Spink 1990)

## Summary

Spink's (1990) study in the *Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology* is a foundational empirical examination of whether **group cohesion** and **collective efficacy** are positively related in volleyball teams, and whether this relationship varies by competitive level. Spink surveyed elite and recreational volleyball teams using the Group Environment Questionnaire (task and social subdimensions) and a measure of collective efficacy (the team's shared belief in its own capability), then compared the cohesion-efficacy correlation across competitive levels.

The headline finding: **the cohesion–collective efficacy relationship is level-dependent**. Specific measures of group cohesiveness positively related to collective efficacy in elite volleyball teams but **not** in recreational teams. This was one of the earliest demonstrations that team-level psychology (collective efficacy) interacts with competitive context — what holds at the elite level does not generalize automatically to lower competitive levels. The study also showed that **task cohesion** (commitment to shared goals, role clarity) ties more strongly to collective efficacy than **social cohesion** (interpersonal liking). The work is a foundational citation in volleyball team-dynamics literature; subsequent studies (Heuze et al. 2006 in basketball, Marcos et al. 2010 in volleyball) extended Spink's finding into longitudinal and intervention designs.

## Key claims / ideas

- Group cohesion and collective efficacy are distinct but empirically linked at elite competitive levels.
- The cohesion-efficacy link is level-dependent: present in elite teams, absent in recreational teams.
- Task cohesion is more performance-relevant than social cohesion.
- For elite teams, raising cohesion (clear roles, shared goals) is a candidate lever for raising collective efficacy.
- Practical implication: in HS varsity / college / elite club settings, role-clarity and shared-goal interventions plausibly raise team belief in capability — a chain that downstream literature has linked to performance.
