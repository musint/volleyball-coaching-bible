# Muscular Imbalance and Shoulder Pain in Volleyball Attackers (Kugler et al. 1996)

## Summary

Kugler and colleagues (1996, *British Journal of Sports Medicine*) is a foundational paper in the overhead-athlete shoulder literature applied specifically to volleyball. The study examined 30 competitive volleyball attackers (mean age 25), with half symptomatic for shoulder pain and half asymptomatic, against 15 recreational athletes with no overhead-sport history. The findings document a now-classic adaptation pattern: in the playing shoulder, attackers showed depression of the shoulder relative to the non-playing side, lateralization of the scapula, and shortening of the dorsal (posterior) musculature plus the posterior-inferior shoulder capsule. Crucially, every adaptation was *more pronounced* in attackers with shoulder pain than in those without, suggesting a dose-response relationship between training-induced muscular imbalance and pain emergence. The paper's conclusion — that "muscular balance of the shoulder girdle is critical" and that prevention requires both stretching the tight posterior structures and strengthening the imbalanced rotator cuff — was an early, clean statement of what later became standard overhead-athlete prehab orthodoxy. For the wiki, Kugler 1996 anchors any claim that volleyball attacker shoulder pain is overwhelmingly an overuse imbalance, that posterior shoulder mobility plus rotator cuff strengthening are the prevention targets, and that warm-up/prehab programming for hitters and jump servers must address those specifically.

## Key claims / ideas

- Volleyball attackers develop characteristic asymmetries: depressed playing shoulder, lateralized scapula, tight posterior capsule, shortened dorsal musculature.
- Asymmetries correlate dose-responsively with pain — symptomatic attackers show more pronounced imbalance than asymptomatic peers.
- Shoulder pain in volleyball attackers is fundamentally an overuse imbalance, not acute trauma.
- Prevention requires both stretching (posterior capsule, dorsal musculature) and strengthening (rotator cuff balance, scapular control).
- Foundational early evidence for the modern overhead-athlete prehab template.
