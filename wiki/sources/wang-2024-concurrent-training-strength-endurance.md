# Wang & Bo (2024) — Concurrent training: factors enhancing muscle strength (review)

## Summary

Peer-reviewed narrative review published in *Medicine (Baltimore)* synthesizing the variables that modulate the "interference effect" — the reduced strength / hypertrophy adaptation seen when endurance training is combined with resistance training. Bottom line for volleyball coaches: **interference is real but manageable, and most volleyball-relevant programming choices already minimize it**. Untrained subjects show minimal interference (good news for 14U / HS); trained athletes are the population where the effect bites. **2 sessions/week of endurance** at **20–30 minutes** is more strength-friendly than 3+ sessions of 50–60 min. **HIIT does not produce the same penalty** as steady-state — and HIIT happens to mirror volleyball's match-intensity profile. **Resistance before endurance** in mixed sessions, **6–24 hours separation** between modes, and **post-exercise protein** all reduce interference. Same-day stacking is the worst case. Sex differences: women may need longer recovery windows.

## Key claims / ideas

- Interference is detectable mainly in trained athletes, not untrained — junior / developing players are largely insulated.
- 2 endurance sessions / week at 20–30 min is the strength-friendly upper bound.
- HIIT does not show the same interference penalty as steady-state — match-mimicking conditioning is also the smart choice for strength preservation.
- Resistance training before endurance within a session preserves strength outcomes.
- 6–24 hours separation between strength and endurance modalities reduces molecular interference.
- Same-day stacking of strength and endurance is the worst case for strength gains.
- Post-exercise protein supplementation mitigates strength-adaptation impairment.
- Sex differences: women may need longer recovery windows; hormonal responses differ.

## Topics covered

- Concurrent strength + endurance training
- Interference effect (mTOR vs AMPK pathway)
- HIIT vs steady-state endurance comparisons
- Exercise sequencing within concurrent sessions
- Recovery-interval programming between modes
- Post-exercise protein and adaptation
