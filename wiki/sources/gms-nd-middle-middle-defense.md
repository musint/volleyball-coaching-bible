# Trinsey — Mastering the Middle-Middle Defense (Gold Medal Squared)

## Summary

Joe Trinsey's foundational coaching piece on the Middle-Middle defensive system. Built on **10,000+ charted attacks** documenting where balls land in elite women's matches, Trinsey argues that placing the best defender at "middle-middle" (mid-court depth, ~6m off net, centered) covers the highest-frequency landing zone — effective against approximately **90-95% of attackers**.

Critically, the article includes an explicit caveat that has become load-bearing for modern interpretation: **"put your best defensive players where most balls go doesn't necessarily mean put your libero in middle middle."** Trinsey frames the principle as personnel-driven rather than positionally rigid — the libero may or may not be the right player to anchor middle-middle, depending on roster talent.

The piece challenges the traditional "middle-back at the end line" approach with chart data showing attacks landing **"at least 10' in from the end line"** — supporting the mid-court middle-middle position over a deep-end-line anchor.

## Key claims / ideas

- **"Put your best defenders where the most balls go"** — primary principle.
- **10,000+ charted attacks** establish the empirical base for middle-middle as the highest-frequency landing zone.
- The middle-middle position is **mid-court (~6m off net), centered** — NOT at the end line.
- **Approximately 90-95% of attackers** land balls consistent with this pattern; remaining 5-10% are exceptional cases requiring scoutable adjustments.
- **Critical caveat:** the principle dictates that "a defender" belongs at middle-middle; it does NOT dictate that the defender must be the libero. Outsides or middles may be a team's best defensive players for that zone.
- **"Principles are liberating"** — frames the defense as a personnel-decision framework rather than a rigid template.
