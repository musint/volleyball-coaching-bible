# Avoiding Instructions Under Pressure in Volleyball Serving (Görgülü & Gökçek 2021)

## Summary

Görgülü and Gökçek (2021) tested **Wegner's ironic processes theory** in a volleyball-serving context. The classical claim: instructing a performer to *avoid* a specific outcome ("don't serve into the net") under pressure causes errors precisely into that forbidden zone. Forty-three female teenage volleyball players completed 10 serves under both low- and high-anxiety conditions; anxiety was manipulated through competitive framing, financial incentives, and social evaluation, and verified via heart rate, heart rate variability, and self-reported mental readiness. Each serve was scored as +5 (target zone), -5 (out/net = ironic error), or +1 (any other in-court).

Contrary to the classical prediction, performance in target zones and error zones remained statistically consistent across anxiety conditions — Wegner's ironic-processes theory was **not** supported in this volleyball serving task. However, the data did support **Woodman et al.'s differentiation between ironic and non-ironic errors**, with distinct error patterns across different court zones suggesting attentional-control disruption rather than pure ironic processing. The study is one of the cleanest volleyball-specific tests of pressure-performance theory and tempers the common coaching belief that "don't miss" instructions are uniquely catastrophic.

## Key claims / ideas

- Wegner's ironic processes theory does not survive empirical test in volleyball serving under pressure.
- Anxiety manipulation worked (HR/HRV verified) — the null effect is real, not a manipulation failure.
- Woodman's ironic-vs-non-ironic distinction better fits the volleyball data.
- Attentional-control theory is the more defensible mechanism for pressure-induced volleyball serving errors.
- Practical: coaches can ease up on the dogma that negative instructions ("don't serve into the net") cause unique catastrophic ironic errors. The effect is attentional, not ironic.
