# Upper Limb Biomechanics During the Volleyball Serve and Spike

## Summary

Reeser et al. (2010) is the canonical reference for shoulder and elbow loading across volleyball-specific upper-limb skills. Fourteen female collegiate athletes performed five skills (hard cross-body spike, hard straight-ahead spike, jump topspin serve, standing float serve, roll shot) under 3-D motion analysis. The authors quantified joint forces, torques, angular velocities, and kinematics with the clinical question: *which volleyball skills place the most stress on the throwing shoulder, and what is a reasonable substitution when an athlete needs to deload?* Headline findings: (1) shoulder abduction at ball contact averages ~130°, substantially greater than tennis or baseball overhand actions, indicating a more vertical arm path that volleyball coaches should not coach away; (2) hard spikes and the jump topspin serve produce the highest shoulder/elbow loads — comparable to or exceeding baseball pitching in some kinetic measures; (3) float serves and especially roll shots are biomechanically lighter, making them legitimate substitutions for athletes managing shoulder pain. The paper grounds the coaching practice of substituting roll shots into warm-ups and managing high-rep spike days.

## Key claims / ideas

- Shoulder abduction at volleyball ball contact averages ~130° across all upper-limb skills — much greater than tennis or baseball; a sport-specific norm, not a flaw.
- Hard spikes and jump topspin serves are the highest-load shoulder skills; jump-serve and power-spike kinetics are comparable.
- Float serves produce lower shoulder loads than spikes; roll shots are the lowest of any tested skill.
- No biomechanical difference between cross-body and straight-ahead spike attempts — the choice is tactical, not kinetic.
- Substituting roll shots / float serves for hard reps is a defensible deload strategy for sore shoulders.
