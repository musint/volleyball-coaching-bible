# A systematic review of volleyball spike kinematics: Implications for practice and research

## Summary

Oliveira and colleagues (2020) systematically reviewed 18 peer-reviewed studies of 3-D-kinematic volleyball-spike performance, drawn from PubMed, SCOPUS, Web of Science, and SPORTDiscus. The review synthesizes what coaches can claim with evidence-based confidence about spike-jump height, ball velocity, and shot-direction biomechanics. Headline conclusions: (1) **spike-jump height** is most strongly tied to centre-of-mass velocities — horizontal velocity during the approach and vertical velocity at takeoff; (2) **ball velocity at impact** is most closely related to **wrist linear velocity** (the most-distal segment in the kinetic chain), with trunk rotation as the secondary contributor — meaning "hit harder" cues should target the wrist and trunk-rotation contribution, not the shoulder alone; (3) **cross-court spike performance from position 4** is associated specifically with greater trunk rotation than line spikes — coaching different attack directions means coaching different biomechanical patterns; (4) the **proximal-to-distal velocity sequencing** of the upper limb (shoulder → elbow → wrist) is a consistent feature of high-velocity spikes. The review also notes methodological heterogeneity across studies and motivates protocol standardization for future research.

## Key claims / ideas

- Spike-jump height depends most strongly on centre-of-mass velocities (horizontal in approach, vertical at takeoff).
- Ball velocity at impact correlates most strongly with wrist linear velocity (distal-segment dominance).
- Trunk rotation is the secondary ball-velocity contributor; cross-court spikes specifically demand greater trunk rotation than line spikes.
- Proximal-to-distal velocity sequencing (shoulder → elbow → wrist) is a hallmark of high-velocity spikes.
- "Hit harder" coaching cues should target the wrist and trunk, not the shoulder alone.
- The literature is large enough to support evidence-informed coaching but heterogeneous; single-study claims should be triangulated.
