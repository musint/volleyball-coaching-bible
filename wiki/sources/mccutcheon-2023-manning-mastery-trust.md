# Coach Hugh McCutcheon on How to Inspire Individual Mastery and Trust Among Teams

## Summary

Interview-style feature on Katharine Manning's leadership blog in which McCutcheon articulates a coach's job as empowering athletes to make good choices autonomously. He emphasizes teaching as the real leverage — if athletes are taught well, they can play well without a coach in the middle — and frames coaches as service providers who earn authentic influence rather than impose authority.

The conversation extends his philosophy beyond results: mastery and trust are the twin outputs, with humility and principle alignment as preconditions. Closely tracks the dual-responsibility model of his book *Championship Behaviors*.

## Key claims / ideas

- A coach's job is to empower athletes to make the right choice at the right time — giving them the tools and mental capacity to read information and decide.
- Athletes should choose the right thing because it is the right thing, not because they fear consequences.
- If athletes are taught the right way, they can play well on their own; coaches should not get in the way of that.
- Humility, clear expectations, and principle alignment are essential components of successful teams.
- Coaches are service providers who create value through authentic influence.
- Development horizon extends beyond the sport — set athletes up for the next phase of life.

## Topics covered

- Empowerment vs. fear-based compliance
- Teaching as the highest-leverage coaching act
- Humility and shared principles as team foundations
- Coach-as-service-provider model
- Long-horizon athlete development
