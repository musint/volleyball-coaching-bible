# Palao, Ureña, Moreno & Ortega-Toro 2024 — U-14 Female Rule Modifications

## Summary

A quasi-experimental study comparing official-rules play vs. two rule-modification conditions in U-14 female volleyball (n=29 Spanish club players, 13.4 ± 0.68 yrs; 5,315 ball actions across 9 matches). Tested net-height reduction (2.10 → 2.00 m), court reduction (9×9 → 8×8 m), and serve limits (no jump serves, max 2 serves per player). Results: lower-net-only tilts the game toward serve dominance; combined modifications (lower net + smaller court + serve limits) produce more balanced serve-reception dynamics with significantly more attacks, more blocks, increased side-out efficacy, and longer rally continuity.

The most coach-relevant single finding: **21.5% of U-14 female serves were already jump serves under official rules** — substantial uptake before puberty completes, anchoring the wiki's progression of jump-float introduction at top-club 13s with empirical U-14 female data.

## Key claims / ideas

- **Jump-serve rate under official rules at U-14 female: 21.5%** — the technique is in the developmental pipeline well before senior pro.
- **Lower net alone (2.00 m, 9×9):** serve efficacy ↑, reception efficacy ↓, block efficacy ↓ (p<0.01) — net-height-only modification skews toward serving dominance.
- **Combined modifications (2.00 m net + 8×8 m court + serve limits):** serve efficacy ↓ (1.74 vs 1.97), reception efficacy ↑ (1.64 vs 1.46), attacks ↑ (p<0.01, ES=0.588), blocks ↑ (p<0.001), side-out efficacy ↑ — produces game dynamics more similar to senior development stages.
- **Rally continuity:** 84.55% in combined-mods vs. 79.46% in control.
- **Two-serve cap binding effect was modest** — only 10–12% of multi-serve players actually exceeded the cap.
