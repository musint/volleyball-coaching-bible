# How to Structure Volleyball Practice for Maximum Improvement

## Summary
A GMS blog article presenting the organization's operational view of practice
design. The piece distinguishes blocked practice (slower, isolated,
predictable — useful for cognitive-stage learners) from random practice
(game-speed, variable — the driver of real improvement once foundational
skills exist) and invokes the motor-learning concept of the "challenge
point" to argue that coaches should move to random, game-like conditions as
quickly as possible.

Beyond the blocked-vs-random framing, the article operationalizes three
practice-structure principles (design for transfer, maximize game-like
reps, use whiteboards) and two motor-learning guidelines (external-focus
cues via analogies, intentional rather than reflexive feedback). Together
these form the most complete public statement of GMS's practice-architecture
philosophy on their website.

## Key claims / ideas
- Challenge point: tasks too easy do not produce learning; too hard erodes
  confidence. Aim for an appropriately challenging zone.
- "The single most correlative activity to being a good volleyball player is
  having played a lot of volleyball." → maximize game-like reps.
- External focus of attention (movement effects, analogies like "cracking a
  whip") is preferred over internal body-mechanic cues.
- Feedback should be intentional, not applied to every repetition — athletes
  need to develop self-evaluation.
- Warm-ups should be re-purposed as ball-contact opportunities.

## Topics covered
- Random vs. blocked practice.
- Transfer and specificity.
- External vs. internal focus cues.
- Feedback frequency and self-evaluation.
- Practice-management tools (whiteboards, small-group work).
