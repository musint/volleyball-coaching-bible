# Balance Board Ankle-Sprain Prevention in Volleyball (Verhagen et al. 2005)

## Summary

Verhagen and colleagues (2005, *British Journal of Sports Medicine*) ran a cluster-randomized trial across 116 Dutch volleyball teams during the 2001–2002 season — 66 intervention teams (628 players) added a proprioceptive balance-board program to warm-ups, and 52 control teams (494 players) followed normal routines. The trial answered both clinical-effectiveness and cost-effectiveness questions about a low-tech, time-cheap intervention. On effectiveness, balance-board training significantly reduced recurrent ankle sprains, with the strongest effect in players with prior sprain history. On cost-effectiveness, the program cost roughly €444 per ankle sprain prevented, with intervention teams spending more per player (€36.99 vs. €18.94 in controls) but generating measurable injury reduction. Sensitivity analysis suggested the program was most economically defensible when targeted at players with previous ankle sprains. The paper is one of the canonical volleyball-specific injury-prevention RCTs and provides clean, direct evidence that a 5–10 minute proprioceptive warm-up reduces recurrent ankle sprains. For the wiki, Verhagen 2005 anchors any claim that balance-board work belongs in warm-ups for any player with sprain history, and that universal-team adoption is also defensible (with diminishing returns for first-timers).

## Key claims / ideas

- Proprioceptive balance-board training in warm-ups significantly reduces recurrent ankle sprains.
- Effect is strongest in players with prior sprain history (secondary prevention).
- Per-sprain prevention cost ≈ €444 — economically defensible especially when targeted.
- Intervention is low-tech, low-time-cost, and embedable in pre-practice warm-up.
- One of the few volleyball-specific injury-prevention RCTs at this evidence quality.
