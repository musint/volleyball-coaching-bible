# Repetition without Repetition or Differential Learning of Multiple Techniques in Volleyball?

## Summary

Apidogo, Burdack, and Schöllhorn (2021) is the flagship volleyball-specific test of **differential learning (DL)** — Schöllhorn's "noise-injection" framework where every repetition is intentionally varied so that no two reps are identical. The authors compared DL against repetitive (RG) and passive-control (CG) groups on **three volleyball techniques learned in parallel** — underhand pass, overhand pass, and overhand serve — across a 6-week, 12-session intervention with 42 female adolescent absolute beginners (~15.6 yr).

DL beat RG and CG significantly on **every** individual technique AND on the combined three-technique score (p < 0.001 across the board), and the advantages held into a 2-week retention test. Authors interpret this through Bernstein's "repetition without repetition": broader sampling of movement solutions allowed the neuro-motor system to adapt more efficiently to all three techniques simultaneously.

For coaching, this is one of the strongest empirical pieces against the assumption that beginners need blocked practice and should learn one skill at a time. Differential, multi-technique parallel practice — with deliberate variation rather than groove-the-perfect-rep repetition — outperformed both repetitive and passive controls.

## Key claims / ideas

- Differential learning (DL) produced superior outcomes vs. repetitive learning AND control on **all three** volleyball techniques AND their combination (p < 0.001 each).
- Effect held at retention 2 weeks post-intervention — not just acquisition.
- DL group **never repeated the same movement twice** by design; instead, each rep introduced controlled variation in stance, plane, distance, etc.
- Population was 42 adolescent female absolute beginners — directly relevant to early HS / late-club beginner context.
- Mechanism (per authors): broader sampling of movement solutions → better neuro-motor adaptation than repetitive grooving of a "model" technique.
- Provides evidence against the "block-then-random" sequencing assumption when alternatives like DL are available.
