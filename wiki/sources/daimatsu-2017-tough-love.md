# Japan's Team — The Oriental Witches of the 1964 Olympics, Part 2

## Summary
Retrospective feature surveying the debate over Daimatsu Hirobumi's training regimen: was the Nichibo / national-team program abusive, or a culturally contextualized "tough love" that players endorsed? The piece is the densest single source this cluster captured for **training atmosphere, hours, and specific technique names**.

Core technique identified in Japanese: **"kaiten reshibu"** (receive and rotate) — the repeated somersault-recovery drill on hard courts that built the team's defensive identity. Practice schedule: year-round with a single week off around Easter; evening sessions that pushed past the 7 PM meal; the oft-cited **"7 days a week, 51 weeks a year"** formulation. Includes the blunt Daimatsu quote directed at struggling players ("If you'd rather be home with your mother, then go") and Captain Masae Kasai's later defense ("He was a man we could trust").

Treats the 1964 *Sports Illustrated* piece "Driven Beyond Dignity" as the period's critical counter-voice, and union complaints ignored by Nichibo management as organizational context.

## Key claims / ideas
- "Kaiten reshibu" (receive and rotate) as the signature defensive drill
- Training volume as explicit compensation for size disadvantage
- The abuse/tough-love debate is old, not a modern re-reading
- Players themselves credit the methods for the undefeated run

## Topics covered
- Defensive philosophy (compensation through volume + technique)
- Training-load history
- Coaching ethics and athlete welfare
- Japanese coaching stereotypes ("demon coach")
