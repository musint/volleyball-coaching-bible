# A review of the contextual interference effect in motor skill acquisition

## Summary

Magill and Hall's 1990 review in *Human Movement Science* is the canonical synthesis of the first decade of contextual-interference research following Shea and Morgan (1979). It organizes the literature along two axes: whether the CI effect generalizes across tasks, learners, and conditions; and what theoretical mechanisms (elaboration/distinctiveness vs. forgetting/reconstruction) explain it.

The review's most consequential contribution is the **"Magill & Hall hypothesis"**: the CI effect is most robust when the task variations being practiced are governed by **different generalized motor programs (GMPs)**, and weaker or inconsistent when the variations are merely parameter modifications of the same GMP. This hypothesis shaped a generation of follow-up experiments and helps explain why the clean 1979 effect doesn't always replicate — the tasks have to be different enough to matter.

## Key claims / ideas

- CI benefits retention and transfer; CI harms acquisition-phase performance.
- The effect generalizes best when task variations span **different GMPs** (different movement classes), not just different parameters of the same GMP.
- For within-GMP parameter variation (e.g., same skill, different force or distance), variability-of-practice accounts (Schmidt's schema theory) may predict learning better than CI alone.
- Two candidate mechanisms — **elaborative processing** (Shea & Zimny) and **forgetting/reconstruction** (Lee & Magill) — both receive empirical support; neither is definitively falsified.
