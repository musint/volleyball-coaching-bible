# Contextual Interference Effects on the Acquisition, Retention, and Transfer of a Motor Skill

## Summary

Shea and Morgan's 1979 study is the **foundational empirical demonstration of the contextual interference (CI) effect in motor learning**. Participants learned three barrier knock-down tasks under either a blocked (low-interference) or random (high-interference) practice schedule. During acquisition, the blocked group performed better — exactly what a naive observer would predict. But on retention tests after 10 minutes and 10 days, and especially on transfer tests under novel conditions, the random-practice group outperformed the blocked group.

The finding is counterintuitive and is the original reference behind the modern coaching claim that "harder, messier practice produces better learning than clean blocked drills." It anchors random-vs-blocked, varied-vs-constant-practice, and "desirable difficulties" literatures across motor learning and education. Full text was not cleanly extractable from the archived PDF, but the task paradigm, design, and headline findings are confirmed across multiple secondary sources (Magill & Hall 1990; Brady 1998/2004; Wright & Kim meta-analysis 2024).

## Key claims / ideas

- Random (high-CI) practice yields **worse acquisition performance** than blocked (low-CI) practice.
- Random practice yields **better retention and transfer** than blocked practice, with effects strengthening over longer delays.
- Practice that feels harder in the moment can be more effective long-term — an early statement of what Robert Bjork later called "desirable difficulties."
- The effect is tied to Battig's (1966) earlier hypothesis about interference benefits in verbal learning; Shea & Morgan extended it to motor skill.
