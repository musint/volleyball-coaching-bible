# Mixed Methods Analysis of Imagery Use Over a Collegiate Volleyball Season (2025)

## Summary

This 2025 study in Movement & Sport Sciences — Science & Motricité is one of the few mixed-methods, season-long examinations of how collegiate (NCAA-level) volleyball players actually use mental imagery — not in a controlled experimental task, but in their day-to-day competitive practice. Quantitative imagery-use questionnaires (administered repeatedly across the season) were paired with qualitative interviews about *how, when, and why* athletes employ imagery. The work is naturalistic and descriptive rather than interventional; it captures the messy reality of imagery practice in high-level volleyball.

The study reveals that imagery use is **highly heterogeneous within a single team** — players differ widely in imagery frequency, content (technical vs motivational vs strategic), and timing (pre-practice, pre-match, in-match resets). It also shifts across the season, with strategic and recovery-related imagery rising as competition intensifies. Critically, without coach scaffolding, imagery practice remains **idiosyncratic and inconsistent** — many players self-report using imagery but few have been formally trained. The practical takeaway for coaches at HS varsity and college levels: imagery is a coachable skill that needs explicit structure (cue scripts, timing prompts, vividness training) to compound, not just a vague "visualize success" reminder.

## Key claims / ideas

- Imagery use is naturally embedded in volleyball but is mostly informal and unstructured.
- Within-team variance in imagery practice is large — players inherit very different imagery habits.
- Imagery content shifts across the season (more strategic-cognitive late season, more recovery-motivational during heavy weeks).
- Coach-led scaffolding (cue scripts, structured imagery sessions) is a defensible coaching intervention with apparent uptake.
- Mixed methods is the appropriate methodology for these naturalistic patterns; pure experiments miss within-season variation.
