# Spike jump biomechanics in male versus female elite volleyball players

## Summary

Fuchs et al. (2019) is the most-cited modern study of sex differences in volleyball spike-jump biomechanics. Thirty sub-elite players (15 men, 15 women) performed maximal spike jumps captured by a 12-camera 3-D motion-capture system, force plates, and EMG. The authors aimed to characterize differences across approach, transition (penultimate step + plant), takeoff, and arm action — and to assess whether observed differences could be explained by strength/power alone or whether they were technical-coordinative. Findings: women approached at slower speeds, used a shorter penultimate step, and showed different plant angles and neuromuscular activation patterns. Centre-of-mass displacement, knee/hip joint angles, torso inclination, and arm angular velocity also differed. Crucially, the authors concluded that strength and power could not fully explain the observed gap, implying technique-based interventions (penultimate-step length, approach velocity, plant timing) are legitimate leverage points for improving women's spike-jump performance — not only weight-room work. The paper grounds female-specific approach-coaching cues that differ from a generic male-default model.

## Key claims / ideas

- Men jump significantly higher than women in spike jumps (matched-elite comparison).
- Women approach at slower speeds and use shorter penultimate steps than men.
- Plant angle and neuromuscular activation patterns differ between sexes.
- Strength and power do not fully explain the gap — technical-coordinative differences are real.
- Coaches working with female athletes should specifically train approach velocity and penultimate-step length, not just lower-body strength.
