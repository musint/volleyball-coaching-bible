# Lockie et al. (2020) — Power, sprint, and change-of-direction in NCAA D-I women's volleyball

## Summary

Peer-reviewed primary research published in *Biology of Sport* quantifying correlations between vertical / horizontal lower-body power tests and linear-speed / change-of-direction (COD) performance in 15 NCAA D-I women's volleyball players. Key findings: **vertical-jump and power-to-body-mass correlate with 20-m sprint** (r = −0.534 to −0.557); **standing broad jump and relative broad jump correlate strongly with pro-agility shuttle** (r = −0.589 to −0.729); **no significant correlations with 10-m acceleration** — early-acceleration mechanics are a separate quality requiring dedicated sprint-mechanics work. Practical takeaway: **horizontal jumping (broad jump, bounding) is the highest-leverage power target for COD**, vertical jump alone won't carry early acceleration, and most volleyball court movement happens in 3–5 m windows so the standard 20-m / pro-agility tests should be supplemented with shorter-distance sprints.

## Key claims / ideas

- Vertical jump and power-to-body-mass correlate with 20-m sprint times (r = −0.534 to −0.557).
- Standing broad jump and relative broad jump correlate strongly with pro-agility shuttle COD performance (r = −0.589 to −0.729).
- No significant correlation between any power measure and 10-m acceleration — early acceleration is a distinct quality.
- Horizontal jumping (broad jump, bounding) is the highest-leverage power target for COD development.
- Most volleyball court movement happens in 3–5 m windows; standard 20-m sprint tests overstate value of long-distance speed.
- Test-design recommendation: 5-m shuttle and pro-agility provide more volleyball-relevant signal than 40-yard / 20-m sprints.
