# An Approach to Quantify the Float Effect of Float Serves in Indoor and Beach Volleyball

## Summary

Reiser and colleagues (2020) operationalize "float" as a measurable in-flight trajectory phenomenon rather than a subjective qualitative descriptor. The authors developed two trajectory metrics — *mean residual* (deviation from regression-fitted parabolic flight) and *anticipation error* (how far off the ball lands from where its early-flight regression would predict) — and applied them to 24 elite float serves (12 indoor, 12 beach). The vertical plane was the dominant float component: vertical residuals were roughly twice the magnitude of horizontal residuals in both formats. Indoor servers achieved higher mean release velocity (17.79 m/s vs. 15.14 m/s on the beach), but velocity explained almost none of the float magnitude (r² < 0.033), confirming that release velocity is necessary but not sufficient for float. Anticipation error reached up to 1.5 m vertically and 1.0 m horizontally — a measurable spatial difficulty receivers face. The paper anchors coaching content for both servers (the contact, not the release velocity, drives float) and receivers (vertical drop is the dominant deviation; expect the ball to fall sharper than parabolic).

## Key claims / ideas

- Float is a quantifiable trajectory phenomenon — vertical residuals roughly twice horizontal residuals.
- Higher release velocity does not produce more float; ball-contact technique drives unpredictability.
- Indoor float serves average ~17.8 m/s release velocity; beach ~15.1 m/s (lower release velocity, comparable float magnitude).
- Anticipation error up to 1.5 m vertically — receivers should expect the ball to drop sharper than parabolic.
- Coaching cue: chase float magnitude through contact (no spin, off-centre strike), not through arm speed.
