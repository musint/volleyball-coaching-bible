# The Language of Coaching — Nick Winkelman, 2021

## Summary

*The Language of Coaching: The Art and Science of Teaching Movement*
(Human Kinetics, 2021, 336pp, ISBN 9781492567363) is Nick Winkelman's
applied translation of 25+ years of motor-learning research — primarily
the attentional-focus literature led by Gabriele Wulf and colleagues
since the late 1990s — into a working coach's manual for cue selection.
Winkelman, head of athletic performance and science for the Irish
Rugby Football Union, holds a PhD in coaching cues and was previously
director of education and movement at EXOS / Athletes' Performance.

The book is organized in three parts: **Learn** (motor learning,
attention, memory foundations), **Coach** (cueing principles and
analogy), and **Cue** (applied chapters by movement family — habit
development, strength, power, speed). Twenty-five-plus video movement
sequences accompany the text. The book's central claim — that
**external cues consistently outperform internal cues** for both
acute performance and long-term retention — is the strongest research-
backed cueing finding currently available to coaches.

For volleyball, the book is sport-agnostic in its examples but **directly
applicable**: volleyball is composed almost entirely of fast,
externally-paced motor skills (passing, setting, hitting, blocking,
serving, defense, transition footwork) where the external-cue advantage
holds, yet most volleyball coaches default to internal cues ("get your
platform low," "snap your wrist," "transfer your weight"). The book
provides the operational frame for upgrading cueing across the
technique catalog and across the [[age-lens-14u]],
[[age-lens-hs]], and [[age-lens-college]] pages.

## Key claims / ideas

- **External cues outperform internal cues.** Across balance, postural
  tasks, force/velocity expression, change-of-direction speed, sport
  skills with implements, and continuous skills, external-focus cueing
  produces larger acute performance gains AND better long-term
  retention than internal-focus cueing.
- **Acute vs. chronic learning are different problems.** Cueing that
  optimizes the next rep can degrade durable learning. Retention tests
  conducted days after practice show external-cued learners retain
  skills better than internal-cued learners. Coaches whose cueing
  decisions are calibrated only to immediate execution can
  unintentionally select against long-term mastery.
- **The "Three D's" of external cueing.** Operational framework for
  cue construction:
  - **Distance** — close (nearby objects: ball, net, floor) vs. far
    (distant objects: back line, opponent's shoulder, ceiling).
    **Novices benefit from close-distance cues; experienced athletes
    perform better with far-distance cues.**
  - **Direction** — "towards" cues outperform "away" cues in jumping
    and acceleration tasks.
  - **Description** — action verbs ("push," "explode," "snap") and
    analogies ("like a slingshot," "spring-loaded," "punch through")
    are both effective; analogies encode complex motor patterns in a
    single mental image the athlete already understands.
- **Analogy as primary instructional unit.** A well-chosen analogy is
  more powerful than a list of technical instructions because it
  activates a mental map the athlete already possesses, then borrows
  the motor solution attached to that map.
- **Cue construction as deliberate craft.** Six-step process:
  identify the desired outcome → choose internal vs. external →
  select distance → choose direction → choose description (verb or
  analogy) → test against athlete response and iterate.
- **Habit-development frame for coach behavior change.** Borrowed from
  Duhigg/Clear: cue trigger → routine → reward, applied to upgrading
  the coach's own cueing patterns over time.
- **Safety/technical-correction carve-out.** Internal cues retain a
  legitimate role for *very early* skill acquisition, especially where
  body-part attention is necessary for safety or for fundamental
  setup positioning.
