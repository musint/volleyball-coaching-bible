# Coaching cues

## Overview

A cue is the exact phrase a coach says — five words or fewer in most cases — that lands a teaching point on the next rep. Good cues compress technique into something a player can hear, hold, and execute under live pressure; bad cues sound right in clinic and decay into noise on the court. This area of the wiki catalogs the canonical cues used to teach the seven primary skills, with each cue tied to the coach who codified it, the age band where it lives, and what it does (and does not) teach.

The seven cue dictionaries below are the working voice of the program. Every assistant coach, every camp counselor, every parent volunteer should be saying the same things in the same words when teaching the same skill. That standardization is what lets a player at one practice hear the same correction she would hear from any other coach in the club at any other practice — and what lets a player who graduates from 12s to 13s arrive on day one already speaking the language of the new age band.

The cues collected here lean modern. Where two cue traditions exist for the same teaching point — for example, "snap your wrist" (older arm-and-wrist hitting cue) versus "high hand and torque" (modern arm-and-torso cue from MacDonald) — the dictionary leads with the modern one and notes the legacy phrasing as an anti-cue. This is the recency preference applied to vocabulary: modern Art of Coaching Volleyball (AOC) corpus and Munciana's working drill library are the primary sources, Gold Medal Squared (GMS) supplies motor-learning rationale for why a cue lands or fails, and Japanese-tradition cues are preserved where defensive identity material is uniquely strong [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill][munciana-2022-camp-drills].

## Schools of thought

The cues catalog leans **modern AOC plus Munciana** as the primary voice, with motor-learning rationale from **GMS** and defensive identity from the **Japanese tradition** filling in where each is uniquely strong. This lean is a deliberate framing preference, not a rejection of classics.

**Modern AOC + Munciana** dominates the catalog because the cue language has converged in the last decade across Kiraly's USAV reading-first corpus, Mattox's Lift/Step/Swing serve teaching, Schmidt's "start slow, accelerate the last two steps" approach cue, MacDonald's torque-and-high-hand arm-mechanics frame, Slabe's hand-press blocking instruction, Stone's hips-under-the-ball digging cue, and the entire 2024–2026 AOC fundamentals revision [aoc-2024-attack-approach][aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics][aoc-2024-slabe-blocking][aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]. Munciana adds the working-club layer: aim small/miss small, the three P's of defense, outside-foot-high blocking, and the aggressive-serve five-before-ten framing — language tested in front of camp athletes and stamped into Lingenfelter's drill library [munciana-2022-camp-drills].

**Gold Medal Squared (GMS)** is referenced in the *rationale* under each cue more than in the cue language itself. GMS's motor-learning evidence base — random over blocked practice, external-focus over internal-focus cues, contextual interference for retention — explains *why* certain cues land (e.g., "set the angle" externalizes attention to the platform's relationship with the target rather than to the arms themselves) and why others fail (e.g., "snap your wrist" focuses internally on a body part and fragments the kinetic chain).

**Japanese tradition** holds the strongest claim on defensive cue identity. The dig-up-everything ethic, the midline-the-ball priority, and the recover-to-ready-after-every-contact culture all carry vocabulary worth preserving even when the local Munciana phrasing is the working dialect on the floor.

**Ecological dynamics** sits in dialogue with all three traditions and supplies a corrective: cues land best when they emerge inside representative tasks, not in decomposed-then-recombined drill sequences. A cue spoken during a live serve-receive against a real server outperforms the same cue spoken against a coach toss, because the perception-action coupling the cue is supposed to refine is only present in the live task. The dictionary preserves this where the cue itself is a constraints-led prompt ("aim with your shoulders," "set the angle") rather than a movement instruction.

When Schools genuinely disagree on a cue — for example, "swing through the ball" (Russian-school power cue) versus "stop the platform at the target" (modern GMS/AOC platform cue for forearm passing) — the cue dictionary keeps both, attributes each to its school, and explains how the program resolves. Neutrality on contested cues is preserved per SCHEMA §9.

## Getting started

If you are joining the staff and need to standardize on the program's working vocabulary, read in this order:

1. **[[passing-cues]]** — passing is the highest-volume teaching surface across age bands; the cue vocabulary here is the most repeated.
2. **[[serving-cues]]** — every player serves, so the cue language gets the most coach-mouths repeating it. Aim small/miss small and Lift/Step/Swing are the two phrases every coach in the club should be saying.
3. **[[defense-cues]]** — the three P's (get back, get low, get a touch) are the load-bearing cue cluster and a Munciana signature.
4. **[[hitting-cues]]** — modern arm-mechanics cues differ enough from legacy "snap-your-wrist" teaching that this dictionary is a useful re-grounding for any coach trained before 2015.
5. **[[setting-cues]]** — the hand-hinge cue language is the modern entry into setting; legacy "push set" vocabulary is preserved as an anti-cue.
6. **[[blocking-cues]]** and **[[transition-cues]]** — read together as the back-half of the rally.

For the broader skill context behind each cue, the relevant skill hub is [[passing]], [[setting]], [[hitting]], [[blocking]], [[serving]], [[defense]], or [[transition]]. The age-band development context is on the corresponding age-guide pages.
