# Read Blocking

## Overview
Read blocking is the modern default team blocking scheme at HS+ levels. All three blockers — middle and both pins — start in a flexible base and wait to commit until they read the setter's hands, the set's trajectory, and the hitter's approach. The middle's job is to read the set and close to the pin the ball is going to; the pin's job is to read the set and either hold the pin or move toward the middle to form a seam block. The scheme's premise is that modern offenses run multiple attack options per rotation, and a blocker who commits early is a blocker who is wrong most of the time.

The bunch-read variant pinches the pin blockers closer to the middle in their ready position, which shortens the pin blocker's travel time on seam sets and makes middle help almost automatic on quick attacks — the pin then releases back to the pin on a read of the set [aoc-2026-bunch-read-blocking]. GMS documents bunch-read as their default scheme of 25+ years and names it explicitly as a "read-and-react" system with dynamic footwork [gms-nd-blocking]. Read blocking — in bunch-read or spread-read variants — is essentially synonymous with "blocking at the modern college / HS level," with the understanding that specific teams vary the starting positions (bunched vs. spread) and the reading cues they prioritize.

## Personnel requirements
Read blocking depends on trained eyes across all three blockers. Specifically:
- **Middle blocker:** the central reader. Must see setter hands at contact, decide direction instantly, and close to pin in time to press. Speed closing (Q3, X2, X3 footwork in GMS vocabulary) matters as much as vertical jump [gms-nd-blocking].
- **Pin blockers:** must read set height and hitter approach angle. In bunch-read variants, they must also read whether the set is coming to them (release to pin) or to the middle (hold as seam help). The read must be fast enough to allow clean closing footwork.
- **All three blockers:** must share an eye-work sequence. Kiraly's ball-setter-ball-hitter sequence is the industry-standard teaching framework — eyes travel in the same order for every blocker on every rep [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill].
- McCutcheon emphasizes that middles should have freedom to read and block independently rather than always closing to the pin — an important structural commitment of the read scheme [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys].

## Alignment
Bunch-read base (one common variant):
- MB at zone 3, hands at eye level, 6 inches off the net in static posture [aoc-2024-blocking-fundamentals].
- LF (pin) pinched inward, roughly halfway between zone 4 and zone 3, ready to seam-help middle or release to pin based on set read.
- RF (pin) pinched inward symmetrically.

Spread-read base (alternative):
- MB at zone 3.
- LF and RF at traditional pin positions (shoulder-width from the antenna).
- Blockers close to the set on read, with the MB traveling the farthest.

Regardless of variant, the starting shape assumes no commitment to a specific attacker. The blockers' feet are neutral; their eyes are everywhere relevant at once.

## Strengths
- Adapts to any attack option. Read blocking covers the middle quick, the pin set, the back-row attack, and the second-tempo combination — because the blockers haven't committed, they can close to wherever the ball goes.
- Protects against middle-fake-and-pin deception. A commit blocker falls for a middle fake; a read blocker doesn't leave the middle until they see the ball released elsewhere.
- Modern consensus scheme. The scheme is what HS+ blocking is, in almost every program; variants (bunch vs. spread) differ, but the read-first principle is universal [aoc-2026-bunch-read-blocking] [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys].
- Rewards integrated eye-work training. The same reading sequence used in defense (ball-setter-ball-hitter) powers read blocking; training reading across skills compounds [aoc-2020-reading-every-skill].

## Weaknesses
- Requires extensive training. Blockers without trained reads commit at the wrong moment and produce late blocks with split hands. The scheme's ceiling is the training investment.
- Late reads produce late blocks. A read blocker who reads a set a quarter-second late arrives with only one hand up — the block fails to press, and the attacker hits over the top.
- Undersized block footprints. A read blocker can be in the right place but arrive so late that only one person is up; the two-block scheme turns into a one-block with a soft help.
- Against very fast offenses, read blocking may be too slow: the set-to-hit window doesn't give the reader time to decide, and coaches may shift to commit or bunch-read variants for specific attackers.

## When to use / when not to use
Use read blocking when:
- You coach HS+ and your blockers are trainable in the reading sequence.
- The opponent runs multiple attack options per rotation and you can't afford to commit to one.
- You have practice time to train reading explicitly — two or three reps a week on reading drills, at minimum.
- Your middle has the foot speed to close on pin sets from a base position.

Avoid read blocking when:
- You coach 14U — the scheme demands a reading baseline most 14U blockers don't yet have. Commit blocking is often the right choice at that level against the opponent's one quick attack.
- Your middle is slow closing — read puts them at the mercy of their travel time; a bunch-read variant helps, but the footwork demand is still real.
- The opponent has a single dominant attacker whose rep rate justifies commit blocking that one threat.

## Alternatives and adjustments
- [[Commit-blocking]] is the tactical alternative when you want to take a specific hitter away — works against dominant quick middles or in situational late-match tactics.
- [[Swing-blocking]] is a technique overlay, not a replacement. Most read blockers at HS+ use swing technique to close on pin sets; read + swing is the current modern default [aoc-2024-slabe-blocking].
- Bunch-read vs. spread-read: pinch the pins inward (bunch) to gain seam help and shorter closes, or spread them for more pin coverage on outside sets [aoc-2026-bunch-read-blocking] [gms-nd-blocking].
- Train the reading sequence relentlessly: look-see-decide compartmentalization is a productive coaching frame for diagnosing where reads break down [aoc-2025-training-reading-look-see-decide].
