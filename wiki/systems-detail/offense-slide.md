# Slide (middle-blocker behind-the-setter one-leg attack)

## Overview
The slide is a middle-blocker attack in which the middle transitions laterally along the net BEHIND the setter and attacks from a one-leg takeoff — running out from the setter's back-right toward (or past) the right-side antenna, and attacking a set delivered high and wide behind the setter. Functionally, the slide turns the middle into a second right-side attacker on any given play, creating attack angles the front-row block cannot match without repositioning. The one-leg takeoff is the slide's mechanical signature and also its biggest coaching risk — it loads asymmetric forces through a single knee and requires specific takeoff and landing mechanics to run safely.

At HS varsity and above, the slide is one of the three highest-leverage tempo attacks (alongside the [[offense-quick]] 1/31 and the [[offense-pipe]]). It lets a middle hit against a single pin blocker or against an out-of-position opposing MB who committed to defending the front-row quick. Tod Mattox's modern 4-2 argument centers the slide as the primary weapon — defending a good slide, he argues, opens up every other attack option [aoc-2022-4-2-system]. In a [[offense-6-2]] with the hybrid setter-middle pairing [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle], the slide benefits directly from repeated setter-middle reps. Below 14U, the slide is inappropriate: the one-leg takeoff demand and the timing requirements are developmentally beyond the window, and the injury-risk profile (landing mechanics) is a real factor at younger ages.

## Personnel requirements
- **A middle who can move laterally, plant on one leg, and attack at height.** The slide demands leg strength (one-leg takeoff produces roughly 1.5x the per-leg force of a two-leg takeoff for the same vertical), air awareness (the middle is moving laterally as she jumps), and arm swing speed (the ball is past the setter, and the block is already close by — the middle has to contact the ball quickly).
- **A setter who can deliver a high-and-wide back set.** The slide set is roughly 3–6 feet off the right antenna (inside the antenna) at a height the moving middle can read and attack. Consistency here is the chemistry investment — in a [[offense-6-2]], each middle trains with two setters and the slide timing suffers unless paired [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle].
- **Practice anchor on landing mechanics.** One-leg landings load the knee differently than two-leg landings. Coaching the athletic landing (absorb through both legs, shoulders forward, knees over toes) is non-negotiable. Mattox's spiking progression explicitly flags landing mechanics as a coaching priority [aoc-2021-teach-spiking]. Medical and S&C specifics on ACL-risk reduction belong with a qualified CSCS or athletic trainer (per §8 medical disclaimer).
- **In-system passing.** Out-of-system, the slide disappears — the setter bails to a pin.

John Dunning's hitter-type framework applies to middles: a middle with good lateral mobility and a quick arm swing is a natural slide hitter; a bigger, slower middle with a strong straight-ahead quick may not be [aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types].

## Diagram or text description of alignment
The slide is a layered play, not a base alignment. It sits on top of a [[offense-5-1]] / [[offense-6-2]] / [[offense-4-2]] base. The key is the middle's transition path and the setter's back-set trajectory.

**Middle's transition (in-system, from the front-row MB rotation):**
1. Middle starts in position 3 at the serve.
2. After the first ball is played, she transitions BEHIND the setter (right-to-left facing the setter; she moves toward the right-side antenna).
3. As the setter contacts the ball, the middle plants on her inside leg (left leg if moving right-to-left) and takes off on one leg, continuing her lateral momentum into a jumping, sliding attack.
4. Contact is ideally on the setter's right-antenna side, 3–6 feet inside the antenna, with the middle facing the net at an angle.

**Setter's set:** a high back set with enough air that the middle's transition can cover the distance. The set is typically not as low-tempo as a 1 or 31 — the slide trades tempo for location and angle. Setter reads the opposing MB's positioning and the middle's approach before releasing.

**Block read the slide creates:** opposing MB has to honor the middle's lateral transition, which pulls her out of the middle of the court. If she doesn't honor the slide, the middle attacks against a single blocker (the opposing OH). If she does honor it, the front-row quick from a combo or a trailing hitter attacks against a single blocker.

## Strengths
- **Creates a second right-side attacker.** The slide effectively turns the MB into an opposite for that play. Blocks that were set up to defend two pins and a middle have to rotate to defend two right-side attack zones.
- **Attacks angles the front-row block can't match.** The setter's back set produces an angled attack vector the opposing middle has to slide (pun intended) laterally to defend; most MBs can't.
- **Fast tempo with lateral deception.** The opposing MB is reading both the tempo and the location. If she reads 1 and the ball goes slide, she's out of position. If she reads slide and the ball goes 1, the middle attacks the front zone she vacated.
- **Featured weapon in Mattox's modern 4-2.** In a 4-2, defending a slide is what opens up every other attack [aoc-2022-4-2-system]; the slide anchors the offense.
- **Compounds with combos.** A tandem where the opposite runs a 1 and the middle runs a slide behind her forces the opposing block into a full-court decision — which usually loses.
- **Matchup weapon vs. shorter opposing OH.** A slide attacks the opposing left-side pin block (the OH, usually not the team's best blocker); the middle matched up against the opposing OH is a height-and-tempo advantage.

## Weaknesses
- **One-leg takeoff safety.** The slide is the highest-injury-risk attack in the standard HS+ vocabulary when landing mechanics are not drilled. Landing on one leg and pivoting to absorb is a learnable skill; landing on one leg without that coaching is an ACL risk. This is a real cost and has to be addressed in practice planning — proper landings, strength work, and progressive introduction.
- **Setter-middle timing chemistry.** Slide is harder to install than a 1 or 31 because the set trajectory is more complex (high, back, angled) and the middle's transition is longer. In a [[offense-6-2]], the hybrid setter-middle pairing [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle] is the standard mitigation.
- **Requires in-system passing.** Out-of-system kills the slide — the setter must bail to a pin.
- **Predictable if over-used.** Scouted slide tendencies (approach direction, contact zone, preferred shot) let the opposing block pre-rotate to defend.
- **Physically demanding on the middle.** Full-slide reps every possession are expensive; HS+ teams usually feature the slide in specific rotations and rest it in others.
- **Not appropriate below HS.** The combination of one-leg takeoff, lateral transition, and back-set timing is developmentally beyond most 14U teams. Teaching the approach and the landing as skills (without full-tempo slide reps) is reasonable; calling the slide in a 14U match is usually premature.

## When to use / when not to use
**Use when:**
- Your pass is in-system and your setter-middle pairing has drilled the slide set.
- The opposing team's MB is over-committing to the front-row 1 or 31 — a slide attacks the space she vacated.
- The opposing left-side pin block (OH) is weaker than the right-side pin block — the slide creates a matchup against the weaker blocker.
- You want a fourth attacker in a [[offense-5-1]]'s setter-front-row rotation and your OH is not a pipe hitter.
- You are running a modern [[offense-4-2]] in the Mattox style and the slide is your primary weapon [aoc-2022-4-2-system].

**Do not use when:**
- Your middle's landing mechanics are not drilled. The injury risk outweighs the scoring benefit.
- Your pass is off-net or broken — run [[offense-high-ball]] or a pin set.
- Your middle is fatigued; one-leg takeoff on fatigued legs is when landing injuries cluster.
- You are at 12U or early 14U. Install the skill progression in practice; do not call the slide in matches.

## Alternatives and adjustments
- **[[offense-quick]]** — the front-row counterpart. A 1 or 31 is faster-tempo and less lateral; pair with the slide to give the opposing MB a real read-vs-commit problem.
- **[[offense-pipe]]** — the OH back-row attack. A slide-alternative fourth attacker when the middle is scouted or fatigued.
- **[[offense-high-ball]]** — out-of-system bailout.
- **Slide progression for young middles** — start with a two-leg approach behind the setter (a "back 2") and progress to one-leg as jump training and landing mechanics mature. This approach is consistent with the age-appropriate framing in [[usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]] and is the standard build-up.
- **Slide-featured 4-2** — Mattox's modern 4-2 [aoc-2022-4-2-system]. Setter attacks the second ball, sets the slide, and keeps the defense honest.
- **Hybrid 6-2 setter-middle pairing** — locks a specific setter with a specific middle so the slide set's consistency compounds [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle].
- **In-match rotation featuring** — call the slide in specific rotations where the matchup favors it; avoid running it every ball.
- **Landing and jump-training anchors** — Liskevych's ASICS hitting series addresses approach and contact mechanics [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-hitting]; Schmidt's approach-tempo piece grounds the consistency cue [aoc-2024-attack-approach].
