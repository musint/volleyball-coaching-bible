# Quick Attack (middle tempo: 1, 31, and fast-middle family)

## Overview
The quick attack is a family of fast-tempo sets delivered to the middle hitter (and occasionally to a front-row opposite or OH running a combo) that are designed to beat the opposing block by tempo, not by location. Where a normal "high ball" outside set gives the block a full second-plus to form, close, and press — a quick set is in and out of the setter's hands so fast that the middle blocker cannot transition from her base position to the point of attack in time. The classic quick-attack vocabulary:

- **"1"** (also called a "A" set or "51"): the middle hitter is already in the air or peaking as the setter sets. Ball comes out of the setter's hands roughly 1 foot above the net on the setter's immediate right. Timing requirement is near-perfect.
- **"31"** (also called a "shoot" or a "3" in some systems): a quick set delivered about 3 feet off the setter's hands toward the left-side gap; middle hitter approaches into the gap between positions 3 and 4 and attacks on a faster-than-high-ball tempo.
- **Slide**: technically part of the quick-attack family but so distinct it gets its own page — see [[offense-slide]]. The middle hitter transitions along the net BEHIND the setter and attacks on one leg.
- **"Shoot"**: a faster-than-normal set to the outside pin — not a middle attack, but often grouped into the quick-tempo call library.
- **Combos** (X, tandem, double-quick): a middle runs a 1 or 31 while another front-row attacker trails behind her — a decoy attack designed to split the block.

The quick attack is the offensive spine of HS varsity, college, and professional volleyball. Above the 14U level, a team without a legitimate middle-quick threat is trivially over-blocked on the pins. Below the 14U level, the timing demands — on the pass, the setter, and the middle's approach — are usually beyond the developmental window. Installing the quick attack is why 14U setter-middle pairings matter (see [[offense-6-2]] hybrid [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle]).

## Personnel requirements
- **A setter who can deliver a set on a 1–3 foot tempo from any in-system pass.** Hand-speed, quick-set location consistency, and the judgment to pick the right option on a given pass — all non-negotiable.
- **A middle blocker who can approach, jump, and attack on the setter's tempo.** The middle's footwork must be drilled to arrive at the peak of her jump as the ball leaves the setter's hands, not after. Kiraly's *Championship Volleyball* treats setter-hitter timing as the central chemistry investment of any offense [kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball]; Liskevych's ASICS hitting series treats the middle approach and tempo as a distinct skill from the outside approach [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-hitting].
- **Consistent passing.** A quick attack needs an in-system pass (within roughly 5 feet of the setter, at a height the setter can set quickly). A shanked or off-net pass kills the quick — the setter must bail to a high ball instead.
- **A decision-maker.** Setter must read the opposing middle blocker's position and commitment and choose between the 1, the 31, a slide, or a pin set on every in-system pass. John Dunning's hitter-type framework — each hitter knowing her style and its gaps — applies just as much to middles as to outsides [aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types].

## Diagram or text description of alignment
The quick attack is not a full system — it is a set of plays layered on top of a base system (usually a [[offense-5-1]] or [[offense-6-2]]). The alignment that matters is the middle's approach path and the setter's hand position.

- **1 (A set)** — middle approaches straight toward position 3 as soon as the ball leaves the passer's hands. She jumps as the setter releases the ball. Contact is directly above the setter's right shoulder, roughly 1 foot above the net. The middle's arm swing is tight — a full wind-up is too slow for the tempo [aoc-2021-teach-spiking].
- **31 (shoot)** — middle approaches on a diagonal toward the gap between position 3 and position 4 (roughly 3 feet off the setter's left hand). She jumps later than on a 1 — the ball travels a few feet laterally before she contacts it. The 31 creates more separation from the opposing MB than a 1 does, and it's a more forgiving set for young middles because the tempo is slightly slower while still being faster than a high ball.
- **Combo (X, tandem)** — MB runs a 1 while the OH or opposite runs a trailing set (a "2" or "back 2") behind her. If the opposing MB commits to the 1, the trailing hitter attacks against a single blocker — or against no blocker at all.

Base approach tempo rules from Gina Schmidt apply: start slow, accelerate on the last two steps, and be at the same spot in the approach when the ball leaves the setter's hands [aoc-2024-attack-approach]. For quick attacks, "the same spot" is earlier in the motion — the middle is already in her jump as the setter sets.

## Strengths
- **Breaks the block.** A well-timed 1 or 31 attacks before the opposing MB has transitioned — the attack goes against a single blocker (the MB herself, beaten by tempo) or no blocker at all.
- **Forces the opposing middle to commit.** A middle blocker who has to respect a 1 cannot read and transition to the pin. That opens the OH and opposite to attack against a single pin blocker rather than a double. This is the quick attack's true value — it protects the rest of the offense.
- **Stats-positive.** At HS+ and above, middle attack efficiency tends to run higher than OH or opposite efficiency specifically because the block is beaten by tempo rather than by placement. The tradeoff is volume — middles take fewer swings per match because the in-system pass rate is never 100%.
- **Creates matchup nightmares for opposing block schemes.** A read-blocking defense struggles with quick middles; a commit-blocking defense exposes the pin. Either way, the quick attack forces the opposing block into a tradeoff.
- **Compounds with slide and pipe.** The quick establishes that the middle is a threat; the slide and pipe then attack on the margins the block vacates.

## Weaknesses
- **Demands near-perfect passing.** Out-of-system, the quick attack disappears — the setter has to bail to a high ball [[offense-high-ball]] or a slower pin set. Teams whose pass-in-system rate is below roughly 60–65% cannot run a quick attack as a reliable weapon.
- **Setter-middle chemistry is expensive.** Timing lives in hundreds of in-system reps with a specific setter-middle pairing. In a [[offense-6-2]] with two setters, each middle halves her reps with any given setter — the hybrid-6-2 setter-middle pairing is the explicit response to this [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle].
- **High-error profile when mistimed.** A mistimed 1 is a broken play — the middle is in the air with nothing to hit, or hits a stationary ball above the net for a trivial block. The error rate on quick attacks is structurally higher than on high balls.
- **Not appropriate below roughly HS JV.** At 14U and below, the timing demands usually exceed the developmental window. Most 14U programs install only a slow-tempo 31 (sometimes a "2" or a "shoot") rather than a true 1 [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules is upstream for age-appropriate principles].
- **Exhausts the middle.** Running quick attacks on every in-system pass is physically demanding. Middles in a quick-heavy offense need deeper jump-training and recovery planning.

## When to use / when not to use
**Use when:**
- Your passing is reliable enough to keep the setter in-system consistently.
- Your middle can approach, jump, and attack on the setter's tempo — meaning her footwork and timing are drilled in, not hoped for.
- Your opposing block is reading or swing-blocking in a way a quick attack can exploit.
- You are at HS varsity or above and need to keep your pin attackers from being over-blocked.

**Do not use when:**
- Your pass is not in-system — run [[offense-high-ball]] instead.
- Your middle-setter chemistry is not drilled — forcing a 1 with an untrained pairing produces broken plays at a rate that outweighs the scoring benefit.
- You are at 12U or early 14U and the timing demand is developmentally beyond your team.
- Your middle is fatigued or injured — the one-leg and fast-arm-swing loads are non-trivial, and pushing quicks on a tired middle produces injury risk as well as error risk.

## Alternatives and adjustments
- **[[offense-slide]]** — the quick attack's cousin; one-leg middle attack behind the setter. Feature when you want to attack a weaker left-side pin blocker or when the opposing MB is over-committing to 1s and 31s.
- **[[offense-pipe]]** — back-row OH attack. A quick-attack-only offense runs out of options when the middle is scouted or over-blocked; pipe creates a fourth attacker.
- **[[offense-high-ball]]** — the out-of-system bailout. Always your default when the pass is broken.
- **Slower middle tempo (a "2" or "slow 31")** — training-wheels versions of a 1 / 31 for younger middles or new setter-middle pairings. Slower ball trajectory, more forgiving timing, lower ceiling.
- **Combos (X, tandem)** — layer a trailing attacker behind the quick to punish an opposing MB who commits. Requires another front-row attacker's approach and timing to align with the middle's.
- **Setter-middle pairing drills** — the hybrid-6-2 setter-middle pairing drill [aoc-2022-hybrid-6-2-setter-middle] is the canonical practice structure for locking setter-middle timing. See also [[offense-4-2]]'s slide-featured variant for a setter-as-attacker wrinkle.
