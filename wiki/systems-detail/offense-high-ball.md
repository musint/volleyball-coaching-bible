# High-Ball (out-of-system / bail-to-outside)

## Overview
The high-ball is the default out-of-system offense: when the pass is broken (off the net, off the setter's target, or into the back row), the setter delivers a high set to an outside hitter (typically the OH on the left pin) who attacks against a formed double block with hitter coverage backing up the swing. The high-ball is not glamorous — it is deliberately simple, deliberately predictable, and universally available. But it is the offense that every team runs most often, because every team is out-of-system on a meaningful fraction of possessions, and the high-ball is the play that keeps the rally alive under those conditions. The tradeoff is attack efficiency: a high ball against a formed double block converts at roughly half the rate of a quick-attack in-system swing. Teams win at the top level by pushing in-system rate up (better passing) AND by raising out-of-system efficiency (better high-ball attacking). Both matter. Liskevych's ASICS hitting series covers the fundamentals of the OH's high-ball swing at length [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-hitting].

## Personnel requirements
- **An OH who can hit against a formed block.** This is the high-ball's chemistry. The OH must be able to see the block, shape her swing to the line / seam / crosscourt depending on the block's formation, and survive contact — often with her swing arm being touched by the block. Hitting against a block is a distinct skill from hitting against a broken block, and needs its own rep load.
- **A setter who can deliver a hittable high ball from anywhere.** In-system, the setter sets from target; out-of-system, she's passing-set from position 5, position 1, or deeper. The high ball she produces has to land consistently at the antenna, high enough for the OH to attack at height, and repeatable even from a bump-set.
- **A passer who at least keeps the ball on the team's side of the net.** The pass doesn't have to be perfect for a high ball — but it has to be pass-able, not a shank over the net.
- **Hitter coverage.** High-ball attacks are the plays most likely to be blocked back into your court. The team has to rotate into a hitter-coverage formation behind the attacker so tips and blocks-back are played up rather than given away as dead-ball kills.
- **An OH who can take the full swing load.** In a high-ball-heavy offense, one OH may take 15–20+ swings per set. Physical durability, arm care, and approach repetition are all implicated.

Russ Rose's offensive-systems clinic covers the reality of how much of the competitive game runs through out-of-system plays [aoc-2018-rose-offensive-systems]. Kiraly's *Championship Volleyball* frames OH attacking against a block as one of the central skills in the sport, alongside the passing platform and the setter hand-set [kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball].

## Diagram or text description of alignment
The high-ball sits on top of any base system. The alignment that matters is the setter's set and the attacker's approach.

**Setter's set:** high and wide to the left antenna. "High" means enough air time that the OH can get from her back-row defensive position (or her front-row attack reset) to her approach and jump. "Wide" means close to the antenna so the OH can attack at an angle that makes the block honest. A good bump-set high ball drops 2–3 feet inside the antenna at a height of roughly 12–15 feet above the net.

**OH's approach:** a standard full approach (3 or 4 step — the last two steps are the acceleration per Schmidt [aoc-2024-attack-approach]). The OH contacts the ball at the top of her jump, on or just inside the antenna, facing the middle of the court. Arm-swing mechanics follow the Mattox keywords: Sight 90, Elbow Lead, Lift and Whip [aoc-2021-teach-spiking]. Liskevych's hitting series emphasizes contact point and shot selection as part of the OH's full kit [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-hitting].

**Shot selection vs. a formed block:** the OH has four standard shots — the line (down the sideline inside the antenna), the crosscourt (angled into position 5 or deeper), the high hands (attacking the top of the block for a kill or a touch-out-of-bounds), and the tip or roll shot (placed into the seam or behind the block). John Dunning's hitter-type frame applies: each OH has a dominant style and should know her strengths and gaps [aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types].

**Hitter coverage:** the three closest teammates collapse into a low, compact triangle behind the attacker — ready to play a tip, a block-back, or a deflection. Standard covers are MB/S in a close low arc and the opposite OH deeper.

## Strengths
- **Universally available.** Works from any pass, any rotation, any system, any level.
- **Simple to install.** The OH's approach, the setter's bump-set, and hitter coverage are all fundamental skills that every team drills anyway. No audibles, no pairings, no chemistry investment beyond the basics.
- **Keeps rallies alive.** A broken pass doesn't end the point — the offense still runs, just at lower efficiency. This is the high-ball's core value proposition: it keeps the team in the point.
- **Trainable.** OHs who practice against a block in live 6v6 reps will improve at hitting against a formed block. The skill compounds over a season.
- **Low-error floor.** A high ball hit out or into the net is an error, but the rate is structurally lower than on a quick or slide because the attacker has more time to see, adjust, and contact the ball.
- **Works at every level.** From 14U through professional, the high-ball is the offense that appears in every rally book.

## Weaknesses
- **Low efficiency vs. formed block.** At HS varsity and above, a double block against a single OH attacker wins more often than the attacker does. Kills per attempt drop from the in-system quick-attack range (roughly .350+ for top teams) to the out-of-system range (roughly .150–.200 is common).
- **Predictable.** Every team knows a broken pass means a high ball to the outside. Scouting an OH's tendencies — her line shot, her crosscourt angle — lets the opposing defense anchor their dig positions.
- **Exhausts the OH.** An OH taking 40+ swings a match pays a physical cost. Rotate sets, use the opposite as an alternative bail-to target, and monitor arm load.
- **Easy to block for a strong HS+ block.** Teams with a tall and well-coached block can treat the high-ball as a predictable attack angle and win matches against otherwise-competitive opponents who can't raise their in-system rate.
- **Coverage failures give away free points.** Hitter coverage that doesn't rotate produces dead-ball kills off the block; this is a common way high-balls become free points for the other team.
- **Hides over-reliance on OH #1.** Teams that funnel every out-of-system ball to the same OH eventually produce a scouting target and a durability problem.

## When to use / when not to use
**Use when:**
- Your pass is off-target or broken and the quick attack / slide / pipe are not available.
- You are stabilizing a young team's offense (14U, HS JV) where the simplicity of "bail high to outside" reduces decision load.
- Your OH is your most reliable attacker and the percentage play is to feed her the swing.
- You need to burn clock or stabilize momentum — a controlled high-ball possession is a steady possession.

**Do not use when:**
- Your pass is in-system. Running a high-ball off a perfect pass is leaving in-system attack value on the table — call a [[offense-quick]], [[offense-slide]], or [[offense-pipe]] instead.
- Your OH is fatigued, injured, or being over-blocked. Rotate the bail target — set the opposite, set a middle for a slower 2-tempo, or use a back-row bail.
- The opposing block is defending the pin effectively. Change the attack angle — pipe, slide, back-row swing, or a setter dump.

Pin sets are generally safer than middle sets for out-of-system plays — the tempo demands on a middle set require a quality of set the out-of-system setter often can't produce. This principle appears consistently in modern out-of-system coaching.

## Alternatives and adjustments
- **[[offense-quick]]** — the in-system alternative; uses a middle attack when the pass is on-target.
- **[[offense-slide]]** — an angled middle attack that can still work on semi-in-system passes with a drilled setter-middle pairing.
- **[[offense-pipe]]** — back-row OH attack; an option on clean out-of-system balls where the OH is front-row-blocked or fatigued.
- **Bail to the opposite.** Instead of always setting the OH, occasionally set the right-side for a high-ball attack from position 2. Changes the block's read.
- **Setter dump / second-ball attack.** On a semi-out-of-system ball where the opposing block is late to set up, the setter can attack the second ball directly — Mattox's slide-featured 4-2 builds this into the base system [aoc-2022-4-2-system reference applies upstream].
- **Hitter-coverage drills.** Drill coverage formations explicitly, not just in scrimmage. A team that reliably covers its hitters converts blocks-back into second-chance rallies.
- **Out-of-system shot-selection work.** Train the OH to pick the line, the seam, and the tool-the-block shots deliberately against different block formations. Dunning's hitter-type self-awareness frame applies — each OH should know her strongest high-ball shots [aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types].
