# Perimeter Defense

## Overview
Perimeter defense positions all five non-blocking defenders along the edges of the court — the deep corners, the sidelines, and the back third — so the "ring" of the fishbowl is covered by bodies. The block handles the middle of the court, and diggers stay out of the block's shadow. The structure assumes the attacker is most likely to hit a hard ball that flies toward a sideline or to the deep corners, and it prioritizes giving every hard-driven ball a body to read before it lands. Perimeter is the simplest read-defense base to teach because each defender has a landmark (the sideline, the end-line, the attack line) rather than a moving responsibility.

Because the scheme asks defenders to start wide and deep, it concedes the short middle and the area immediately behind the block. Tips, off-speed roll shots, and short-angle cuts drop into vacant floor unless defenders break cleanly toward them at the contact read. The team's blocking plan has to account for this: perimeter works when the block is big enough to stuff or slow hard middle-court attacks so the diggers' job is truly to chase sidelines and deep corners, not to recover from a block that leaks everywhere. At the 14U level coaches often pair perimeter with a "wing defenders at the 10-foot line in base" adjustment so the defense can still touch the tips that young teams live on, then release to perimeter positions on the attack read [aoc-2026-team-defense-reading].

## Personnel requirements
No specialized personnel. Any six can run perimeter; the scheme's simplicity is a feature. That said, the scheme rewards:
- Floor defenders with platform-to-the-target discipline — the angle digs are long and get passed up to the setter from 25 feet.
- A libero who reads the hitter's shoulder and approach angle so the deepest dig starts on balance.
- Blockers who can press the middle of the court and seal the seam so hard middle-court hits become the exception rather than the rule.

Perimeter is often the first team-defense scheme a 14U squad learns because the starting positions are sticky — they are real landmarks on the court, not floating zones.

## Alignment
With a left-side attack (opponent's OH on our right-front block):
- Our LF and MF block the OH; our RF drops off the net to cover the sharp cross tip or shoot a short dig along the 10-foot line.
- Our LB starts deep in zone 5 (left-back corner) on the line to take the hard line shot.
- Our MB (libero in most lineups) plays deep zone 6, aligned with the block's seam, covering the deep cross and long tools off the block.
- Our RB plays zone 1 at the sideline, ready for the high-angle cross that comes over the block.
- Block height and seam integrity define how deep the perimeter can afford to sit. Against a bigger block the diggers can cheat deeper; against a smaller one the wings pull up slightly.

Mirror the layout for right-side attacks and middle attacks: the block forms in the zone of the set, and the remaining four defenders hold the perimeter landmarks, covering line, deep middle, cross, and the short-angle in front of the off-blocker.

## Strengths
- Fastest base to teach: landmarks, not responsibilities. A new 14U defender can memorize "sideline three feet off the line" and hold their spot.
- Strong against teams that drive balls hard into the back half of the court — the default shape of many power-hitting opponents [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-defense].
- Because diggers start out of the block's shadow, the scheme forces blockers to earn the middle and clarifies responsibility: hard middle = block, everything else = perimeter.
- Pairs cleanly with the reading sequence taught across defense: ball, setter, ball, hitter — with defenders anchored on the perimeter, their eyes and movement are the variable, not their feet [aoc-2015-liskevych-mccutcheon-defense-blocking].

## Weaknesses
- Short tips, roll shots, and soft cuts land in the vacated middle. A tip-happy opponent will exploit the gap in front of the block until the off-blocker and back-row wings pinch inward on the read.
- The two wing diggers (LB and RB) cover a lot of ground — a ball hit between them and the off-blocker requires a hard read-and-move off the contact.
- The scheme depends on the block to be present in the attack zone; a late or split block exposes the middle of the court and turns every cross hit into a point.
- At 14U the "perimeter as taught at HS+" starting positions are usually too deep: the coach has to pull wings up to the 10-foot line in base or the team will bleed tips [aoc-2026-team-defense-reading].

## When to use / when not to use
Use perimeter when:
- The opponent drives hard and deep more than they tip.
- You are in the early weeks of teaching team defense and need a base that sticks.
- Your block is intact and centered on the attack zone — the perimeter relies on the block owning the middle.
- Your roster is a young or new-to-team group; perimeter is the cheapest scheme to install.

Avoid perimeter (or adjust out of it) when:
- The opponent's attack leans on tips, cuts, and short angles. Those balls are free points against a standard perimeter.
- Your block consistently gets split or beaten, exposing the middle.
- You face a three-hitter rotation with a fast tempo — rotation-based or read-based variants give you more coverage where the ball actually goes.

## Alternatives and adjustments
- Pinch the wings to the 10-foot line in base, release to perimeter on the attack read — the standard 14U adjustment so defenders can still touch tips. Halaz's 14U framework treats this as the default starting configuration [aoc-2026-team-defense-reading].
- Shift to [[defense-rotation]] when you want defenders to rotate toward the ball on the read, trading the clean landmarks of perimeter for more adaptive coverage.
- Shift to [[defense-middle-back]] when your libero is elite and your block can own the seam — middle-back buys back some coverage in the short roll-shot zone.
- Layer reading training underneath any scheme choice; perimeter positions are empty without trained eyes on the setter and the hitter [aoc-2017-liskevych-asics-defense].
