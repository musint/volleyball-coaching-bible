# Pipe (back-row OH attack)

## Overview
The pipe is a back-row attack by a back-row outside hitter (usually the OH who has rotated to position 6 — middle back) attacking a set delivered to the middle of the court behind the 3-meter line. Technically, the back-row attack family includes three sets by location: A (deep left, behind position 5), pipe (middle-back, behind position 6), and D (deep right, behind position 1) — but "pipe" in common usage refers specifically to the middle-back attack, and that is what this page covers. The pipe creates a fourth attacker without needing a substitution: the OH who was the primary pin threat in her front-row rotations is now a primary back-row threat from a different launch point. At HS varsity and above, the pipe is the standard answer to the [[offense-5-1]]'s setter-front-row rotations (where the offense would otherwise have only two front-row attackers) and to teams that over-read the pins. Below HS, it is uncommon — the jump-from-behind-the-3-meter-line demand requires jump training and approach mechanics that are usually not installed at 14U. Russ Rose's clinic on back-row skill development treats back-row play as foundational, including the attacking component [aoc-2018-rose-back-row-skills].

## Personnel requirements
- **An OH capable of attacking from the back row.** Physical requirement: a vertical jump enough to attack downward from behind the 3m line — roughly 9'0"–9'6" touch is the competitive HS varsity threshold, with legs and landing mechanics drilled enough to survive the workload.
- **A setter who can deliver a back-row set from any in-system pass.** A pipe set has a slightly different trajectory than a high OH set — lower and further off the net — and the setter has to produce it consistently in-system.
- **A passer who keeps the ball in-system.** Out-of-system, the pipe disappears; the setter bails to a pin.
- **Mature approach mechanics.** The OH must approach from behind the 3m line, time her jump so her takeoff is behind the line (the penetration line — an infraction if she takes off on or in front of it), and contact the ball at height. Tod Mattox's 4-2 piece discusses "Bic" (essentially a back-row attack variant) specifically as a way to remove congestion from the middle [aoc-2022-4-2-system]. The approach-tempo and arm-swing principles from Schmidt [aoc-2024-attack-approach] and Mattox's 2021 spiking guide [aoc-2021-teach-spiking] both apply, with the additional constraint that the takeoff must be behind the line.
- **Roster that can train it.** Pipe is not a free addition — it adds rep load, jump-training volume, and coaching attention. Teams that cannot absorb that training cost will run an empty pipe call.

John Dunning's hitter-type framing applies: not every OH is a pipe hitter by physical profile, and forcing a pipe onto an OH who isn't built for it wastes the call [aoc-2018-dunning-hitter-types].

## Diagram or text description of alignment
The pipe is a layered attack, not a base alignment — it sits on top of a [[offense-5-1]] or [[offense-6-2]] base. The back-row OH starts in position 6 (middle back) or position 5 (left back, depending on rotation) at the moment of serve, then transitions to her back-row approach lane after the first ball is played.

**Approach path (rotation where OH is in position 6):**
1. OH starts in position 6 (middle back) at the serve.
2. After the pass, she transitions roughly 3–5 steps backward and to the left/center, positioning her launch point behind the 3m line in the middle-back zone.
3. As the setter contacts the ball, she begins her approach toward her takeoff spot.
4. Takeoff is behind the 3m line; contact is ideally at the top of her jump, with the ball arriving from the setter on a trajectory that drops into her attack window roughly 8–10 feet above the net, mid-court.

**Back-row attack nomenclature:**
- **A** (or "A-ball"): back-row attack deep left, behind position 5.
- **Pipe** (or "B" in some systems): back-row attack middle, behind position 6. The default "pipe" referred to in this page.
- **D** (or "C" in some systems): back-row attack deep right, behind position 1 — typically by a back-row opposite.

The pipe is most often run by the OH in the rotation where the setter is front row and only two front-row attackers are on the pins. The pipe then becomes a third attacker (fourth counting the middle's quick option), which restores the offense's attacker count.

## Strengths
- **Creates a fourth attacker without a sub.** This is the structural win. A 5-1 offense with a front-row setter has two pin attackers; add a pipe and you have three. Add a middle quick and you have four.
- **Breaks read-blocking.** A read-blocking middle commits to the front-row hitters; the pipe attacks a zone she is not covering. If the read-blocker honors the pipe, the pin attackers face a single blocker.
- **Attacks from a different angle.** Pipe sets come in steeper trajectories than pin sets; the opposing MB has to protect a different zone with different footwork.
- **Extends offense against tall opposing blocks.** When the opposing front-row block is physically superior to your OH on pins, the pipe lets your OH attack from an angle the block can't fully close.
- **Uses roster talent without extra rotation substitution.** The OH was already on the court as a back-row defender; adding the pipe uses her on both sides of the ball.
- **Pairs with slide and quick.** Once installed, the combination of quick ([[offense-quick]]) + slide ([[offense-slide]]) + pipe makes an offense genuinely four-attacker, which is the modern HS+ standard.

## Weaknesses
- **Jump-and-timing demand.** Takeoff behind the 3m line requires more leg strength than an in-front-of-the-line OH attack; contact at height requires a higher vertical. Young or under-trained OHs will under-contact the ball or land over the line (a foot fault).
- **Approach lane conflicts.** A back-row OH approaching to pipe has to navigate the path the setter and MB are also using. Timing collisions produce broken plays.
- **Requires in-system passing.** Out-of-system, the pipe disappears — the setter bails to an outside pin [[offense-high-ball]].
- **Physically taxing.** Full pipe reps every possession cost the OH significant energy; HS+ teams usually pick their rotations to feature the pipe rather than running it every ball.
- **Safe to block if scouted.** If the opposing block has film on a specific OH's pipe tendencies (approach timing, contact zone, preferred direction), they can set up to defend it without giving up the pins. The pipe's value lives in its unpredictability.
- **Not appropriate below HS.** At 14U and below, the takeoff-behind-the-line rule is usually cleared by one or two players on the roster at best; installing the pipe is expensive relative to its scoring payoff until the team matures.

## When to use / when not to use
**Use when:**
- Your pass is in-system.
- You have an OH with the jump, approach, and timing to attack effectively from the back row.
- The opposing block is read-blocking or over-reading the pins and middle — the pipe attacks the gap they are leaving.
- You are in a rotation where your offense has only two front-row attackers (e.g., the setter-front-row rotations in a [[offense-5-1]]), and you need a third.

**Do not use when:**
- The pass is off-net or broken — run [[offense-high-ball]].
- Your OH's vertical or approach is not yet at the back-row attack threshold. Forcing a pipe with an under-ready hitter costs reps the team cannot afford.
- You are at 12U or early 14U. At this level the back-row attack is a skill to introduce in practice (down-ball from back row, back-row approach tempo) rather than a game-plan weapon.
- The opposing block is commit-blocking the middle hard; run a [[offense-slide]] or push the middle-quick combo instead.

## Alternatives and adjustments
- **[[offense-quick]]** — front-row middle attack; the pipe's natural companion. Quicks pull the middle blocker; the pipe attacks the space she vacates.
- **[[offense-slide]]** — middle transition behind the setter on one leg. An alternative fourth-attacker tool when the pipe is not available (e.g., the OH is not a pipe threat but the middle has a slide).
- **[[offense-high-ball]]** — out-of-system bailout. Always the default when the pass is broken.
- **Back-row attack by rotation** — run the pipe only in specific rotations where you need the fourth attacker (typically setter-front-row rotations in a 5-1). Running pipe in every rotation is high-cost for a low-leverage scoring gain.
- **A and D attacks** — the deep-left and deep-right back-row attacks. Less common than the pipe because the deeper set is harder to hit at height, but useful as an occasional change-up.
- **"Bic" (back-row quick)** — a quick-tempo back-row attack; Tod Mattox mentions the Bic as a way to remove congestion from the middle in a modern 4-2 [aoc-2022-4-2-system]. Essentially a faster-tempo pipe; demands very high setter-OH timing.
- **Back-row attack drill anchors** — Rose's back-row skills clinic is the closest cited anchor for structured back-row attack training [aoc-2018-rose-back-row-skills]. Pair with the approach-tempo work from Schmidt [aoc-2024-attack-approach] to install the takeoff-timing piece.
