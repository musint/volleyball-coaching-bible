# Hiring Assistant Coaches

## Purpose

Hire assistant coaches the way a competitive juniors club should: by intentional design, with on-court evidence, and with credentialing complete before any athlete contact. Assistant hiring is the highest-leverage operational decision a director makes — assistants spend more contact hours with athletes than the head coach does on a typical 14U–18U travel team, and parents read coaching staff quality faster than any marketing collateral. This page covers the full hiring cycle: role definition, sourcing, screening, interview, on-court evaluation, references, mandatory USAV credentialing, and onboarding.

## Process

**1. Write the role description before sourcing.** Define the team(s) the assistant supports, expected weekly hours (practices, tournaments, off-season), age group, primary teaching responsibilities (e.g., back-row defense, hitter footwork, scoreboard), travel expectations, and compensation range. Hire for complementary strengths to the head coach, not duplicative ones — "a carbon copy of you doesn't add anything to the equation" [aoc-2024-kiraly-assistant-coach]. If the head coach is a setter-development specialist, prioritize candidates strong in serve-receive and defensive systems.

**2. Source through three channels — not one.** Personal networks are fast but narrow the candidate pool and bias toward demographic homogeneity. Always supplement with: (a) regional USAV coach directories, (b) AVCA / college program assistant pipelines, (c) public job posting (club website + region listserv). Quality coaches are foundational and budget should support both their continued development and competitive recruiting [aoc-2014-denney-10-steps-start-club].

**3. Screen on paper.** Before scheduling interviews, verify: USAV membership status, IMPACT completion (or willingness to complete), SafeSport certification status, current background-check status, prior coaching age groups, and tournament experience. Eliminate any candidate not willing to complete or maintain credentialing — this is non-negotiable per USAV requirements [usav-2026-coach-education].

**4. Interview with structure.** Use a fixed question bank (see Templates) so candidates are compared on the same dimensions. Cover: coaching philosophy, conflict scenarios with parents and athletes, technical depth (have them teach a skill cold, e.g., "walk me through how you'd correct a passer who is dropping their right shoulder"), and self-development habits.

**5. Run an on-court evaluation.** Skipping this is the single most common hiring failure. Bring the finalist to a live practice. Have them run a 15–20 minute block — a warm-up segment, a skill block, and a transition into a competitive drill. Evaluate: voice projection, demonstration quality, ability to give a single high-leverage cue per rep, time management, ability to read the room when a drill isn't working, and whether athletes' faces light up or close down.

**6. Check references — and call, don't email.** Two coaching references and one non-coaching (parent or athlete from a prior season). Ask: what they would have improved about this coach, how they handled mid-season conflict, and whether the reference would hire them again.

**7. Verify USAV credentialing in writing before first contact.** Per USAV, IMPACT, SafeSport, and a background check are required for all member coaches, and SafeSport must be completed before any interaction with athletes [usav-2026-coach-education]. Get screenshots of completion in SportsEngine. Do not let an uncredentialed assistant on the floor for any reason — not a "tryout shadow," not a "guest demo." This is both an athlete-safety requirement and an insurance/liability requirement.

**8. Onboard deliberately.** Walk new assistants through: the team handbook, club code of conduct, parent communication norms, SafeSport reporting protocol, emergency action plan, and the head coach's expectations for in-practice authority and post-practice debriefs. Pat Madia's "you don't coach volleyballs, you coach people" framing is the cultural anchor for new staff [usav-2026-so-you-want-to-be-a-volleyball-coach]. Schedule a 30-day check-in.

## Templates

**Role description**
> [Club Name] is hiring an assistant coach for our [age group] [team tier] team. Season runs [dates]. Practices are [days/times] at [venue]; tournaments are approximately [N] weekends including [travel destinations]. Primary responsibilities: [3–4 bullets — e.g., lead serve-receive block, manage scoresheet/lineup, assist with tournament logistics]. Required: USAV membership, IMPACT certification, SafeSport, current background check. Compensation: $[range] per season. Send résumé and a 60-second video introducing yourself to [email] by [date].

**Interview question bank**
- Walk me through your coaching philosophy in two minutes.
- Teach me one technical correction — pick a skill, demonstrate, give me the cue.
- A parent emails you angry about playing time. Read the email aloud, then tell me what you do.
- Describe a moment last season you handled poorly. What would you do differently?
- What's the last coaching book, podcast, or clinic you consumed, and what did you change?
- How do you complement what you've heard about our head coach's strengths?

**Offer letter (template)**
> [Date]. Dear [Name], We are pleased to offer you the position of Assistant Coach for [Club]'s [team] for the [season] season. Compensation: $[amount], paid [schedule]. Start date: [date]. Required prior to first practice: current USAV membership, IMPACT, SafeSport, and background-check completion documented in SportsEngine. This offer is contingent on those items remaining current throughout the season. Please sign and return by [date]. Welcome to [Club]. — [Director name]

## Common pitfalls

- **Skipping the on-court evaluation.** Résumés and interviews systematically over-rate confident talkers and under-rate quiet teachers. The floor reveals which is which.
- **Not verifying SafeSport before first contact.** Any athlete interaction by an uncredentialed coach is a SafeSport and insurance violation, full stop [usav-2026-coach-education].
- **Over-relying on personal networks.** Faster, cheaper, and narrower than open posting. It also reproduces existing staff demographics and blind spots.
- **Hiring a duplicate of the head coach.** If both coaches teach the same skill the same way, the staff has redundancy where it should have coverage [aoc-2024-kiraly-assistant-coach].
- **No written role description.** Without one, expectations drift and first-conflict resolution defaults to "the head coach decides" — corrosive to assistant retention.
