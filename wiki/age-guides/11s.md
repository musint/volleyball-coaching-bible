# Age Guide: 11s

## Overview & development phase

The 11U athlete is at the **introduction phase transitioning toward fundamentals** — past the movement-discovery window of ages 3–9 [usav-2026-youth-volleyball-tips], not yet at the durable skill-acquisition stage where 12U technique milestones become non-negotiable [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals]. For most clubs 11U is the bridge year: a player who was only loosely organized at [[10s]] starts forming a recognizable platform, attempts an overhand serve for the first time, and meets a real 6v6 entry-level offense. Calendar-age-wise the 11U athlete is typically 10–11 by USAV calendar age, pre-pubertal through early puberty.

Two USAV instruments shape this phase. **Simplified Youth Rules** still nominally cover ages 3–11 — the 11U athlete is in USAV's last codified small-sided window, where 2v2 / 3v3 / 4v4 with 7-foot nets, lightweight youth balls, and unlimited substitution remain the official defaults [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]. **Growing Kids' Volleyball** carries the 12U-and-under philosophy that engagement, pair-based practice, and over-the-net-first principles drive retention [usav-2026-growing-kids-volleyball]. A competitive 11U program straddles both: USAV's small-sided defaults remain the practice-design baseline, while **6v6 elements are introduced progressively** so a player can graduate to [[12s]] with rotations recognized, an overhand serve rehearsed, and a real platform under construction.

The "introduction" label means: technique and systems are *introduced*, not mastered. Ingrained mechanical errors at 11U are costly to retrain later, so the coach's job is to install good shapes (platform, hand triangle, approach footwork) before high-volume reps cement bad ones [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals]. The recency-leaning operating model on this wiki [[age-lens-14u]] applies in compressed form: modern coaching language (Kiraly's 4 keys, Munciana's pursuit-and-closure framing, Mattox's accelerate-the-last-two-steps) lands more cleanly on a beginner than legacy phrasing. The Munciana club-wide-alignment principle — an 11U player already speaks the same vocabulary an 18U player speaks [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide] — implies serious programs install the *right* cues at 11U, not the *easy* ones.

## Skill milestones

### Passing

By season end, an 11U athlete should demonstrate a recognizable forearm platform on every contact, even off-center: thumbs together, wrists straight, arms slightly off the chest, knees bent, shoulders over the front foot [aoc-2024-kids-passing-fundamentals]. Kiraly's 4 keys frame the cognitive load at age: **stopped and balanced before contact, thumbs together, set the angle with the platform, simple one-way motion** [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]. "Stopped and balanced" is the highest-yield 11U focus because beginners reach instead of move; Munciana's drill library opens with a no-ball pursuit shuffle for exactly this reason [munciana-2022-camp-drills].

Milestones: pass a tossed or slow-served ball to setter target with consistent arm shape; "shuffle, don't reach" footwork on balls outside the body; hold the platform finish a beat after contact; recognize overhead-pass territory vs. forearm territory (concept only). See [[passing-cues]] and [[passing-forearm]].

### Setting

11U setting is a **catch-and-set progression** for most athletes, with a small number of taller / coordinated kids ready for live setting by season's end [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals]. The hand shape installed here is the same one elite setters use: triangle above the forehead, thumbs not touching, elbows slightly forward, feet shoulder-width with right foot forward, knees bent [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals]. Modern AOC coaching emphasizes the **hand-hinge** (wrist cocks back and snaps forward as legs extend) as the day-one setter skill [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge].

Milestones: catch-and-set 10 reps with hand triangle and no thumb-collision; for setter-track candidates, live-set a tossed ball 5–10 feet above the net to target — Nelson's "ready to transition from passing to setting" criterion [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals]; left-to-right shuffle footwork; legs-extend-up power source, not "all wrist." Most 11U athletes will not be cleanly live-setting 5–10 feet by season end; ingraining the right hand shape in catch-and-set reps counts. See [[setting-cues]] and [[setting-hands]].

### Hitting

11U hitting follows Nelson's three-stage progression: down-ball no-jump, then 2-step modified approach, then 3-step introduction late season for advanced athletes [aoc-2024-kids-attacking-fundamentals]. The 2-step "Flamingo" stance is the workhorse [aoc-2024-kids-attacking-fundamentals]. Schmidt's tempo cue applies: **start slow, accelerate the last two steps** [aoc-2024-attack-approach].

Milestones: down-ball arm swing from puppet position (elbow up, hand high, contact in front); 2-step approach with isolated jump on accurate toss; 3-step approach attempted by season's end where coordination supports it; contact at peak of jump (concept, not consistent execution). See [[hitting-cues]], [[hitting-approach]], and [[hitting-arm-swing]].

### Blocking

Single-blocker concept under USAV simplified rules [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]. Skill focus: **hand-on-the-net ready position and a controlled stationary jump** — no swing-blocking footwork yet. Most 11U attacks are free balls and down-balls; blocking practice is more about **net awareness and not getting injured** than stopping attacks.

Milestones: hands above the net with thumbs up, fingers spread, balanced pre-jump posture; stationary 2-foot jump straight up without net contact; recognize when to block vs. back up to defense. See [[blocking-cues]], [[blocking-footwork]], and [[blocking-hand-position]].

### Serving

**11U is the year the overhand serve is introduced** — at 10U the underhand serve is the default and overhand is exploratory [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]; at 12U standing-float and standing-topspin should be the defaults [aoc-2024-kids-serving-fundamentals]; 11U is the bridge. Nelson's five-serve taxonomy frames the entry: the **static floater** is the workhorse, with topspin (mirrors the hitting swing) and Japanese floater (rewards wrist control) introduced as concepts only [aoc-2024-kids-serving-fundamentals]. Newkirk's "Deliver and Pop" — reach + drop + pop — is a clean entry sequence [aoc-2021-youth-15-drills].

Milestones: underhand serve consistently clears the net (10U baseline preserved); overhand serve attempted from developmentally-appropriate distance (2-3 feet inside the end-line for smaller athletes; back up over weeks); toss-first emphasis (consistent toss precedes any swing coaching); aggressive intent — **serve at a target**, not just over the net [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. See [[serving-cues]] and [[serving-float]].

### Defense

11U defense is **read-and-pursue** more than platform precision. Munciana's Three P's — **get back, get low, get a touch** — frame the defensive ready position and pursuit decision [munciana-2022-camp-drills]. Platform mechanics transfer from passing; the new content is the **read**. Kiraly's framing that "reading is not separate from skill; it is part of every skill" applies in compressed form [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill] — embed reading questions ("where is the setter looking?") into every defensive rep rather than running isolated reading drills.

Milestones: ready position (knees bent, weight forward, hands in front); pursue with a recovery touch (not a clean pass — a touch that keeps the rally alive); recognize free balls vs. attacks (concept). See [[defense-cues]], [[defense-platform]], [[defense-reading]].

### Transition

A single concept at 11U: **after a contact, get back to your ready spot**. Block-to-attack and defense-to-attack patterns are not yet executable. The unit-level transition skill is **pass-set-hit** as a shape — passer to setter, setter to hitter, hitter swings — with every player attempting all three contacts in 6v6 cooperative play.

Milestones: recover to ready position after every contact; pass-set-hit shape executed cooperatively (low success rate is fine; the shape is the point); urgency awareness (concept). See [[transition-cues]].

## Systems repertoire

**Offense.** [[offense-6-6]] (every player rotates through every position) remains the developmentally-correct practice default for 11U athletes still figuring out where they belong [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]. **Simple [[offense-4-2]] is the entry-level competitive offense** — front-row setter in all six rotations, two designated setters opposite each other, three front-row attackers [aoc-2022-4-2-system]. Mattox's framing that the 4-2 is "easier for less experienced players to learn than 5-1 or 6-2" applies most strongly at 11U: the front-row setter is always the same, the two setters are interchangeable, rotation tracking is minimal [aoc-2022-4-2-system]. The Slide and back-row Bic that Mattox cites as 4-2 weapons at HS+ levels are **out of scope at 11U** — timing too tight, approach too complex. The 11U 4-2 is the *simple* 4-2: setter sets, front-row OH or middle hits. **Ceiling: simple 4-2 entry by season's end.** [[offense-6-2]] and [[offense-5-1]] are explicitly out of scope [aoc-2021-teach-rotations-beginners][aoc-2022-4-2-system].

**Serve receive.** A **3-passer or 4-passer** U or W shape with overlap rules taught conceptually [aoc-2021-teach-rotations-beginners] — front-row players ahead of back-row partners, back-row players behind their front-row partners, server "not on the court" [aoc-2021-teach-rotations-beginners].

**Defense.** [[defense-perimeter]] is the only viable shape: each athlete owns a zone, simple geometry, low reading load.

**Blocking.** Single-blocker only per USAV simplified rules [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]; [[commit-blocking]] as the conceptual framing.

**Positions.** No specialization at 11U. Some athletes trend toward setter, hitter, or libero profiles by season's end and the coach **notices** signals — but assigning a position is premature [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules][aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. Rotate every athlete through every position across the season.

## Athletic profile

11U is mostly pre-pubertal with some early-puberty athletes. Growth is uneven; coordination shifts underneath the player. Jump-training and plyometrics are a **CSCS conversation, not a coach conversation** — see [[physical]]. Priorities:

1. **Movement quality first.** Multi-directional ready-position footwork (shuffle, drop step, cross-over) drilled in warm-ups; jumping mechanics taught for safe landing more than vertical gain.
2. **Multi-sport encouragement.** Post et al. (2019) is unambiguous: high volleyball specialization at the youth level produces OR ~2.3 for overuse injury, year-round (>8 months/year) OR ~2.0, with 45.7% of youth volleyball athletes reporting prior overuse injury — highest among three sports studied [post-2019-specialization-overuse-volleyball]. **Multi-sport play at 11U is evidence-backed injury prevention, not philosophical preference.**
3. **Load management literacy for parents.** Most 11U overuse comes from accumulated practice load across sports; the coach's lever is communication.
4. **No max-effort jump training.** Plyometric-heavy conditioning is deferred to a CSCS.

## Mental & coachability markers

11U is the year a player starts processing feedback **with intent to apply it** rather than just nodding. Markers worth observing:

- **Self-correction within a rep cycle.** After feedback, does rep N+1 attempt the correction, or look identical to the prior one? The first is a coachability signal; the second is a "needs more reps" signal, not a deficit.
- **Tolerance for repetition.** Does the athlete accept that platform shape needs many reps, or push to "play games" before fundamentals are stable?
- **Asks questions vs. nods** — asking is a coachability strength at 11U.

USAV's stance on 3–11U is clear: **FeedForward** (encouraging reminders about *what to do next* rather than criticism of past errors) is the dominant feedback mode, and "Catch them doing it right" outweighs error-correction [usav-2026-simplified-youth-rules]. Cue-language evidence at this age is honest about its limits: a multi-site youth replication of the Wulf external-focus paradigm on jumping and sprinting found **no significant differences** between external, internal, analogy, and neutral-control cues — and *neutral control was actually superior to internal cueing on vertical jump* [moran-2023-verbal-coaching-cues-youth]. Implication: don't over-engineer cue selection. **Rep volume and consistent vocabulary outweigh cue-type optimization at this age**. Munciana's rep-based confidence principle — "reps equal confidence" — applies cleanly: anxious 11U athletes are usually under-repped, not under-talented [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].

## Promotion criteria

Promotion criteria specify what a player must demonstrate to graduate from 11U to [[12s]]. **The corresponding [[tryout-rubric-11s]] translates these criteria into a 1-5 scoring scale**, so the milestones below are the unifying spine of 11U evaluation. Each criterion is observable in a 60-90-minute tryout setting or across season-end practices.

**Passing — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Recognizable platform on every contact, even off-center balls (thumbs together, wrists straight, knees bent).
- Shuffle-not-reach on balls outside the body for at least 60% of trials in tryout-pace reps.
- Can pass a tossed or slow-served ball to the setter target with the platform shape intact for a majority of reps.

**Setting — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Hand-triangle shape above forehead with no thumb collision in catch-and-set reps.
- For setter-track candidates: live-set a tossed ball 5–10 feet above the net to a target [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals].
- Recognition of overhead-pass vs. forearm-pass territory.

**Hitting — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Down-ball arm swing from puppet position with elbow-up, contact-in-front shape.
- 2-step approach onto an accurate toss with isolated jump.
- Attempt at a 3-step approach (form not yet expected to be clean).

**Blocking — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Hands above the net in pre-jump posture without net contact.
- Recognition of when to block vs. when to back up.

**Serving — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Reliable underhand serve (the 10U baseline holds at 11U-promotion).
- **Overhand serve attempted at a developmentally appropriate distance, with successful contact in at least 30% of attempts.** This is the load-bearing 11U-specific milestone — the overhand attempt does not need to score, but it needs to exist as an attempted skill, not a hypothetical one.
- Toss consistency on the overhand attempt (same arc each time, ball coming down to a repeatable contact zone).

**Defense — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Ready position held during opponent's offensive contact.
- Touches a pursued ball with intent (recovery touch counts — does not need to be a clean dig).
- Recognizes free ball vs. attack at concept level.

**Transition — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Recovers to ready position after contact rather than admiring the ball.
- Participates in a pass-set-hit shape with teammates without freezing.

**Mental & coachability — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Attempts coach feedback on the next rep (self-correction signal).
- Tolerates repetition without protest.
- Engages with teammates positively during cooperative play.

**Systems — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Recognizes own position in a simple 4-2 lineup.
- Knows which way to rotate after a side-out.
- Understands "stay" vs. "switch" at concept level (overlap rules taught conceptually, not rigorously) [aoc-2021-teach-rotations-beginners].

**Athletic — promotion-ready demonstrates:**
- Multi-directional movement (shuffle, drop step) without falling or losing balance.
- Lands jumps with knees-over-toes and bent-knee absorption.

A player meeting most of these criteria is ready for [[12s]], where the platform must become stable, the standing-float serve must be reliable, basic 4-2 must be executable in real games, and overhead passing is added.

## Common coaching corrections

- **Reaching with the platform instead of moving the feet.** Cue: "Move your feet first, platform second" — Munciana's pursuit-shuffle drill addresses this directly [munciana-2022-camp-drills].
- **Setter thumbs colliding above forehead.** Cue: "Triangle window — see the ball through the window" [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals].
- **Hitter approaching too fast and out of control.** Cue: "Start slow, accelerate the last two steps" [aoc-2024-attack-approach].
- **Overhand server tossing inconsistently.** Drill the toss alone — 10 tosses, no swing, ball must land at the same spot each time [aoc-2024-kids-serving-fundamentals].
- **Players standing flat-footed on defense.** Cue from Munciana's Three P's: "Get low" before the ball comes [munciana-2022-camp-drills].
- **Reaching back to the net during blocking.** Hands above net, fingers spread, do not retreat — backing up creates the gap.
- **Server cleating the net or court.** Serve from a closer distance until contact is clean, then back up.

## Recommended drills

The 11U list inherits the 10U warm-up + cooperative ball-control core and adds **overhand-serve drills, simple shuttle-passing (2-line), and 2-step approach drills**. Full curated list at [[11s-drills]]; must-do core:

**Pursuit & footwork:** [[munciana-two-man-shuffle]], [[dynamic-warmup-volleyball]], [[jump-warmup]] (movement quality, not max-vertical).
**Passing:** [[butterfly-passing]] (shortened distance), [[two-line-passing]], [[shuttle-passing]], [[pepper]]. Lingenfelter's short-ball/long-ball warmup with 3 passers is age-appropriate [aoc-2021-lingenfelter-short-ball-long-ball].
**Setting:** [[target-setting]] (catch-and-set for most, live for ready candidates). Newkirk's toss-up → fizzles → four-sets progression is well-suited [aoc-2021-youth-15-drills].
**Hitting:** [[approach-and-swing]] with 2-step Flamingo [aoc-2024-kids-attacking-fundamentals]; down-ball reps from puppet position.
**Serving:** [[serve-targets]] with closer-distance progression, [[zone-serving]] as concept. Newkirk's "Deliver and Pop" works for the overhand introduction [aoc-2021-youth-15-drills].
**Mixed / cooperative:** [[cooperative-25-goal]] (Kessel-style net game) [usav-2026-growing-kids-volleyball], [[pass-set-hit]] in slow form, [[loser-becomes-the-net]] when nets are unavailable [usav-2026-youth-volleyball-tips].

Munciana's drill library is selectively appropriate at 11U: opening pursuit drills work cleanly, but 6v6 psychology games (Biggie Smalls, Prove It, Tug of War) are 13U+ material — cognitive pressure exceeds 11U capacity.

## Recommended cues

The 11U cue library is intentionally **small and consistent**. Per Moran et al. 2023, cue-type optimization is not robustly supported by youth data — consistent vocabulary repeated until the motor pattern is stable matters more [moran-2023-verbal-coaching-cues-youth].

- **Passing:** "Stopped and balanced" (Kiraly) [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]; "Thumbs together"; "Move your feet first, platform second"; "Quiet platform" (Munciana) [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. See [[passing-cues]].
- **Setting:** "Triangle window above the forehead"; "Legs make the set, hands shape it" [aoc-2024-kids-setting-fundamentals]; "Hand-hinge" for ready candidates [aoc-2024-setting-hand-hinge]. See [[setting-cues]].
- **Hitting:** "Start slow, accelerate the last two steps" (Schmidt) [aoc-2024-attack-approach]; "Elbow up, hand high"; "Contact in front." See [[hitting-cues]].
- **Blocking:** "Hands above the net, fingers spread"; "Don't back up." See [[blocking-cues]].
- **Serving:** "Toss to the same spot every time"; "Aim small, miss small" (Munciana) [munciana-2022-camp-drills]; "Reach up, drop, pop" [aoc-2021-youth-15-drills]. See [[serving-cues]].
- **Defense:** "Get back, get low, get a touch" (Munciana Three P's) [munciana-2022-camp-drills]; "Eyes up." See [[defense-cues]].
- **Transition:** "Where do you go next?"; "Recover to your spot." See [[transition-cues]].

The recency-leaning bias on this wiki points the cue library toward **modern AOC + Munciana** language as primary, with USAV CAP defaults as the developmental scaffold. Legacy cues ("snap your wrist" for hitting; isolated-platform passing language) are avoided at 11U because they install patterns that become anti-cues later.
