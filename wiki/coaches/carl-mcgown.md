# Carl McGown

## Overview

Carl McGown (1937–2016) was the United States' most influential evangelist for a motor-learning-grounded approach to volleyball instruction — a pioneering figure who taught generations of American coaches that skill acquisition in volleyball is an empirical question answerable with sport science rather than an opinion [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering] [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. He was head coach at [[brigham-young-university]] for thirteen seasons (two NCAA men's titles, 1999 and 2001), a fixture on the USA Men's National Team staff across seven consecutive Olympic Games (1984–2008), and co-founder in 1985 of [[gold-medal-squared]] (GMS), the methodology-propagation business that remains the primary institutional carrier of his teaching today [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering] [gms-nd-about-us].

His student tree includes Olympic head coaches, national-team staff, and a large cohort of currently sitting NCAA Division I head coaches, making him a legacy figure whose influence on the post-2000 American game is difficult to overstate [gms-nd-about-us] [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

## Coaching career

**Early national-team years (1970–1976).** McGown first joined the USA Men's National Team as an assistant coach at the 1970 FIVB World Championship, then served as the program's head coach from 1973 to 1976 [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. He returned to the national-team staff in assistant, advisory, and consulting capacities for the remainder of his career [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering].

**USA Men's National Team — seven Olympics, seven World Championships (1974–2008).** McGown attended seven consecutive Olympic Games (1984 Los Angeles, 1988 Seoul, 1992 Barcelona, 1996 Atlanta, 2000 Sydney, 2004 Athens, 2008 Beijing) and seven consecutive FIVB World Championships (1974 through 2002) as a member of the USA staff — a tenure that, even within a staff-rotation-heavy program, is extraordinary [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. He was on staff for three Olympic gold medals: Los Angeles 1984, Seoul 1988, and Beijing 2008 [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. In Beijing, with head coach [[hugh-mccutcheon]] away from the team for three matches after the Drum Tower attack that killed his father-in-law, McGown was one of the consulting voices (alongside [[marv-dunphy]] and [[john-speraw]]) who supported acting head coach Ron Larsen through the crisis [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav].

**[[brigham-young-university]] — NCAA titles 1999 and 2001.** As BYU men's head coach, McGown compiled a 225–137 record across thirteen varsity seasons and led the Cougars to back-to-back NCAA Division I men's championships in 1999 and 2001, earning AVCA Coach of the Year honors in both title seasons [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. His BYU assistant roster in the 1995–2001 stretch included [[hugh-mccutcheon]], who earned three BYU degrees (BA in PE, MS in Exercise Science, MBA) while on staff and later parlayed that apprenticeship into the 2008 Olympic gold and subsequent career at USA Women and [[minnesota-volleyball]] [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

**Gold Medal Squared — founded 1985.** McGown co-founded GMS in 1985 with Hall of Fame coaches [[marv-dunphy]] and [[doug-beal]] as a methodology-propagation business — a vehicle for taking the motor-learning-grounded training ideas then being proven on the national team and delivering them to collegiate and club coaches through camps, clinics, and (later) online coach education [gms-nd-about-us]. Four decades on, the GMS coaching network spans multiple Olympic-staff coaches (Mike Wall, Rob Browning, Luka Slabe, [[john-speraw]], Courtney Thompson) and a large roster of sitting NCAA Division I head coaches, including Heather Olmstead at BYU, Keegan Cook at Minnesota, Ryan McGuyre at Baylor, Chris Tamas at Illinois, Tom Black at Georgia, Jason Watson at Arkansas, Jose Gandara at Miami, Giovana Melo at Cal State Bakersfield, and Rob Nielson at Utah State [gms-nd-about-us]. His son [[chris-mcgown]] is a GMS co-founder and current head coach in the organization [gms-nd-about-us].

**Honors.** AVCA Hall of Fame 2011; James E. Coleman National Team Award 2014 [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. He died December 30, 2016, at age 79; the USA Volleyball remembrance ran January 4, 2017 [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering].

## Core teaching principles

1. **Motor-learning science over opinion.** McGown's organizing commitment was that coaching has empirically answerable questions and that a coach's job is to do the reading [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. He championed a principle-based, data-driven approach rather than opinion- or tradition-based methods and was openly skeptical of unproven constructs such as "general athletic ability," which he saw as a folk-psychology placeholder for trainable specific skills [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown].

2. **Specificity of training.** Corollary to the point above: because volleyball skills are task-specific and context-sensitive, the transfer from general-athletic drills to volleyball performance is weak, and the fastest route to competitive skill is practicing volleyball in volleyball-like conditions [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown] [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The GMS article that operationalizes this framing states it bluntly: "the single most correlative activity to being a good volleyball player is having played a lot of volleyball" — a direct descendant of McGown's specificity stance [gms-nd-structure-practice].

3. **Random / variable practice over blocked practice.** McGown advocated early and hard movement from blocked practice (slow, isolated, predictable — useful only while an athlete is in the cognitive stage of learning a brand-new pattern) to random, game-speed, variable practice, where retention and transfer actually live [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The GMS-school statement invokes the motor-learning concept of the "challenge point" to argue that coaches should move to random, game-like conditions as quickly as the learner's current skill allows [gms-nd-structure-practice].

4. **Reading-and-reaction over prescriptive movement.** "First we have to teach them how to move. Then, we need to teach them how to see" — the principle highlighted on the GMS tribute page — captures McGown's two-stage framing: a base of efficient movement patterns exists so that attention can be freed for reading the game [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. In the blocking domain this shows up as the bunch-read system GMS describes as having been used "for over 25 years," with blocker eyework progressing from setter to hitter by jump time and footwork progressions moving from predetermined sets to variable triggers only after mechanics are sound [gms-nd-blocking].

5. **External-focus cues and simplified skill language.** The GMS practice article, descended from McGown's pedagogy, prefers external-focus cues (movement effects, analogies like "cracking a whip") over internal body-mechanic instructions, because the motor-learning literature consistently finds better retention and transfer from external focus [gms-nd-structure-practice]. Paired with a deliberately small drill library (a GMS clinic observed by an external attendee used roughly twelve core activities rather than a sprawling drill catalog), this yields a teaching environment where athletes see the same problems repeatedly, coach language is standardized, and coaching cues compound in meaning over time [gms-2018-clinic-report].

6. **Intentional feedback, not reflexive feedback.** Feedback, in the McGown/GMS frame, is a scarce learning resource and must be deployed intentionally rather than sprayed onto every rep — athletes need to develop self-evaluation for performance to survive outside the practice gym [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The teaching style side of this is captured in the tribute page: McGown gave direct, challenging feedback but delivered it with genuine care for the learner and high expectations for continuous improvement [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown].

7. **Training efficiency and "comfort with discomfort."** Colleagues in the USA Volleyball remembrance captured McGown's coaching emphasis in the phrase "developing comfort with discomfort," paired with a relentless focus on training efficiency — a practice hour either moved athletes' skill forward or it was a misallocation [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. McCutcheon, the most prominent of his former BYU assistants, characterized his teacher as "fundamentally sound" yet adaptive to evolving game dynamics [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering].

## Contributions to the game

**Founding a methodology-propagation business.** GMS was not the first US volleyball instructional business, but it was the first to organize itself explicitly around a motor-learning research base and a stable body of teaching commitments rather than a single coach's personality [gms-nd-about-us]. By pairing McGown with Dunphy (the Pepperdine and 1988 USA Olympic gold head coach) and Beal (the 1984 gold head coach), GMS bundled three men's-side national-team architects into one teaching brand at a moment when the American men's game had just won back-to-back gold medals, giving the methodology credibility it has traded on for four decades [gms-nd-about-us] [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering].

**The US coaching student tree.** The GMS "About Us" roster alone — multiple Olympic-staff coaches and a large cohort of sitting NCAA Division I head coaches — is a partial snapshot of McGown's coaching-education reach [gms-nd-about-us]. The most visible individual case is McCutcheon, whose 1995–2001 BYU apprenticeship under McGown (during two NCAA titles and three McGown-era graduate degrees) is the direct antecedent of the 2008 Beijing gold, the 2012 London silver with the USA Women, and the subsequent Minnesota and FIVB career [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio] [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav].

**Bunch-read blocking as a defensible US default.** GMS's public-facing blocking guide explicitly claims over twenty-five years of use for the bunch-read system — blockers starting closer to the middle of the court, using dynamic footwork, with eyework progressing from setter to hitter by jump time and a cue vocabulary (Q3, X2, X3; "elbows to elbows" left, "hands to hands" right) that is now standard within GMS-aligned programs [gms-nd-blocking]. This is one of the most concrete operational legacies of McGown's teaching: not just a philosophy but a block system taught with a shared vocabulary to a national network of coaches [gms-nd-blocking].

**Training-efficiency culture at the national-team level.** The seven-Olympics presence [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering] meant McGown's "training efficiency and specificity" ethos was a near-permanent influence in the USA Men's program across four decades, independent of which head coach was in charge at any given quadrennial. His contribution to the Beijing 2008 gold run was both long-tail (as McCutcheon's mentor) and acute (as one of the consulting voices around Ron Larsen during McCutcheon's absence) [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav].

**Publications and clinic reach.** The tribute page cites a University of Portland blocking program transformed through focused instruction, an AVCA 2010 publication on whole-versus-part training methodology co-authored by McGown, and international clinic work [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. The AVCA 2010 whole-versus-part piece in particular is the methodology spine that GMS still teaches — whole-skill training in game-like conditions, rather than isolated part-practice — and was visible in the 2018 GMS clinic that John Forman reported on from Charleston Academy [gms-2018-clinic-report].
