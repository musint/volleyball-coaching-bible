# Kim Yeon-koung

## Overview

Kim Yeon-koung (born **February 26, 1988**, Ansan, South Korea; **192 cm / 6'4"**) is the most-decorated South Korean women's-volleyball player of the modern era and the long-tenured Korean NT captain through Tokyo 2020 [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. She is included in this wiki as a coach-adjacent profile because she is **the player figure whose career is the most-cited anchor of the modern [[korean-school]] tradition** — a single virtuoso outside hitter on a non-tall national-team roster, developed through the Korean V-League and exported to the highest-tier Japanese, Turkish, and Chinese professional leagues [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia].

Kim's three Olympic appearances (Beijing 2008 youth role; **London 2012, 4th place + Olympic MVP**; **Tokyo 2020, 4th place + captain**) frame the 2010s era of Korean women's volleyball [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia] [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. She retired from international competition after the August 2021 announcement following Tokyo 2020 [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia].

## Coaching career

Kim has not held a head-coach position. The "career" section here documents her player career — which functions for this wiki as the structural reference for the modern Korean-school player-development model.

**Club career path (2005-2025).** Kim's career club path documents the international-transfer pipeline that defines the modern Korean school's developmental ladder [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]:

- **Heungkuk Life (2005-2009)** — three Korean V-League championships as a rookie phenomenon [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]
- **JT Marvelous, Japan V-League (2009-2011)** — first South Korean professional player overseas; led team to championship [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]
- **Fenerbahçe, Turkish SuperLig (2011-2017)** — six seasons including the 2011-12 CEV Champions League title and tournament MVP, **the first Asian player ever to win the CEV Champions League MVP** [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]
- **Shanghai, Chinese Super League (2017-2018, 2021-2022)** — two stints with runner-up finishes [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]
- **Eczacıbaşı VitrA, Turkey (2018-2020)** — Turkish Cup and Super Cup victories; coached by [[stefano-lavarini]] [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]
- **Heungkuk Life Pink Spiders (2020-2025)** — return to Korea; **2024-25 V-League championship** [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]

The Heungkuk → Japan → Turkey → China → Turkey → Korea → China → Korea path is the documented exemplar of the [[korean-school]]'s **international transfer pipeline as developmental vector** principle.

**National team career (2005-2021).** Korea NT debut at age 17 [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. Major-tournament results during her career:

- **2008 Beijing Olympics** — youth roster role
- **2012 London Olympics — 4th place + Olympic MVP and Best Scorer (207 points, Olympic record)**, the first non-medalist Olympic MVP in volleyball history [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]; head coach [[kim-hyung-sil]]
- **2014 Incheon Asian Games — gold medal** [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]
- **2016 Rio Olympics** — fifth-place finish [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia]
- **2020 Tokyo Olympics — 4th place** as captain under [[stefano-lavarini]] [lavarini-2021-volleyballworld-feature]; including the 3-2 quarterfinal upset over 4th-ranked Turkey [lavarini-2021-volleyballworld-feature]
- Retired from international competition August 2021 [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]

## Core teaching principles

Kim is documented as a player rather than a coaching figure; the "principles" below are player-development-pattern observations from the documented career trace.

**1. Outside hitter as both attacker and primary first-contact passer.** Kim's career shows the dual-position role that defines the modern Korean-school OH: not a classical European outside hitter who passes only when rotated to the back row, but a long-system serve-receive specialist who also leads in attack [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. Her Tokyo 2020 11-points-in-the-bronze-match scoring share is the result-side anchor of the structural dependency [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia].

**2. Compete-while-undersized through tempo and precision.** At 192 cm Kim is tall by Korean-NT roster standards but undersized by 2010s-2020s European and post-Soviet outside-hitter standards (1.95-2.00 m typical) [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. Lavarini's framing of Kim as *"all in one. She's one of the best outside hitters in the world and also a really charismatic leader"* [lavarini-2021-volleyballworld-feature] is the coaching trace of the structural dependency Korean-school tactical systems built around her.

**3. International transfer as developmental vector.** Kim's 16-year transnational club career — including the **first Asian CEV Champions League MVP** [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia] — is the player-side anchor of the [[korean-school]] development pattern: domestic V-League → Japan → top-tier European leagues → return to Korea. The pattern runs both directions: foreign coaches (Lavarini, [[cesar-hernandez-gonzalez]]) take the Korean NT head-coach role; Korean players develop at the top-tier European clubs the foreign coaches arrived from [lavarini-2021-volleyballworld-feature] [volleyballworld-2021-hernandez-korea-appointment].

## Contributions to the game

**2012 London Olympic MVP — first non-medalist OMVP in volleyball history.** Olympic scoring record of 207 points (breaking Yekaterina Gamova's 204) [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. The award marked the first global recognition of Kim as a world-class outside hitter and is the result-side anchor of [[kim-hyung-sil]]'s tactical system at London 2012 [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia].

**Modern Korean-school player figure.** Kim's career trajectory is the player figure most associated with the modern [[korean-school]] in the available English-language sources [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia] [lavarini-2021-volleyballworld-feature]. Style descriptor preserved by Wikipedia: *"Russian physical strength, American power, Korean technique, and Brazilian agility"* [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia].

**FIVB Athletes' Commission (2016).** Federation-administrative role concurrent with playing career [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia].

**Single-player dependency cost — post-2021 succession problem.** Kim's August 2021 retirement immediately exposed the absence of a like-for-like successor in the Korean program; the Hernández González 2022-2024 tenure failed to produce Paris 2024 qualification, and the Korean women's NT was relegated from the Volleyball Nations League after 2025 [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia]. The trajectory is the structural cost of star-anchored systems.
