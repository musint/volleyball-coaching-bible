# Brian Rosen

## Overview

Brian Rosen is the head coach of the Creighton University women's volleyball program and an Art of Coaching Volleyball (AOC) contributor whose published work emphasizes ball-control drill architecture — multi-angle passing, small-court 1v1 competitions, and serve-receive relays — as the foundation of practice tempo and team tone [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. His organizing claim: the first touches of practice set the ball-control quality for the rest of the session, so the coach's job is to design those opening touches deliberately rather than let them default to easy warmup reps [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

Two different coaches named Rosen appear in the AOC corpus. Brian Rosen (Creighton) is the subject of this page; **[[mark-rosen]]** (University of Michigan, 1999-2022) is a separate coach whose AOC catalog covers swing-block beginner progressions, in/out-of-system transition training, competitive serve-pass drills, block-versus-OOS tactics, and OH shot-selection — do not conflate citations across the two.

## Coaching career

Rosen is the head women's volleyball coach at Creighton University, a Big East Conference program in Omaha, Nebraska [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. ## Core teaching principles

**Early touches set the tone.** Rosen's organizing framing is that ball-control quality for the entire session is determined by the first few reps: if the opening touches are sloppy, the remaining two hours of practice inherit that sloppiness; if the opening touches demand precision under movement, the rest of the day holds a higher baseline [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. This reframes the warmup block from "getting loose" into "setting the ball-control standard," and it explains why Rosen's drill architecture leans competitive rather than cooperative from rep one [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

**Competitive energy plus clear communication as the non-technical backbone.** Rosen names two non-technical inputs as the backbone of first-contact quality — competitive energy and clear communication [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. The implicit argument: platform execution rides on arousal level and on teammate calls that precede the contact; coaches who only coach mechanics are missing half of what's actually moving the ball-control number [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

**Movement under time pressure as the default passing rep.** Four-corner passing — Rosen's featured format — makes passers move across multiple angles while keeping the ball alive under a time constraint [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. The design choice is that precision is trained under load, not in stationary platform drills; only moving-passer reps transfer to live-serve reception [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

**Small-court 1v1 competition for aggressive second contacts.** Rosen's 1v1 ladder tournament is a small-court format in which each player's second contact must be aggressive and varied under first-contact stress [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. Shot-selection confidence is a trained habit — players only learn to vary the second touch if the format rewards variation, and small-format reps do this better than 6v6 where the second-contact burden is distributed [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

**Serve-receive relays layer reading and decision-making.** Rosen's relay variations add movement, reading, and decision-making to passing training rather than isolating platform mechanics [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. This is structurally aligned with the reading-first emphasis in the [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] teaching cluster — see [[karch-kiraly]]'s four-question serve-receive diagnostic — though Rosen's framing is drill-format-first [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

**Drills as patterns, not prescriptions.** Each Creighton drill is explicitly tunable for age, team size, and gym environment [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. The transferable unit is the pattern (movement, time pressure, competition, communication), not the specific setup [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

## Contributions to the game

**Program-level drill architecture as published content.** Rosen's AOC session doesn't teach a single drill but a multi-drill opening block Creighton uses to set practice tone [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. Published "here's the opener we use" content from a sitting D-I head coach is rare — most coach-published drills are isolated formats, not the first block of an actual program's practice — and the transferable value is the sequence and rationale, not the individual drills [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control].

**Competitive-opener template for practice design.** Rosen's opener is a template coaches at any level can copy: (a) multi-angle passing under time pressure, (b) small-court 1v1 with aggressive second contacts, (c) serve-receive relays [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. For 14U and HS coaches, the operating lesson is that competitive and game-like pressure can be installed from rep one — the warmup does not have to be cooperative — which raises the ball-control baseline for the rest of the session [aoc-2025-rosen-creighton-ball-control]. See [[age-lens-14u]] for the 14U-specific adoption call.
