# Diane Flick-Williams

## Overview

Diane Flick-Williams is the women's volleyball head coach at Western Washington University, a NCAA Division II program in the Great Northwest Athletic Conference (GNAC) based in Bellingham, Washington [yt-aoc-unknown-pass-for-points-diane-flick]. She is one of the most prolific bylined contributors to [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] among D2 head coaches, with a published library spanning setter development (posture, footwork, jump-setting progression, setter-knowledge exams), competitive-passing drill design (the flagship "Pass for Points" video, the serve-receive range drill, the passer-reading-the-serve-early drill), team-level sideout competition (The Chase; 6v6 first-ball-kill rotation chase), and hitter psychology (shot-selection myths, the "superpower" framework for hitter identity) [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-when-jump-set] [yt-aoc-unknown-pass-for-points-diane-flick] [aoc-2021-flick-williams-sr-range-drill] [aoc-2019-flick-williams-passer-read-serve-early] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-the-chase] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-first-ball-kill-6v6] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-hitting-myths] [aoc-2026-flick-williams-mental-hitting]. Her AOC output functions as a working-season reference for coaches at the D2, D3, and NAIA levels — and, because the drills are stripped-down in personnel and equipment requirements, for club programs at the 14U-through-18U level as well [aoc-2021-flick-williams-sr-range-drill] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-imperfect-back-set].

## Core teaching principles

**The setter is the hub; build confidence through knowledge, not encouragement.** Flick-Williams's single most load-bearing coaching claim is that the setter is "the hub of your team's wheel" and that setter confidence is built through deliberate knowledge transfer rather than through praise or volume of contacts [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence]. Western Washington operationalizes this with a written setter exam that forces the setter to (a) rank the team's hitters honestly by fact rather than friendship, (b) commit to a post-error set-allocation plan (who gets the next ball after a given hitter has just been blocked or killed), and (c) match set choices to opposing block size [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence]. She pairs the exam with a strong warning, citing [[john-dunning]], that over-critiquing the setter damages more than it helps — the setter's brain is a fragile competitive asset [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence].

**Posture and footwork before jump setting.** Before tempo or disguise, Flick-Williams drills two fundamentals. The first is posture — "nose to toes" (feet and eyes find the target together), hands above the forehead, and finding the target with the right eye [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-posture]. The second is footwork — "circle the ball" (begin moving in the direction of the set early, so you arrive already squared) rather than running straight at it, because running straight kills the pivot [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork-drill]. The cadence she teaches is a final "left, right, walking through" into contact, and a "fewest steps possible" rule in how many steps it takes to get feet to the ball [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork-drill]. Only after these floor-release fundamentals are consistent in direction and height does she train jump setting — cued explicitly as "two separate actions: jump, then set" [aoc-2023-flick-williams-when-jump-set]. Jump setting is high-value (it makes the offense harder to read) but is not for beginners [aoc-2023-flick-williams-when-jump-set].

**Overload and isolate: the setter's decision tree.** Flick-Williams teaches offensive setter decision-making as a pair of schemes — overload (cluster hitters from close proximity to force the block to choose) and isolate (leave a weak-side hitter alone against one blocker) — with a single mental sequence guiding the choice: who-what-who, meaning who do I want to attack, what set executes that, who runs that set [aoc-2023-flick-williams-overload-isolate]. The always-on target is the weakest blocker on the other side [aoc-2023-flick-williams-overload-isolate]. Overload/isolate is where the setter-exam knowledge (hitter rankings, block-size matchups) converts into live-rep decisions.

**Competitive passing, every day.** The flagship Flick-Williams AOC piece is a video drill titled "Pass for Points" [yt-aoc-unknown-pass-for-points-diane-flick], and the family of drills it headlines turns serve-receive passing into a scored game rather than a repetition count. Her serve-receive range drill for the libero is the most fully captured example: one server, one libero in middle back, a 3/2/1 base scoring scale for perfect/mediocre/poor pass, plus bonus points for extending range — +2 for a perfect pass on a sideline ball and +1 for a perfect pass on a ball outside the body, racing to 15 [aoc-2021-flick-williams-sr-range-drill]. The structural insight: pass-rating gamified and range-extension rewarded produces libero range growth that warm-up volume does not [aoc-2021-flick-williams-sr-range-drill]. Paired with the competitive-passing drill family, she also isolates reading the serve as a trainable cognitive skill: a caller 10 feet inside the court faces the server and must shout the target passer and depth (short/deep) as soon as the ball leaves the server's hand — no passing, just perceptual reps [aoc-2019-flick-williams-passer-read-serve-early]. That reading-first emphasis aligns with the broader AOC house voice articulated on [[karch-kiraly]]'s profile [aoc-2019-flick-williams-passer-read-serve-early].

**First-ball kill as the team's offensive engine.** The 6v6 competitive-drill format Flick-Williams calls "1 swing, 2 times, 3 chances" makes first-ball kill (FBK) the scoring engine of team offense: two consecutive FBKs earn the receiving team a rotation, a rally win without an FBK does not advance the rotation but preserves a chance (up to three), and a rally loss returns the serve to the opponent [aoc-2023-flick-williams-first-ball-kill-6v6]. Her sideout drill "The Chase" uses a parallel point-ladder with a 10–6 asymmetric start and a 2-rung reward for any first-ball-swing win, 1 rung otherwise — so the in-system first-ball swing is the behavior explicitly trained [aoc-2023-flick-williams-the-chase]. Both formats make the sideout urgency of a real match the default practice environment.

**The hitter's cognition: inventory, role clarity, superpower.** Flick-Williams frames hitting percentage as a cognitive skill, not just a mechanical one [aoc-2023-flick-williams-successful-hitter]. The three operating pieces: (1) role clarity — understand whether you are a 6-rotation passer, a pure point-scorer, or a decoy today, and optimize accordingly; (2) taking inventory — after every swing, note which defender dug, which zone was empty, what the block revealed about the opposing scheme; (3) identifying a personal "superpower" (shot variety, raw velocity, or block-tool skill) rather than trying to imitate elite hitters wholesale [aoc-2023-flick-williams-successful-hitter] [aoc-2026-flick-williams-mental-hitting]. She dismantles four shot-selection myths on top of that: don't avoid repeat shots, don't avoid the block (its edges are a high-value tool because ricochets are unpredictable), don't give up on a bad approach (every ball has a best play, and a push to the corner is still offense), and invert the in-system/out-of-system tip heuristic — tip when the opponent expects a swing, swing when they expect a tip or free ball [aoc-2023-flick-williams-hitting-myths].

**Rotation-specific serve-receive and serving strategy.** Flick-Williams treats serve-receive as a rotation-by-rotation design problem rather than a generic system choice. In Ro.1 the prescription is "serve to disrupt the pattern" — put the ball between the setter and a nearby teammate — and to shift the lineup so the strongest passers receive, especially when stuck siding out; the right-side hitter must pull back to pass and the setter releases from behind her [aoc-2019-flick-williams-ro1-strategies]. In Rotation 4 with a front-row setter, stack the slide-capable middle by the setter and front-left-stack all front-row players to disguise the offense, and allow a good-passing right-front to pass from left-back as long as she stays in front of right-back [aoc-2019-barnard-flick-williams-rot4-front-setter]. The receiving-team corollary is that the best passers should cover the serve-receive area they are best at — the same "expose your best player" principle that Kiraly articulates at the national-team level [aoc-2019-flick-williams-ro1-strategies].

**Imperfect conditions are a trainable class of rep.** A recurring Flick-Williams move is to treat out-of-system reps as a dedicated training category rather than an accident. The imperfect-pass back-setting drill puts the setter in Zone 3 off a high toss, self-sets off the net, chases, and back-sets to a Zone 2 catcher — coaching body position, hands above the head, even follow-through, and either squaring the body to target or setting off the net-side shoulder [aoc-2023-flick-williams-imperfect-back-set] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-imperfect-pass-back-set]. The setter-footwork range extension pushes the setter to deliver an accurate Zone 4 ball from the farthest off-net point possible, including from the right half of the court behind the 10-foot line [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork]. The hitter "bad approach = bad play" myth is the same principle applied to the attacker [aoc-2023-flick-williams-hitting-myths].

## Contributions to the game

As a D2 head coach with sustained AOC publication, Flick-Williams's main contribution is pedagogical reach disproportionate to program size. The catalog above — approximately 17 distinct AOC articles and one flagship video across 2019-2026 on passing, setting, hitting, sideout design, rotation strategy, and hitter psychology — functions as a kind of working D2 curriculum that is accessible to club and small-college coaches who do not have D1 recruiting budgets, full-time assistants, or national-team resource access [yt-aoc-unknown-pass-for-points-diane-flick] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-the-chase] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-first-ball-kill-6v6] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-hitting-myths] [aoc-2026-flick-williams-mental-hitting]. The drills are deliberately stripped-down in personnel and equipment requirements — a tosser, a setter, a catcher, a half-court, balls — which makes them portable to small staffs [aoc-2023-flick-williams-imperfect-back-set] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork].

Three concrete methodological contributions stand out. First, the written setter exam as a confidence-through-knowledge tool is a rare named artifact in the AOC catalog — most setter-development material is technical rather than cognitive, and the exam's three axes (hitter rankings, post-error set allocation, block-size matchups) give coaches a concrete instrument to adopt [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence]. Second, the family of scored passing-drill formats (Pass for Points, the serve-receive range drill) operationalize "compete in the gym every day" — the same thesis Kiraly runs at the national-team level — in a form that is reproducible with one server and one passer at a time [yt-aoc-unknown-pass-for-points-diane-flick] [aoc-2021-flick-williams-sr-range-drill]. Third, the two team-level first-ball-kill competitive games ("The Chase" and the "1 swing, 2 times, 3 chances" rotation chase) externalize first-ball-swing priority from a setter-only objective into a scored team goal [aoc-2023-flick-williams-the-chase] [aoc-2023-flick-williams-first-ball-kill-6v6].

## Quotes & representative passages

On the setter's structural role and the role of knowledge in confidence: Flick-Williams treats the setter as "the hub of your team's wheel" and asserts that setter confidence is built through a plan and a deep game understanding, not through volume of reps alone [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence]. On over-coaching the setter specifically, she cites [[john-dunning]]: over-critiquing the setter's brain hurts more than it helps [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-confidence].

On setter footwork: the core cue is "toes and nose" facing the target at contact, the core error is "running straight at the ball" — the fix is "circle the ball," starting the movement in the direction of the set early so the setter arrives already squared [aoc-2023-flick-williams-setter-footwork].

On hitting decision-making, her four-myth reframe captures the operating posture: keep hitting the same spot until they prove they can dig it; the block is a tool, not an obstacle; a push to the corner is still offense when the approach is bad; and tip when the opponent expects a swing — the inverse of the conventional in-system/out-of-system tip heuristic [aoc-2023-flick-williams-hitting-myths].

On first-ball kills, "The Chase" encodes the priority in its scoring: first-ball swings pay 2 rungs, rally wins without a first-ball swing pay 1 — the scored game itself is the pedagogy [aoc-2023-flick-williams-the-chase].
