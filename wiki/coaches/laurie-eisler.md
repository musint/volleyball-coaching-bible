# Laurie Eisler

## Overview

Laurie Eisler is a long-tenured Canadian university head coach whose public coaching voice reaches the wider North American audience primarily through the Art of Coaching Volleyball (AOC) platform — in a 2013 Calgary Clinic on-court Q&A with [[hugh-mccutcheon]] she is the second name on the canonical AOC reference for the *competitive cauldron* practice-design idiom, and in later AOC appearances she has been the featured clinician on error-detection methodology and one of four panelists on AOC's "One Big Thing" cross-federation panel [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron] [aoc-2017-one-big-thing-panel] [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection]. She is the head women's volleyball coach at the [[alberta-volleyball|University of Alberta]] and has been active at that post through the 2010s and into the 2020s [aoc-2017-one-big-thing-panel] [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection].

## Core teaching principles

### 1. Competitive cauldron as practice design (with McCutcheon)
Eisler is the co-named voice on the AOC reference that treats the *competitive cauldron* as a deliberate practice design: an environment in which daily ranking and contest create real stakes — who plays, who serves, who rotates in — while the team remains cohesive rather than fractured [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]. The AOC public summary frames the cauldron as "an environment in practice that surfaces competitive edges while keeping the team unified" [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]. Because the Q&A's full on-court content is AOC Premium, the precise drill scoring, ladder-reset cadence, and role-flex rules Eisler uses inside the cauldron at Alberta are not documented in the source on file here and are queued for backfill [unsourced]. The operational read for any coach working from the public material is that the cauldron is not a single drill — it is a practice architecture in which most practice games carry scoreboard stakes for roles and reps, not just for team mood [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]. The idea is paired with [[hugh-mccutcheon]]'s *coopetition* framing — internal battles for roles and reps co-existing with shared program goals and trust [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-coopetition] — and the two concepts are typically read together in the AOC and AVCA coach networks [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron].

### 2. Error diagnosis before error correction
Eisler's 2019 AOC error-detection video opens with a diagnostic taxonomy that is itself the teaching content: "You can't fix an error until you get to the root of what is causing it. Is it physical? Is it psychological? Is it technical? Is it tactical?" [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection]. The claim is that no correction is possible until the error's root cause is correctly classified — and that misclassifying root cause produces wasted correction effort, because a physical limitation will not respond to a technical cue and a psychological error will not respond to a conditioning fix [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection]. The AOC editorial framing places this content under coach-development and volleyball-practice-strategies, targeted at high-school, club, and youth audiences, which pitches it as a foundational coaching-skill piece rather than an elite-only tactic [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection].

### 3. Cross-federation, cross-level perspective
Eisler's 2017 panel appearance slots her inside a four-coach conversation explicitly pitched by AOC for "high school, club, and youth coaches looking for cross-generational philosophy," with her role reading as the mid-career college voice alongside retired college and national-team voices [aoc-2017-one-big-thing-panel]. The through-line — "one big thing" each coach would emphasize, framed around team inspiration, program growth, and the role of social media in the modern coach's job — is the structural move AOC uses to get senior coaches to commit to a single-sentence coaching priority [aoc-2017-one-big-thing-panel]. Eisler's specific "one big thing" is behind the AOC Premium paywall and is queued for backfill [unsourced].

### 4. Canadian-program context within a U.S.-dominated coach-education pipeline
Eisler's presence on cross-federation AOC panels — U.S. women's NT (Liskevych), Canadian men's NT (Hoag), U.S. college (Dunning), Canadian college (Eisler) — quietly anchors the AOC pipeline to a Canadian university voice rather than making her a guest [aoc-2017-one-big-thing-panel]. The coaching read is that program-building at a non-U.S. university, inside the U Sports / Canada West competitive structure rather than NCAA Power-Five, is a distinct developmental context — different scholarship rules, different recruiting timelines, different tournament calendar — and that her voice on AOC is a marker of how thin the direct-to-U.S.-coach representation of that context normally is [unsourced].

## Contributions to the game

**Co-credit on the competitive cauldron idiom.** The single most portable Eisler contribution surfaced in this wiki is her co-panelist role on the AOC reference for the *competitive cauldron* — a term now widely used across the AOC and AVCA coach networks for a specific practice-design philosophy [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]. The [[hugh-mccutcheon]] profile on this wiki credits the Calgary Clinic Q&A with popularizing the term, and Eisler is the second name on the byline [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]. She is routinely paired with McCutcheon in coach-community references to the cauldron, even though the fuller published articulation of the idea has come from McCutcheon's side of the conversation — via *Championship Behaviors* (2022) and surrounding interviews — rather than from a published Eisler text [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors].

**Error-detection teaching as a coach-skill artifact.** Eisler's 2019 AOC error-detection video is her solo clinician contribution on file: a method for classifying errors (physical / psychological / technical / tactical) before selecting a correction path [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection]. In the context of the wider AOC library — which is heavier on drills and match video than on coach-development methodology — a dedicated error-diagnosis piece is a distinct contribution aimed at coaches improving their own diagnostic skill rather than at players improving a specific technique [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection].

**Cross-federation presence on AOC panels.** Her ongoing AOC editorial relationship through the 2010s — 2013 Calgary Clinic, 2017 "One Big Thing" panel, 2019 error-detection video — is itself a contribution: a Canadian university coach's voice inside the dominant English-language coach-education platform [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron] [aoc-2017-one-big-thing-panel] [aoc-2019-eisler-error-detection]. The specific long-arc influence of her Alberta program on Canadian volleyball's high-performance pipeline is not enumerated in the sources on file here and remains an open research item [unsourced].

**Alberta program building.** Her multi-decade head-coach tenure at the University of Alberta — a long-stay, deep-institutional-equity pattern — is consistent with the program-building archetype across U Sports women's volleyball, though the quantified Alberta record (seasons coached, Canada West titles, U Sports tournament appearances and medals, player-to-national-team pipeline) is not on file in this wiki and is queued for backfill [unsourced].
