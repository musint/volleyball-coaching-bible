# Kerry MacDonald

## Overview

Kerry MacDonald is Volleyball Canada's Chief Sports Officer and former head coach of the UBC Thunderbirds men's volleyball program [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics] [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing]. On the [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] platform his teaching voice is concentrated around modern attacking mechanics — torque-driven arm swing, high-hand contact, two-footed landing safety, and seeing the block as an offensive tool rather than an obstacle [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics] [aoc-2024-macdonald-advanced-attacking-block] [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing]. His practical drill voice on AOC covers gap-attacking quick-middle tempo (the 31 set) and competitive 6v6 structures that invert the role of the serve within a wash drill [aoc-2021-running-effective-31-attack] [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. MacDonald is one of the primary modern-attack sources on this wiki's [[hitting-arm-swing]] page and the [[age-lens-14u]] attacking progression.

## Coaching career

MacDonald's coaching résumé, as it surfaces on AOC, runs through two anchors: **head coach of UBC Men's Volleyball** (U SPORTS Canada's top men's program for long stretches of its history) and **Chief Sports Officer at Volleyball Canada**, the national governing body [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics] [aoc-2024-macdonald-advanced-attacking-block] [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing] [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. The CSO role puts him in charge of the high-performance program's athletic direction — the Canadian men's and women's national-team pipelines, U SPORTS development funnels, coach-education resources, and technical standards for the federation. MacDonald's AOC-contributor arc spans from at least 2021, with the 31-attack breakdown, through 2024's trio of attacking-mechanics articles and into 2025's reverse-wash drill [aoc-2021-running-effective-31-attack] [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics] [aoc-2024-macdonald-advanced-attacking-block] [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing] [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. ## Core teaching principles

**Torque generates the swing; the hand must finish high.** MacDonald's single most-cited teaching frame is that hitting power comes from **torque** — torso rotation driven by a strong core and the hips coming through — and that the second decisive lever is **hand height at contact**, with the hitting arm finishing high [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics]. Strong mechanics on both levers protect the lower back and shoulders, which is the reason the torque-and-high-hand emphasis matters at least as much for injury prevention as for ball speed [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics]. His warm-up progression for this concept — partner throws across the net using the attacking arm, then progressing to full snap-hit — is a shoulder-safe route into the full swing that sidesteps cold muscling [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics]. This framing anchors the modern-attack section of [[hitting-arm-swing]] and the [[age-lens-14u]] attacking progression.

**Landings are a shared setter-hitter responsibility.** One-leg landings are a common bad habit ingrained during club years and a real injury risk pattern, and MacDonald's fix runs through both roles [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing]. The hitter stays level through the shoulders and avoids reaching across the body on contact — which depends on arriving on time. The setter errs toward the inside of the net rather than outside the antenna, because hitters can reroute their approach inside more safely than they can reach across to chase an outside ball [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing]. Attack-landing safety is not purely a hitter-side problem in this frame; it's shared responsibility with the setter's ball-placement bias [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing].

**See the block before you swing.** MacDonald's advanced-attacking thread (co-authored with Gina Schmidt) reframes the block as an offensive input rather than an obstacle [aoc-2024-macdonald-advanced-attacking-block]. Attackers first train their **vision of the block**: its strength, its location on the net, whether any holes exist, and whether it's penetrating. They then find holes and seams — a late middle blocker opens a gap between two blockers that can be attacked into a dig-coverage gap — or work the block itself with high-off-the-hands balls against tall blockers, tooling the edges, swiping, and tipping [aoc-2024-macdonald-advanced-attacking-block]. Vision precedes tactical execution; hitters don't pick a shot until they've read what the block is giving them.

**The 31 is a gap-attack isolation tool — including out-of-system.** MacDonald defines the 31 as a fast, flat set to the middle placed right **in the gap between two blockers**, creating either a one-on-one matchup for the middle or a direct hit into an undefended seam [aoc-2021-running-effective-31-attack]. Its distinctive value is that it stays viable out-of-system: (1) with the setter pushed off the net, the 31 remains an aggressive play; (2) with the setter pushed to the right-side pin, the middle shades toward mid-court and runs a 31 against a single blocker, attacking the angles [aoc-2021-running-effective-31-attack]. Modern offense has more late-set, fast-tempo variants than simply "setter-is-off-so-send-it-outside" — the 31 is one of the clearest examples.

**Competitive drills make serving the decisive act.** MacDonald's reverse-wash drill flips the usual serve-then-free-ball-then-free-ball sequence into free-ball-then-free-ball-then-serve — coach bounces a ball to one team, the rally plays out, the other side receives a free ball, and the serve arrives as the decider [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. Lead-dependent scoring makes the stakes scale: a 2-0 lead means the serve is worth two points, and a 1-1 tie means one [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. He insists the free balls must be difficult, not lazy, to keep the drill game-like [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. The deliberate design payoff is his phrase: "the last serve is now really meaningful" [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash].

## Contributions to the game

MacDonald's most visible contribution on this wiki is the **modern arm-mechanics framing** — torque-and-high-hand — which sits at the core of [[hitting-arm-swing]] and drives the modern-hitting section of [[age-lens-14u]] [aoc-2024-attack-arm-mechanics]. His landing-safety article is one of the few AOC pieces that locates attack-injury risk inside the **setter-hitter connection** rather than attributing it purely to hitter technique [aoc-2024-macdonald-one-leg-landing]. His advanced-attacking material puts **block-reading** on the offensive side of the net as a trainable skill (vision → holes → using the block), alongside the defense-side block-reading tradition from coaches like Karch Kiraly and Jim Stone [aoc-2024-macdonald-advanced-attacking-block]. Drill-side, his **reverse wash** is a distinct competitive structure that isolates serving-under-pressure without rebuilding the rest of a wash drill from scratch [aoc-2025-macdonald-reverse-wash]. At the federation level, as Volleyball Canada's CSO, he shapes the high-performance direction of the Canadian program — though the specifics of that administrative work sit outside the AOC corpus ingested here [unsourced].
