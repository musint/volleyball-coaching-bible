# Park Man-Bok

## Overview

Park Man-Bok (박만복, August 30, 1936 – September 26, 2019) is the **first Korean inducted into the International Volleyball Hall of Fame** (2016 class) [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. His professional life broke into two distinct phases that together anchor an unusual transnational coaching career: a foundational Korean phase (1965–1973) running Soong Eui Women's College, the Korean Air club, and finally the Korea women's national team at the 1973 FIVB World Cup, where he delivered Korea a bronze medal in Uruguay [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]; and a much longer Peruvian phase (1974–1992, 1999–2001) as Peru's women's national team head coach, peaking with **Peru's 1988 Seoul Olympic silver medal** — Peru's most decorated international volleyball result, achieved on Korean soil against the USSR in a five-set final [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. The transnational arc also included a six-year head-coaching stint with Ito Yokado in the Japan V-League (1993–1998) [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].

For this wiki, Park is the **chronological origin point of the [[korean-school]] coaching lineage** — the figure under whom Korean women's volleyball produced its first major international medal (1973 World Cup bronze) and the figure whose subsequent Peruvian work imported a Korean-coaching profile into the South American regional ecosystem decades before transnational head-coach mobility became routine.

**Important boundary:** the Korean women's NT 1976 Montreal Olympic bronze, frequently cited as the canonical historical achievement of [[korean-school]], is **not** Park's work — by 1976 he had already moved to Peru, where he finished 7th in Montreal at the same Olympics with Peru [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The 1976 Montreal Korean head coach is a separate figure whose name is not surfaced in the IVHF biography or Korea Herald induction coverage and remains [unsourced] in this wiki pending Korean-language source ingest.

## Coaching career

**Player and education (1947–1964).** Park began volleyball in 11th grade at his South Korean high school and continued at Kyung-Hee University, where he majored in pharmacy and competed on the college volleyball team [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. He served as a sergeant in the Korean Army from 1960 to 1964 — military service that interrupted his playing career and during which he married Duck Soon Lee [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The Hall of Fame record does not preserve specifics of his playing-position résumé, but the move directly into a college head-coaching role at Soong Eui in 1965 implies that Park's authority rested on the player and educational base built across the late 1950s and military years rather than on a senior-NT playing career [unsourced].

**Korean coaching phase (1965–1973).** Park's three Korean coaching posts ran in sequence:

- **Soong Eui Women's College (1965–1968)** — Park's initial post; the four-year block established him as a women's-side specialist [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].
- **Korean Air, V-League / domestic top tier (1969–1972)** — the four-year Korean Air tenure professionalized his profile and prepared the national-team appointment [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].
- **Korea Women's National Team (1973)** — Park's national-team year delivered a **bronze medal at the 1973 FIVB World Cup in Uruguay**, Korea's first podium finish at the World Cup level [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].

The 1973 World Cup bronze is the seed result of [[korean-school]] in international competition. Park's tenure ended in 1973 because his next chapter, the 18-year Peruvian arc, began the following year [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].

**Peruvian coaching phase (1974–1992).** Park's Peruvian tenure spanned five Olympic cycles and five FIVB World Championships [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]:

- 1974 World Championship: 8th place
- 1976 Montreal Olympics: 7th place — Peru's first Olympics under Park
- 1977, 1979 South American Championships: gold (back-to-back regional titles)
- 1979 Pan-American Cup: silver
- 1980 Moscow Olympics: 6th place
- **1982 World Championship (Peru host): silver medal** — first major podium
- 1983 Pan-American Cup: bronze
- 1984 Los Angeles Olympics: 4th place
- 1986 World Championship: bronze medal
- 1987 Pan-American Cup: silver
- **1988 Seoul Olympics: silver medal** — Peru's most decorated international result, a five-set final against the USSR

Park ended his initial Peruvian run in 1992 with a regional-dominance record that included **seven South American Volleyball Championship titles and 11 medals** in regional play [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf].

**Japan club phase (1993–1998).** Park spent six years in the Japan V-League as head coach of Ito Yokado [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The Japanese chapter is the least-documented in English-language sources [unsourced] but represents a third national-context coaching stint and reinforces the transnational profile.

**Peruvian return (1999–2001).** Park resumed the Peru women's national-team head-coaching post for one Olympic cycle, finishing 11th at Sydney 2000 [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. He subsequently moved into a Peruvian Volleyball Federation advisory role and remained resident in Lima until his death in 2019 [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].

## Core teaching principles

The Hall of Fame record and Korea Herald induction coverage are results-record documents rather than doctrinal documents — neither preserves training-method material in Park's voice [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. The principles below are inferences from the documented results record; Korean-language and Spanish-language source ingest is the path to deepen this section.

1. **A national-team head coach can be a working pedagogy specialist who carries methods across borders.** Park's Korea-Peru-Japan-Peru career is itself a structural claim: a women's-volleyball head coach with a Korean college and club apprenticeship can deliver podium-level results in three different national-program ecosystems across four decades [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. The pattern predates the modern era of routine transnational head-coach mobility (Lavarini Italy→Korea, Anastasi Italy→Poland, etc.) by roughly two decades.

2. **Build a roster around a single virtuoso outside hitter and a tactically-sharp setter.** [unsourced] Park's documented Peruvian rosters featured Hall of Fame outside hitter Cecilia Tait — one of the most-decorated South American players of the 1980s — and Hall of Fame middle / setter Gabriela Pérez del Solar as the program's competitive spine [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The development of two Hall of Fame players inside one head-coach's program is the strongest available signal of his player-development profile, but the specific training mechanisms underlying that development are not documented in the ingested sources.

3. **Compete against larger, more physical opponents through tempo and precision.** [unsourced] The Peruvian women's program of the 1980s, like the Korean women's program of the same era, lacked the size profile of the USSR, Cuba, China, and East Germany; the documented results — silver at home in 1982, silver in Seoul 1988 — were achieved against opponents with substantial physical advantages. The compensating-with-tempo-and-precision pattern is a hypothesis consistent with the [[japanese-training]] and modern [[korean-school]] template but is not directly attributed to Park in the ingested sources.

4. **Stay in one program long enough to develop a generation.** Park's 18-year initial Peruvian tenure (1974–1992) is the documented commitment level [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The pattern resembles [[nikolay-karpol]]'s 51-year Uralochka tenure in scale of investment, though Park alternated club and NT roles rather than holding a single post.

## Contributions to the game

**1973 FIVB World Cup bronze — Korea's foundational international medal.** Park's single year as Korea women's NT head coach delivered Korea's first World Cup podium, a bronze medal in Uruguay [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The result anchored Korean women's volleyball as a podium-capable program at the international level and preceded the 1976 Montreal Olympic bronze (under a different head coach) by three years.

**1988 Seoul Olympic silver with Peru.** Peru's most decorated international volleyball result was delivered by a Korean head coach on Korean soil [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. The five-set final against the USSR is one of the canonical Olympic volleyball matches of the 1980s. The result also marks the high water of South American women's volleyball in the Olympic era.

**Korea-to-Peru transnational coaching template.** Park's 1974 move to Peru and the development of two Peruvian Hall of Fame players (Cecilia Tait, Gabriela Pérez del Solar) under his program established a template — Asian coaching expertise developing South American women's volleyball — that would not become routine for several decades [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame]. The pattern's institutional importance is currently under-documented in English-language sources.

**First Korean International Volleyball Hall of Fame inductee (2016).** Park's 2016 induction was the precedent under which subsequent Korean coaching recognition at the international institutional level became plausible [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame] [park-man-bok-2016-koreaherald-ivhf]. The pattern parallels [[yasutaka-matsudaira]]'s 1998 induction as the first Japanese person honored — an 18-year gap between the first Japanese and the first Korean induction.

**Long-arc player development across four programs.** Across Soong Eui, Korean Air, two Peruvian NT tenures, and the Ito Yokado club role, Park accumulated a roughly 30-year head-coaching record with podium-level results in two different countries [park-man-bok-2016-hall-of-fame].
