# Hirobumi Daimatsu

## Overview

Hirobumi Daimatsu (大松 博文, 1921-1978; given name also romanized Hirofumi) coached Japan's women's national team to gold at the 1964 Tokyo Olympics — the first Olympic women's volleyball event — defeating the Soviet Union 3-0 in the final on October 23, 1964 [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. Built primarily from the [[nichibo-kaizuka]] company team he had coached since the early 1950s, his program ran a 175-match international winning streak and is the historical anchor of the [[japanese-training]] tradition: size-disadvantage compensation through extreme training volume, defense-first identity built on the rolling dive, and athlete development as a years-long commitment [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. He was nicknamed *Oni no Daimatsu* — "Daimatsu the Demon," the "Devil of Osaka" — for the severity of those methods, and remains one of the most studied and most contested coaching figures in the sport's history [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-2017-tough-love].

## Coaching career

Daimatsu was born February 12, 1921 in Ayauta-gun, Kagawa Prefecture, played volleyball in high school and at Kansai Gakuin University, and began head coaching in **1953** when the Nichibo Kaizuka textile mill in Osaka hired him to lead its company women's team [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-wikipedia-biography]. The 1953-vs-1954 date discrepancy across the literature resolves as a two-stage institutional chronology: Dai Nippon Spinning Co., Ltd. (later Unitika) approved the women's-team founding on **November 27, 1953**, and the team was officially stood up at the Kaizuka factory on **March 15, 1954** [witches-of-orient-wikipedia]. Sources citing 1953 refer to the company decision and Daimatsu's coaching appointment; sources citing 1954 refer to the team's on-the-factory-floor official establishment. Company sports clubs were the dominant pathway to elite women's volleyball in postwar Japan; the Nichibo roster was drawn from young women working at the mill who trained after their factory shifts [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-2017-tough-love].

Under Daimatsu, Nichibo Kaizuka rapidly took over the Japanese domestic league. That dominance opened the door to international competition under 6-player rules — a real adaptation, because Japan's native domestic game was the 9-player code. Daimatsu "synthesized gymnastics into totally new concepts in volleyball," producing technique innovations that made the team viable internationally despite their size deficit against European and Soviet opponents [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame].

The international competitive arc:

- **1960 World Championship (Brazil):** silver medal (Japan finished behind the Soviet Union) [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame].
- **1962 World Championship (Moscow):** gold medal — Japan defeated the Soviet Union on Soviet soil [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame].
- **1964 Olympic Games (Tokyo):** gold medal in the inaugural women's Olympic volleyball event, again over the USSR (3-0 in the final) [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-2017-tough-love].
- **175 consecutive international wins** by Japan during the run [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame].

After the Tokyo gold, Daimatsu retired from the national-team role in 1965 and traveled to the People's Republic of China at Beijing's invitation to help build a Chinese women's program — an early instance of formal cross-border coaching exchange in the sport [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame]. He subsequently entered politics: in **1968** he ran as a Liberal Democratic Party candidate and was elected to the **House of Councillors** (Sangiin), the upper chamber of Japan's National Diet, and continued to publish [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-wikipedia-biography]. He died November 24, 1978 in Tokyo, age 57, and was enshrined in the International Volleyball Hall of Fame in 2000 [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [daimatsu-wikipedia-biography].

## Core teaching principles

**Volume as compensation for size.** Daimatsu's foundational premise was that Japan's women, on average significantly shorter than the Soviet, Polish, or American teams they had to beat, could not match opponents on physical attributes and therefore had to outwork them on every other variable [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. The operational consequence was a regimen that ran six-plus hours per day, year-round, with effectively one week off around Easter — summarized in retrospective accounts as **"7 days a week, 51 weeks a year"** [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. Evening sessions extended past the 7 PM company supper, with Daimatsu accelerating intensity rather than pausing for meals when the team was failing a drill [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. Players' lives outside training were essentially eliminated, and Daimatsu characterized this as a feature: "There is time for nothing else. The players know absolutely no other life" [daimatsu-2017-tough-love].

**Receive-everything defense via the rolling dive.** The signature Daimatsu drill was *kaiten reshibu* (回転レシーブ) — "receive and rotate," the repeated full-body somersault recovery that lets a defender play a ball past her standing reach and return to ready position via a rolling motion absorbed by the shoulder and back rather than the knees [daimatsu-2017-tough-love] [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame]. The Hall of Fame biography credits this as one of two technique innovations Daimatsu introduced internationally, alongside the **floating change-up service**; both were derived from gymnastics and both rewarded relentless repetition over physical attributes [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame]. The rolling dive became the somatic identity of the team: players performed it on hard wooden floors until exhaustion, and the conditioned reflex to chase every ball, however hopeless the recovery looked, became the team's competitive signature [daimatsu-2017-tough-love].

**Repetition until the technique is autonomic.** Daimatsu's pedagogy did not separate technique from conditioning; the same drill, repeated enough, was both. Players reported that drills ended at a fixed standard, not a fixed time, and the standard was set by the coach's judgment of whether the response had become reflex [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. The principle is the lineage ancestor of practices still recognizable in Japanese youth programs decades later — heavy allocation of training time to pure motor-skill and coordination work, and the cultivation of "a standard of excellence rather than expectations to meet" [japan-2016-five-strengths].

**Training the will, not only the skill.** Player-era accounts describe what was being trained as the willingness to continue past exhaustion, as much as the volleyball technique itself [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. The disciplinary register Daimatsu used was severe; to players who were faltering, his blunt formulation was "If you'd rather be home with your mother, then go. We don't want you here" [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. Captain Masae Kasai later reframed the same atmosphere as trust rather than coercion: "I had a lot of trust and respect for Coach Daimatsu... He was a man we could trust" [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. Both characterizations are in the record; Daimatsu's program required them to be true simultaneously.

## Contributions to the game

**The Witches of the Orient phenomenon.** The 1964 Tokyo gold made the Nichibo-based Japan women — internationally the [[witches-of-the-orient]] (*Toyo no Majo*) — a domestic cultural event without much precedent in any sport, and a public symbol of postwar national reconstruction tied directly to the Olympics Tokyo had just hosted [daimatsu-2017-tough-love] [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame]. The October 23, 1964 final against the USSR at Komazawa Olympic Park drew an NHK-measured **66.8% average television rating** with peak ratings **above 80%** of the viewing population during the match — the most-watched sporting event in Japanese television history, a distinction that still held more than five decades later [tokyo-1964-women-volleyball-japan-gold-olympics].

**Two technique innovations carried into the international game.** The rolling dive and the floating change-up serve are both attributed in the institutional record specifically to Daimatsu's adaptation work, and both diffused outward from his program into the international game [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame]. The rolling-dive lineage in particular runs through the entire post-1964 Japanese defensive tradition — the dig-up-everything posture, the willingness to leave the feet, the libero-era emphasis on ground-game recovery — even where later coaches have rejected the broader severity of his training methods.

**Books and a public coaching voice.** Daimatsu published two widely circulated coaching books — *Don't Hesitate, but Just Follow Me* (often shortened to *Follow Me*) and *If You Try It Harder, You Can Undoubtedly Accomplish It* — both codifying the volume-and-will philosophy for a Japanese coaching audience [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame].

**Cross-border coaching exchange.** Daimatsu's 1965 work in China to help build the women's program is among the earliest documented instances of formal cross-border high-performance coaching exchange in volleyball, predating by more than a decade the period in which Chinese women's volleyball would itself become a global power [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame].

**Anchor of a contested tradition.** Daimatsu is the historical anchor of the [[japanese-training]] school: defensive identity, ground-game emphasis, and very high training volumes that distinguish Japanese national programs from contemporaneous Western traditions [daimatsu-2000-hall-of-fame] [japan-2016-five-strengths]. Modern Japanese coaches have inherited the defensive-priority and repetition emphases while moving away from the absolutist hours-and-severity of the Daimatsu era. This profile does *not* assert a direct line of descent to specific later national-team coaches; in particular, the Munich 1972 men's gold-medal head coach is not identified in the sources captured for this cluster, and Yuichi Nakagaichi (born 1967) is the 2017-2021 men's head coach, not the Munich-era architect a casual reading might assume [nakagaichi-2021-biography].

## A note on the abuse / tough-love debate

The contemporary record contains two strong, opposing voices, and this profile preserves both without resolving them. The 1964 *Sports Illustrated* article "Driven Beyond Dignity" documented players "openly sobbing" under physical punishment, and labor-union complaints about the Nichibo schedule were ignored by company management at the time [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. Against that, the players themselves — Kasai and her teammates — credited the methods for the undefeated run, characterized the coach-team relationship as one of trust, and continued to defend the program in interviews decades later [daimatsu-2017-tough-love]. By any modern Western safeguarding standard the regimen would not be ethically defensible if introduced today; by the historical standard of consent and result, the players carried it through and won.
