# Mike Hebert

## Overview

Mike Hebert is an American NCAA women's volleyball head coach — Pittsburgh, New Mexico, Illinois, and Minnesota across a 35-year career — who finished 952–392 with five NCAA Final Four appearances, 1985 AVCA National Coach of the Year honors, and an AVCA presidency from 1987 to 1990 [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. His 2013 book *Thinking Volleyball* (Human Kinetics) is his published coaching statement: a philosophical and programmatic treatise rather than a drill compendium, organized around the thesis that a coach should be "ready, willing, and able to think beyond the conventional" only after understanding why convention exists in the first place [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. Hebert is most often associated in coach circles with the "reading defense" approach — players posture to set/hitter tendencies rather than to fixed rotational positions — which the book treats as his signature tactical contribution [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball].

**Disambiguation.** Hebert's *Thinking Volleyball* is the Human Kinetics, 2013 book (ISBN 9781450442626). It is not the same as [[hugh-mccutcheon]]'s 2022 *Championship Behaviors* (Triumph Books). Some coach-community summaries have mislabeled *Thinking Volleyball* as McCutcheon's title; the two books share an audience and some themes (motor learning, program culture) but are distinct works by distinct authors [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. When citing, always anchor with author plus year.

## Coaching career

### Pittsburgh, New Mexico (1976–1982)
Hebert coached both men's and women's programs at Pittsburgh from 1976 to 1979, finishing 128–53 with the women and 60–21 with the men, before taking over the women's head-coaching job at New Mexico (1980–1982), where he went 60–57 across three seasons and reached the NCAA regionals in 1981 [hebert-wikipedia-bio][hebert-usav-obituary].

### Illinois — rise to national prominence (1983–1995)
Hebert led the [[illinois-volleyball]] Fighting Illini for 13 seasons, finishing 323–127 with four Big Ten titles and two NCAA Final Four appearances [hebert-usav-obituary][hebert-illinois-hof]. The 1988 Illinois squad was the first volleyball team east of the Mississippi River to be rated No. 1 nationally [hebert-illinois-hof]. He earned the 1985 AVCA National Coach of the Year award at Illinois [hebert-wikipedia-bio][hebert-illinois-hof]. Hebert served as AVCA President across a three-year term during this period — published sources disagree on the exact span: the Illinois Hall of Fame page and the USAV obituary give 1985–1988 [hebert-illinois-hof][hebert-usav-obituary], while Hebert's own book lists 1987–1990 [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball].

### Minnesota — late-career capstone (1996–2010)
Hebert's final head-coaching job was at the [[minnesota-volleyball]] Golden Gophers for 15 seasons, where he finished 381–137 overall (173–76 Big Ten) with 11 NCAA Tournament appearances [hebert-wikipedia-bio][hebert-usav-obituary]. He won Big Ten Coach of the Year at Minnesota in 1999 and 2002, AVCA Mideast Region Coach of the Year in 2002, and Volleyball Magazine National Coach of the Year in 2003 [hebert-wikipedia-bio]. He announced his retirement at the end of the 2010 season [hebert-usav-obituary].

### Career totals and honors
Hebert's published career record is 952–392 across 35 years of head coaching, with five NCAA Final Four appearances [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball][hebert-usav-obituary]. USA Volleyball lists several coaching distinctions unique to him: only coach to achieve 300 wins at two different NCAA Division I programs, only coach to lead two different programs from the same conference to the national semifinal, and only coach to bring four different programs to the NCAA postseason tournament [hebert-usav-obituary]. He produced 17 All-Americans including Lindsey Berg, Nicole Branagh, Mary Eggers, and Cassie Busse [hebert-usav-obituary]. Hall-of-fame induction years: AVCA Hall of Fame 2006, USA Volleyball Donald S. Shondell All-Time Great Coach 2011, University of Illinois Athletics Hall of Fame 2023 (posthumous) [hebert-wikipedia-bio][hebert-usav-obituary][hebert-illinois-hof]. He served USA Volleyball as head or assistant coach on multiple international assignments between 1987 and 2013, including the 2003 Pan American Games (head coach, bronze) and 2011 Pan American Games (assistant, bronze) [hebert-usav-obituary].

## Core teaching principles

### 1. Think beyond convention — but only after understanding why the convention exists
The organizing thesis of *Thinking Volleyball* is that coaches should be "ready, willing, and able to think beyond the conventional" [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. Hebert is careful to distinguish this stance from reckless contrarianism: critical thinking requires understanding why a convention exists, what it solves, and what its limitations are before replacing it [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. The operational read for other coaches is that innovation has a prerequisite — fluency in the received tradition — and that "different" is not the same as "better."

### 2. Program management is a first-class coaching skill
Of the 10 chapters in *Thinking Volleyball*, only two are narrowly tactical (offense and defense); the remaining eight treat program management, organizational structure, team playing style, gym culture, trust, match coaching, team policy, goals, event management, injured-player management, and communication systems as first-class concerns [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. The book's shape is itself the argument: a head coach's effectiveness is determined at least as much by structural decisions outside of practice drills as by anything that happens inside them [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball].

### 3. Reading defense over rigid base defense
Hebert's signature tactical contribution is the reading-defense approach: defenders read set and hitter tendencies and posture themselves accordingly, rather than playing a rigid rotational base [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. The idea anticipates the read-oriented defense language that later became common across [[gold-medal-squared]] and Art of Coaching materials, though the on-file biographical sources for Hebert — Wikipedia, the USAV obituary, and the Illinois Hall of Fame page — do not trace specific lines of influence or citation between Hebert's formulation and the later GMS/AOC framings [unsourced]. The coaching consequence is that defensive teaching has to include explicit work on what cues to read and when, not just where to stand.

### 4. Gym culture and trust rival systems in program outcomes
*Thinking Volleyball* devotes substantial attention to team chemistry, trust-building, learned optimism, and the communication systems that make a program function under pressure [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. Hebert's framing is that a well-designed system executed by a fractured group produces worse outcomes than an ordinary system executed by a cohesive group — so culture work is not the soft complement to tactics, it is a primary driver of the scoreboard [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball].

### 5. Statistics skepticism from a former evangelist
Hebert began his career as an advocate for detailed statistical analysis but later came to question whether the time cost of collecting and processing extensive statistics actually paid back in program outcomes [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. The resulting stance is not anti-data — he proposes alternative analytical approaches — but it is a deliberate downgrade of stat-collection-as-a-goal in favor of information that changes coach behavior in-match [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. For working coaches, the portable lesson is to measure the thing that will change a decision, not the thing that is easy to count.

### 6. Learned optimism as a coaching variable
Hebert treats learned optimism — the explicit training of how athletes explain setbacks to themselves — as something a coach builds into the program, not as a personality trait an athlete either has or doesn't have [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. This is one of several threads in the book that connects coaching to broader mental-performance literature rather than to volleyball-only frames.

## Contributions to the game

### A philosophical coaching text aimed at experienced coaches
*Thinking Volleyball* (Human Kinetics, 2013) is Hebert's most durable contribution outside of program results. It is explicitly not a drill book, and that is itself a choice: the book addresses program design, policy, culture, and critical thinking as the content an experienced coach most needs in writing, on the theory that drills are easy to obtain elsewhere and good judgment about why to run a drill is not [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. Third-party reviewers note that personal anecdotes run throughout the book and are biased toward positive outcomes, which is worth knowing when extracting general principles from his case studies [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball].

### Multi-decade NCAA program building
Four head-coaching stops (Pittsburgh, New Mexico, Illinois, Minnesota) across 35 years and 952 wins is the on-paper contribution — a body of work rather than a single signature team [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball][hebert-usav-obituary]. Illinois's rise to national contention under Hebert and the 1985 AVCA National Coach of the Year honor anchor the program-building reputation [hebert-wikipedia-bio][hebert-illinois-hof]. USA Volleyball notes three distinctions unique to Hebert as a multi-program builder: 300+ wins at two different D-I programs, two different programs from the same conference led to the national semifinal, and all four of his programs taken to the NCAA postseason [hebert-usav-obituary]. His peer-level mentor role for current national-team coaches is captured in Karch Kiraly's tribute that he "looked to him as a mentor" [aoc-2019-hebert-a-coach-who-mattered]; players developed under Hebert include Olympic setter Lindsey Berg, Nicole Branagh, Mary Eggers, and Cassie Busse [hebert-usav-obituary]. A full enumeration of Hebert assistants who became head coaches is not laid out in any single on-file source; Kelly Sheffield (Wisconsin) and Scott Swanson (South Carolina) are named in peer tributes as coaches who cite Hebert's influence, but a comprehensive coaching tree remains an open research item [unsourced].

### AVCA governance
Hebert served as AVCA President for a three-year term during a formative period in the organization's growth — book-sourced as 1987–1990 [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball] and Illinois/USAV-sourced as 1985–1988 [hebert-illinois-hof][hebert-usav-obituary]. The governance work sits alongside 30+ years of USA Volleyball contribution (international head-coaching assignments in 1987, 1991, and 2003; assistant assignments in 2011 and 2013) as a second strand of profession-wide influence distinct from the program-level work [hebert-usav-obituary].

### GMS / McGown-lineage affiliation
Hebert is often grouped with the [[gold-medal-squared]] / [[carl-mcgown]] methodological lineage in coach-community discussions, particularly on motor-learning-grounded coaching [unsourced]. He does not appear on the contemporary GMS coaching roster on record, and none of the primary biographical sources now on file — the USAV obituary [hebert-usav-obituary], the Wikipedia bio [hebert-wikipedia-bio], the Illinois Hall of Fame page [hebert-illinois-hof], or the AOC tribute [aoc-2019-hebert-a-coach-who-mattered] — mention a GMS affiliation, McGown collaboration, or shared clinic work. The AOC tribute instead positions Hebert as a peer-mentor figure whose influence traveled through individual relationships rather than through a branded methodology [aoc-2019-hebert-a-coach-who-mattered]. The GMS tag is retained in the frontmatter for research-backfill reasons; it should be treated as a hypothesis until a primary or secondary source is found.

### Reading-defense framing
Hebert's reading-defense framing is the tactical idea most directly associated with his name in coach circles [hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]. Later reading-oriented defensive teaching across [[gold-medal-squared]], Art of Coaching Volleyball, and USA Volleyball materials is consonant with Hebert's framing, though neither the Wikipedia bio [hebert-wikipedia-bio], the USAV obituary [hebert-usav-obituary], nor the Illinois Hall of Fame page [hebert-illinois-hof] traces explicit lines of influence or acknowledgment between Hebert's formulation and the GMS/AOC/USAV teaching vocabularies — none of those later schools cites *Thinking Volleyball* directly in the public materials surveyed [unsourced].
