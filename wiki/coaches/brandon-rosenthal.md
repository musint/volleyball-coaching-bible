# Brandon Rosenthal

## Overview

Brandon Rosenthal is the head coach of the Lipscomb University women's volleyball program and an ongoing Art of Coaching Volleyball (AOC) contributor whose public footprint in the wiki's ingested sample is concentrated on competitive team-drill design [aoc-2015-rosenthal-rapid-fire] [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. Across a nearly decade-long AOC publishing window, his bylined pieces share a single throughline: pack the most consequential volleyball actions per minute into a simple scoring frame, then make the rally — not the coach's whistle — drive the next rep [aoc-2015-rosenthal-rapid-fire] [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. ## Core teaching principles

**Rep density through back-to-back entries.** Rosenthal's rapid-fire drill is built on the premise that a team can simulate rally rhythm without live opposition if the coach enters the next ball the instant the previous action ends. Six players sit in base; the coach enters a free ball; the team attacks; the coach immediately enters a coverage ball; the team transitions to attack again; groups rotate every three or so balls [aoc-2015-rosenthal-rapid-fire]. The design choice is to collapse the gap between contacts — no reset between the free-ball attack and the coverage ball — so the team reps the transition-attack-to-coverage-attack chain at a density most 6v6 play can't deliver [aoc-2015-rosenthal-rapid-fire].

**Single-score scoreboards as competitive scaffolding.** In the 2024 Volleyball Tug of War drill, both teams play a single shared scoreboard that starts at 3 and can be driven up to 6 or down to 0. Every rally moves the number one point in the winning team's direction; the rally winner also earns the next downball entry [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. The mechanical shift from two-team competing totals to one shared number makes every rally directly and symmetrically consequential — the losing team isn't just failing to score, they're watching an opposing point arrive in the same tick of the clock [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war].

**Close-out as the trainable goal.** Rosenthal's explicit coaching point on the tug-of-war drill is that it is not about reaching game point — it is about finishing [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. The narrow 0-to-6 range is chosen so games resolve in a few minutes and can be run as a competitive block multiple times per practice, which keeps the close-out moment recurring rather than rare [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. Combined with a momentum-multiplier variation (consecutive wins can double or triple the point swing), the drill rewards runs — and implicitly trains the psychological challenge of not letting an opponent's run go unanswered [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war].

**Scoring systems that reward kill intent.** Rosenthal's framing is that the tug-of-war structure pressures hitters to swing to finish rather than swing to keep the ball in play [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. This is a coaching-cue layer on top of the scoring mechanic: the scoreboard design rewards kills, and the hitter's shot selection follows [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war].

## Contributions to the game

**Rapid-fire drill design.** The 2015 AOC rapid-fire piece gives the drill a durable name inside the AOC drill vocabulary. The specific contribution is compact: a six-player rotation in which the coach enters two balls in close succession (free ball → coverage ball) so the team reps the coverage-to-transition-attack chain at maximum density, with short rotations keeping freshness high and reps distributed across groups [aoc-2015-rosenthal-rapid-fire].

**Tug-of-war scoring as a portable drill mechanic.** The 2024 tug-of-war drill contributes a scoring template — shared scoreboard, bidirectional push, rally-winner serves next, momentum multipliers, close-out emphasis — that is usable on any 6v6 or small-sided team drill, not just the team-offense context it's published under [aoc-2024-rosenthal-tug-of-war]. ## Quotes & representative passages
