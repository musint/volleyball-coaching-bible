## Overview
Mike Lingenfelter is a co-director of [[munciana-volleyball-club]], a nationally-recognized girls' club program based in Muncie, Indiana, and a recurring contributor to [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]]'s (AOC) video library [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo]. His public coaching voice on AOC centers on four linked pieces: a disciplined three-tempo framework for middle attackers, accountability-driven passing design built on high-volume reps, youth warmup drills scaled for middle-school entry-level practices, and — with co-director Alyssa Lingenfelter — a club-wide skill-training system that keeps players progressing across age groups without having to relearn technique [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing] [aoc-2021-lingenfelter-short-ball-long-ball] [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. For coaches at nationally-recognized clubs — Song's immediate context — Lingenfelter is the most operationally relevant voice on AOC on the specific question of how a multi-team club runs consistent technical training end to end.

## Coaching career
Lingenfelter's coaching career is anchored at Munciana Volleyball Club, where he serves as co-director alongside Alyssa Lingenfelter [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. Munciana is an Indiana-based nationally-recognized club program; the identifying detail in Munciana's publicly-shared coaching philosophy is that every age group in the club runs the same skill-training and systems vocabulary, so players graduate upward without relearning basics [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].

His AOC publishing record reaches back to at least January 2021 with a short-ball/long-ball pursuit warmup drill published on the AOC video platform [aoc-2021-lingenfelter-short-ball-long-ball]. His more substantial 2026 AOC corpus — two middle-tempo videos and a competitive-passing video — landed in February 2026 and sits behind AOC's premium paywall [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing]. The Coach Connection LIVE session that Mike and Alyssa Lingenfelter ran on September 25, 2025 included a downloadable sample Munciana practice plan — rare public visibility into how a nationally-recognized club structures a single session [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].

## Core teaching principles

### 1. Middle-attack tempo defined by left-foot plant timing
Lingenfelter's middle-attack framework defines three tempos — Tempo 3, Tempo 2, Tempo 1 — **indexed by when the middle plants the left foot relative to the setter's contact** [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo]. All three tempos share the same three-step approach; the only variable is plant timing:

- **Tempo 3** (high ball to the pins): middle plants the left foot **once the ball has left the setter's hands** [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo].
- **Tempo 2**: middle plants the left foot **while the ball is in the setter's hands** [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo].
- **Tempo 1** (true quick): middle is **already in the air while the ball is in the setter's hands** [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo].

The coaching cue is deliberately non-abstract — it is tied to one observable event (left foot hits the floor) and one observable reference (ball leaving the setter's hands). That choice is the principle: tempo is trained as a timing relationship between two visible moments, not as a speed attribute of the hit itself.

The training progression starts at the net, has the middle pull off, then has the middle approach to hit a coach's toss [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo]. That sequence — start-net, pull-off, approach-to-attack — simulates the in-game transition timing a middle actually faces rather than the cleaner open-ended rep a non-transition tempo drill would produce [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo]. Later progressions add a self-set off a coach's toss, then a platform-to-net pass followed by a hitter's attack, then a full passer-to-setter-to-attacker sequence [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo]. Team-level drills — **Pin to Pin** and **Repeat Recovery** — close the progression [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo].

### 2. Rep-based confidence
"Reps equal confidence. The more I can rep, the more I feel good" is Lingenfelter's single-sentence summary of how skill training produces match-day belief [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing]. Operationally, this drives a high-volume rep bias in passing drills — controlled coach tosses with immediate feedback → tosses thrown from over the net → live serves — each stage emphasizing different coaching points but all carrying enough volume to let confidence compound [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing]. The framing is specific rather than generic: Lingenfelter does not just assert that practice matters; he asserts that per-rep confidence accumulation is the mechanism, which implies a practice-design constraint (don't starve the skill of reps in the name of variety).

### 3. Accountability built into every drill
"Every passing drill should have a winner and a loser" [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing]. Lingenfelter's passing drills are competitive by design — someone wins, someone loses, scoring is tracked. That sits adjacent to Kiraly's "compete every day in the gym" principle ([[karch-kiraly]]) but applied at the club-level drill unit rather than at the national-team roster level [aoc-2023-kiraly-competing-in-practice]. The cultural implication: accountability is structural, not rhetorical — the drill itself creates the pressure; the coach does not have to manufacture it.

### 4. Quiet platform + balance + pass-and-cover as trainable micro-skills
Lingenfelter names four focal points inside his passing training that most coaches treat as assumptions [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing]:

- **Balance** — trained by having players pass while standing on one foot [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing].
- **Quiet platform** — trained with one-armed passes and with multiple passes while moving side to side [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing].
- **Pass and cover** — players pass, then sprint to a designated spot, preventing the common habit of passers watching their own pass [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing].
- **Avoiding jammed passes** — passers hop back and take a jamming ball lower on the platform rather than reaching up into the chest [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing].

Each one is a named sub-skill with a named drill. The principle embedded here is that passing is decomposable into trainable micro-components, and the coach's job is to name the failure mode and supply the drill that targets it [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing].

### 5. Club-wide system alignment
The Munciana operating thesis, publicly articulated in the September 2025 Coach Connection LIVE session, is that **club-wide consistency of skill technique and systems is itself a strategic advantage — players never have to relearn** as they move through age groups [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. The same technique vocabulary, the same system shapes, the same drill names apply from the youngest Munciana teams through the oldest [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. For a multi-team club, this is a club-architecture choice more than a practice-planning choice — and it contrasts with the more common club pattern of each team running its own head coach's preferred system.

Four sub-principles named in the same session operationalize this [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]:

- **Two teams on one court** is a designed structure, not improvisation — practice plans account for the court share [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].
- **Short teams do not mechanically split hitting and blocking time equally** — allocation follows what wins sets at that team's competitive level [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].
- **A dual-position player needs a designed role during positional training** — not left to improvisation [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].
- **Self-feedback capability is a developmental milestone** that has a concrete age, not just "older teams can do it" [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide].

The Coach Connection session also surfaced a philosophical position on **pay-to-practice vs. pay-to-play** club economics, which Munciana treats as a values choice rather than an operational one [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. The specific Munciana position on that question is not spelled out in the source on file here [unsourced].

### 6. Warmups as skill-training, not conditioning
The short-ball/long-ball warmup drill — three passers, two on the 10-foot line and one middle back, coach feeds rapid-fire short/long/short balls, rotation through positions, target 15-20 solid passes before rest — is tagged as middle-school and beginner/youth material [aoc-2021-lingenfelter-short-ball-long-ball]. The design choice is the content of the principle: even a warmup carries pursuit-passing reps with rotation pressure, rather than generic ball-control touches. Lingenfelter's youth warmups are practice content from the first whistle.

## Contributions to the game

### A publicly-shared club-wide alignment model
Many successful clubs run club-wide alignment internally; Munciana's contribution is **publishing** the operating choices on AOC, with downloadable sample practice plans and question-and-answer sessions [aoc-2025-cc-skill-training-clubwide]. For a coach at another nationally-recognized club wrestling with multi-team consistency, the Munciana Coach Connection session is one of the few widely-available primary sources on how an Indiana-caliber club designs end-to-end skill continuity.

### A concrete middle-tempo framework coaches can adopt verbatim
The three-tempo framework — indexed by left-foot plant timing relative to setter contact — is immediately usable as-is [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-tempo] [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-middle-attack-tempo]. That is not trivial. Many coach-educators on middle-attack tempo describe tempos by ball-height or by set-hang-time; Lingenfelter's version is anchored on **two observable events** (ball-leaves-setter, left-foot-plants) that a coach can call out in real time from the sideline. The adoption cost for a club that wants to standardize middle-tempo vocabulary across teams is low.

### Accountability-first passing drill design
Lingenfelter's "every drill has a winner and a loser" [aoc-2026-lingenfelter-accountability-passing] is one of the cleanest operational expressions of competitive practice at the club level. It pairs naturally with [[practice-planning]] frameworks that emphasize game-like training and ties into age-appropriate practice design for the youngest competitive groups ([[age-lens-14u]]).
