# Mick Haley

## Overview

Mick Haley is one of the most-credentialed multi-program head coaches in NCAA women's volleyball history: head coach of three NCAA national-championship teams (Texas 1988; USC 2002 and 2003), USA Women's National Team head coach 1997–2000, and AVCA Hall of Fame inductee [haley-wikipedia-bio]. Born August 18, 1943 in Angola, Indiana, Haley played setter at Ball State University under Don Shondell's program, winning MIVA titles in 1964 and 1965, then opened his coaching career at Kellogg Community College (1973–1979) where he won back-to-back NJCAA national titles in 1978 and 1979 with a 251–51 overall record [haley-wikipedia-bio]. From there he took the Texas head-coaching job in 1980, where his 17-season tenure produced the AIAW championship (1981), the 1988 NCAA national championship (Texas's first NCAA volleyball title), and three Final Four appearances (1986, 1987, 1995) on a 522–137–1 record [haley-wikipedia-bio]. He left Texas after the 1996 season to take the USA Women's NT head-coach role through the Sydney 2000 Olympics (fourth-place finish), then returned to the NCAA at USC in 2001 [haley-wikipedia-bio]. The USC tenure produced consecutive NCAA championships in 2002 and 2003, with the 2003 team going undefeated 35–0 — the first NCAA repeat champion to do so unbeaten — and earning Haley the 2003 AVCA National Coach of the Year award [haley-wikipedia-bio]. He retired from USC after the 2017 season.

In the Texas-volleyball lineage that this wiki tracks, Haley is the pre-Elliott era anchor: he built the program's national-championship credentials before Jerritt Elliott extended the tradition with subsequent NCAA titles.

The transferable principle is that the championship-program-building methodology survives the institutional transition; the Haley track record is the empirical evidence.

2. **Undefeated repeat-champion proof of culture.** The 2003 USC team's 35–0 undefeated repeat-champion run is, on the Wikipedia record, unique in NCAA history [haley-wikipedia-bio]. The structural read: an undefeated championship season requires the team to close every match (including ones the program could plausibly drop on a road trip or with a banged-up roster). ## Contributions to the game

- **First Texas NCAA national championship (1988).** Beating the field in 1988 put Texas on the NCAA volleyball-championship map and is the foundational credential of the Texas-volleyball tradition [haley-wikipedia-bio]. The 1988 title is what subsequent Texas head coaches — including Jerritt Elliott — built on. The 2024 AVCA Champion Texas program traces its institutional pedigree to this 1988 championship [[texas-volleyball]].
- **First repeat-undefeated NCAA champion (USC 2003).** The 35–0 second-consecutive-NCAA-championship USC team is, on the Wikipedia record, the first NCAA repeat champion to complete the title run undefeated [haley-wikipedia-bio]. The 2003 USC season is a case study in sustaining the championship culture after the breakthrough title.
- **USA Women's National Team head-coach lineage.** Haley's 1997–2000 USA Women's NT head-coach tenure — culminating in Sydney 2000 — places him in the program's head-coach lineage that runs through Toshiaki Yoshida, Lang Ping, Hugh McCutcheon, Karch Kiraly, and Erik Sullivan [haley-wikipedia-bio]. He was the program's head coach across the run-up to a quadrennial Olympics, a tenure pattern shared with most subsequent USA Women's NT head coaches.
- **Shondell-tree placement.** As a Ball State setter under Don Shondell who became a multi-NCAA-champion head coach, Haley is one of the most-decorated graduates of the Shondell coaching tree, alongside subsequent generations of Ball State alumni who became collegiate head coaches [haley-wikipedia-bio]. This is a structural-pedigree claim worth highlighting because the Shondell tree is documented at depth at this wiki via the *Volleyball Coaching Bible* (Shondell ed.) source pages.
