# Hugh McCutcheon

## Overview

Hugh McCutcheon is a New Zealand-born, United States-based coach whose primary body of head-coaching work is in **women's volleyball**: four years with the USA Women's National Team (2009–2012, London 2012 silver, three FIVB World Grand Prix titles, world #1 ranking in 2011 and 2012) followed by a decade as head coach at the [[university-of-minnesota]] (2012–2022, three NCAA Final Fours, two Big Ten regular-season titles, AVCA National Coach of the Year 2015) [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. Of his 17 years as a head coach across men's and women's programs, **fourteen were spent coaching women's volleyball** — the bulk of his career, his most decorated stretch, and the lens that matters most for any coach in the women's-game pipeline [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

He is also the only head coach in volleyball history to medal at the Olympics with both a men's and a women's program: gold with the USA Men in Beijing 2008 (his first national-team posting) and silver with the USA Women in London 2012 [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. He published *Championship Behaviors: A Model for Competitive Excellence in Sports* (Triumph Books, 2022) — a framework text built around motor learning, pattern recognition, and athlete-centered teaching [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors]. He is widely regarded, alongside [[karch-kiraly]], as one of the defining American volleyball voices of the post-2000 era and now serves as FIVB Secretary General (elected November 2024) [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

**Disambiguation.** McCutcheon's book is *Championship Behaviors* (2022). The similarly titled *Thinking Volleyball* (Human Kinetics, 2013) is by [[mike-hebert]], not McCutcheon. The two books are often confused in coach circles and should be cited distinctly [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors].

## Coaching career

**Player and early coaching (1980s–2001).** A New Zealand junior-national-team middle blocker, McCutcheon played collegiately at [[byu]], then professionally in Finland and Japan before earning BA, MS (Exercise Science), and MBA degrees at BYU [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. He returned to BYU as an assistant to [[carl-mcgown]] from 1995–2001, a period during which the Cougars won NCAA men's titles in 1999 and 2001 [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. He finished his playing and early-coaching arc with a 2001–2002 Austrian-league sweep at Vienna Hotvolleys [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

**USA Men (2005–2008): Beijing gold.** Hired as head coach of the USA Men's National Team after serving as an assistant, McCutcheon led the program to its first Olympic gold since 1988 at Beijing 2008 [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav]. The campaign was marked by tragedy: on the day of the opening ceremony his father-in-law Todd Bachman was killed and mother-in-law Barbara seriously injured in an attack at Beijing's Drum Tower. Assistant coach Ron Larsen served as acting head coach for three matches with advisory support from John Speraw, Marv Dunphy, and Carl McGown; McCutcheon returned August 16 for the China pool match [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav]. Team USA defeated Brazil in the final, with middle blocker David Lee blocking decisively and opposite Clay Stanley — tournament MVP — delivering the clinching kill. The same quadrennial produced USA's first-ever FIVB World League title (2008) [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav].

**USA Women (2008/2009–2012): London silver, three Grand Prix, world #1.** McCutcheon was announced as the USA Women's head coach on December 15, 2008, immediately after the Beijing run [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. The four-year tenure produced FIVB World Grand Prix golds in **2010, 2011, and 2012** — defeating Brazil in the final each time — and a world #1 FIVB ranking in 2011 and 2012, neither of which the program had previously held [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. The London 2012 Olympic campaign ended with a four-set loss to Brazil in the gold-medal match for silver [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. He was named USOC Volleyball Coach of the Year in 2010 and 2011 [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. His cumulative record with USA Women was 106–39 (.731) [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. A consequential late-tenure decision was bringing in [[karch-kiraly]] as an assistant coach and grooming him as the program's successor head coach — a hand-off that has held: Kiraly took over in 2013 and has run USA Women ever since, winning World Championship gold (2014), Olympic gold (Tokyo 2020/21), and continuing the program's top-three world ranking [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

**University of Minnesota (2012–2022): the women's-college decade.** McCutcheon was hired by Minnesota on February 10, 2011, and began coaching the [[minnesota-volleyball]] Golden Gophers in fall 2012 immediately after London [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. Over eleven seasons he went **277–74 (.789)** — the highest winning percentage in program history — and built Minnesota into a Big Ten power that did not exist before his arrival [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. The headline accomplishments:

- **Two Big Ten regular-season titles** (2015, 2018) and **two Big Ten Coach of the Year awards** (2015, 2018) [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]
- **Three NCAA Final Fours** (2015, 2016, 2019); **five Elite Eights**; **ten Sweet Sixteens**, including eight consecutive Sweet 16-or-better appearances — a sustained-excellence run that few non-blueblood programs have matched in the modern NCAA women's tournament [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]
- **AVCA National Coach of the Year, 2015** [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]
- **2× AVCA North Region Coach of the Year** (2013, 2015) [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]
- **6 Big Ten Players of the Year**, **1 National Player of the Year**, and **26 All-Americans** developed at Minnesota during the tenure [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]

Minnesota under McCutcheon is the cleanest live demonstration in the modern NCAA D-I women's volleyball record of the *Championship Behaviors* framework applied at the college level — the same athlete-centered, behavior-focused, science-rigorous teaching he later wrote up in book form was first run as a multi-year program at a single school [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors][mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. He announced on October 16, 2022 that 2022 would be his last season and stepped into a Minnesota Assistant AD / Sport Development role on January 1, 2023 [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

**Post-coaching (2023–present).** He joined the FIVB as Senior Advisor in 2023 and was elected Secretary General in November 2024 [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. His combined head-coaching record across Minnesota, USA Men, and USA Women is 490–146 (.770) [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. Honors include the International Volleyball Hall of Fame (2018), USA Volleyball Hall of Fame (2024), AVCA Hall of Fame (2024), and MNZM (Member of the New Zealand Order of Merit, 2016) for services to volleyball [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio].

## Core teaching principles

1. **Behaviors over outcomes.** The organizing thesis of *Championship Behaviors* is that "championship results require championship behaviors — it's as simple as that" [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors]. Outcomes are lagging indicators; the coach's job is to install the daily habits — technical, tactical, mental, physical — that make winning repeatable. The frame splits responsibility cleanly: athletes work, learn, and compete; coaches teach, coach, and mentor [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors].

2. **Play well, not just hard.** McCutcheon's framing in his VolleyBrains interview centers on "committing to a standard of competitive excellence" rather than chasing wins per se — winning flows from excellence, not the other way around [mccutcheon-2021-volleybrains-ep30]. The corollary is that effort is a floor, not a ceiling: a team that plays hard but not well will still lose to a team that plays well.

3. **Mastery, trust, and empowerment.** A coach's job is to give athletes the tools and mental capacity to read information and make the right choice at the right time — autonomously, because it is the right thing rather than because they fear consequences [mccutcheon-2023-manning-mastery-trust]. If athletes are taught well, they can play well without a coach in the middle; coaches should not get in the way of that [mccutcheon-2023-manning-mastery-trust]. Coaches are service providers who earn authentic influence rather than impose authority [mccutcheon-2023-manning-mastery-trust].

4. **Practice-to-competition transfer as the test.** In *Championship Behaviors* the central test of any drill is whether it transfers to competition; tradition alone is not enough to justify what a team does in practice [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors]. This aligns with his broader stance that coaching ideas should be evaluated with scientific rigor, not accepted because they are conventional [mccutcheon-2021-volleybrains-ep30].

5. **Coaching lives in the gray.** McCutcheon repeatedly argues that coaching is gray rather than absolute, and that polarized, confident claims should be treated skeptically [mccutcheon-2021-volleybrains-ep30]. Patience (development does not happen on the coach's timeline) and intellectual humility are the two traits he recommends to younger coaches [mccutcheon-2021-volleybrains-ep30].

6. **Repeatability, variance, risk management.** At USA Volleyball's High Performance Coaches Clinic, McCutcheon framed skill development around three measurable dimensions: **repeatability** (consistent execution across competition levels, not just on good days), **variance** (minimizing unnecessary movement — "simpler is better, consistency comes with such economy of movement"), and **risk management** (positional choices that enlarge the acceptable-outcome window, e.g., setting the setter's target five to six feet off the net rather than tight) [usav-2026-hp-clinic-lessons-shared].

7. **Generalized specialists, not early specialization.** The development target at the HP level is a player who is broadly skilled across all game areas but exceptional in one or two — a "generalized specialist" — not a narrowly specialized prospect [usav-2026-hp-clinic-lessons-shared]. Foundational sport science, not any one coach's style, is what makes coaching effective [usav-2026-hp-clinic-lessons-shared].

8. **Blocking is overweighted in coach attention.** In his AOC blocking piece, McCutcheon argues that blocking is the skill least correlated with winning and that practice time should be reweighted toward serving, passing, and sideout; middles should have the freedom to read and block independently rather than always closing to the wing [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys]. He is not a fan of soft blocking; swing blocking has a place but is not a universal answer; and the three-person block can be used as a weapon when deployed intentionally [aoc-2015-mccutcheon-blocking-keys].

## Contributions to the game

**Sustained-excellence template at a non-blueblood NCAA women's program.** Minnesota under McCutcheon (2012–2022) is the clearest live demonstration in modern women's D-I of *Championship Behaviors* applied as an eleven-year program, not a one-off run. Three Final Fours, eight consecutive Sweet 16-or-better appearances, AVCA National Coach of the Year, the highest winning percentage in program history, six Big Ten Players of the Year, and 26 All-Americans were produced at a school that was not historically a top-five women's volleyball recruit destination — the gap between recruiting class rankings and on-court output is the operational footprint of the methodology [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. For a girls-club director or HS varsity coach mapping the women's college pathway, this is the program model that did the most with the least pedigree advantage in the McCutcheon-Wise-Rose-Cook generation.

**USA Women coaching-staff hand-off and program continuity.** Recruiting [[karch-kiraly]] onto the USA Women staff during the 2009–2012 run and grooming him as the head-coach successor produced the cleanest national-team continuity arc in the modern era — silver in London 2012 → World Championship gold 2014 → Olympic gold Tokyo 2020/21 — across two head coaches working from a shared system [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. National-team programs typically suffer system discontinuity when the head coach changes; the McCutcheon → Kiraly hand-off is the working counter-example.

**The "competitive cauldron" as practice-design idiom.** McCutcheon and [[laurie-eisler]] popularized the *competitive cauldron* — a practice design in which daily ranking and contest create real stakes without fracturing team cohesion — in a 2013 Calgary Clinic Q&A that has since become a canonical reference for coaches trying to balance internal competition with cohesion [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]. The term is widely used across the AOC and AVCA coach networks today, with Eisler's UNI women's-program track record and McCutcheon's Minnesota and USA Women records as its primary case studies [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron].

**"Coopetition."** The paired concept — internal battles for roles and reps co-existing with shared goals and trust — is McCutcheon's preferred frame for the culture question every head coach faces: how to keep teammates pushing each other without tearing the team apart [aoc-2013-mccutcheon-coopetition].

**Crisis leadership as program design.** The Beijing 2008 run was a live demonstration that a "staff of many" — Ron Larsen, John Speraw, Marv Dunphy, Carl McGown all contributing during McCutcheon's three-match absence — can carry a program through personal catastrophe [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav]. McCutcheon's own framing was the culture point: "It's never been a one-person show. We've always been talking about team — and that goes for the coaches, too" [mccutcheon-2008-beijing-gold-usav].

**Cross-sport coaching influence.** McCutcheon is cited by peers in other sports as a reference point on communication with modern athletes, alongside Mack Brown in college football — explicitly noted as an influence on [[todd-dagenais]]'s approach at UCF [aoc-2018-coaching-iy-generation].

**Integrated defense and blocking language.** McCutcheon's 2015 AOC clinic with [[terry-liskevych]] helped anchor a read-based, integrated view of defense and blocking in which middle blocker decisions and backcourt positioning are taught as one read rather than two skills [aoc-2015-liskevych-mccutcheon-defense-blocking].

**Published framework.** *Championship Behaviors* (Triumph Books, November 15, 2022; foreword by Thad Levine; endorsed by Angela Duckworth) is his definitive published statement — addressed to athletes, parents, and coaches simultaneously, with its largest section dedicated to "The Championship Model for Skill Acquisition and Application" [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors]. The book treats mental and physical health, learning from failure, and empathy toward disruptive players as first-class coaching concerns, and questions the "all-powerful coach" archetype that still shapes a lot of US sports culture [mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors].
