# Jim Stone

## Overview

Jim Stone is an American head coach, author, and clinician whose active career spans roughly five decades: head coach of Ohio State women's volleyball from 1982–2007, then head coach of the U.S. Girls Youth National Team from 2008–2019, and subsequently a lead on-camera faculty member for [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] (AOC), where his bylined pieces on defense, serve-receive design, practice planning, and skill biomechanics run from at least 2016 through 2025 [aoc-2019-stone-practice-priorities] [aoc-2020-stone-morgan-hentz-dominant] [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]. Unlike Kiraly or McCutcheon, Stone's public fingerprint is less a single championship program and more a teaching voice: the AOC archive houses 30+ pieces under his byline or in conversation with national-team peers such as [[terry-liskevych]], [[john-dunning]], and [[tod-mattox]], which has made him one of the most consistently-cited modern American authorities on defensive body positioning, serve-receive pattern selection, and competitive practice design [aoc-2020-stone-liskevych-using-video] [aoc-2020-stone-dunning-effective-practices] [aoc-2019-stone-mattox-utilizing-competition-practice].

## Coaching career

Stone's tenure as head coach at [[ohio-state-volleyball]] from 1982 to 2007 is cited in AOC biographical sidebars as the 25-season anchor of his public identity [aoc-2018-stone-platform-management] [aoc-2019-stone-practice-priorities] [aoc-2020-stone-morgan-hentz-dominant]. After leaving Ohio State, he moved into the [[usa-volleyball]] pipeline as head coach of the U.S. Girls Youth National Team from 2008 through 2019 — a post-collegiate lane that aligned him structurally with the age-appropriate development work that now runs through USA Volleyball's coach education materials [aoc-2018-stone-platform-management] [aoc-2019-stone-practice-priorities] [aoc-2020-stone-dunning-effective-practices].

From roughly 2016 onward his primary public output has been on AOC, which operates as the clinic-to-coach conduit for the U.S. collegiate and club-coaching community. He appears in three AOC modalities: short "chalk talk" video explainers (down-ball and free-ball defense shapes, W/U/line receive patterns, knee set with Jordyn Poulter) [aoc-2016-stone-downball-freeball-defense] [aoc-2018-stone-chalk-talk-receive-patterns] [aoc-2022-stone-poulter-knee-set]; long-form AOC 2020 Virtual Clinic sessions during the COVID-19 shutdown that he co-led with national-team peers [aoc-2020-stone-liskevych-using-video] [aoc-2020-stone-dunning-effective-practices]; and the multi-part AOC "HS Prep Series" with Tod Mattox, which systematizes how a coach taking over a new team should build a practice-priority hierarchy, organize components of a productive practice, and integrate competition [aoc-2019-stone-practice-priorities] [aoc-2019-stone-practice-organization] [aoc-2019-stone-mattox-utilizing-competition-practice]. He has also published downloadable full-practice PDFs on AOC — his 150-minute transition-focused practice plan is one of the most concrete coach-to-coach operational artifacts in the platform's archive [aoc-2019-stone-practice-plan-transitions].

## Core teaching principles

**Defense as a forward game, with hips under the ball.** Stone's defensive teaching centers on a forward-biased base posture and hip position under the ball. On hard-driven attacks he teaches the collapse dig — already-low defender drops forward onto knees and hips, keeps the torso upright, and lets the platform appear underneath the ball [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-collapse-dig]. The same hips-under-ball framework drives the dig-and-dive (lunge, dig first, then chest-dive to absorb the fall) [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-dig-and-dive], the knee slide for short off-speed balls [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-knee-slide], and his 2025 diagnostic of controlling dig trajectory: "get hips under the ball so the torso can remain upright" [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig]. The same body-positioning rule reappears in Stone's setter teaching — Jordyn Poulter's knee set drops the knee closest to the pass, hips under the ball, chest vertical, preserving the push to either pin [aoc-2022-stone-poulter-knee-set]. The through-line: torso-over-hips-over-contact is a single mechanical principle that unifies defense and out-of-system setting, and it's why Stone treats dropping to the knees as a legitimate advanced defensive posture rather than a failure mode [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig].

**Face the action; attack the attacker.** Stone's elite-defender diagnostic — built most explicitly around Stanford/USA libero Morgan Hentz — has four components: athleticism plus attitude and work ethic, defensive aggressiveness ("attack the defense" before contact), hips and shoulders faced to the action of the court, and multiple movement patterns for extended coverage [aoc-2020-stone-morgan-hentz-dominant]. The hips-and-shoulders cue is load-bearing: the common error is rotating the torso to "jab" at the ball, which produces an un-playable dig; the correct behavior is sliding the hips and shoulders behind the ball so platform angle redirects to midcourt [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-hips-shoulders-court]. Aggression gets the same mechanical treatment — a defender should be positioned inside the middle blocker's left shoulder to cover the block seam before contact, and pre-positioning is what converts a dig into a transition-ready pass [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-face-action-aggressive]. Shoulders stay down through forward movement; rocking back before moving forward is the young-player error that costs catchable tips [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-shoulders-down].

**Reading as the primary defensive skill.** Stone's defense writes reading as a cause, not a downstream habit. Down-ball vs. free-ball shapes differ because the attacker's body orientation tells you which is coming; blockers and setters each have role-specific read-based responsibilities on each [aoc-2016-stone-downball-freeball-defense]. Reading the off-speed commitment (tip vs. hit vs. shove-to-corner) drives the knee slide decision; without the read the defender stays in base rather than charging [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-knee-slide] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-read-off-speed]. On the youth side, Stone formalizes reading as a coaching practice — every skill derives from visual cues and the coach should ask "What did you see?" rather than grading only the outcome [aoc-2023-stone-motor-skills].

**Platform management: late, calm, angled.** In passing, Stone's core teaching is don't pre-form the platform on the way to the ball — stay relaxed, let the lower body arrive first, and form the platform at the last moment so the angle can still be adjusted to the serve's actual path [aoc-2018-stone-platform-management]. "A passer should never be stiff" [aoc-2018-stone-platform-management]. The lower and upper body have separated tasks: feet light and multi-directional, platform the steadiest component, adjusting calmly rather than jerking to the ball [aoc-2018-stone-feet-moving-receive]. Drill design should train angled platforms — right-to-left, left-to-right, and midline — rather than repeatedly delivering serves straight into the platform [aoc-2020-stone-passing-angles] [aoc-2025-stone-biomech-passing-simpler].

**Serve receive as a personnel-driven system.** Stone's serve-receive work treats pattern choice as personnel optimization, not system orthodoxy. Two guiding levers: the best passers should cover the most area; the best hitters should be freed from coverage to attack [aoc-2020-stone-organizing-serve-receive] [aoc-2020-stone-organizing-serve-receive-v2]. Strong outside → shift reception right so the OH stays free to swing; strong right side → outside takes more receive and the RS takes a sliver [aoc-2020-stone-organizing-serve-receive]. His four-pattern catalog gives explicit right-of-way rules: best passer across both seams; seam-to-right priority; seam-to-left priority; or tuck the best hitter in a corner to free her [aoc-2020-stone-sr-pattern-choice]. Pattern choice must also honor rotational overlap rules and the team's intended attack looks [aoc-2018-stone-chalk-talk-receive-patterns]. A signature Stone position: middles who can absorb a short serve are "dynamite" — keeping the middle attack and pipe live rather than conceding the slide every time the ball goes short [aoc-2020-stone-training-middle-hitter-receive].

**Practice design, stats, video.** On practice, Stone is a prioritization-and-components coach. A new team gets a skill-priority hierarchy before practice design begins, with some skills touched every day and others rotated in [aoc-2019-stone-practice-priorities]. A productive practice has four integrated components — drill selection, skill development, competition, after-practice evaluation — and pre-planning is the differentiator [aoc-2019-stone-practice-organization]. In conversation with John Dunning, Stone frames effective practice as three separable skills: error detection, skill correction, and drill selection, in that order (drill selection follows detected errors, not the other way around) [aoc-2020-stone-dunning-effective-practices]. Competition appears at individual, small-group, and team-on-team scales across a season, with Tod Mattox [aoc-2019-stone-mattox-utilizing-competition-practice]. His 150-minute transition practice plan operationalizes an arousal curve (high → lower → high) and forbids stop-and-talk during drills — pull players aside instead; consecutive-day emphasis is required to move a metric like transition attack efficiency [aoc-2019-stone-practice-plan-transitions]. On stats, Stone teaches the standard passing scale (4 = perfect, 3 = in-system, 2 = out-of-system but playable, 1 = freeball, 0 = error) and uses serve geometry to pick strategically which serve is hardest to receive [aoc-2019-stone-biomech-serving-passing-stats]. On video, his 2020 session with Terry Liskevych organizes coaching video into three buckets — scouting, match analysis, and teaching (both remote and embedded in live practice) — with Liskevych's companion handouts on highlight videos, practice-setting video use, and scouting [aoc-2020-stone-liskevych-using-video].

**Motor-skill foundations for youth.** In his youth-national-team voice, Stone pushes back on rushing young players into live game formats before the foundational motor skills are built — throwing mechanics (T and Yes positions, baseball-sized ball), jumping (arms assist legs) and landing (knees over toes), running coordination, catching, and visual-cue reading [aoc-2023-stone-motor-skills]. The eyes focus on one thing at a time, so the coach must specify the key visual component for each skill rather than gesturing at "watch the ball" [aoc-2023-stone-motor-skills].

## Contributions to the game

Stone's largest durable contribution is as an educator rather than a single-program championship coach. The AOC archive's defensive-body-positioning canon — collapse dig, dig-and-dive, knee slide, hips-under-ball, hips-and-shoulders-to-action, shoulders-down, off-speed reading — is effectively a Stone-authored textbook-via-video, co-demonstrated with Morgan Hentz at roughly the peak of her Stanford/USA career [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-collapse-dig] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-dig-and-dive] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-knee-slide] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-hips-shoulders-court] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-face-action-aggressive] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-shoulders-down] [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-read-off-speed]. His 2025 "Controlling the dig with body positioning" reasserts the same framework almost a decade later — durable rather than trend-chasing [aoc-2025-controlling-the-dig].

On serve receive, his public writing is the most systematic English-language breakdown of receive-pattern choice as a personnel and overlap-aware decision — across W/U/line, OH-strong vs. RS-strong shifts, and four right-of-way patterns for the best passer and best hitter [aoc-2018-stone-chalk-talk-receive-patterns] [aoc-2020-stone-organizing-serve-receive] [aoc-2020-stone-sr-pattern-choice]. His position on middle-hitter serve receive is a minority-but-defensible take in U.S. coaching and has direct club/HS application [aoc-2020-stone-training-middle-hitter-receive].

On practice and coach education, Stone bridges the competitive-coaching and USAV coach-education pipelines. The HS Prep Series with Mattox and the Dunning collaboration give coaches-inheriting-new-teams an end-to-end workflow (prioritize → organize → detect errors → correct → drill-select → evaluate) [aoc-2019-stone-practice-priorities] [aoc-2019-stone-practice-organization] [aoc-2020-stone-dunning-effective-practices] [aoc-2019-stone-mattox-utilizing-competition-practice]. The published-PDF transition practice plan is rare in how operational it is for a national-team-level coach [aoc-2019-stone-practice-plan-transitions]. Book publication as a Human Kinetics author on *Defensive Volleyball Drills* is widely cited in AOC biographical placards but is not substantiated in the AOC-only material available at ingest time [unsourced].

On the offensive side, his 2024 "How to make the court larger" catalogues three ways to use the block as an offensive weapon — high hands, holes in the block, and tooling — and reframes an opposing block as a resource rather than a constraint [aoc-2024-stone-make-court-larger]. On footwork, his outside-hitter piece isolates two distinct transition patterns — receive-to-attack (three shuffles, four-step approach) and block/dig-to-attack (drop-step, crossover, hop, three-step approach) — that coaches routinely blur into one [aoc-2020-stone-outside-transition-footwork].

## Quotes & representative passages

On platform management: "Keep the arms relaxed, and don't prematurely form your platform until your lower body has adjusted to the serve location" [aoc-2018-stone-platform-management]. And: "A passer should never be stiff, as they constantly have to readjust to the serve before actually making contact" [aoc-2018-stone-platform-management].

On defensive posture: "Defense is a forward game" — the base posture must bias forward, not backward or upright; you can't collapse from a tall stance [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-collapse-dig].

On elite defenders: they "attack the attacker" and commit to position before contact, not after — pre-positioning is what converts a dig into a transition-ready pass [aoc-2020-stone-hentz-face-action-aggressive].

On passing biomechanics: "One move to the ball with the platform" — minimize mid-contact adjustments; most movement is done by the legs in shuffle steps before contact [aoc-2025-stone-biomech-passing-simpler].

On coaching reading: every skill derives from visual cues — the coach should ask "What did you see?" rather than correcting only the outcome [aoc-2023-stone-motor-skills].
