# Karch Kiraly

## Overview

Karch Kiraly is the only player in volleyball history to win Olympic gold in both the indoor and beach formats, and — after winning three Olympic titles as a player (1984 Los Angeles indoor, 1988 Seoul indoor, 1996 Atlanta beach) — he became the head coach of the U.S. Women's National Team from September 2012 through October 2024, where he compiled a 327-81 record, the first-ever U.S. Women's World Championship (2014), three Volleyball Nations League titles, and the program's first Olympic gold at Tokyo 2020 [kiraly-2024-usav-bio] [kiraly-2001-ivhf-bio] [kiraly-wikipedia-bio]. In October 2024 USA Volleyball announced him as head coach of the U.S. Men's National Team for the LA 2028 cycle, making him the first person to lead both senior U.S. programs [kiraly-2024-usav-announcement] [kiraly-2024-volleyballworld-mens-transition]. The FIVB has called him "the finest volleyball player in the world for the last century" [kiraly-2001-ivhf-bio].

## Coaching career

Kiraly's coaching path began on The Art of Coaching Volleyball (AOC) platform, where he has been a publishing coach-educator since the early 2010s — his "4 keys to the forearm pass" video published in 2013 is one of the earliest AOC features under his byline [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]. He joined the USA Women's National Team as head coach in September 2012, replacing Hugh McCutcheon after London 2012 [kiraly-2024-usav-bio].

Across twelve seasons with the women, Kiraly assembled a deep trophy cabinet: Olympic bronze at Rio 2016, the program's first-ever Olympic gold at Tokyo 2020 (played in 2021), Olympic silver at Paris 2024, the 2014 World Championship (also a program first), the 2013 World Grand Champions Cup, the 2015 Pan American Games title, and three consecutive FIVB Volleyball Nations League titles (2018, 2019, 2021) [kiraly-2024-usav-bio] [kiraly-2024-volleyballworld-mens-transition]. Mid-tenure, he described the 2014 Worlds — not the later Olympic runs — as his proudest coaching memory [kiraly-2021-karch-at-60].

On October 24, 2024, USA Volleyball announced that Kiraly would transition to head coach of the U.S. Men's National Team for the 2028 cycle, filling the opening created when John Speraw moved to USAV president and CEO on October 1, 2024 [kiraly-2024-usav-announcement] [kiraly-2024-usav-mens-coach]. The U.S. men sit third in the world after Paris 2024 bronze and are auto-qualified for LA 2028 as hosts, so Kiraly's first cycle is about building rather than qualifying [kiraly-2024-volleyballworld-mens-transition]. His first international competition with the men is scheduled for the 2025 VNL [kiraly-2024-volleyballworld-mens-transition].

## Core teaching principles

**Learning-first culture.** The sentence Kiraly returns to most often is that "the number one job of everyone in the program is to be a learner" — players, staff, and head coach alike [aoc-2024-kiraly-culture] [aoc-2024-legends-liskevych-kiraly]. A learning culture is not a slogan; it is operationalized through questions being welcomed (including from players who don't yet know what they don't know), through psychological safety where it is "OK not to know," and through feedback being framed as the currency players actually want from their coaches [aoc-2024-kiraly-asking-questions] [aoc-2024-kiraly-things-learned]. Standards flow from coach behavior, not slogans: "Coaches get what they tolerate" is Kiraly's blunt framing for why modeled accountability outperforms preached accountability [aoc-2024-kiraly-things-learned].

**Reading is part of every skill.** Kiraly teaches reading not as a separate topic but as a layer inside every skill, with a three-question framework — *What do I see? What do I know? How do I respond?* — and a trained eye-work sequence of ball → setter → ball → hitter (B-S-B-H) for defense and blocking [aoc-2024-kiraly-reads-every-skill]. In passing, that translates to reading the server and server's body cues before platform shaping; his on-court 4 Keys video is built on that reading-first priority [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass]. This is a point where Kiraly's AOC-era emphasis sits slightly differently from the [[gold-medal-squared]] public framing, which treats platform mechanics and footwork timing as the primary levers and reading as a downstream habit; neither school denies the other's inputs, but the order of emphasis differs [aoc-2013-kiraly-4-keys-forearm-pass] [aoc-2023-kiraly-precision-passing].

**Purposeful practice over practice volume.** "The benefit you get from practice is more about HOW than it is about HOW MUCH" is Kiraly's single-sentence program for practice design, grounded explicitly in Anders Ericsson's deliberate-practice literature [kiraly-2019-smarter-training]. The USWNT operationalizes this with per-rep habits — *repetition without the ball* (the passer re-performs the motion immediately after a live contact to reinforce or correct) and *ghost passing* (the next passer mirrors the active passer's footwork and platform to double perceptual reps) — plus explicit goal-setting, stat-tracking, and a bias toward discomfort rather than safe reps [kiraly-2019-smarter-training]. At the program level, practice time efficiency is the decisive coaching lever: volleyball-specific warmups replace generic conditioning, 6v6 formats are preferred when time is scarce, and random practice beats blocked practice for retention and transfer [aoc-2024-kiraly-training-efficiently] [aoc-2024-legends-liskevych-kiraly].

**Compete every day in the gym.** Kiraly frames the USA women's composure in Olympic-pressure matches as a trained product of daily in-practice competition: "Lots of coaches don't have their teams compete in their gyms. They're handing us a competitive advantage" [aoc-2023-kiraly-competing-in-practice]. He cites the Rio 2016 pool match against the Netherlands, where the U.S. was down 1-2 and held composure to win, as the dividend of that practice design [aoc-2023-kiraly-competing-in-practice]. The 50-percent 6v6 drill (attacks capped at half-speed, rotation through every position) and the "validation" 6v6 game (must win a bonus point after reaching 25) are two concrete drill expressions of the same thesis [aoc-2024-kiraly-50-percent-drill] [aoc-2024-kiraly-validation-6v6].

**Measure the few stats that matter.** Kiraly attributes the measurement discipline to his former UCLA/USA coach Marv Dunphy — "You can't manage what you don't measure" — but is emphatic that stat quality beats stat quantity: serving and passing are his two priority stats at every level [aoc-2024-kiraly-stats-matter-most]. Serve-receive passing percentage in particular anchors practice design, drill scoring (e.g., Neville's Pepper Drill rewards dig quality; poor effort zeroes out points), and roster thinking [aoc-2024-kiraly-training-libero].

**Tactical specificity in serving and serve-receive.** Kiraly's public tactical voice is most detailed around the serve. His three-part serve plan: target the weakest passer with a hard, flat float; serve Zone 2 toward the setter to tangle the passer-setter connection; and vary depth rather than side-to-side placement, because "if I'm a passer, I can identify (a serve moving) left and right a lot quicker than I can identify depth" [kiraly-2024-serving-placement]. He couples this with a risk-reward position on serving errors — if a team lands every serve, it isn't pressuring enough [aoc-2024-kiraly-serving-errors] — and trains servers to handle big-point pressure via heckle-the-server drills and personal cue words such as Reid Priddy's "Just me and the ball" [aoc-2024-kiraly-serving-pressure]. On serve-receive, his framework is a four-question diagnostic: technique choice, system and passer responsibilities, tactics (2- or 3-person patterns, platform vs. hands), and gamelike rep design [aoc-2024-kiraly-serve-receive-unit].

**Expose your best player.** A rule Kiraly returns to across offense, serve-receive, and defense: put your best specialist in the role and area they are best at. Your best passer covers the largest serve-receive area; your best digger sees the most attacks (with the block shaped to funnel attacks toward them); a single, elite setter runs the offense [aoc-2021-kiraly-expose-best-player]. A single-setter 5-1 offense is the tactical application of the same principle [aoc-2021-kiraly-expose-best-player].

**Give players freedom to decide.** Kiraly's late-career emphasis is that constant coach chatter interferes with real-time decision-making, and that players glancing at the bench after every rally is a symptom of over-coaching, not engagement — the prescription is "let them play" [aoc-2024-kiraly-give-freedom]. A team culture exercise he uses, "reverse brainstorming" (asking "how could we do this as badly as possible?"), surfaces micro-behaviors to eliminate — explicitly including eye-rolling, which he flags as "never a positive action" [aoc-2024-kiraly-reverse-brainstorming].

## Contributions to the game

Kiraly is the single most decorated American volleyball player and the only player to win Olympic gold in both disciplines — indoor 1984, indoor 1988, and beach 1996 — with 148 career beach tournament wins (including 74 on the AVP tour from 1992-1996 alone) across 29 seasons [kiraly-2001-ivhf-bio] [kiraly-2024-usav-bio]. He is a member of the International Volleyball Hall of Fame (2001), the U.S. Olympic Hall of Fame (2008), and the UCLA Hall of Fame (1992), and was named by FIVB the greatest volleyball player of the 20th century [kiraly-2001-ivhf-bio] [kiraly-2024-usav-bio].

As coach, his 327-81 twelve-year women's record and the program's first Olympic gold in 2021 reset expectations for what USAV's senior programs can achieve [kiraly-2024-usav-announcement]. His transition to the men's program makes him the first person to coach both senior U.S. national teams — a structural change USAV President/CEO John Speraw framed as continuity, not departure: "It feels right that Karch Kiraly should be coaching the men in the Los Angeles Olympics in 2028" [kiraly-2024-usav-mens-coach].

As author and educator, his written and video output defines a large slice of the modern U.S. coach-education canon. He has authored three books: the technical *Championship Volleyball* (1990, revised 1996) [kiraly-1997-championship-volleyball] [kiraly-wikipedia-bio]; *Beach Volleyball* (1999) [kiraly-wikipedia-bio]; and the mental/developmental *Chasing Greatness* (2023), co-authored with Don Patterson and structured around 15 chapters on purposeful practice, embracing adversity, willpower, teamwork, and relative-vs-absolute greatness [kiraly-2023-chasing-greatness]. His bylined AOC column — covering topics from serve strategy to practice design to program culture — is the most accessible ongoing expression of a working national-team coaching philosophy in English [aoc-2024-kiraly-culture] [aoc-2024-kiraly-training-efficiently] [kiraly-2024-serving-placement]. He has also broadcast for ESPN and NBC Sports [kiraly-wikipedia-bio].
