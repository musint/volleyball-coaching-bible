# Kim Hyung-sil

## Overview

Kim Hyung-sil (김형실, born July 21, 1951) is the South Korean coach who delivered Korea's **first Olympic women's volleyball semifinal appearance in 36 years** at the 2012 London Olympics, where Korea finished 4th [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile] [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia] [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. Kim's London 2012 cycle bridged a long quiet era for Korean women's volleyball at the Olympic level — between the 1976 Montreal bronze and his 2012 fourth-place finish, Korea did not reach an Olympic semifinal — and set up the 2020 Tokyo cycle under [[stefano-lavarini]] in which Korea again reached the semifinal, also finishing 4th [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia].

Kim played as a setter [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile] — a positional background he shares with [[yasutaka-matsudaira]], [[carl-mcgown]], and many other prominent international head coaches — and reached the senior NT head-coaching role in 2011 after a long assistant-coaching apprenticeship documented across the late 1970s and 1980s [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile]. After the 2012 Olympic cycle he returned to the club ranks; he served as head coach of the AI Peppers / Pepper Savings Bank women's V-League team in Gwangju from the 2021/22 season through 2022/23, then transitioned to Sports Director for the 2023/24 season [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile].

For this wiki, Kim is the **bridge figure between [[park-man-bok]]'s 1970s [[korean-school]] origins and the modern Lavarini-Hernández-Morales era** — the documented Korean-born head coach whose tenure produced the most prominent Korean women's Olympic result of the 21st-century pre-Lavarini period.

## Coaching career

**Assistant-coaching apprenticeship (1978/79–1985/86).** Kim's structured assistant-coaching career runs through roughly eight Korean domestic seasons across the late 1970s and early-to-mid 1980s [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile]. The eight-season apprenticeship is consistent with a long-arc Korean coaching pipeline from setter-player to NT head coach.

**Korean women's national team head coach (2011–2012).** Kim's documented NT head-coaching tenure spans the 2011–2012 international cycle [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile] [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia]:

- **2011 Asian Women's Volleyball Championship — bronze medal.** Kim's first major NT result [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile].
- **2012 London Olympics — 4th place.** Korea's first Olympic semifinal appearance since the 1976 Montreal bronze, a 36-year gap [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia] [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. Korea defeated Italy 3-1 in the quarterfinals to reach the semifinal, lost to the eventual champions, and lost the bronze-medal match to Japan.

The London 2012 cycle was anchored on captain Kim Sa-nee and a 24-year-old [[kim-yeon-koung]] — the latter was named the **Olympic MVP** at London 2012, becoming the first non-medalist Olympic MVP in volleyball history and setting the Olympic scoring record at 207 points (breaking Yekaterina Gamova's 2004 record of 204) [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. Kim Hyung-sil's tactical system around an emerging-superstar OH on a roster without size advantages is the structural pattern that subsequent Korean head coaches (Lavarini, Hernández) would inherit.

By 2019 the Korean women's NT was in transition between Cha Hae-won's 2018 dismissal and Stefano Lavarini's mid-2019 appointment, so a Kim involvement during that interregnum is plausible but requires Korean-language source verification.

**AI Peppers / Pepper Savings Bank head coach (2021/22–2022/23).** Kim moved into the V-League women's club ranks as head coach of the Gwangju-based AI Peppers franchise (operated under the Pepper Savings Bank corporate sponsor) for two seasons [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile]. AI Peppers is among the youngest of the seven-team V-League women's clubs [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia], and the on-bench tenure represents a returning-veteran club-development assignment.

**Sports Director, AI Peppers (2023/24).** Kim transitioned from the bench to a front-office Sports Director role for the 2023/24 V-League season, retaining institutional involvement with AI Peppers without on-court head-coaching duties [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile].

## Contributions to the game

**2012 London Olympic 4th place — Korea's first Olympic semifinal in 36 years.** Kim's defining national-team result. The 4th-place finish ended a 36-year drought between the 1976 Montreal bronze and the next Korean women's Olympic semifinal appearance [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia] [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. The result reset the institutional standard for Korean women's volleyball at the Olympic level and established the Lavarini-era expectation of consistent semifinal contention.

**2012 Olympic MVP under his system — Kim Yeon-koung.** Kim Hyung-sil's tactical system at London 2012 produced the first non-medalist Olympic MVP in volleyball history and the Olympic scoring record (207 points), both attributed to Kim Yeon-koung [kim-yeon-koung-2026-wikipedia]. The development of a generational outside hitter inside a Korean-school program with structural size disadvantages is the strongest available signal of Kim's player-development profile.

**Bridge figure in Korean coaching lineage.** Between the 1970s [[park-man-bok]] era and the 2019-onward foreign-head-coach period (Lavarini, Hernández, Morales), Kim Hyung-sil's London 2012 cycle is the most prominent documented Korean-born NT head-coaching success [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile] [korea-women-nt-2026-wikipedia]. The role is structurally similar to [[yuichi-nakagaichi]]'s mid-period bridge function in modern Japanese men's coaching lineage.

**V-League institutional continuity.** Kim's 2021–2024 AI Peppers tenure, including the front-office Sports Director transition, contributed to the institutional development of one of the newer V-League women's franchises [kim-hyung-sil-2026-volleybox-profile]. The under-documented club-level work is consistent with the Korean-school post-NT continuity pattern and provides one data point on how the Korean women's club system absorbs returning national-team staff.
