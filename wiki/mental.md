# Mental

## Overview

"Mental" is the catch-all for everything that shapes how a team behaves under load: culture, leadership, pressure, motivation, adversity, communication, and the routines that make those behaviors repeatable. Modern volleyball coaching has moved decisively away from "mental toughness" as a trait some athletes have and others don't, and now treats mental as a set of trainable, observable behaviors coaches design into the program — the same way you design serve-receive or a blocking scheme. The instrumentation literature reflects this shift: the most-used mental-toughness measure (MTQ48) underwent factorial-validity scrutiny that produced 18-item Short and 6-item Very Short refinements — coaches who want to monitor MT longitudinally can now do so without the original 48-item burden, but the construct itself remains theoretically contested [kawabata-2021-mtq48-refinement].

Five modern framings anchor the area. Hugh McCutcheon's *Championship Behaviors* (2022) is the flagship: championship results require championship behaviors; coaches teach, coach, and mentor while athletes work, learn, and compete. The frame is behaviors over outcomes, mastery and trust over star hunting, playing well (not just playing hard) as the daily goal. Its living expression is the "competitive cauldron" McCutcheon built at Minnesota with associate head coach Laurie Eisler — a coopetition environment where teammates push each other in practice and carry that standard into matches.

Karch Kiraly frames the mental landscape through culture and curiosity: becoming-a-learner as a lifelong coaching posture, asking questions rather than lecturing, giving players freedom within clear standards. Russ Rose's "everything matters" distills the same point operationally — every rep, stat, scouting detail, and practice minute carries competitive weight; "no free balls" codifies that the moment you stop giving opponents free points is the moment culture stops being a poster and starts being real.

Terry Liskevych frames the emotional and interpersonal layer: coaches must control their feelings and attitude before they can ask that of players, and "players and staff" thinking treats the program as one organism. John Dunning adds the construction-side answer to "how do you build a team that sings?" — culture and chemistry are deliberate coaching outputs, not accidents. Mike Hebert's *Thinking Volleyball* (2013) sits alongside these as the classical mental-model-of-play text: how players and coaches should think about the game itself during the flow of a match.

The practical payoff is that mental work shows up on the practice plan, not just in the pregame speech. Competing-in-practice, post-game talk structure, statistics that measure behavior not just outcome, and post-loss recovery routines are all concrete expressions of a mental program.

## Major subtopics

- **Team culture** — the standards and behaviors that persist when no one's watching. McCutcheon's mastery-and-trust, Dunning's "deliberate outputs" stance, Kiraly on culture, and Rose's program-level rigor all articulate the same claim: culture is a coaching output, not a vibe. The empirical case for the cohesion-collective-efficacy linkage is **level-dependent**: specific measures of group cohesion correlated positively with collective efficacy in elite volleyball teams but **not** in recreational teams, with task cohesion (commitment to shared goals, role clarity) tying more strongly to collective efficacy than social cohesion (interpersonal liking) [spink-1990-cohesion-collective-efficacy-volleyball].
- **Pressure & performance** — training athletes to execute when the scoreboard tightens. Competing-in-practice (Kiraly, Rose), serving-pressure drills, the competitive cauldron (McCutcheon + Eisler), and "everything matters" are pressure-inoculation tools; pressure is a training stimulus, not a personality trait. The choking-under-pressure literature splits into two complementary theoretical families — **attention-based models** (distraction theory; Masters' "reinvestment" / conscious-processing theory: under pressure, skilled performers revert to step-by-step monitoring of automated skills, disrupting fluent execution) and **self-presentation models** (anxiety from being judged drives the breakdown). Personality vulnerabilities include high trait anxiety, high self-consciousness, low dispositional reinvestment, perfectionism, and fear-of-negative-evaluation; intervention evidence supports pressure-acclimatization training, pre-performance routines, attentional cueing, and quiet-eye training across sport contexts [mesagno-2017-choking-models-interventions]. Volleyball-specific pressure tests temper coaching dogma: serving-routine consistency was **not** related to serving accuracy in club-level competitive play, suggesting routine *components* (breath, ball bounces, focal point, cue word) likely matter more than routine presence/absence [billing-2014-pre-performance-routines-volleyball]; and "don't serve into the net" prohibition under high anxiety did **not** produce the predicted ironic-error spike (failing to replicate Wegner's classic prediction in volleyball serving), though distinct attentional-control disruption patterns emerged [gorgulu-2021-avoiding-instructions-volleyball-pressure]. Pre-match anxiety has individual-differences geometry: the **Individual Zone of Optimal Functioning (IZOF)** model predicts that optimal pre-match anxiety differs substantially between athletes — confirmed in volleyball-specific season-long testing — meaning uniform "calm down" or "fire up" pre-match talks miss the architecture [nogueira-2019-izof-volleyball-anxiety].
- **Leadership — coach side** — becoming-a-learner (Kiraly), controlling-your-feelings (Liskevych), the dual responsibility frame (McCutcheon), asking-questions pedagogy. The coach's job is not to be the loudest voice in the gym; it's to build the environment where athletes become their own voices. The mediator architecture is empirically clean: **perceived support** (athletes believe support is available if needed) uniquely predicts self-confidence through coach-athlete relationship quality, while **received support** (support actually delivered) uniquely predicts psychological wellbeing through the same relational pathway — meaning relationship-building is the load-bearing first step, with both visible-availability and consistent-delivery needed afterwards [coussens-2024-coach-support-confidence-wellbeing].
- **Leadership — player side** — captains, seniors, and in-play leaders who carry standards between coach interventions. Dunning's "why players sing" frame is about what makes athletes buy in and then propagate team standards.
- **Motivation & mental toughness** — Rose separates responsibilities cleanly: coaches train competitive behavior, players own mental toughness. Liskevych's five-things and Kiraly's things-I've-learned sit in the same neighborhood — motivation is built through standards, not speeches.
- **Communication & post-game** — how coaches talk to players and what a post-match conversation should do. The AOC post-game-talk piece is the operational reference; coaching the Instagram-Year (iY) generation adds a modern layer on how younger athletes receive feedback. Self-talk training works as a **sustained block, not a one-shot tool**: 8-week self-talk interventions reduced somatic state anxiety, increased state self-confidence, improved general self-efficacy with sustained gains at follow-up, and produced measurably better coach-rated performance in junior sub-elite athletes; 1-week interventions produced only transient effects [walter-2019-self-talk-junior-athletes]. Imagery use parallels the same pattern — collegiate volleyball players use imagery heterogeneously across a season, and without coach scaffolding, practice remains idiosyncratic and inconsistent; **imagery is a coachable skill that needs explicit structure** (cue scripts, timing prompts, vividness training), not just a "visualize success" reminder [pourmohseni-2025-imagery-collegiate-volleyball-season].
- **Adversity & post-loss recovery** — the nine-reasons-teams-fail frame catalogs failure modes; Rose's excellence-vs-success distinction reframes losses as information, not verdicts. Hebert's *Thinking Volleyball* supplies the classical mental-model lens: a team that understands the game diagnoses and recovers faster. The longitudinal volleyball burnout pattern is specific: across a 10-month competitive season, **reduced sense of accomplishment** and **sport devaluation** rose progressively while physical/emotional exhaustion stayed relatively stable; coping strategies did **not** change across measurement points, while stress profiles rose — meaning coaches should periodize psychological load alongside physical load, and confidence/motivation monitoring is a defensible leading indicator of burnout risk [pires-2021-burnout-coping-volleyball-season].

## Schools of thought

- [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]] — arguably the richest mental-work corpus in coach education, built from Kiraly, Rose, Dunning, Liskevych, and McCutcheon content. Treats culture, communication, and competing-in-practice as foreground content.
- [[gold-medal-squared]] — less explicit culture language, but competing-in-practice and representative learning operationalize much of what other schools call "mental training." The mental side is built through training design.
- [[japanese-training]] — the Daimatsu-rooted tradition treats mental toughness and group cohesion as outputs of training intensity itself; high volume with competitive context is a culture-building mechanism.
- [[usa-volleyball]] — the Coach Academy's pillar-based curriculum lists Mind, Heart, and Team as three of its five pillars (alongside Craft and Body); the federation treats mental-domain coach education as first-class.
- [[ecological-dynamics]] — doesn't foreground "mental" as a separate construct; perception-action coupling absorbs what other frames label mental. Useful as a reminder that "mental" and "technical" are less separable than older frames imply.

## Getting started

If you are new to the mental side of coaching and want a reading order that builds from philosophy to operational practice:

1. [[mccutcheon-2022-championship-behaviors]] — start here. The behaviors-over-outcomes, mastery-and-trust, dual-responsibility frame is the cleanest modern articulation.
2. [[aoc-2013-mccutcheon-eisler-cauldron]] and [[aoc-2013-mccutcheon-coopetition]] — how the competitive cauldron works in practice.
3. [[aoc-2024-kiraly-culture]] and [[aoc-2024-kiraly-asking-questions]] — culture-building from the current (2012–2024) USA Women's head coach.
4. [[aoc-2017-liskevych-controlling-feelings]] — the coach's emotional work before and during a match.
5. [[rose-2021-volleybrains-everything-matters]] and [[aoc-2026-rose-no-free-balls]] — Rose's operational rigor and the accountability side of "everything matters."
6. [[aoc-2022-rose-coach-for-life]] and [[aoc-2018-dunning-team-culture]] — how to build and sustain the team over years, not seasons.
7. [[aoc-2017-dunning-makes-players-sing]] — what makes athletes buy in.
8. [[hebert-2013-thinking-volleyball]] — the classical mental-model-of-play layer.
9. Your age-lens page — [[age-lens-14u]], [[age-lens-hs]], or [[age-lens-college]] — how mental work is developmentally shaped.
