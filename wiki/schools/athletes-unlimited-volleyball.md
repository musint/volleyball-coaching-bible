# Athletes Unlimited Pro Volleyball (AU Pro Volleyball Championship)

## Overview

Athletes Unlimited Pro Volleyball — rebranded the **AU Pro Volleyball Championship** in May 2025 — is the **first** of the three modern U.S. women's professional indoor volleyball leagues, having launched in February–March 2021 in Dallas under the parent organization Athletes Unlimited [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia][auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. As a girls club director, you should think of AU as the structural disruptor that proved an athlete-led, individual-scoring U.S. women's pro volleyball league could exist — pre-dating PVF (2024) and LOVB Pro (2025) by three and four years respectively, and shaping how those later leagues think about athlete equity and pay-for-performance [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand].

The parent organization was founded in 2020 by Jon Patricof (CEO) and Jonathan Soros [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. Athletes Unlimited explicitly rejects the conventional team-ownership model: "There are no team owners, and league investors are capping their returns. Athletes share in the league profits, and are involved in the daily decision making" [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. The volleyball arm operates a five-week season — historically late-winter (Dallas, then Mesa AZ); reformatted in 2025 to a fall window (October 1–November 3) split across Omaha and Madison [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia][auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Five seasons completed (2021–2025) with champions Jordan Larson (2021, 4,569 pts), Bethania de la Cruz (2022, 4,652 pts — all-time AU volleyball record), Leah Edmond (2023, 4,313 pts), Brittany Abercrombie (2024, 4,521 pts), and Jordan Thompson (2025, 3,563 pts) [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia].

The format you should explain to your athletes: **there are no permanent teams**. Each week, the top four players in the standings become captains and draft new rosters from the rest of the field. Players earn points from team match outcomes plus individual statistics (kills, digs, assists, blocks, aces). The athlete with the most accumulated points at the end of the five-week season is the individual champion [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. There is no team title; there is no franchise to root for; you root for athletes.

## Core principles

### Athlete-led governance
The league's most distinctive structural commitment is athlete equity. Athletes Unlimited has stated publicly that "athletes share in the league profits, and are involved in the daily decision making" [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. Investors cap their returns; athletes hold governance roles. Cassidy Lichtman, a former U.S. national-team player, serves as VP and was the on-record voice for the 2025 championship rebrand [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. As a coach mentoring a college senior considering pro options, this is the differentiator: AU is the only league of the three where athletes have a direct governance stake.

### No fixed teams
Players sign individual contracts with the league rather than with a team [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. Each week of the season begins with a draft: the top four players in the prior week's points standings serve as captains and pick their teammates from the available pool. This means a player you watched play for "Team A" in Week 1 may be on "Team B" in Week 2 and a captain of "Team C" in Week 3. Casual fans cannot follow a team; they follow players. This is a deliberate design choice: AU is staking out individual-athlete branding as the storytelling unit, betting that in a world where Olympic athletes are personal-brand-driven, individual-scoring leagues build deeper fan attachment than franchise loyalty.

### Weekly draft format
The five-week season concentrates 24–30 contests into roughly a month [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia][auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Each week is its own micro-tournament: captains and rosters reset Monday; matches are played Wednesday through Sunday; new captains are crowned at week's end. The compressed schedule is part of why AU has been able to attract Olympic-tier players who can't commit to a four-month PVF or LOVB season — the calendar cost is roughly 30–40 days, not 4 months [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand].

### Individual-points champion
AU is the only U.S. women's pro volleyball league where the season-end title is **individual** [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. There is no team championship banner. Champion Bethania de la Cruz's 4,652 points (2022) is the all-time league record; Jordan Thompson's 3,563 points won the 2025 title in a five-week, 24-contest format [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia][auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Points come from match outcomes (team result) plus per-stat individual contribution. The exact point-per-stat formulas are proprietary and not detailed in publicly cited sources [unsourced — specific AU per-stat point formulas not detailed in cited sources].

### Athlete equity and profit share
AU's investor structure caps investor returns and channels league profits back to athletes [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. This is structurally closer to a cooperative than to a conventional pro league. The model has been influential: PVF's salary disclosure ($60K–$175K) and LOVB's prize-pool / positional-award framing both implicitly respond to AU's earlier articulation that athletes deserve a defined share of the league's economic upside, not just a wage.

## Methodology

AU does not centralize coaching methodology — each weekly captain functionally coaches her own roster for that week, with league-provided support staff. There is no head-coach institution at the franchise level because there are no franchises. The methodological consequence is that AU matches feature **constantly shifting tactical chemistry**: a setter and outside hitter who built timing in Week 2 may be on opposing teams in Week 3. This rewards players with broad system fluency over players who specialize in one team's specific offense.

The 2025 reformat (AU Pro Volleyball Championship) retained the format but adjusted the venue strategy [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Instead of hosting the entire season in one city (Dallas 2021–22, Mesa AZ 2023–24), AU split the 2025 championship across **Omaha** (Liberty First Credit Union Arena, October 1–15) and **Madison** (Veterans Memorial Coliseum at Alliant Energy Center, October 22–November 3) [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Both cities are also LOVB markets — meaning AU effectively uses LOVB venues for a fall championship that draws on LOVB and PVF rosters during the winter–spring leagues' off-season.

The 44-athlete invited roster for 2025 came from "the top domestic and international leagues (LOVB, PVF, Brazil's Superliga, Turkey's Sultanlar Ligi)" per AU's announcement [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. This positions AU not as a competitor to LOVB or PVF but as a fall all-star event that aggregates talent from all of them. ESPN platforms broadcast 15 of the 30 contests in 2025; remaining matches distributed across global streaming and regional sports networks [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand].

## Notable practitioners

AU has no fixed coaching staff at the franchise level (because there are no franchises). The league office VP role is held by **[[cassidy-lichtman]]** (former USA national-team player) — forward reference [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand].

Champions to date — Jordan Larson, Bethania de la Cruz, Leah Edmond, Brittany Abercrombie, Jordan Thompson — represent overlap with USA, Dominican Republic, and current PVF/LOVB rosters [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. Bethania de la Cruz's 4,652-point all-time record (2022) was set in a five-week format and remains the AU benchmark.

## Contrasts with other schools

### AU vs. [[lovb]]
LOVB players sign with **one team** in **one city** for a four-month season; AU players have **no fixed team** and play a five-week individual-points championship [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia][lovb-2026-wikipedia]. AU launched in 2021, four years before LOVB Pro [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. Crucially, AU's 2025 host cities (Omaha, Madison) are also LOVB markets, so AU functions as a fall all-star event that draws on LOVB rosters during their off-season [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. The leagues are coexistent on the calendar: an athlete can play LOVB January–April and AU October–November.

### AU vs. [[pvf]]
PVF runs a traditional 16-game team-table season with $1M championship prize for the winning team; AU runs a 24–30 contest individual-points season with the championship going to a player [pvf-2026-wikipedia][athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. PVF has independent franchise owners; AU has no team owners and athletes hold equity. PVF has fixed home-and-away rivalries; AU has weekly captain-rerolls. PVF runs January–May; AU runs October–November. The two leagues are nearly opposite design choices for the same general goal of professional volleyball — and they coexist because AU's fall window doesn't conflict with PVF's winter–spring schedule.

### AU vs. [[svleague]]
Japan's SV.League is a fully professional team-and-franchise league successor to the V.League [pvf-2026-wikipedia comparison context]. SV.League and AU are structurally near-opposite: SV.League rests on decades-old corporate-team continuity; AU dissolves team identity entirely and rebuilds it weekly. The cross-comparison is useful when athletes you coach consider Japanese pro contracts vs. AU: SV.League offers a single-team, single-city experience and substantial corporate-backed compensation; AU offers a 5-week U.S.-based individual-stat showcase.

### AU vs. [[vakifbank-istanbul]] / European club tradition
European top clubs build multi-year rosters around marquee stars (the VakıfBank-Guidetti era is the canonical example). AU dissolves rosters every week — the structural opposite of European-club-style team-building. American players who have signed European contracts (e.g., Jordan Larson at Eczacıbaşı in earlier years; Micha Hancock and others in Italian and Turkish leagues) often play AU as their fall-window U.S. event without disrupting their European club commitments — AU's 5-week timeline fits between the European preseason and mid-season Champions League schedule.

## Critiques and limitations

### Brand-loyalty challenge
With no fixed teams, casual fans cannot anchor on a hometown franchise [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia]. AU bets that individual-athlete loyalty replaces team-loyalty in a personal-brand era. Whether this generates sustainable fan equity at scale, or remains a niche enthusiast product, is unsettled. The 2025 rebrand (championship-format framing, Omaha + Madison venue tie-in to LOVB markets) suggests AU is hedging by leveraging LOVB's residential-fan-bases for its events.

### Compressed-season tradeoffs
A 5-week season is logistically convenient but limits athlete development time. Players don't get the multi-month tactical refinement of a PVF or LOVB season; they get a compressed showcase. For elite athletes already developed at the college or overseas level, this is fine. For mid-career athletes still developing, a longer-season league offers more practice repetitions [athletes-unlimited-volleyball-2026-wikipedia][auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand].

### Pay-scale opacity
AU has not publicly disclosed its athlete compensation structure beyond the equity-and-profit-share framework [unsourced — specific AU compensation figures not in cited sources]. Athletes know they have governance voice and prize money, but exact dollar figures comparable to PVF's published $60K–$175K band are not in the public record. This makes apples-to-apples comparison with PVF or LOVB offers harder for an athlete deciding among options.

### League-fragmentation effect
With three concurrent U.S. women's pro leagues sharing a finite pool of national-team-caliber athletes, AU's existence as a fourth-quarter add-on may help — by extending the calendar for athletes who'd otherwise sit out the fall — or may hurt by spreading sponsor and broadcast attention thinner. AU's 2025 broadcast deal with ESPN platforms (15 of 30 contests) suggests the format retains broadcaster interest five seasons in [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand].

### Player-development thinness for younger athletes
AU is an elite-only invitational (44 players in 2025) [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Unlike PVF and LOVB, which develop younger USA prospects on rosters alongside Olympians, AU does not currently provide a development tier. For most college seniors, AU is not a first stop; PVF or LOVB is. AU is a destination once an athlete has established herself in PVF, LOVB, or a top European league.

### Format-stability question
The 2025 rebrand from "Athletes Unlimited Pro Volleyball" to "AU Pro Volleyball Championship," along with the host-city change from Mesa AZ to Omaha + Madison, signals AU is iterating its format aggressively [auprosports-2025-championship-rebrand]. Iteration can mean improvement, but it also means athletes signing AU contracts cannot rely on next year looking like this year. PVF and LOVB offer more format predictability — at least until they themselves prove out their economic models.
