# Gold Medal Squared

## Overview

Gold Medal Squared (GMS) is the principal U.S. volleyball methodology-propagation organization built around motor-learning science as the empirical basis for coaching decisions. Founded in 1985 by [[carl-mcgown]] with Hall of Fame coaches [[marv-dunphy]] and [[doug-beal]] — three architects of the men's-side Olympic gold medals of the 1980s — GMS was a vehicle for taking the training ideas being proven on the USA Men's National Team and delivering them to collegiate, high-school, and club coaches through camps, clinics, and the GMS+ online platform [gms-nd-about-us]. The organization self-describes as "simple, science-based" methodology tested across four decades [gms-nd-about-us].

The school's organizing commitment is that volleyball coaching has empirically answerable questions, and that a coach's job is to do the reading rather than rely on opinion or tradition [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. This puts GMS in the lineage of motor-learning research that runs from Shea and Morgan's 1979 contextual-interference work [shea-1979-contextual-interference] through Magill and Hall's 1990 review [magill-1990-contextual-interference-review] and the schema-theory and challenge-point literatures, and translates that research into a stable body of teaching commitments — specificity, random/variable practice, reading-and-reaction, external-focus cues, intentional feedback, deliberate skill simplification — that the GMS network has trained at clinic scale for forty years [gms-nd-structure-practice] [gms-2018-clinic-report]. GMS today is three things at once: an instructional methodology with named commitments; a coaching network anchored by McGown's son [[chris-mcgown]] and including multiple Olympic-staff coaches and a large roster of sitting NCAA Division I head coaches [gms-nd-about-us]; and a delivery business running camps, clinics, and online coach education. The methodological commitments — not the personalities or the camps — define the school as a school.

## Core principles

1. **Motor-learning science over opinion.** GMS's organizing commitment is that coaching has empirically answerable questions and a coach's job is to do the reading [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. The school is openly skeptical of unproven constructs such as "general athletic ability," treating it as a folk-psychology placeholder for trainable specific skills [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown].

2. **Specificity of training.** Because volleyball skills are task-specific, the transfer from general-athletic drills to volleyball performance is weak; the fastest route to competitive skill is practicing volleyball in volleyball-like conditions [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown] [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The GMS practice-design article states it bluntly: "the single most correlative activity to being a good volleyball player is having played a lot of volleyball" [gms-nd-structure-practice].

3. **Random / variable practice over blocked practice.** GMS advocates early movement from blocked practice (slow, isolated, predictable — useful only while installing a new pattern) to random, game-speed, variable practice, where retention and transfer live [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The principle is anchored in the contextual-interference literature: Shea and Morgan (1979) found blocked practice yields better acquisition-phase performance, but random practice yields better retention and transfer on delayed and novel tests [shea-1979-contextual-interference] — a result Magill and Hall (1990) confirmed and refined, with the caveat that the effect is most robust when task variations span different generalized motor programs [magill-1990-contextual-interference-review]. GMS invokes the "challenge point" concept to argue coaches should move to random, game-like conditions as fast as the learner allows [gms-nd-structure-practice].

4. **Reading-and-reaction primacy.** "First we have to teach them how to move. Then, we need to teach them how to see" — the principle highlighted on the GMS tribute page — captures McGown's two-stage framing: efficient movement patterns first so attention can be freed for reading the game [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. In blocking, this shows up as eyework that progresses from setter to hitter by jump time, with footwork progressions moving from predetermined sets to variable triggers only after mechanics are sound [gms-nd-blocking].

5. **External-focus cues and skill simplification.** GMS prefers external-focus cues (movement effects, analogies like "cracking a whip") over internal body-mechanic instructions, because the motor-learning literature consistently finds better retention and transfer from external focus [gms-nd-structure-practice]. Paired with a deliberately small drill library — the 2018 Charleston Academy clinic ran on roughly twelve core activities — this yields an environment where athletes see the same problems repeatedly and coach language is standardized [gms-2018-clinic-report].

6. **Intentional feedback.** Feedback is a scarce learning resource and must be deployed intentionally rather than sprayed onto every rep — athletes need self-evaluation for skill to survive outside practice [gms-nd-structure-practice]. McGown gave direct, challenging feedback delivered with genuine care for the learner [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown].

7. **Game-like training and training efficiency.** The GMS clinic curriculum is organized around four pillars — motor-learning science, game-pattern research, specific skill development, and practice efficiency — and endorses whole-skill training in game-like conditions over isolated part-practice [gms-2018-clinic-report]. The USA Volleyball remembrance captures McGown's ethic in one phrase: "developing comfort with discomfort," paired with a relentless focus on training efficiency [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering].

## Methodology

GMS's translation of these principles into practice design is documented in three publicly available areas: practice structure, the bunch-read blocking system, and the clinic curriculum.

**Practice structure.** GMS frames practice design around the challenge-point heuristic — tasks too easy don't produce learning, tasks too hard erode confidence — with the goal being the appropriately-challenging zone for the current learner [gms-nd-structure-practice]. Three operational commitments follow: design for transfer (the test of any drill is whether it transfers to competition, not whether it looks clean); maximize game-like reps (warm-ups become ball-contact opportunities; whiteboards organize small-group work that keeps athletes touching the ball rather than waiting in line); and move toward random practice as fast as the athlete can handle [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The 2018 Charleston Academy clinic corroborates this with a programmatic detail: drill variety was capped at roughly twelve core activities, on the theory that depth of repetition with shared coach vocabulary beats novelty [gms-2018-clinic-report].

**Bunch-read blocking.** GMS's blocking guide documents the bunch-read team-blocking system, in use at GMS for over twenty-five years [gms-nd-blocking]. Blockers start closer to the middle of the court and use dynamic footwork to read and react, typically placing two or three blockers in front of the set [gms-nd-blocking]. Blocking is broken into five skill areas (spacing, footwork, armwork, eyework, tactical positioning) and a specific footwork lexicon (Q3, X2, X3 etc.) [gms-nd-blocking]. Spacing cues are concrete ("elbows to elbows" left, "hands to hands" right); eyework progresses from setter to hitter by jump time; the teaching progression starts with predetermined sets and adds variability gradually; practice dose is 15–30 focused minutes daily; and early in learning, "allow athletes to be late" — mechanics before speed [gms-nd-blocking]. The bunch-read system is one of the most concrete operational legacies of McGown's teaching: not just a philosophy but a block scheme taught with shared vocabulary to a national coaching network [gms-nd-blocking].

**Clinic curriculum.** The 2018 GMS clinic at Charleston Academy, led by then-USA Men's NT staff Mike Wall and Rob Neilson, organized three days of instruction around the four pillars [gms-2018-clinic-report]. Concrete programmatic choices visible from outside: middle-middle defense with Position 6 at roughly twenty feet from the net; conservative pass and set targets off the net; whiteboards for practice organization; and "teaching in the game paradigm" — small-sided games with corrective reps instead of punitive conditioning [gms-2018-clinic-report]. Forman summarizes the operational core in one phrase: "reps and feedback" [gms-2018-clinic-report]. The clinic format itself is the methodology-propagation vehicle: one McGown-trained coach trains hundreds of subsequent coaches and thousands of athletes [gms-nd-about-us].

## Notable practitioners

[[carl-mcgown]] (1937–2016) is the school's founder and animating intellect: seven consecutive Olympic Games (1984–2008) and seven consecutive FIVB World Championships (1974–2002) on USA Men's NT staff, three Olympic gold medals (1984, 1988, 2008), and 225–137 across thirteen seasons as BYU men's head coach with NCAA titles in 1999 and 2001 [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering]. [[chris-mcgown]] is GMS co-founder and current head coach, continuing the methodology and the family lineage [gms-nd-about-us].

[[hugh-mccutcheon]] is McGown's most prominent former assistant: BYU staff 1995–2001 during the two NCAA titles, then 2008 Beijing Olympic gold as USA Men's NT head coach, 2012 London silver with USA Women, eleven seasons at [[minnesota-volleyball]], and the 2022 book *Championship Behaviors* [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. His school of-record is [[usa-volleyball]] rather than GMS, but his methodological roots in McGown's teaching are explicit in the biography [mccutcheon-2024-wikipedia-bio]. [[john-speraw]] sits on the contemporary GMS coaching roster, placing him inside the extended McGown–Beal–Dunphy lineage that ran through the 2008 USA Men's staff [gms-nd-about-us]; his school of-record is also [[usa-volleyball]], with the GMS connection more lineage than methodological endorsement.

[[mike-hebert]] (Pittsburgh, New Mexico, Illinois, Minnesota — 952–392 across thirty-five years, 1985 AVCA Coach of the Year, AVCA President 1987–1990) is grouped with the GMS / McGown lineage in coach-community discussions, and his reading-defense framing is consonant with later GMS reading-oriented teaching, though the specific influence lines and formal GMS affiliation are not fully documented in the sources on file [unsourced].

GMS-aligned NCAA head coaches today (per the GMS roster) include Heather Olmstead at BYU, Keegan Cook at Minnesota, Ryan McGuyre at Baylor, Chris Tamas at Illinois, Tom Black at Georgia, Jason Watson at Arkansas, Jose Gandara at Miami, Giovana Melo at Cal State Bakersfield, and Rob Nielson at Utah State [gms-nd-about-us].

## Contrasts with other schools

**GMS vs. traditional block-drill methodology.** GMS's clearest opposition is to the tradition that organizes practice around long blocked drills (passing line, setting line, hitting line) before "putting it together" in scrimmage. Citing the motor-learning literature, GMS argues blocked practice produces better acquisition-phase performance — athletes look cleaner during the drill — but random, variable, game-like practice produces meaningfully better retention and transfer to competition [shea-1979-contextual-interference] [magill-1990-contextual-interference-review] [gms-nd-structure-practice]. Long blocked drills also consume practice time at low information density, where small-sided games deliver more live reps with the additional information of an opposing block and defensive read [gms-nd-structure-practice] [gms-2018-clinic-report]. GMS does not dismiss blocked practice categorically — early-stage learners benefit from low-interference repetition while a pattern is being installed — but treats the move to random practice as the load-bearing decision in practice design [gms-nd-structure-practice].

**GMS vs. constraints-led / [[ecological-dynamics]].** Both schools agree, against traditional block-drill methodology, that practice must look more like the game and that decoupling perception from action degrades learning. The disagreement is theoretical, and consequential. GMS sits in the **information-processing motor-learning tradition** descended from Schmidt's schema theory and the contextual-interference literature: skill is the development of generalized motor programs and decision rules the athlete encodes through structured practice [shea-1979-contextual-interference] [magill-1990-contextual-interference-review] [gms-nd-structure-practice]. The constraints-led / ecological-dynamics tradition argues that skill is not a stored motor program at all but an emergent functional relationship between performer and environment, assembled in the moment under the perception-action couplings the practice task affords [davids-2013-ecological-dynamics-talent] [woods-2020-sport-ecology-designers]. Pinder and colleagues (2011) make the operational consequence explicit: practice tasks must preserve **information-movement coupling** — a spiking drill off a tossed ball is not "representatively designed" because the hitter is decoupled from setter-read information, no matter how many random-vs-blocked reps are interleaved [pinder-2011-representative-learning-design]. The practical disagreement: GMS will teach a four-step attack approach with shared vocabulary, while the eco-dynamics coach designs constraints under which an effective approach emerges from the athlete's interaction with the task [gms-2018-clinic-report] [davids-2013-ecological-dynamics-talent].

**GMS vs. [[art-of-coaching-volleyball]].** GMS and AOC are methodologically aligned on most load-bearing commitments — both endorse motor-learning-grounded practice design, random/variable practice, reading-and-reaction primacy, and game-like training — and AOC's contributor roster overlaps materially with the GMS / McGown network. The difference is emphasis. AOC is more **coach-pedagogy-first**: content is organized around named coaches' framings, panel-format clinics, and how-do-you-actually-teach-this video, with motor-learning science cited but not centered in the brand identity (Jim Stone's AOC "Motor learning" piece frames motor learning as the coach's core task in practitioner vocabulary rather than research vocabulary) [aoc-2024-motor-learning] [aoc-2020-passing-principles]. GMS is more **motor-learning-science-first**: the brand foregrounds the scientific basis and the operational vocabulary (challenge point, contextual interference, external focus, transfer, specificity) is closer to the research literature [gms-nd-structure-practice] [gms-2018-clinic-report]. The two are complementary: AOC for coach-craft and practitioner translation, GMS for research framing and a more standardized teaching system.

**GMS vs. [[usa-volleyball]] / federation coaching education.** The two are not opposed in content — McGown was a fixture in USAV coaching education and his motor-learning ethos shaped federation curriculum — but they are different vehicles. CAP (now succeeded by USAV Coach Academy) is the federation's certified-coach pipeline, broader in scope (rules, safety, athlete development, age-appropriate progressions) and lighter on any single methodological commitment; GMS is the McGown-lineage methodology delivered without federation certification overhead [gms-nd-about-us] [mcgown-2017-usav-remembering].

## Critiques and limitations

The most substantive critique of the GMS school is theoretical, and it comes from inside the motor-learning research community itself. The information-processing model on which GMS rests — skill as encoded motor programs and decision rules the coach helps the athlete build through structured practice — has been directly challenged by ecological dynamics over the last fifteen years. Davids and colleagues (2013) argue that the proper unit of analysis is the **performer-environment system**, not the athlete alone, and that movement variability is **functional** rather than error to be eliminated, with the coach's job being to manipulate constraints rather than dictate technique [davids-2013-ecological-dynamics-talent]. Woods and colleagues (2020) push the critique further, arguing that "skill acquisition" is itself a misnomer because athletes do not store reproducible motor memories but develop adaptive relationships with competitive environments through practice ecologies [woods-2020-sport-ecology-designers]. Pinder and colleagues (2011) supply the practice-design version: even random/variable practice can fail the test of **representative design** if the perception-action couplings of the practice task do not match the competition environment [pinder-2011-representative-learning-design]. The disagreement is real and unresolved; this wiki presents both views without picking a side.

A second, narrower critique concerns the contextual-interference research base itself. Magill and Hall (1990) noted that the clean Shea-and-Morgan random-vs-blocked finding does not always replicate, and is most robust when task variations span **different generalized motor programs** rather than mere parameter modifications of the same GMP [magill-1990-contextual-interference-review]. The applied implication: random rotation across passing, setting, and hitting (different GMPs) is well supported by GMS's framing, while within-GMP parameter variation is on weaker empirical ground and may be better explained by Schmidt's schema theory — a distinction GMS's public materials do not always make explicit [magill-1990-contextual-interference-review].

A third limitation is documentation density. GMS's strongest first-party public materials are short blog-format articles and an About page [gms-nd-structure-practice] [gms-nd-blocking] [gms-nd-about-us]; the longer-form curriculum lives in paid clinics and the GMS+ platform. Independent attendee reports such as John Forman's 2018 clinic write-up are valuable precisely because the school's full operational detail is not freely public [gms-2018-clinic-report].

A fourth observation is social rather than scientific: the GMS network has a consistent register and shared vocabulary that can read as ideological from outside. The school's confidence in its motor-learning grounding is part of its institutional identity [gms-2017-thank-you-carl-mcgown]. Coaches comparing GMS to the constraints-led / ecological-dynamics tradition should treat both schools' confident framings with the skepticism the underlying research literatures themselves would advise.
