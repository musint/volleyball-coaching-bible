# Blocking

## Overview

Blocking is the first line of defense and the only skill in volleyball that
directly scores on the attacker — a stuffed ball is a point, a touched ball
is a slowed ball your diggers can handle, a missed block is free real
estate. The modern block asks four things of the athlete simultaneously:
read what the opposing offense is doing, travel efficiently from ready
position to the point of attack, plant and jump in balance, and finish with
hands pressed over the net into the opposing court. Any one of those
breaking down turns the block into a hole in the defense.

The last twenty years have seen the most significant generational shift in
this skill since the introduction of the reach-over rule. Swing blocking —
arms swinging like a sprinter's through the lateral close and loading for
the jump at the plant — is now the default teaching at most competitive
HS and college programs, replacing the shuffle-and-lift pattern that
dominated for decades. Gold Medal Squared, Art of Coaching Volleyball, and
most national-team programs have moved in this direction together, though
not without dissent (Terry Liskevych calls swing blocking the "flavor of
the month" and argues traditional technique is more durable, and Ken
Murczek's AOC piece questions whether the higher jump actually translates
into the net penetration that matters). At 14U that debate is academic —
shuffle blocking is the entry point, swing blocking is deferred until
fundamentals are solid. This is a skill where age-lens and level decide
the right teaching, not dogma.

Four schools of thought frame the modern methodology landscape, and they
disagree most about how reading is trained rather than about the
mechanics at the plant. AOC treats reading as an explicit teachable skill
anchored on Kiraly's B-S-B-H eye-progression (ball → setter → ball →
hitter). GMS builds the same reading skill through motor-learning
principles — random and variable practice, progressing from predetermined
sets to full variability. Ecological dynamics reframes the problem
entirely: blockers don't collect discrete cues, they directly perceive
affordances from the coupled information of the setter-hitter system, and
reading is trained by preserving representative task constraints in
practice. Japanese training, working historically with shorter blockers,
emphasizes anticipation, timing, and precise feet over raw reach —
treating the block as one layer of a defense-first system rather than a
terminator in its own right. The debates surface repeatedly on the
subskill pages below; this hub links them together.

## Major subtopics

- **[[blocking-footwork]]** — how the blocker travels from ready position
  to the point of attack. Static shuffle and crossover patterns as the
  literacy layer, swing-block footwork as the modern HS/college default.
  GMS documents a named lexicon (Q2, Q3, X2, X3); AOC teaches swing as
  the modern default at HS and above. The generational shift lives here.
- **[[blocking-hand-position]]** — near-consensus on the target (press
  over the net, fingers spread and stiff, thumbs up, palms angled down),
  ongoing disagreement on starting height, width, and whether the outside
  blocker seals the seam or turns the line in. The difference between
  a stuff, a soft block, and getting tooled is measured in inches here.
- **[[blocking-read]]** — the perceptual skill that drives everything
  else. B-S-B-H eye-progression, setter keys, hitter keys, commit-versus-
  read for middles, patterned tendencies ("teams set left off bad
  passes"). AOC, GMS, and ecological dynamics all agree this is the
  primary skill and disagree on how to train it.
- **[[blocking-swing]]** — the specific mechanical pattern now dominant
  at HS and college: sprint-arms through the lateral close, arms loaded
  at the plant, drive up and over. A distinct mechanical pattern from
  static blocking, not a faster version — installing it is a coaching
  investment that trades against other priorities.

Related drills include [[block-footwork-ladder]], [[block-touch-drill]],
[[commit-block-trigger]], [[read-blocking-progression]], and
[[swing-block-shuffle]]; system-level blocking schemes (bunch-read
vs. dedicated) live at [[read-blocking]], [[commit-blocking]], and
[[swing-blocking]] in the systems catalog.

## Schools of thought

- **[[gold-medal-squared]]** — motor-learning orthodoxy applied to
  blocking: named footwork lexicon, bunch-read as default system of 25+
  years, random/variable practice for reading transfer, early learning
  that "allows athletes to be late" so mechanics lock in before speed is
  demanded.
- **[[art-of-coaching-volleyball]]** — pedagogically eclectic, reading-
  centric, swing-blocking-as-modern-default-at-HS-and-above; Luka Slabe,
  Hugh McCutcheon, Karch Kiraly, and Matt Rockwell as the dominant
  voices, with Terry Liskevych and Ken Murczek as in-house dissenters on
  swing blocking specifically.
- **[[ecological-dynamics]]** — perception-action coupling; the blocker
  picks up affordances from the setter/hitter system in situ rather than
  processing discrete cues; training implication is preserve
  representative task constraints over drilling eye-targets in isolation.
- **[[japanese-training]]** — shorter-blocker tradition: timing, hand
  work, precise feet compensating for reach; the block is one layer of a
  defense-first system that expects to dig swings off the block, not
  necessarily stuff them.

## Getting started

If you are installing or upgrading blocking in your program, read in this
order:

1. **[[blocking-read]]** first. Reading is the primary skill; footwork
   and hand position without a read just move the block to the wrong
   place fast. The B-S-B-H eye-progression is where most programs
   underinvest.
2. **[[blocking-hand-position]]** next — it's where consensus is
   cleanest and the fewest coaching hours yield the most KPI improvement.
3. **[[blocking-footwork]]** — the generational decision point. Start
   with shuffle and crossover; introduce swing blocking only when
   fundamentals are stable and your program has the coaching hours to
   invest.
4. **[[blocking-swing]]** — read this alongside footwork so you
   understand swing as an acceleration layer on top of static literacy,
   not a replacement for it.
5. Age-lens overlay: **[[age-lens-14u]]** for why swing blocking is
   deferred at club 14U, **[[age-lens-hs]]** for the installation
   decision at HS varsity, **[[age-lens-college]]** for full swing-block
   systems with bunch-read alignments.
