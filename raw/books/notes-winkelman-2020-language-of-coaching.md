## Structure (three parts)

**Part I — Learn.** Foundations of motor learning, attention, and memory
formation. Establishes the science underpinning the practical guidance
in Parts II–III.

**Part II — Coach.** Cueing principles, cue construction, and the use of
analogy. Translates Part I theory into cueing decisions a coach makes
in real time.

**Part III — Cue.** Application chapters by movement family: habit
development, strength movements, power movements, speed development.
Cue catalogs and worked examples.

## Core framework — internal vs. external attentional focus

The book's central claim, grounded in 25+ years of motor-learning
research (Wulf and colleagues from the late 1990s onward):

- **Internal cues** direct attention to body parts or body actions
  ("bend at the hips," "extend through your knees"). They tend to
  *disrupt* automatic motor control by forcing conscious organization
  of movement that would otherwise self-organize.
- **External cues** direct attention to the movement *outcome* or to
  objects in the environment ("push the ground away," "stay long and
  low," "drive your feet through the floor"). They allow the motor
  system to self-organize and *consistently outperform* internal cues
  on:
  - Balance and postural tasks
  - Force and velocity expression (jumping, throwing)
  - Change-of-direction speed
  - Sport skills with implements
  - Continuous skills (sprinting, swimming, agility)
- **Normal cues** (no instruction beyond prior learning) underperform
  external cues but vary widely depending on the athlete's
  previously-acquired self-cueing.

### Acute vs. chronic learning

A key Winkelman/Wulf finding the book makes operational: short-term
performance lifts from any cueing approach do not guarantee long-term
retention. **Retention tests conducted days after practice show
external-cued learners retain skills better than internal-cued learners.**
Coaches whose cueing decisions optimize for the next rep can
unintentionally select against durable learning.

## The "Three D's" of external cueing

Winkelman's practical framework for constructing effective external
cues:

- **Distance.** Close (focus on a nearby object — the ball, the
  net, the floor near the feet) vs. far (a distant object — the back
  line, the opponent's tendon line, the ceiling). **Novices benefit
  from close-distance cues; experienced athletes perform better with
  far-distance cues.** This is the operational pivot for coaches
  scaling cues across age levels, including 14U → HS → college on
  this wiki.
- **Direction.** "Towards" cues (pull, drive, push toward) outperform
  "away" cues (escape, get off, retract from) in jumping and
  acceleration tasks. The grammatical direction of the cue is itself
  a coaching variable.
- **Description.** Action verbs ("push," "explode," "snap") and
  analogies ("like a slingshot," "spring-loaded," "punch through") are
  both effective. Analogies are highlighted as especially valuable
  because they encode complex motor patterns in a single mental image
  the athlete already understands.

## Analogy as primary instructional unit

A throughline of Part II: a well-chosen analogy is *more powerful than
a list of technical instructions* because it activates a mental map
the athlete already possesses, then borrows the motor solution attached
to that map. Examples in the book span weight-room and sprint
applications and generalize directly to court sports.

## Coaching cue construction process

The book frames cue selection as a deliberate craft, not an off-the-cuff
verbal habit. Steps it operationalizes:

1. Identify the movement outcome the cue must produce.
2. Choose internal vs. external focus (default: external, except in
   specific safety/technical-correction contexts).
3. Select distance (close for novice, far for advanced).
4. Choose direction ("towards" preferred where applicable).
5. Choose description: action verb or analogy that the specific
   athlete or group will decode automatically.
6. Test the cue against the athlete's response; iterate.

## Habit formation — three-step coaching upgrade

Part III opens with a habit-development frame for coaches changing
their own cueing patterns:

- **Cue trigger.** When in practice does the new cue need to fire?
- **Routine.** What is the upgraded cueing behavior?
- **Reward.** What confirms the cue worked? (Athlete behavior change,
  outcome metric, retention on next session.)

The frame is borrowed from behavior-change literature (Duhigg, Clear)
and applied to coaching practice.

## Sport-specific coverage

The book is multi-sport in its examples (rugby, sprint, weight-room,
basketball) but the principles are sport-agnostic motor-learning
foundations. **Volleyball-applicability is direct** because:

- Volleyball is composed almost entirely of fast, externally-paced
  motor skills (passing, setting, hitting, blocking, serving, defense,
  transition footwork) where external cues outperform internal cues
  by the same margins observed across the broader research base.
- Volleyball coaches at all levels typically rely heavily on internal
  cues ("get your platform low," "snap your wrist," "transfer your
  weight") — exactly the pattern the book argues *against* for
  retention.
- The "Three D's" map cleanly to volleyball cueing tradeoffs: novice
  14U passers benefit from close cues ("contact the ball just below
  the wrist line"); HS and college passers benefit from far cues
  ("send the ball to the setter's right hand").

## Limitations

- The book is not volleyball-specific; its examples are
  multi-sport. Volleyball-specific worked examples must be supplied by
  the wiki when applying its principles.
- The book is recent (2021); its long-term influence on volleyball
  coaching curricula is still propagating. USAV CAP / Coach Academy
  curricula may or may not yet cite it in training. [unsourced]
- Internal cue advocates push back on the strict external-cue
  preference for *very early* skill acquisition (where attention to
  body parts can be necessary for safety / fundamental setup). The
  book acknowledges this nuance in the safety/technical-correction
  carve-out.
