# Matsudaira — Japanese coaching philosophy & Winning Volleyball — composite fair-use notes

coaching-philosophy writings — the book widely referenced as **Winning
Volleyball** — together with the Japanese-language coaching-memoir lineage
implied by his Wikipedia bibliography (Tokyo Metropolitan Assembly Proceedings

## The verifiable anchor — Winning Volleyball

**Title (English working title):** Winning Volleyball
**Author:** Yasutaka Matsudaira (松平康隆, 1930-2011)
**Composition period:** Pre-1972 — written by Matsudaira *before* the Munich
Olympic gold, in the period during which Japan men's NT was building from the
1964 Tokyo bronze and 1968 Mexico City silver toward the Munich result
[matsudaira-2026-wikipedia-biography] [matsudaira-1998-hall-of-fame].
**English translation arranged:** 1975 — permission obtained by Vic Lindal;
translation by Mr. Kikuchi; technical evaluation by Lorne Sawuala (per Vic
Lindal's commenter note on the Mark Lebedew blog post).
**Distribution:** Limited / out of commerce — described by Mark Lebedew in 2012
as "doesn't seem to be available anywhere" commercially; preserved by Lebedew
for historical interest.

The book documents Matsudaira's three-Olympic medal progression (bronze 1964,
silver 1968, gold 1972) and the methodological work behind the Japanese men's
program — though composed before Munich and therefore stopping short of the
gold-medal consummation it implicitly anticipates. The most commonly
extracted thematic content from the available descriptions:

- **The power of vision** — Vic Lindal's commenter framing emphasizes
  Matsudaira's vision-driven program design: the long-arc commitment to
  Olympic gold across multiple cycles rather than year-by-year tactical
  optimization (per Mark Lebedew comment thread).
- **The "sacrifice system" in 9-person volleyball** — the domestic Japanese
  9-player game's tactical structure (five attacking players with only one
  setter receiving the set) is documented in the book as a precursor to the
  6-player system Matsudaira built (per Vic Lindal's commenter description).
- **Soviet-study-trip methodology** — the early-1960s study of Soviet six-a-side
  coaching as the methodological pivot for the Japanese men's program (per
  Wikipedia biography and the Mark Lebedew blog framing).
- **Multiple quick attack / time-differential attack design rationale** — the
  offensive-system reasoning anchoring Matsudaira's most-cited technical
  contribution, designed around Japanese players' physical reality rather than
  imitation of Soviet/Polish/East-German power volleyball
  [matsudaira-1998-hall-of-fame].

The detailed content of the book — chapter structure, specific drill
prescriptions, tempo-naming conventions for the multiple-quick-attack system —

## Direct quotes preserved in English

The single most-quoted Matsudaira first-person framing in the English-language
record is the **orchestra-conductor metaphor**:

> "Volleyball was a heaven-sent present, like an orchestra, with himself making
> strenuous efforts and devoting a lifetime of service to become the best
> conductor in the world." [matsudaira-1998-hall-of-fame]

The Hall of Fame record extends the metaphor: Matsudaira viewed his coaching
role as enabling players to prepare optimally by **"unifying and harmonizing
the unique tone quality of each different player."** This frames coaching as
integration-of-specialties rather than universal development — each player's
physical and technical profile is a specific instrument whose best music
requires specific scoring.

Beyond the orchestra-conductor metaphor, English-language direct Matsudaira
quotation is essentially absent in the sources captured for this composite.
Direct Matsudaira quote material requires Japanese-language ingest of the
sources flagged above [translation-needed].

## Themes most likely developed at length in the original Japanese material

Drawing on the broader Japanese-training-school documentation and the existing
wiki coach profile, the original Japanese-language Matsudaira material is most
likely to develop the following themes at length [unsourced] [translation-needed]:

1. **Design tactics around your players' physical reality, not opponents'
   reality** — the Munich-era compensate-what-you-cannot-match logic.
2. **Foreign study + domestic synthesis + original contribution** — the
   methodological pattern of his Soviet study trip → Japanese tactical
   redesign → multiple-quick-attack invention.
3. **Coach-as-orchestra-conductor doctrine** — the integration-of-specialties
   coaching identity preserved in the Hall of Fame record.
4. **Olympic medal progression as program standard** — the 1964→1968→1972
   bronze-silver-gold arc as proof-of-concept for multi-cycle program building.
5. **Player career prepares coaching authority** — the 20-year nine-a-side
   playing résumé as foundation for national-team coaching authority.
6. **Post-coaching FIVB and Asian-volleyball institutional work** — the
   1979-2011 administrative arc as the institutionalization phase of his
   coaching philosophy.

These six themes are the framework currently used on
`wiki/coaches/yasutaka-matsudaira.md`. Direct Matsudaira-text citations to
support them require Japanese-language ingest [translation-needed].
