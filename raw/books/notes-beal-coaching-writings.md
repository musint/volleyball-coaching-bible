# Doug Beal — Coaching Writings (Composite Notes)

This is a composite raw-notes file covering Doug Beal's published coaching writings,
since Beal does not have a single book-length coaching manual that consolidates his
methodology. The composite spans his 1985 autobiographical-and-program book, his
chapter contribution to the AVCA's *The Volleyball Coaching Bible* anthology, and
his 1991 *Coaching Tips for the 90's* contribution. Per SCHEMA §8.4, all material
is fair-use synthesis from publisher previews, third-party reviews, biographical
encyclopedias, and Beal's own AOC-platform interview material already in the wiki's
source base.

## Confirmed published works

### 1. *Spike!* (1985) — primary autobiographical / program book

- **Title:** *Spike!*
- **Author:** Doug Beal (with Marc Katz, per Highland Park Public Library catalog
  attribution)
- **Year:** 1985 (April 1)
- **Publisher:** Avant Books / Slawson Communications Inc.
- **ISBN:** 0932238300 / 9780932238306
- **Page count:** ~155 pages (one review notes "just over 100 pages")
- **Format:** Hardcover, with 32 pages of action photos
- **Review source:** John Forman, "Book Review: Spike! by Doug Beal" (coachingvb.com, 2015)
- **Companion text:** Sean Murray's *If Gold is Our Destiny* (alternative narrative
  account of the 1984 USA men's gold; Forman recommends as more readily available
  substitute when *Spike!* is hard to find)

**Scope and content:**
- Autobiographical narrative of the 1977-1984 USA Men's National Team rebuild
- Pre-1984 program history covering Beal's player era and the structural problems
  of the U.S. men's program before the 1978 Dayton training-center founding
- Detailed account of the 1984 Olympic cycle: roster assembly, training-center
  decisions, system changes, Olympic competition itself
- Player-by-player treatment, including:
  - Karch Kiraly (the youngest 1984 gold-medalist, future indoor-and-beach legend)
  - Sinjin Smith (Beal's published candor about disliking Smith and "beach players"
    is a documented feature of the book)
  - Tim Hovland (similarly candid coverage)
  - Pat Powers and other 1984-era roster members
- Beal's account of the coaching decisions behind the gold — including handling
  personality conflicts, system changes, and the cuts that shaped the final 12
- The book attributes formative influence to Soviet coach Yuri Chesnokov, who
  according to Beal "instructed [him] on necessary improvements during the late
  1970s" — one of the few cited cross-cultural coaching-mentor relationships in
  the modern American men's-side record

**Tone (per Forman review):**
- "Beal seems pretty forthright about sharing his own short-comings and missteps
  along the way" — humble rather than self-promotional
- "Both interesting from a historical perspective and quite relevant" to current
  coaching challenges
- Categorized as historical narrative rather than coaching manual, but includes
  real explanation of decision-making behind major program choices

### 2. *The Volleyball Coaching Bible* (Vol. 1, 2002) — chapter contribution

- **Anthology editor(s):** Don Shondell + Cecile Reynaud (AVCA)
- **Publisher:** Human Kinetics
- **Year:** 2002 (Vol. 1; Vol. 2 was published 2014)
- **ISBN:** 0736039678 (Vol. 1)
- **Beal's chapter:** Part 2, Chapter 4 — "Seeking Excellence in a Program—Going
  for the Gold"
- **Anthology scope:** 24 volleyball experts contributing chapters across coaching
  philosophy, program management, technique, systems, and conditioning

The chapter title — "Seeking Excellence in a Program — Going for the Gold" — is
itself the methodology compass: Beal's published coaching writing is consistently
about *program-level excellence*, not drill libraries. "Going for the gold"
references the 1984 result but the chapter is reportedly forward-looking rather
than retrospective.

### 3. *Volleyball: The Keys to Excellence / Coaching Tips for the 90's* (1991, 2nd ed.)

- **Publisher:** Sports Support Syndicate
- **Year:** 1991 (2nd edition, February)
- **ISBN:** 1878602365
- **Format:** Multi-author practical-tips compendium, with Beal contributing
- **Scope:** Era-specific coaching strategies and training methods aimed at U.S.
  collegiate and club coaches in the post-1988 environment

## Documented coaching positions (synthesized across the corpus)

These are positions Beal has published, lectured, or stated in on-the-record
interview material. Each is anchored to a specific source.

**On the modern game's loss of national style** [marklebedew-2014-beal]:
> "Everyone plays the same, same speed, same game system."
Beal's view, per Lebedew's essay: contemporary international volleyball has
homogenized to a single tactical-tempo template, and the loss of distinctive
national styles is itself a problem for the sport's spectator appeal.

**On rules changes that favor defense** [marklebedew-2014-beal]:
- Beal argues tipping is poor for the game
- Rules should favor defense to "make the rallies longer"
- Rally scoring he likes overall ("I really like the rally scoring system" —
  for the team-responsibility implications) but he finds 25-point sets "a bit
  short, especially for men's volleyball"

**On the coach-athlete relationship** [marklebedew-2014-beal]:
> "A coach should take the athletes to their limits... A coach needs to be
> respected, to have authority."
This is a positional contrast with the more facilitator-style coaching language
Beal's successors (McCutcheon's "championship behaviors," Kiraly's "give players
freedom") have favored. The wiki should present both views with attribution.

**On the 1984 program's structural innovations** [marklebedew-2014-beal] [beal-wikipedia-bio]:
- Two-player passing system (Kiraly + Aldis Berzins) replacing the five-player
  passing approach then dominant internationally
- Blocking system position changes
- Both system changes were tied to year-round center-based training (1978
  Dayton center, 1981 San Diego relocation) that was structurally enabling

**On training passing married to approach (2025 AOC interview, with Ben Josephson)**
[aoc-2025-beal-josephson-dont-train-passing-isolation]:
- "It's really important that those two skills are married"
- Passing alone is never a thing in the game, so it should never be a thing in
  practice
- Most aces come from seams between two passers, not shanked individual contacts —
  so passing drills must always involve at least two passers
- Aligns Beal directly with the GMS specificity stance and the USAV game-like-training
  spine

## Honors / institutional record

(Already documented on `wiki/coaches/doug-beal.md`; reproducing key items for
this notes file's completeness)
- 1984 Olympic gold, USA Men's NT head coach (Los Angeles)
- 1985 World Cup gold + 1986 World Championship gold (the "Triple Crown" — Beal's
  personnel and systems carrying through, with Marv Dunphy as immediate successor)
- 1988 Seoul Olympic gold (Dunphy as HC; Beal as Center Director)
- 1989 Volleyball Hall of Fame inductee
- 1995 USAV first All-Time Great Coach Award recipient
- FIVB Greatest Coach of the Century finalist
- USAV CEO January 2005 - January 2017 (12 years)
- Three-time Olympic head coach: 1984, 2000, 2004
