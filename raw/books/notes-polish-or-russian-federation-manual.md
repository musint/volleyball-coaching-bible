## Primary anchor — Vyacheslav Platonov, *My Profession: The Game*

**Title:** *My Profession: The Game*
**Author:** Vyacheslav Platonov (Вячеслав Платонов, 1939-2005)
**Original Russian publication:** Pre-2005 (described as Platonov's final book
before his December 2005 death; specific original Russian title and original
publisher not surfaced in the English-language sources fetched [translation-needed]).
**English translation released:** February 2014
**Translator:** Mark Lebedew's father (Russian-origin) translated; English
edition prepared by the Lebedew family (Mark Lebedew is a credentialed
coach and author, lending technical fidelity to volleyball-specific terminology).
**Distribution:** Lulu.com — paperback, hardcover, ePub, and iTunes ebook
formats.

### Author background

Vyacheslav Platonov was head coach of the **USSR men's national volleyball
team 1977-1985 and 1990-1991**, with Olympic gold (1980 Moscow), World
Championship golds (1978 Italy, 1982 Argentina), and **five consecutive
European Championship golds 1977-1985** [platonov-2026-wikipedia]. The
1977-1983 stretch is described in coaching-press coverage as a stretch in
which the Soviets "won every competition they entered" — two World
Championships, two World Cups, four European Championships, one Olympic
Gold. Platonov also coached Automobilist Leningrad to USSR Championships in
1983 and 1989, establishing the Leningrad / Saint Petersburg organizational
lineage that continues as modern Zenit Saint Petersburg.

### Book structure and content

Per the coachingvb.com review: the book is a **blend of theory and practice
without instructional drills**. It is a coaching-philosophy and program-
methodology manual rather than a drill-book. The marklebedew.com publisher's
description frames it as a handbook covering practical coaching wisdom
accumulated over decades, organized around five thematic axes:

1. **Developing your own coaching style** — the meta-question of what kind of
   coach to be, given the Soviet program's institutional context.
2. **Building a team** — roster construction, player roles, team-culture
   commitments.
3. **Qualities of a successful coach** — Platonov's list of dispositional
   commitments.
4. **Training and preparation methods** — periodization, practice-design
   philosophy, the program's commitment to high-volume disciplined repetition.
5. **In-game coaching strategies** — match management, tactical adjustment
   philosophy, the relationship between pre-trained patterns and real-time
   reading.

A notable structural exception: a **single in-depth technical chapter devoted
entirely to blocking fundamentals** — the technical area Platonov treated as
the program's most distinctive doctrinal contribution, consistent with the
Russian-school identification of block-and-defense as the program's center of
gravity.

### Key teaching themes (from review and publisher description)

The following themes are documented in the English-language reviews of *My
Profession: The Game* and are appropriate for citation from this composite:

- **The coach is the program's intellectual center.** Platonov's framing
  positions the head coach as carrying the program's tactical and cultural
  vision; the book is structured as advice from one head coach to another.
- **Pre-trained team patterns over individual freelance.** The Soviet
  "systems" approach — every rotation has drilled defensive coverage,
  serve-receive patterning, and blocking assignment — is the implicit
  methodological frame for the book's training-and-preparation chapters
  [unsourced — implied by review framing, not directly quoted].
- **Block-and-defense as program identity.** The single in-depth technical
  chapter on blocking fundamentals signals the Russian-school commitment to
  block-and-defense as the most differentiating tactical area — consistent
  with the broader Russian-school documentation in this wiki.
- **Coach as program-builder rather than match-tactician.** The book's
  five-axis structure (style → team → coach qualities → training → in-game) is
  a program-building framework, not a play-by-play match-coaching manual.
- **Reading multiple times yields more.** The reviewer frames the book as
  "merits multiple readings" — Platonov's compressed delivery rewards
  re-engagement; "bite-sized chunks" structure suits busy coaches.
- **Pre-modern game with anticipated evolution.** Some content reflects the
  pre-rally-scoring era; Platonov anticipated certain rule and tactical
  changes, but readers must contextualize against the pre-2000 game.

The specific drill prescriptions, periodization-week structures, and Russian-
language tactical terminology in the book are [unsourced] [translation-needed]
in this composite pending direct ingest of the English text.

### Notable absence — no instructional drill book

Coaches expecting a drill-prescription manual will be disappointed; the book
is philosophy and program-methodology, not skill-by-skill drill instruction.
The single in-depth chapter on blocking is the technical exception. Pair this
book with a drills-oriented Eastern-bloc reference (or with the Karpol-era
women's-program documentation) for full Russian-school coverage.

### Hubert Wagner (1941-2002)

Polish men's NT head coach 1973-1976 (continuing intermittently through 1980s),
**1974 World Champion** (Mexico) and **1976 Olympic gold** (Montreal). FIVB
"Coach of the Century" (2000); his 1974-1979 Polish men's NT named "Team of
the Century" (FIVB 2000). Hall of Fame inductee 2010.

**Methodology characteristics** (per Wikipedia):
- Demanding training intensity — Polish team court-time at 1976 Olympics
  reportedly 11+1⁄2 hours vs. opponents' 5 hours.
- Aggressive serving emphasis.
- Eastern European discipline blended with creative play.
- "Wonder Team" / "Wonder Team of Wagner" (Cudowna Drużyna Wagnera) program
  identity.
- Rigorous central-training-camp model.
- Philosophy: "Any other place beyond first is irrelevant."

**Authored coaching textbook:** None surfaced in English-indexed search
[unsourced] [translation-needed]. A Polish-language search of PZPS publications
or the Polish National Library catalog may surface a Wagner-authored
methodology text; no such text is documented here.

### Andrzej Niemczyk (1944-2016)

Polish women's NT head coach during the "Golden Girls" era — **2003 and 2005
European Championship golds**, the foundation of modern Polish women's
volleyball.

**Memoir / biography:** *Andrzej Niemczyk. Life tie-break* (co-authored with
Marek Bobakovsky), in Polish. This is a personal-narrative memoir, not a
federation methodology manual. Ingest would require Polish-language work
[translation-needed].
