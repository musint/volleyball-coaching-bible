## The verifiable anchor — 1971 IVBF Coach Training Course textbook

**Title:** "Textbook for Volleyball Coaches: For First IVBF Coach Training Course
31 May - 10 June 1971, Tokyo"
**Author:** Japan Volleyball Association (institutional)
**Co-publisher:** Fédération Internationale de Volleyball (FIVB / IVBF in 1971
nomenclature)
**Year:** 1971 (course held); textbook issued for the course
**Page count:** 276 pages (Google Books listing)
**Language:** English (course was an international FIVB coach-development course
held in Tokyo)

This textbook is the institutional anchor for Japanese federation
coaching-literature dissemination into the international game. It was produced
by the JVA — at the time the federation that had just delivered Daimatsu's 1964
Tokyo women's gold and was building toward Matsudaira's 1972 Munich men's gold
— specifically for the FIVB's first international coach-development course,
held in Tokyo in 1971. The pattern (national federation produces the canonical
coaching textbook; international federation co-publishes for global distribution)
is the institutional template that the Japanese federation pioneered in
volleyball; specific authorship attribution within the textbook (which Japanese
coaches contributed which sections) is not visible in the Google Books metadata
fetched [unsourced] [translation-needed].

## Themes likely covered in JVA coaching-textbook lineage

The pattern of Japanese coaching-textbook output in the 1970s-1980s — which
"Mizoguchi" plausibly contributed to — would be expected to cover the following
themes, drawing on what *is* documented about the broader Japanese-training
school. All claims in this section are inferences from the school-level
documentation, not direct citations from a Mizoguchi text [unsourced]
[translation-needed]:

- **Multiple quick attack / time-differential attack methodology** — the
  Matsudaira-era offensive system staggering middle-and-combination attacks
  on overlapping timing, designed to compensate for height disadvantage against
  taller European opponents.
- **Receive-platform fundamentals and serve-receive system construction** —
  the Japanese tradition's commitment to ball-control as the program's central
  technical commitment.
- **Block-and-defense reading** — the read-block plus active-floor-defense
  combination that characterizes the Japanese defensive tradition.
- **Player-led practice culture** and **catch-and-set early-setting
  progressions** — youth-development conventions documented in modern Japanese
  coaching literature and observed by visiting coaches.
- **Physical-conditioning periodization** designed around a smaller-stature
  player base — the methodological reasoning behind why Japanese players
  developed quickness, anticipation, and tempo as compensating variables.

These themes are the *expected content* of a Japanese federation coaching
textbook of the 1971-1985 era. The specific authorial contributions attributable
to a "Mizoguchi" within that content remain [unsourced] [translation-needed]
