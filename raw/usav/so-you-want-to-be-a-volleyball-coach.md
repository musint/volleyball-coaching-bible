# So You Want To Be A Volleyball Coach

**Author:** Pat Madia-Western Empire Region Coaching Education Chair

---

## Content

### Key Message

The article emphasizes that success in coaching requires humility, preparation, and a focus on developing people rather than merely winning games.

### Main Sections

**1. Your Playing Career Doesn't Define Your Coaching**

The author stresses that "Your players don't care how good you were as a player." Athletes evaluate coaches based on current competence and preparation, not past achievements. Coaches must abandon ego and approach the role as continuous learning.

**2. Embrace Accountability Structure**

According to the piece, coaches are responsible for losses but should credit team victories to players. This framework prevents self-aggrandizement and maintains appropriate perspective on leadership roles.

**3. Organization is Essential**

Successful coaches maintain detailed rosters, insurance documentation, emergency protocols, and weekly practice plans. The author notes that "Your athletes know the difference between someone who is prepared and someone who is making it up as they go along."

**4. Establish Clear Expectations**

A comprehensive team handbook and parent meeting at season's start prevents confusion. Coaches must enforce their own rules consistently or lose credibility with athletes.

**5. Professional Development Matters**

Coaches should pursue certifications (CAP I, CAP II, SafeSport) and engage with coaching resources like John Kessel's blogs and the "Coach Your Brains Out" podcast. Athletes deserve coaches committed to growth.

**6. Emotional Management**

Coaches must control their reactions to setbacks and manage difficult parent interactions professionally. Maintaining perspective prevents bitterness during challenging seasons.

**7. Constructive Thinking**

Rather than dismissing athletes as "bench warmers," coaches should identify specific skills needing development and create pathways for improvement.

**8. Realistic Expectations**

Player development occurs gradually. Coaches should celebrate incremental progress rather than expecting rapid transformations.

**9. Remember Your True Purpose**

The author concludes: "You don't coach volleyballs, you coach people." While competitive success matters, teaching life lessons about teamwork and character yields greater long-term impact than any championship.
