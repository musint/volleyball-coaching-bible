# Youth Volleyball Tips for Success

**Author:** John Kessel

## Overview

Drawing from nearly 40 years coaching 12U volleyball, this guide addresses core concepts for growing youth volleyball programs, particularly for ages 6-12.

## Four Core Topics

### 1. Fitting Volleyball Into Other Sports

**Space-Sharing Solutions:**
The guide recommends requesting a single court section rather than an entire gymnasium from basketball facilities. Programs can establish "three to four courts up on a single regular court space, using the four nets on a rope."

**Multi-Sport Net Systems:**
The Maximum Contact Net System accommodates six sports at different heights:
- Youth volleyball: 5 feet
- Older kids' volleyball: 6'6"
- Badminton: 5 feet
- Tennis: 3 feet
- Sitting volleyball (men): 3'9 1/4"
- Sitting volleyball (women): 3'7 1/4"
- Pickleball: 3 feet

**Alternative Scheduling:**
Programs should consider training at different times—early morning or late evening—rather than only during standard hours.

### 2. Finding Enough Coaches

**Leveraging Available Resources:**
The document highlights underutilized coaching resources: elementary PE teachers managing large class ratios and club members seeking to develop coaching skills. "Every club program has dozens of coaches waiting to improve their coaching skills," yet this remains underdeveloped.

**Open Gym Value:**
Unstructured, minimally supervised open gym sessions develop player leadership and decision-making without direct instruction.

### 3. Coaching Youngest Players (Ages 3-9)

**Movement Training Focus:**
Young players benefit from movement discovery through varied activities—"can you do this?" games, tag games, various-sized balls, foam noodles, and court challenges like hopscotch and dodge ball.

**Cross-Sport Integration:**
Programs should introduce other Olympic and non-Olympic sports as warm-ups or training breaks.

**Parent Involvement:**
Parents serve as teammates during casual play, not coaches—engaging in simple games like two-versus-two over a rope.

### 4. Grills for Little Kids

**Game-Based Learning:**
Children prefer games over drills. "Grills" (game-like drills with scoring) engage young players more effectively than traditional drill sequences.

**Alternative Games:**
When nets are unavailable, the "loser becomes the net" game works with 3-12 players. The net player cannot jump but actively participates by blocking and reaching.

**Play-Based Development:**
Children naturally learn through play, similar to how tag games develop without formal instruction. The guide references the Minivolley Book for scoring variations including "maxi scoring" and circulation rotation systems.
