# An Approach to Quantify the Float Effect of Float Serves in Indoor and Beach Volleyball

**Authors:** Mathias Reiser, Karen Zentgraf, Stefan Kindermann, Stefan Künzell
**Journal:** Frontiers in Sports and Active Living
**Year:** 2020
**DOI:** 10.3389/fspor.2020.559277

## Abstract / study overview

The authors developed two kinematic measures — *mean residual* and *anticipation error* — derived from regression models of 3-D ball trajectories, to objectively quantify the unpredictable in-flight ball motion characteristic of float serves. They tested the measures on 24 float serves from 12 elite servers (six indoor, six beach), validating against expert subjective ratings of float magnitude.

## Key findings

- Vertical-plane deviations were roughly twice the magnitude of horizontal-plane deviations in both indoor and beach float serves.
- Mean release velocity was higher indoors (17.79 m/s) than on the beach (15.14 m/s).
- Release velocity is necessary but not sufficient for ball float — explaining minimal variance in float magnitude (r² < 0.033).
- Anticipation error reached up to 1.5 m vertically and 1.0 m horizontally, quantifying the spatial difficulty for the receiver.
- Expert subjective float ratings correlated with horizontal-deviation measures; vertical-deviation correlations were weaker, attributed to observer-position constraints.

## Key claims / ideas

- "Float" is a measurable trajectory phenomenon, not merely a subjective qualitative descriptor.
- Vertical drop is the dominant float component — passers should expect the ball to fall more sharply than its parabolic prediction.
- Higher release velocity does not produce more float; ball-contact technique drives the unpredictability.
