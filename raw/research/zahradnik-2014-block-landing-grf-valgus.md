# Ground Reaction Force and Valgus Knee Loading during Landing after a Block in Female Volleyball Players

**Authors:** David Zahradnik, Jaroslav Uchytil, Roman Farana, Daniel Jandacka
**Journal:** Journal of Human Kinetics
**Year:** 2014
**DOI:** 10.2478/hukin-2014-0008

## Abstract / study overview

Fourteen elite female volleyball players performed three landing variants — stick landing, step-back landing, and run-back landing — after simulated blocks. Force plates and infrared motion capture measured ground reaction forces (GRF) and knee biomechanics, with particular attention to ACL-injury-risk markers (knee valgus moment, internal rotation variability) on the dominant lower limb.

## Key findings

- Run-back landings produced significantly higher knee valgus moments on the right (dominant) limb than step-back landings (p = 0.019).
- Vertical GRF showed no statistically significant difference among landings, though run-back values trended higher.
- Internal rotation variability was greater during run-back landings, an additional ACL-risk marker.
- Step-back landings sit between stick and run-back in risk profile; stick landings are the safest.
- Female volleyball blockers landing in a "run away from the net" emergency are biomechanically the most exposed.

## Key claims / ideas

- The instruction "block and immediately retreat" should be paired with neuromuscular training that prevents knee valgus collapse on the run-out step.
- The tactical choice (stick vs. step-back vs. run-back after the block) carries an injury-risk gradient that coaches typically don't price in.
- For 14U / HS girls especially, run-back-style landings should be drilled with explicit attention to landing mechanics, given the well-documented ACL-injury rate disparity.
