# Do verbal coaching cues and analogies affect motor skill performance in youth populations?

**Authors:** Jason Moran, Raouf Hammami, Joshua Butson, Matt Allen, Abdelkader Mahmoudi, Norodin Vali, Ieuan Lewis, Phil Samuel, Mike Davies, James Earle, Megan Klabunde, Gavin Sandercock
**Journal:** PLoS One
**Year:** 2023
**Volume/Issue:** 18(3): e0280201
**DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pone.0280201

## Abstract (synthesized from PMC access)

The multi-site study investigated whether **external coaching cues (EC), internal coaching cues (IC), or analogies with directional components (ADC)** enhance motor-skill performance in youth populations relative to a neutral control. Across multiple international locations, **173 participants** performed vertical jumps and 20-metre sprints preceded by their assigned cue. Internal meta-analyses pooling across sites showed **no significant differences** between any of the three experimental cue types and the neutral control — the only exception being that the **neutral control was superior to internal cueing for vertical jump**. Of eleven location-specific analyses, **only three** revealed significant differences, and most of those favored the neutral control. The authors conclude that "the type of cue or analogy provided to a youth performer has little subsequent effect on sprint or jump performance."

## Key claims / ideas
- Large multi-site youth trial (n = 173) of the OPTIMAL-theory / Wulf attentional-focus paradigm, generalized to **vertical jump and 20-m sprint** (whole-body explosive tasks).
- Pooled across sites, **no advantage** for external cues, internal cues, or directional analogies versus a no-cue control.
- The **single significant pooled finding** went the other way: neutral control beat internal cueing on vertical jump.
- Strongly counters the "always use external cues with kids" pop-coaching maxim — the youth evidence base is weaker than the adult evidence base.
- Possible mediators not tested: cue novelty, cue specificity to the task, individual differences in attentional control.
- Rather than blanket rules, supports an **individualization stance**: try cue, observe, retain what works for that player.
- Methodologically valuable as a *negative replication* — pre-registered-style multi-site design with a youth population the original Wulf paradigm rarely tested.
