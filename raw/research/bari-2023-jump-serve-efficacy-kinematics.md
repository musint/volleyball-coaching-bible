# Analysis of the kinematic variables that predict jump serve efficacy among volleyball players

**Authors:** Mohd Arshad Bari, Ali Azeez Aneed Al Mijbilee, Shibili Nuhmani, Amir Iqbal, Ahmad H. Alghadir
**Journal:** Medicine (Baltimore)
**Year:** 2023
**DOI:** 10.1097/MD.0000000000034471

## Abstract / study overview

Thirty male collegiate volleyball players were grouped into U-23 men, U-21 junior men, and U-19 youth boys. Synchronized cameras and motion-analysis software measured kinematic variables of the jump-topspin serve. The authors built linear models predicting ball velocity from kinematic predictors at each level.

## Key findings

- For U-23 men, the optimal model combined approach velocity, shoulder-extension angle during cocking, and centre-of-gravity height — explaining 100% of ball-velocity variation in this sample.
- For U-21 junior men, the model used centre-of-gravity height + shoulder-extension angle, explaining 98% of ball-velocity variation.
- For U-19 youth boys, approach velocity alone explained 89% of ball-velocity variation.
- Predictor complexity grew with maturity: senior players benefited from multi-factor mechanical optimization; younger players were rate-limited by approach velocity.
- Shoulder-extension angle during the cocking phase (set/load) became increasingly explanatory with age, suggesting upper-body coordination develops later than approach mechanics.

## Key claims / ideas

- Coaching the jump topspin serve should be developmentally staged: approach velocity first, shoulder-extension and CoG mechanics second.
- Young servers benefit from explosive approach work before high-volume coaching of the cocking position.
- Multi-segment coordination is a marker of senior-level serving, not a junior-level cue.
