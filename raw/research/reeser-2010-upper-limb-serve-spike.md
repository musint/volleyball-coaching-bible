# Upper Limb Biomechanics During the Volleyball Serve and Spike

**Authors:** Jonathan C. Reeser, Glenn S. Fleisig, Becky Bolt, Mianfang Ruan
**Journal:** Sports Health
**Year:** 2010
**DOI:** 10.1177/1941738110374624

## Abstract / study overview

The authors used 3-D motion analysis on 14 female collegiate athletes performing five volleyball-specific upper-limb skills: hard cross-body spike, hard straight-ahead spike, jump topspin serve, standing float serve, and roll shot. The aim was to compare shoulder and elbow forces, torques, angular velocities, and kinematics across skills, with the clinical goal of identifying which skills place the most stress on the throwing shoulder.

## Key findings

- Shoulder abduction at ball contact averaged ~130° across all skills, substantially greater than tennis or baseball overhand actions.
- Hard spikes and the jump topspin serve produced the highest shoulder/elbow forces, torques, and angular velocities.
- Float serves produced lower shoulder/elbow loads than spikes; roll shots produced the lowest loads of any tested skill.
- No statistically significant biomechanical difference between cross-body and straight-ahead spike attempts.
- Jump-serve mechanics closely mirror power-spike mechanics (a single training/coaching implication: an athlete with a sore shoulder should reduce both spikes and jump serves, not just one).

## Key claims / ideas

- Volleyball spiking and jump-serving are high-load shoulder skills comparable to or exceeding baseball pitching in some kinetic measures.
- For athletes with shoulder pain, replacing hard spike reps with roll-shot or float-serve reps reduces shoulder load while preserving overhead practice.
- The cross-body vs. straight-ahead distinction matters tactically, but not kinetically — both load the shoulder similarly.

## Topics covered

- Shoulder/elbow kinetics in volleyball
- Spike vs. serve load comparison
- Roll-shot biomechanics
- Shoulder injury prevention
- Training-load substitution strategies
