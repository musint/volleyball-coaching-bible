# Return to play and athletic performance in division I female volleyball players following anterior cruciate ligament injury

**Authors:** Nathan P. Smith, Robert A. Gallo
**Journal:** The Physician and Sportsmedicine
**Year:** 2025 (February; epub 7 May 2024)

## Abstract / study overview

Retrospective analysis of NCAA Division I female volleyball athletes who sustained ACL injuries between 2008 and 2020 across seven collegiate conferences (n=99). Examined return-to-competition rates and post-injury performance metrics relative to pre-injury baselines.

## Key findings

- **Return rate:** 93.1% of injured Division I female volleyball athletes returned to competition.
- **Substantial return:** 65.7% played in 35+ sets post-injury (i.e., re-established a meaningful role).
- **Limited return:** 16.2% played in fewer than 35 sets; 6.1% recorded no in-game stats; 12.1% graduated without returning.
- **Position skew:** attackers comprised 54.7% of the control population but sustained 78.8% of ACL injuries — outsized injury share.
- **Performance trajectory:** statistical performance increased linearly with years post-injury — suggesting progressive recovery rather than plateau, given criteria-based rehab.

## Key claims / ideas

- Division I volleyball ACL outcomes are among the better return-to-play numbers in women's collegiate sport — 93.1% is meaningfully higher than the cross-sport ~63% return-to-pre-injury-level baseline.
- Attacker position is doubly affected: highest injury incidence AND most demanding return-to-sport requirements (jumping and landing under fatigue).
- Implication for coaches: sport-specific functional rehab progression matters more than calendar time; volleyball-specific landing/cutting tasks must be in the late-stage rehab program before clearance.
