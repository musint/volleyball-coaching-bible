# Kountouris, Drikos, Aggelonidis, Laios & Kyprianou 2015 — Olympic Men's vs Women's Skills Effectiveness

## Citation

Kountouris P, Drikos S, Aggelonidis I, Laios A, Kyprianou M. Evidence for differences in men's and women's volleyball games based on skills effectiveness in four consecutive Olympic tournaments. *Perceptual and Motor Skills*. 2015;121(1):183-197. https://doi.org/10.2466/30.50.CP.4.9

## Sample & methods

- Four consecutive Olympic volleyball tournaments (women's brackets included). Specific tournament years not directly fetched (paywall).
- Skill-effectiveness analysis comparing men's and women's matches.
- DOI 10.2466/30.50.CP.4.9 verified.

## Headline findings (abstract-level summary)

- At Olympic-level competition, women historically used **standing serve most, jump-float second**; men used **power-jump-serve most**.
- Men show **higher faulty-serve rate**, attributed by the authors to the 19 cm net-height differential (men's 2.43 m vs. women's 2.24 m).
- **Serve was more effective for women than men;** reception was more effective for men.
- Direct serve→match-win correlation is **weaker in women than men** — attack after reception is the dominant predictor for women.

## Coach-relevant interpretation

This is the foundational Olympic-level reference for the gender-difference pattern. The asymmetry — women's game is reception-and-attack-driven, men's game is serve-driven — provides the strategic rationale for why women's pro has consolidated around jump-float and not jump-topspin. Combined with Pawlik et al. 2024, the convergent picture is: women's serve is a deliberate non-decisive change-up; the decisive lever is attack after first-touch.

## Caveats

- **Paywalled.** Full-text content was not directly fetched (Sage paywalled, returned 403 on attempted access). Findings here are based on abstract-level summary; specific n, percentages, and statistical tests are not directly verified.
- The Olympic tournament era covered may pre-date the 2010s+ shift toward jump-float in the women's game (the paper synthesizes data from 4 consecutive tournaments — likely 1996/2000/2004/2008-era based on publication date 2015).
- For NOW-current women's game (post-2015) the Pawlik 2024 Polish-pro data is more directly applicable; this paper anchors the historical pattern.
