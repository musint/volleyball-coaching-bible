# An economic evaluation of a proprioceptive balance board training programme for the prevention of ankle sprains in volleyball

**Authors:** E. Verhagen, M. van Tulder, A. J. van der Beek, L. Bouter, W. van Mechelen
**Journal:** British Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2005 (Vol 39, No 2)

## Abstract / study overview

Cluster-randomized trial across 116 Dutch volleyball teams during the 2001–2002 season. Intervention group (66 teams, 628 players) added a proprioceptive balance-board program to warm-ups; control group (52 teams, 494 players) followed normal routines. Primary aim: assess clinical effectiveness AND cost-effectiveness of the intervention.

## Key findings

- **Effectiveness:** balance-board training significantly reduced recurrent ankle sprains (with stronger effect in players with prior sprain history).
- **Cost per ankle sprain prevented:** approximately €444.03.
- **Cost per player:** intervention €36.99 vs control €18.94 — intervention costs more in raw outlay.
- **Cost-effectiveness:** the program was most economically defensible when targeted at players with previous ankle sprains; for first-time prevention it was harder to justify on cost grounds alone.
- **Implementation:** balance-board exercises were embedded directly into pre-practice warm-up — low time cost (typically 5–10 min), no specialized equipment beyond a cheap balance board.

## Key claims / ideas

- A short proprioceptive warm-up is one of the few injury-prevention interventions with clean RCT-level evidence in volleyball specifically.
- The intervention is most cost-effective in secondary prevention (players who have already sprained) — informs program triage.
- Implication for coaches: every player with a sprain history should be doing balance-board work as part of warm-up; universal application is also defensible but with diminishing returns on first-timers.
