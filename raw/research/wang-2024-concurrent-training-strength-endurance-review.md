# Wang & Bo (2024) — Concurrent training: factors enhancing muscle strength

**Citation (APA).** Wang, T., & Bo, S. (2024). Optimizing concurrent training programs: A review on factors that enhance muscle strength. *Medicine (Baltimore)*, 103(52), e41055. https://doi.org/10.1097/MD.0000000000041055

## Abstract (summary)

Narrative review of variables that modulate the "interference effect" — the reduced strength / hypertrophy adaptation seen when endurance training is layered onto resistance training. Synthesizes the current state of evidence on how to combine the two without sacrificing strength outcomes.

## Key findings — what enhances or minimizes interference

- **Trained vs untrained subjects.** Untrained individuals show **minimal interference**; regularly trained athletes are the population where interference is most detectable. The risk is real specifically for in-season elite athletes.
- **Endurance frequency.** **2 sessions/week endurance** is more strength-friendly than 3 sessions/week. **20–30 min** sessions safer than 50–60 min.
- **HIIT is compatible.** HIIT-format endurance does **not** show the same interference penalty as long-duration steady-state — the volleyball-friendly modality.
- **Sequence.** Resistance **before** endurance is more strength-protective than endurance-first.
- **Inter-modality recovery.** **6–24 hours separation** between strength and endurance bouts substantially reduces molecular interference.
- **Same-day stacking** is the worst case for strength preservation.
- **Protein supplementation** post-exercise mitigates strength-adaptation impairment.
- **Sex differences exist** — women may need longer recovery windows; hormonal responses differ.

## Practical implications for the wiki

1. **Volleyball is conducive to concurrent training when programmed right.** Use HIIT-style conditioning (which already mirrors match demands), keep endurance volume low, and separate from heavy lifts by ≥ 6 hours.
2. **Avoid same-day big-leg lifts and conditioning.** If unavoidable, lift first.
3. **In-season conditioning load can stay low** — match play already provides the cardiovascular stimulus; aim for 2 short sessions/week max.
4. **Untrained 14U and HS players are largely insulated** from interference — no programming acrobatics needed at developing levels.
5. **Protein timing matters** — post-lift / post-conditioning protein is a meaningful adaptation lever, not just a marketing artifact.
