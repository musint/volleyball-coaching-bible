# A systematic review of volleyball spike kinematics: Implications for practice and research

**Authors:** Leonardo dos Santos Oliveira, Túlio Bernardo Macedo Alfano Moura, André Luiz Félix Rodacki, Markus Tilp, Victor Hugo Alves Okazaki
**Journal:** International Journal of Sports Science & Coaching
**Year:** 2020
**DOI:** 10.1177/1747954119899881

## Abstract / study overview

The authors systematically reviewed 3-D-kinematic studies of volleyball spike performance across PubMed, SCOPUS, Web of Science, and SPORTDiscus. Eighteen studies met inclusion criteria; quality was assessed as high (n = 7), good (n = 6), or limited (n = 5). The review synthesizes the kinematic correlates of spike-jump height, ball velocity, and shot direction.

## Key findings

- Spike-jump height most strongly depends on centre-of-mass velocities (horizontal during approach, vertical at takeoff).
- Ball velocity at impact is most closely related to wrist linear velocity (the most-distal segment in the kinetic chain), with trunk rotation as a secondary contributor.
- Cross-court spike performance from position 4 is associated specifically with greater trunk rotation than line spikes.
- The proximal-to-distal velocity sequencing of the upper limb (shoulder → elbow → wrist) is a consistent feature of high-velocity spikes.
- Methodological heterogeneity across studies (motion-capture systems, marker placements, spike conditions) limits direct cross-study comparison and motivates protocol standardization.

## Key claims / ideas

- "Hit harder" coaching cues should target the wrist + trunk-rotation contribution, not the shoulder alone.
- Coaching different attack directions (cross vs. line) means coaching different biomechanical patterns, not just different aiming.
- The literature is large enough to support evidence-informed coaching but heterogeneous enough that single-study claims should be triangulated.
