# Sanders et al. (2025) — Early-season jump load and neuromuscular performance in NCAA D-I volleyball

**Citation (APA).** Sanders, G. J., Skodinski, S., & Peacock, C. A. (2025). Impact of early season jump loads on neuromuscular performance in Division I volleyball: Analyzing force, velocity, and power from countermovement jump tests. *Translational Sports Medicine*, 2025, 7216781. https://doi.org/10.1155/tsm2/7216781

## Abstract (summary)

Tracks 9 NCAA D-I female volleyball players across the first 22 days of competition (17 sessions), correlating high-intensity jump counts with daily countermovement-jump (CMJ) force, velocity, and power metrics. Tests whether jump-intensity thresholds (Jumps 38+, Jumps 50+) predict next-day neuromuscular state better than total-jump count.

## Sample / methods

- **Subjects.** 9 NCAA D-I women's volleyball players (19.4 ± 1.3 years).
- **Period.** 17 training / competition sessions across first 22 days of competition (the "shock-phase" early-season window).
- **Jump tracking.** VERT 3 inertial measurement unit (IMU) — measures jump count and jump-height intensity per athlete per session.
- **CMJ.** Hawkin Dynamics dual force plates at 1000 Hz — force, velocity, power metrics.
- **Sample size.** 134 total observations.

## Key findings

- **High-intensity jumps drive next-day fatigue more than total volume.** 9 of 14 force metrics correlated negatively with Jumps 50+ count (r = −0.194 to −0.570). Total-jump count showed weaker associations.
- **Velocity adapts upward as force drops.** 9 of 16 velocity / power metrics positively correlated with Jumps 50+ (r = 0.175 to 0.466). Athletes shift mechanically — same jump height with reduced force, increased velocity — indicating accumulated neuromuscular fatigue.
- **Threshold matters more than count.** "Higher jump counts at greater intensities are more strongly associated with faster velocities" while reducing force production — the early-season "shock phase" produces measurable mechanical drift.
- **Position effect implied.** Front-row players accumulate more high-intensity jumps than back-row, suggesting differentiated thresholds.

## Practical implications for the wiki

1. **Jump-count caps should be intensity-weighted, not total-volume.** A 50-jump session of high-intensity attack jumps is fatiguing in a way 100 low-intensity warm-up jumps are not.
2. **Force-plate CMJ monitoring detects fatigue before athletes self-report.** The mechanical-drift signal (force ↓ velocity ↑) precedes performance decline.
3. **Early-season "shock phase" is a load-management red zone.** The first 2–3 weeks of competition introduce a load spike; planning recovery and conditioning around it matters as much as the in-season management later.
4. **Position-specific thresholds.** Front-row jumpers (OH, MB, opposite) need lower high-intensity-jump caps than DS / liberos.
5. **Inertial measurement units (VERT, Hawkin) are the practical tool** — what was elite-only a decade ago is now achievable for collegiate / strong-club programs.
