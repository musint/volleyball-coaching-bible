# Group Cohesion and Collective Efficacy of Volleyball Teams

**Author:** Kevin S. Spink
**Journal:** Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology
**Year:** 1990

## Abstract / study overview

A foundational empirical study testing whether **group cohesion** and **collective efficacy** are positively related in volleyball teams and whether this relationship varies by competitive level. Spink surveyed elite and recreational volleyball teams and measured cohesion (Group Environment Questionnaire — task and social subdimensions) and collective efficacy (team's shared belief in its capability), then compared the cohesion-efficacy correlation across competitive levels.

## Key findings

- **Cohesion–collective efficacy relationship is level-dependent.** Specific measures of group cohesiveness positively related to collective efficacy in **elite** volleyball teams but **not** in recreational teams.
- This is one of the earliest demonstrations that team-level psychology (collective efficacy) interacts with competitive context — what holds at the elite level does not generalize automatically to lower levels.
- Task cohesion (commitment to shared goals, role clarity) shows stronger ties to collective efficacy than social cohesion (interpersonal liking).
- For elite teams, raising cohesion is a candidate lever for raising collective efficacy and (by downstream chain) team performance.

## Key claims / ideas

- Cohesion and collective efficacy are distinct constructs but empirically linked at elite competitive levels.
- Task cohesion is the more performance-relevant sub-dimension; social cohesion is more weakly tied to efficacy.
- Practical implication: at higher competitive levels, building cohesion (clear roles, shared goals) directly raises team belief in its own capability — a chain that downstream literature has confirmed correlates with performance.
- Foundational citation in the volleyball team-dynamics literature; later studies (Heuze et al. 2006 in basketball, Marcos et al. 2010 in volleyball) extended this finding.
