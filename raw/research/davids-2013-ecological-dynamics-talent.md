# An Ecological Dynamics Approach to Skill Acquisition: Implications for Development of Talent in Sport

**Authors:** Keith Davids, Duarte Araújo, Luis Vilar, Ian Renshaw, Ross Pinder
**Journal:** Talent Development and Excellence
**Year:** 2013
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 5(1), 21–34
**ISSN:** 1869-0459

## Abstract

The paper frames ecological dynamics as an integrated theoretical framework for understanding how athletes develop skill. Learners are treated as complex neurobiological systems whose inherent self-organizing tendencies yield adaptive behavior under the interacting task, environmental, and organismic constraints they face. Intentions, perceptions, and actions are not separable stages but intertwined processes underpinning the functional movement solutions that each learner assembles across a developmental trajectory.

The authors argue that skill acquisition programs should:
- Sample information from the performance environment to guide behavior in practice tasks.
- Allow performers to use movement variability to explore and create opportunities for action rather than constraining them to passively receive information.
- Design talent-assessment protocols that preserve the perception-action couplings of actual sport settings.

Because sports involve inherently unpredictable task constraints, the paper concludes that talent development should emphasize building individual expertise across time rather than relying primarily on early identification of presumed "high potential" performers.

## Key claims / ideas

- **Performer-environment system** is the unit of analysis, not the athlete alone.
- **Self-organization under constraints** produces adaptive movement solutions.
- **Movement variability** is functional, not noise to be eliminated.
- **Representative practice environments** are essential for both training and assessment.
- **Talent identification** is problematic when based on decontextualized testing.
