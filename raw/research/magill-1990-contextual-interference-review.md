# A review of the contextual interference effect in motor skill acquisition

**Authors:** Richard A. Magill, Kellie G. Hall
**Journal:** Human Movement Science
**Year:** 1990
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 9(3–5), 241–289
**DOI:** 10.1016/0167-9457(90)90005-X

## Abstract (from publisher landing page)

This review examines the contextual interference (CI) effect in motor skill acquisition — a learning phenomenon in which "higher levels of contextual interference lead to poorer practice performance than lower levels while yielding superior retention and transfer performance." The authors synthesize over a decade of work following Shea and Morgan (1979).

Two research directions are organized:
1. **Generalizability** — across what task classes, learner ages/skill levels, and practice conditions does the CI effect hold?
2. **Theoretical explanation** — what mechanisms (elaboration/distinctiveness vs. forgetting/reconstruction hypotheses) account for CI benefits?

## Key claim (the "Magill & Hall hypothesis")

Magill and Hall propose that the CI effect is most robust when the task variations being learned are governed by **different generalized motor programs (GMPs)**. When the variations are only parameter modifications of a **single** GMP, variability of practice is more influential than interference per se. This prediction guided a large subsequent empirical literature.

## Conclusions

- The CI effect is well-established for between-GMP variations; evidence is weaker or equivocal for within-GMP variations.
- The review calls for more research linking CI to schema theory and to applied settings (physical education, sport).
