# Coach–athlete relationships, self-confidence, and psychological wellbeing: The role of perceived and received coach support

**Authors:** Adam H. Coussens, Max J. Stone, Tracy C. Donachie
**Journal:** European Journal of Sport Science
**Year:** 2024

## Abstract / study overview

A large-sample (N = 537 athletes) cross-sectional study testing how two distinct forms of coach support — **perceived support** (athlete believes support is available if needed) and **received support** (support actually delivered) — relate to athletes' self-confidence and psychological wellbeing through the mediating quality of the coach-athlete relationship.

## Key findings

- **Perceived support → self-confidence** mediated by coach-athlete relationship quality. When athletes feel a strong relationship and believe support is available, confidence rises.
- **Received support → psychological wellbeing** mediated by coach-athlete relationship quality. Tangibly delivered support drives wellbeing more than mere availability.
- **Distinct mechanisms:** the two support types are not interchangeable — they predict different outcomes through the same relational pathway.
- Coach implication: build the relationship first (the mediator), then deliver both types of support — perceived availability for confidence, actual help for wellbeing.
- Open-communication policies and consistent visible support are concrete behaviors that materialize "received support."

## Key claims / ideas

- Coach-athlete relationship quality is a true mediator, not just a correlate.
- "Being available" and "actually showing up" are distinct constructs with distinct outcomes.
- Wellbeing and self-confidence are predicted by different coach behaviors — coaches need to do both.
- Practical takeaway for volleyball coaches: tell athletes what kind of support is available (builds confidence), then deliver concrete support during stressors (builds wellbeing).
