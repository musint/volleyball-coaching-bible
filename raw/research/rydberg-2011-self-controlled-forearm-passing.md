# The effect of self-controlled practice on forearm passing, motivation, and affect in women's volleyball players

**Author:** Nels Rydberg
**Institution:** University of Nevada, Las Vegas
**Year:** 2011 (M.S. Kinesiology)
**Advisor / committee chair:** Gabriele Wulf
**Type:** Master's thesis (UNLV) — peer-reviewed in defense format; ProQuest #1045

## Abstract (synthesized from public summary)

The study examined whether allowing collegiate women's volleyball players to self-direct their forearm-passing practice (vs. a yoked group following predetermined exercises) would improve passing accuracy, intrinsic motivation (Intrinsic Motivation Inventory; IMI), and affect (Positive and Negative Affect Scale; PANAS). Across two days — baseline, practice, and post-practice assessment — the self-controlled group designed their own drills while the yoked group performed matched but coach-prescribed drills. Analyses revealed **no statistically significant differences between groups** in forearm passing accuracy, intrinsic motivation, or affect. The author concludes "further research is needed to determine if intrinsic motivation and affect are partially responsible for the learning benefits of self-controlled practice."

## Key claims / ideas
- Self-controlled / autonomy-supported practice has shown learning benefits in lab motor-learning paradigms; this study is one of the rare **volleyball-specific** tests of the effect.
- For collegiate women working on forearm passing, **autonomy did not produce a measurable accuracy advantage** in this 2-day, low-dose protocol.
- Null result complicates the often-cited "autonomy is always good for motor learning" story when applied to advanced sport-specific skills with already-competent learners.
- Possible explanations (per author): low dose (single practice day), already-skilled population, ceiling effects on IMI/PANAS, or genuinely no autonomy effect for this particular task.
- Useful counterweight when arguing for "let players design drills" in coach-education contexts — the pure-autonomy pitch has a weaker volleyball evidence base than commonly claimed.
