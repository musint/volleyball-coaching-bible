# Lower extremity mechanics during landing after a volleyball block as a risk factor for anterior cruciate ligament injury

**Authors:** David Zahradnik, Daniel Jandacka, Jaroslav Uchytil, Roman Farana, Joseph Hamill
**Journal:** Physical Therapy in Sport
**Year:** 2015
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.ptsp.2014.04.003

## Abstract / study overview

Fourteen elite male volleyball players performed two block-landing variants — stick landing and step-back landing. Force plates and 3-D motion capture measured vertical ground reaction force, knee flexion, knee moments, and energy absorption at the hip, knee, and ankle. The clinical question: does the step-back landing, common when a blocker recovers to play defense, elevate ACL-injury risk?

## Key findings

- During step-back landings, the right (dominant) lower extremity showed higher first-peak vertical ground reaction force than the stick landing.
- Knee valgus moment was greater on the right side during step-back landings — a well-established ACL-injury risk marker.
- Knee energy absorption during step-back landings was reduced; hip and ankle compensated by absorbing more.
- Step-back landings concentrate impact and rotational stress on the dominant knee at the moment of initial impact, likely increasing ACL-injury risk.

## Key claims / ideas

- The way blockers transition to backcourt defense matters biomechanically; coaches who teach "land and move" without addressing landing mechanics may be inadvertently raising ACL risk.
- Stick-landings are biomechanically safer; step-back landings should be trained with neuromuscular emphasis on knee tracking and hip absorption.
- The dominant leg is the likely first failure point — bilateral landing training is justified even though jumping is asymmetric.
