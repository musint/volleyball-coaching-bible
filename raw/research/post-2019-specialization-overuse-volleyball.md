# Sport-Specific Associations of Specialization and Sex With Overuse Injury in Youth Athletes

**Authors:** Eric G. Post, Kevin M. Biese, Daniel A. Schaefer, Andrew M. Watson, Timothy A. McGuine, M. Alison Brooks, David R. Bell
**Journal:** Sports Health: A Multidisciplinary Approach
**Year:** 2019

## Abstract / study overview

Cross-sectional study of 716 youth athletes (ages 12–18) from basketball, soccer, and volleyball examining how sport specialization status and sex interact with overuse injury history. The headline question: does specialization carry the same risk across all sports, or are some sport-sex combinations especially dangerous?

## Key findings

- **Volleyball is the high-risk sport for specialization-driven overuse injury.** Specialization was significantly associated with overuse injury *only in volleyball* (not basketball or soccer).
- **Volleyball-specific odds ratios:**
  - High specialization → OR 2.3 for overuse injury (p=0.02).
  - Year-round (>8 months) volleyball participation → OR 2.0 (p<0.05).
- **Overuse injury history:** 45.7% of volleyball athletes reported prior overuse injuries — highest of the three sports.
- **Specialization rates:** volleyball had highest rate of high-specialization athletes among the three sampled sports.
- **Sex effects:** in basketball, female athletes had ~3.7× higher overuse injury risk than males. (No male volleyball participants in sample so volleyball sex comparison not possible.)

## Key claims / ideas

- Volleyball-specific year-round play and high specialization roughly double overuse injury risk in adolescent female athletes.
- Specialization recommendations cannot be generic across sports; volleyball is a high-load, high-specialization sport that magnifies the harm.
- Implication for club programs: multi-sport encouragement is not just developmental philosophy — it's an evidence-backed injury-prevention lever for 14U-HS volleyball athletes specifically. Year-round single-sport participation correlates with measurable harm.
