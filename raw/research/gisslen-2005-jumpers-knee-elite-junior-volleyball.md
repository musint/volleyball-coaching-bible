# High prevalence of jumper's knee and sonographic changes in Swedish elite junior volleyball players compared to matched controls

**Authors:** K. Gisslén, C. Gyulai, K. Söderman, H. Alfredson (Umeå University, Sweden)
**Journal:** British Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2005 (Vol 39, No 5, pp 298–301)

## Abstract / study overview

Compared 57 Swedish elite junior volleyball players (ages 15–19) to 55 matched non-athletic controls using clinical examination plus grey-scale ultrasonography and color Doppler imaging of the patellar tendon.

## Key findings

- **Clinical jumper's knee:** diagnosed in 12 volleyball-player tendons (10 male, 2 female); zero cases in controls.
- **Symptomatic tendons:** all 12 demonstrated structural changes plus neovascularization on color Doppler.
- **Asymptomatic adaptations:** 10 additional volleyball-player tendons (no clinical pain) had structural changes plus neovascularization — suggesting silent, sub-clinical pathology already present in adolescents.
- **Controls:** 11 control tendons showed structural changes, but **no neovascularization** appeared in any control. Neovascularization, not structural change alone, is the volleyball-specific marker.
- **Sex skew:** males disproportionately affected in this elite junior cohort.

## Key claims / ideas

- Jumper's knee in elite junior volleyball is common, sex-skewed (more male in this sample), and frequently has asymptomatic precursors visible on imaging.
- Neovascularization on color Doppler discriminates volleyball-loaded tendons from controls more reliably than structural change.
- Implication for adolescent coaching: load monitoring and tendon health screening matter at the junior elite level — overuse is already detectable structurally before pain begins.
