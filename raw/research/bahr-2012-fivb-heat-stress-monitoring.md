# Bahr & Reeser (2012) — FIVB heat-stress monitoring programme

**Citation (APA).** Bahr, R., & Reeser, J. C. (2012). New guidelines are needed to manage heat stress in elite sports — The Fédération Internationale de Volleyball (FIVB) Heat Stress Monitoring Programme. *British Journal of Sports Medicine*, 46(11), 805–809. https://doi.org/10.1136/bjsports-2012-091102

## Abstract (summary)

Three-year FIVB-sanctioned monitoring program (2009–2011) of heat stress at international beach-volleyball events, measuring Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) at every match and tracking heat-related medical forfeits.

## Sample / methods

- **Events covered.** 51 events on the FIVB Beach Volleyball World Tour and World Championships.
- **Data captured.** 48 of 51 events with full WBGT data.
- **Measurement.** WBGT pre-match on center court at every event.
- **Outcome.** Heat-related medical forfeits (count + circumstances).

## Key findings

- **9 events exceeded the Navy Black Flag threshold** (WBGT > 32.3°C) — indicating "very high" heat-stress conditions traditionally restricting strenuous activity.
- **2 additional events** exceeded 31°C WBGT.
- **Only 1 heat-related medical forfeit** across 3 years (and that case had GI complications, not pure heat illness).
- **Conclusion: existing heat-stress guidelines may be too conservative** for elite acclimated volleyball athletes — prevailing thresholds for activity restriction would have stopped events that proceeded safely.

## Practical implications for the wiki

1. **Acclimated elite athletes tolerate higher WBGT than blanket guidelines suggest** — but this finding is specific to top-tier beach players, not generalizable to junior, club, or non-acclimated populations.
2. **WBGT > 31–32°C is a real flag for non-elite volleyball** — youth, club, and HS programs should hold to conservative limits even if FIVB elites can play through.
3. **Hydration, acclimation, and shade-protocol matter** even when WBGT is below the medical-forfeit threshold — performance and well-being decline well before clinical heat illness.
4. **Document heat protocols at the program level** — particularly relevant for indoor tournaments held in poorly ventilated venues during summer travel.
