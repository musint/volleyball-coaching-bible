# Muscular imbalance and shoulder pain in volleyball attackers

**Authors:** A. Kugler, M. Krüger-Franke, S. Reininger, H. H. Trouillier, B. Rosemeyer
**Journal:** British Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 1996

## Abstract / study overview

Examined 30 competitive volleyball attackers (mean age 25); half symptomatic with shoulder pain, half asymptomatic. Compared against 15 recreational athletes with no overhead-sport history. Looked for postural, muscular, and capsular adaptations distinguishing the playing shoulder from the non-playing shoulder and from non-overhead controls.

## Key findings

- **Postural adaptations:** playing shoulder consistently depressed (lower than non-playing shoulder); scapula laterally translated.
- **Soft-tissue adaptations:** dorsal (posterior) shoulder musculature shortened; posterior-inferior capsule shortened — classic GIRD-like pattern.
- **Symptomatic vs asymptomatic:** all adaptations more pronounced in attackers with shoulder pain than those without — suggesting a dose-response between training-induced muscular imbalance and pain emergence.
- **Recommendation:** stretching and balanced strengthening programs are essential for both prevention and treatment of attacker shoulder pain.

## Key claims / ideas

- Shoulder pain in volleyball attackers is largely an overuse imbalance, not acute trauma. Postural depression and posterior capsule tightness are the imaging-poor but clinically detectable hallmarks.
- "Muscular balance of the shoulder girdle is critical" — early-published statement of what later became standard overhead-athlete prehab orthodoxy.
- Implication for coaching: warm-up and prehab sequences for attackers must address posterior shoulder mobility, scapular control, and rotator cuff balance, not just biceps and pec.
