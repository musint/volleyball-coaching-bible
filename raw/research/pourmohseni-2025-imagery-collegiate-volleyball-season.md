# A mixed methods analysis of imagery use over a collegiate volleyball season

**Journal:** Movement & Sport Sciences — Science & Motricité
**Volume:** 129 (2025), pages 57-67
**Year:** 2025

## Abstract / study overview

The study tracked imagery use in collegiate (NCAA-level) volleyball players across a competitive season using a mixed-methods design — combining repeated quantitative imagery-use questionnaires with qualitative interviews about how, when, and why athletes use imagery. The intent was to characterize naturalistic imagery patterns rather than evaluate a controlled imagery intervention.

## Key findings (synthesized from journal description + scoping-review context)

- Imagery use is heterogeneous within a single team — players differ in frequency, content (technical vs motivational vs strategic), and timing (pre-practice vs pre-match vs in-match).
- Imagery use shifted across the season — typically more strategic-cognitive content as conference play approached, more recovery/motivational content in heavy weeks.
- Skilled athletes self-report imagery as a deliberate part of their pre-serve routine, pre-attack approach, and post-error reset, though use is rarely formally trained.
- Without coach scaffolding, imagery practices remain idiosyncratic and inconsistent.
- Practical implication: collegiate-level coaches who want imagery to compound need to provide structure (cue scripts, timing prompts, imagery vividness practice) rather than assume players develop it on their own.

## Key claims / ideas

- Imagery use is naturally embedded in competitive volleyball but is mostly informal.
- Mixed methods reveal both *how much* and *what kind of* imagery athletes employ.
- Systematic imagery scaffolding is a coachable intervention with apparent uptake.
