# Biomechanic Differences Between Anticipated and Unanticipated Volleyball Block Jump: Implications for Lower Limb Injury Risk

**Authors:** Hongxin Zhao, Xiangyu Liu, Linfei Dan, Datao Xu, Jianshe Li
**Journal:** Life (Basel)
**Year:** 2024
**DOI:** 10.3390/life14111357

## Abstract / study overview

Fifteen elite male volleyball players performed dominant- and non-dominant-direction block jumps under both anticipated and unanticipated conditions. Lower-limb kinematics, kinetics, and joint power were captured to assess injury-risk markers (knee valgus moment, vertical ground reaction force, knee flexion power) under realistic blocking constraints.

## Key findings

- Dominant-side blocks showed greater landing stiffness, higher posterior ground reaction force, and reduced cushioning — markers of greater overuse-injury risk.
- Non-dominant-side blocks showed better shock absorption but elevated knee-valgus moment, a known ACL-injury risk factor.
- Unanticipated blocks produced higher vertical ground reaction force and greater hip moments/power vs. anticipated blocks.
- Under unanticipation, knee-flexion power and ankle dorsi-/plantarflexion velocity dropped — the lower limb cannot fully reorganize from automated patterns when reaction time is limited.
- The combination of unanticipation and lateral movement was the highest-risk profile in the study.

## Key claims / ideas

- Reaction-time constraints (real-game blocking) load the lower limb differently from rehearsed jumps; lab data on rehearsed blocks under-states injury exposure.
- Unilateral training (single-leg strength + balance) is recommended to address asymmetry between dominant and non-dominant sides.
- Practice should include unanticipated blocking (read-blocking, side-randomized cues) to prepare the neuromuscular system for the conditions where injuries actually occur.
