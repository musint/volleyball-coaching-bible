# Jiang et al. (2024) — Muscle-structure predictors of vertical jump in elite male volleyball

**Citation (APA).** Jiang, W., Chen, C., & Xu, Y. (2024). Muscle structure predictors of vertical jump performance in elite male volleyball players: a cross-sectional study based on ultrasonography. *Frontiers in Physiology*, 15, 1427748. https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2024.1427748

## Abstract (summary)

Cross-sectional ultrasound study of 15 elite male volleyball players quantifying which lower-limb muscle architecture variables predict jump height across four jump types (attack jump, countermovement jump, squat jump, drop jump).

## Sample / methods

- **Sample.** 15 elite male volleyball players (age 22.47 ± years; height 195.4 ± cm).
- **Imaging.** B-mode ultrasound (LOGIQ e NextGen) — anatomical cross-sectional area (ACSA), thickness, pennation angle, fascicle length for vastus lateralis (VL), rectus femoris (RF), medial gastrocnemius (MG), lateral gastrocnemius (LG).
- **Reliability.** ICC 0.91–0.99 across measurements.
- **Outcomes.** Attack jump (AJ), countermovement jump (CMJ), squat jump (SJ), drop jump (DJ) heights.

## Key findings

- **Vastus lateralis ACSA is the dominant predictor across all four jump types** — explains 25–52% of variance:
  - SJ: R² = 0.52
  - CMJ: R² = 0.37
  - AJ: R² = 0.32
  - DJ: R² = 0.25
- **Lateral gastrocnemius fascicle length uniquely predicts attack jump height** (R² = 0.18) — not significant for any other jump type.
- **Rectus femoris does not predict any jump type** — quad-bias programming may be inefficient for jump development.
- **Multi-variable model for AJ (VL-ACSA + LG-FL + training age) → R² = 0.73** — three variables explain three-quarters of attack-jump-height variance.
- **Attack jump is biomechanically distinct** from CMJ / SJ / DJ — sport-specific movement requires sport-specific architectural adaptations.

## Practical implications for the wiki

1. **Vastus lateralis hypertrophy is the highest-leverage strength target** for jump development across all four jump types — back squat, front squat, step-ups, lunges, single-leg squats.
2. **Calf eccentric loading lengthens lateral-gastrocnemius fascicles** — programs that ignore the calf are leaving attack-jump height on the table.
3. **Generic plyometrics may not transfer fully to attack jumps** — train the AJ specifically (approach + jump) rather than assuming CMJ or SJ work generalizes.
4. **Training-age effect is real** — long-term continuous training compounds muscle architecture changes; year-round structured S&C beats stop-start programming.
