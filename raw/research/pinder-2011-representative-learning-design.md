# Representative Learning Design and Functionality of Research and Practice in Sport

**Authors:** Ross A. Pinder, Keith Davids, Ian Renshaw, Duarte Araújo
**Journal:** Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology
**Year:** 2011
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 33(1), 146–155
**DOI:** 10.1123/jsep.33.1.146
**PMID:** 21451175

## Abstract

The paper revisits Egon Brunswik's concept of **representative design** for psychological experimentation, which has historically been overlooked or conflated with the different concept of ecological validity. The authors clarify Brunswik's original meaning — that experimental and learning tasks must sample the informational and action properties of the performer's actual environment — and argue for its centrality to sport psychology, experimental design, and coaching practice.

They propose the term **representative learning design** to describe practice tasks that preserve the coupling between perception and action present in competition, ensuring "functionality and action fidelity" in the training environment. The idea is developed in conjunction with the constraints-led approach and nonlinear pedagogy.

## Key claims / ideas

- **Representative design ≠ ecological validity.** Representative design is specifically about the task sampling the information sources and behavioral requirements of the target performance setting.
- **Action fidelity** is distinct from visual/contextual fidelity: practice must preserve the information-movement coupling athletes will rely on in competition.
- **Practice implications:** Drills that decouple perception from action (e.g., hitting balls off a tossed feed with no defender) are less representative than drills in which the athlete perceives genuine task-relevant information and acts on it.
- **Research implications:** Laboratory studies of athletic skill that remove normal perceptual-motor couplings may not generalize to competition.
