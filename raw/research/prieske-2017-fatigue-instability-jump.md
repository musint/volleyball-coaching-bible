# Combined Effects of Fatigue and Surface Instability on Jump Biomechanics in Elite Athletes

**Authors:** Olaf Prieske, Marie Demps, Melanie Lesinski, Urs Granacher
**Journal:** International Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2017
**DOI:** 10.1055/s-0043-111894

## Abstract / study overview

Twenty elite volleyball players (10 women, 10 men, age 18 ± 2) performed repetitive vertical box jumps to exhaustion. Jump height, ground reaction force, and knee angles were measured on stable and unstable surfaces, before and after the fatigue protocol. The study was designed to measure how match-like fatigue interacts with surface variability — and whether men and women drift biomechanically in different ways.

## Key findings

- Fatigue significantly reduced drop-jump performance and ground reaction force.
- Drop-jump knee flexion angles were significantly lower (i.e., the knee absorbed less) under fatigue.
- Sex-specific knee strategies emerged: women showed higher knee-valgus angles under unstable conditions; men showed the opposite pattern in countermovement jumps.
- Unstable surfaces increased knee-valgus angles during drop jumps.
- Combined fatigue + instability drove the biomechanical drift larger than either alone.

## Key claims / ideas

- Late-match fatigue produces measurable biomechanical drift — landings become stiffer (less knee flexion), GRF drops as athletes self-protect, and knee valgus rises.
- Male and female volleyball players drift differently; injury-prevention work should not be sex-symmetric.
- Practice that simulates late-match fatigue (long drills under load) is necessary if landing mechanics are to be tested in conditions where they actually fail.

## Topics covered

- Match fatigue
- Jump biomechanics under fatigue
- Surface instability
- Sex-specific landing strategies
- Late-match injury risk
