# The Effects of Avoiding Instructions Under Pressure: An Examination of the Volleyball Serving Task

**Authors:** Recep Görgülü, Eslem Gökçek
**Journal:** Journal of Human Kinetics
**Year:** 2021

## Abstract / study overview

The researchers tested **Wegner's ironic processes theory** in a volleyball-serving setting: does telling a player "don't serve into zone X" cause more errors into that exact zone under high pressure? They studied 43 female teenage volleyball players serving under low- and high-anxiety conditions, with anxiety manipulated through competitive framing, financial incentives, and social evaluation. Anxiety was verified via heart rate, heart rate variability, and self-reported mental readiness. Each player completed 10 serves per condition, scored as +5 (target zone), -5 (out/net = ironic error), or +1 (any other in-court).

## Key findings

- Players' performance in target zones and error zones remained statistically consistent across low- and high-anxiety conditions — Wegner's ironic processes theory was **not** supported in this volleyball serving task.
- However, the data did support **Woodman et al.'s differentiation between ironic errors and non-ironic errors**: distinct error patterns emerged across different court zones, suggesting attentional control rather than pure ironic processing as the operative mechanism.
- Anxiety manipulation worked (HR/HRV changes confirmed) — null effect is real, not a failed manipulation.
- Practical implication: "don't serve into the net" instructions may not produce ironic errors as classical theory predicts, but the broader mechanism of pressure-degraded attentional control is still operative.

## Key claims / ideas

- Ironic processes theory is not the dominant explanation for volleyball serving errors under pressure.
- Attentional-control theory and the ironic-vs-non-ironic-error distinction (Woodman) better fit the data.
- Coaches should be cautious about negative-instruction self-talk ("don't miss") under pressure but the empirical case for it being uniquely catastrophic is weaker than often assumed.
