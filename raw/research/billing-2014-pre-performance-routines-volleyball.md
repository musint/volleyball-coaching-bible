# Pre-performance routines among club volleyball players: the relationship between routines and Accuracy in serving

**Author:** Lauren Billing
**Type:** Master's thesis, University of Minnesota
**Year:** 2014

## Abstract / study overview

The thesis investigated whether pre-performance routines (PPRs) — habitual sequences of physical and cognitive actions performed before serving — improve volleyball serving accuracy in club competitive play. Secondary aims examined gender differences in routine consistency and the relationship between PPRs and competitive trait/state anxiety.

## Key findings

- **No effect of PPRs on serving accuracy** — players with stable, observable pre-serve routines did not serve more accurately than players without them.
- **No relationship between competitive anxiety and routine maintenance** — anxious players did not abandon routines under pressure more than calm players.
- **Women maintained routines more consistently than men**, but this routine-consistency advantage did not translate into a serving-percentage advantage.
- **Sport-specific limitation:** PPR effectiveness documented in basketball free-throws, golf, and tennis serves does not transfer cleanly to volleyball serving in this sample.
- Author concludes the PPR-volleyball link needs different methodology — possibly examining PPR content and consistency rather than mere presence.

## Key claims / ideas

- PPRs are conceptually well-supported by sport psychology theory but lack empirical support specifically in volleyball serving.
- The cross-sport transfer of PPR efficacy is non-trivial — coaches should not assume "build a routine" wins by default.
- Methodological note: future research should isolate routine **components** (breath, ball bounces, focal point, cue word) rather than presence/absence.
- This finding sits in tension with strong PPR research in golf and basketball; the disagreement is not yet resolved.
