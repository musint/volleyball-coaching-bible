# Contextual Interference Effects on the Acquisition, Retention, and Transfer of a Motor Skill

**Authors:** John B. Shea, Robyn L. Morgan
**Journal:** Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory
**Year:** 1979
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 5(2), 179–187
**DOI:** 10.1037/0278-7393.5.2.179

## Abstract / Summary (reconstructed from landing pages and review articles; full-text PDF was not cleanly extractable)

Shea and Morgan tested Battig's (1966) hypothesis that increased contextual interference during skill acquisition degrades practice performance but enhances retention and transfer. Participants learned three "barrier knock-down" motor tasks — knocking down a specified set of six freely moveable barriers in prescribed orders — under either a blocked (low contextual interference) or random (high contextual interference) practice schedule. Retention was measured after 10-minute and 10-day delays under both blocked and random test conditions.

## Key findings (as summarized in subsequent reviews)

- During acquisition, the blocked practice group outperformed the random group (random schedule causes worse practice performance).
- On retention and transfer tests — especially after the 10-day delay and under novel (random) test conditions — the random practice group outperformed the blocked group.
- The paper established the **contextual interference effect** in motor skill learning: practice conditions that feel harder and yield worse acquisition performance can produce better long-term retention and transfer.

## Historical significance

This study is the foundational empirical demonstration of the contextual interference (CI) effect in motor learning. It has been cited thousands of times and anchors the broader "desirable difficulties" literature (Bjork), the random-vs-blocked practice debate in coaching, and subsequent reviews by Magill & Hall (1990), Brady (1998, 2004), and meta-analyses through 2024.
