# Understanding ACL injuries in volleyball: a systematic review of epidemiology and risk factors

**Authors:** Sassi LB, Miguel AC, Serafim TT, de Menezes FS, Martins TB, Okubo R
**Journal:** Frontiers in Sports and Active Living
**Year:** 2025

## Abstract / study overview

Systematic review covering 15 studies and ~3.3 million athletes aged 10–70 examining ACL injury patterns in volleyball. The review consolidates incidence/prevalence data, injury mechanisms, position-specific risk, and prevention recommendations.

## Key findings

- **Mechanism:** 97% of ACL injuries in volleyball occur via non-contact mechanisms; ~62% specifically during landing after an attack (spike-landing).
- **Sex disparity:** Female athletes have 2–4× higher injury rates than males.
- **Age:** Adolescent female players show greatest vulnerability; high-school prevalence (9.3%) exceeds collegiate (4.4%) — likely reflecting incomplete neuromuscular maturation and less structured conditioning.
- **Position:** Outside hitters carry highest incidence due to volume of high-velocity spike landings and ground reaction forces.
- **Incidence range:** 0.019–0.569 per 1,000 exposures (units vary across primary studies).
- **Prevalence range:** 0.7%–26.9% across studies.
- **Prevention:** Authors recommend 15–20 minute neuromuscular training sessions 2–3×/week, progressing bilateral → unilateral landings with sport-specific spike-landing simulations.

## Key claims / ideas

- ACL injury in volleyball is overwhelmingly a non-contact, spike-landing event — making landing mechanics, neuromuscular control, and progressive jump-landing training the dominant prevention levers.
- Adolescent female outside hitters constitute the highest-risk subpopulation; coach/program targeting should reflect that.
- Off-court neuromuscular training programs (similar in dose to PEP, FIFA 11+, KIPP) are the consensus intervention with documented efficacy.

## Topics covered
- ACL injury epidemiology
- Spike landing mechanics
- Sex-based and age-based risk
- Outside-hitter position load
- Neuromuscular prevention programs
