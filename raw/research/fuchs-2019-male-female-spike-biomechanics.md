# Spike jump biomechanics in male versus female elite volleyball players

**Authors:** Philip X. Fuchs, Hans-Joachim Karl Menzel, Flavia Guidotti, Jeffrey Bell, Serge P. von Duvillard, Herbert Wagner
**Journal:** Journal of Sports Sciences
**Year:** 2019
**DOI:** 10.1080/02640414.2019.1639437

## Abstract / study overview

Thirty sub-elite volleyball players (15 men, 15 women) performed maximal spike jumps captured by a 12-camera 3-D motion-capture system, force plates, and EMG. The aim was to characterize sex differences in approach, transition, takeoff, and arm-action variables, and to assess whether observed differences were strength/power-driven or technique-driven.

## Key findings

- Significant sex differences in maximum jump height, with men jumping higher.
- Women approached at slower speeds and used a shorter penultimate (second-to-last) step.
- Sex differences in plant angle and neuromuscular activation patterns at transition.
- Differences in centre-of-mass displacement, knee/hip joint angles, torso inclination, and arm angular velocity.
- Authors conclude these differences are not fully explained by strength/power alone; technical-coordinative differences also drive the gap.

## Key claims / ideas

- Sex differences in spike-jump biomechanics are partly technique-based, suggesting technique training (not only strength training) is the leverage point for raising women's spike-jump output.
- Penultimate-step length is a tractable coaching cue with measurable jump-height implications.
- Approach-velocity training transfers to spike-jump height for women specifically (a potential coaching emphasis distinct from men).

## Topics covered

- Sex differences in volleyball biomechanics
- Spike-jump approach
- Penultimate-step kinematics
- Technical vs. physical drivers of jump height
- Female-specific coaching cues
