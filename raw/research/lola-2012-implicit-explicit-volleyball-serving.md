# The effect of implicit and explicit practice in the development of decision making in volleyball serving

**Authors:** Afrodite C. Lola, George C. Tzetzis, Helen Zetou
**Journal:** Perceptual and Motor Skills
**Year:** 2012
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 114(2), 665–678
**DOI:** 10.2466/05.23.25.PMS.114.2.665-678
**PMID:** 22755467

## Abstract (verbatim)

"This experiment investigated the effect of explicit, implicit, and sequential learning (implicit-explicit) on the acquisition and retention of decision-making skill in volleyball. The participants were 60 female novices, ages 10 to 12 years. The experimental groups followed three different methods of training: (a) explicit practice for the development of declarative knowledge, (b) implicit practice for the development of the procedural knowledge, (c) sequential practice (implicit first and then explicit), and (d) control group that participated only in the measurements. A pre-test, a post-test, and a retention test measured the response time and accuracy of the decision-making skill. Analysis indicated that all experimental groups improved over time while the control group did not. The sequential group was faster and more accurate than the implicit group, and the latter was faster and more accurate than the explicit one."

## Key claims / ideas
- Three modes of practice (explicit, implicit, sequential implicit→explicit) all beat a no-instruction control on serve-decision speed AND accuracy in 10–12 yr female novices.
- **Sequential (implicit first, then explicit) outperformed pure implicit, which outperformed pure explicit** — both on speed and on accuracy of the decision-making response.
- Implicit-then-explicit ordering benefits decision making more than either mode alone — interpreted as procedural-knowledge accumulation freeing working memory before declarative knowledge is layered on top.
- For young (under-13) novices, front-loading implicit (perceptual-procedural) practice before explicit verbal/declarative coaching is supported by the data.

## Topics covered
- Implicit motor learning
- Explicit motor learning
- Decision making in serving (server reading the receive formation)
- Skill acquisition in young female novices
- Order effects in coaching cues / instruction sequencing
