# High Prevalence of Disc Degeneration and Spondylolysis in the Lumbar Spine of Professional Beach Volleyball Players

**Authors:** Külling FA, Florianz H, Reepschläger B, Gasser J, Jost B, Lajtai G
**Journal:** Orthopaedic Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2014

## Abstract / study overview

MRI evaluation of 29 professional male beach volleyball players investigating prevalence of lumbar disc degeneration, spondylolysis, and their relationship to clinical low back pain.

## Key findings

- **Disc degeneration:** 23 of 29 (79%) players had at least one disc with Pfirrmann grade ≥3.
- **Most-affected levels:** L4–5 (48%) and L5–S1 (52%) showed the highest prevalence.
- **Spondylolysis:** 6 of 29 (21%) showed grade-4 spondylolysis — up to 3× higher than the ~5% normal-population baseline. L5–S1 was the most-affected level (>70% of cases).
- **Clinical pain:** 86% of players had experienced low back pain during their career; 35% within the last 4 weeks.
- **Imaging-pain dissociation:** no significant correlation between MRI findings and current LBP — i.e., severe imaging pathology is common even in pain-free athletes.
- **Mechanism:** repetitive serve motion produces excessive hip-shoulder separation angles, generating shear forces and lumbar stress; explosive jumps and asymmetric trunk rotation compound the load.

## Key claims / ideas

- Lumbar pathology in elite volleyball is the rule, not the exception — spondylolysis rate alone is several-fold higher than the general population.
- Imaging cannot drive return-to-play decisions in isolation; pain and function diverge from MRI severity.
- Implication for practice design: trunk rotation symmetry, anterior-pelvic-tilt control during landing, and posterior chain conditioning are coaching/S&C levers worth prioritizing.
