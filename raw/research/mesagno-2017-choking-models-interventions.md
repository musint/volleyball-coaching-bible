# Choking under pressure: theoretical models and interventions

**Authors:** Christopher Mesagno, Juergen Beckmann
**Journal:** Current Opinion in Psychology
**Year:** 2017

## Abstract / study overview

This invited review synthesizes the choking-under-pressure literature: definitions, personality correlates, theoretical mechanisms, and intervention evidence. Choking is operationalized as a sudden and significant performance decrement in important situations despite the athlete possessing the skill. The authors focus on **self-presentation** and **attention-control** models as the two leading explanatory frameworks.

## Key findings

- **Two dominant theoretical families:** (1) attention-based models (distraction theory, conscious-processing / "reinvestment" theory) and (2) self-presentation models (anxiety from being judged).
- Personality factors that increase choking vulnerability: high trait anxiety, high self-consciousness, low dispositional reinvestment for skilled execution, perfectionism, fear-of-negative-evaluation.
- **Conscious-processing hypothesis** (Masters): under pressure, skilled performers revert to step-by-step monitoring of automated skills, disrupting fluent execution.
- **Distraction theory:** pressure shifts attention to task-irrelevant cues (worry, body monitoring) — capacity is consumed.
- **Effective interventions:** acclimatization training (practicing under pressure), pre-performance routines, attentional cueing, left-hand contraction (Beckmann), quiet-eye training.
- Coach implication: address both attention and self-presentation — reduce judgment salience (private rehearsal, low-stakes practice variants) AND train automaticity/quiet-eye.

## Key claims / ideas

- Choking is real and mechanistically distinguishable from underperformance due to fatigue or low skill.
- Pressure interventions work — there is intervention evidence, not just descriptive theory.
- The two theoretical families (attention vs self-presentation) are complementary, not competing.
