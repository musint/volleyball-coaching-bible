# Specific and general transfer of perceptual-motor skills and learning between sports: A systematic review

**Authors:** Luca Oppici, Derek Panchuk
**Journal:** Psychology of Sport and Exercise
**Year:** 2022
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 59, 102118
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.psychsport.2021.102118

## Abstract (synthesized from publisher abstract + ResearchGate description)

The review systematically examines how (i) **specific and general transfer** of perceptual-motor skills occur between sports in the short-term (skill transfer) and long-term (learning transfer), (ii) how the two types of transfer interact, and (iii) what conditions — **affordance similarity, perceptual-motor exploration, and expertise** — promote transfer. **Specific transfer** rests on the perception and use of *specifying* information shared by the source and target tasks; **general transfer** rests on *non-specifying* "general" information. Electronic searches across SPORTDiscus, PubMed/MEDLINE, and Scopus yielded 17 included studies (low-to-moderate quality). Most studies showed specificity of transfer when the sports shared overlapping affordances for a given skill (e.g., invasion sports, basketball↔darts throwing, climbing, striking sports), and generality of transfer when affordances did not overlap. Six studies showed an **expertise effect** — higher transfer in skilled vs. less-skilled athletes. The review provides little information on the general↔specific interaction in the short-term, and notes limited evidence on mid- and long-term transfer dynamics.

## Key claims / ideas
- "Transfer" in motor learning splits into **specific** (information-shared) and **general** (information-bridging) — both real, both empirically supported.
- **Affordance similarity** between source and target task drives whether transfer is specific or general.
- **Expertise effect:** higher-level athletes transfer more than novices, in both directions.
- For coaches: cross-training between sports is more useful when the affordances overlap (passing in basketball ↔ passing in volleyball would be more useful than e.g., dart throwing ↔ volleyball serve).
- Evidence base is thin: only 17 studies, low-to-moderate quality, most short-term — caution required when arguing for or against multi-sport sampling on transfer grounds alone.
- Volleyball-specific implication: skills shared across volleyball positions (passing → defense; setting → tipping) likely benefit from specific transfer, while broader athleticism (basketball, soccer) supports more general transfer.
