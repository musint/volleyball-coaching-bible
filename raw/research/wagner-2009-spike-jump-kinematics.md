# Kinematic analysis of volleyball spike jump

**Authors:** H. Wagner, M. Tilp, S. P. von Duvillard, E. Mueller
**Journal:** International Journal of Sports Medicine
**Year:** 2009
**DOI:** 10.1055/s-0029-1224177

## Abstract / study overview

The researchers examined how upper- and lower-body movements influence volleyball spike-jump performance in 16 experienced players. Using 3-D motion capture and force platforms, they identified biomechanical correlates of jump height including maximal horizontal velocity of the centre of mass and range of motion of right-knee flexion-extension. The paper is an oft-cited reference for the proposition that approach technique (not only takeoff and arm action) is decisive for spike-jump height.

## Key findings

- Spike jump height significantly correlated with horizontal centre-of-mass (CoM) velocity (r = 0.71, p = 0.002) and minimum CoM height during preparation (r = -0.68, p = 0.004).
- Right-knee flexion-extension range correlated strongly with jump height (r = 0.76, p = 0.001).
- Left-shoulder hyperextension angular velocity correlated with jump height (r = 0.72, p = 0.002).
- Marked left/right asymmetry in spike-jump movement (consistent with the right-handed approach pattern).
- The take-off phase alone does not determine jump height — the approach (horizontal velocity and the depth of the preparatory squat) is critical.

## Key claims / ideas

- The approach phase, not just takeoff, determines maximum spike-jump height.
- A faster horizontal approach combined with a deeper preparatory squat is associated with greater jump height.
- Asymmetric arm and leg involvement is a feature of the spike jump, not a flaw to be corrected.
