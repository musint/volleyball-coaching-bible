# Closs et al. (2020) — Recovery after volleyball (narrative review)

**Citation (APA).** Closs, B., Burkett, C., Trojan, J. D., Brown, S. M., & Mulcahey, M. K. (2020). Recovery after volleyball: a narrative review. *The Physician and Sportsmedicine*, 48(1), 8–16. https://doi.org/10.1080/00913847.2019.1632156

## Abstract (summary)

Narrative review of recovery modalities in volleyball — including nutrition, sleep, mental / psychological strategies, cold-water immersion (CWI), and laser therapy. Synthesizes evidence strength by modality and identifies gaps for future research.

## Recovery modalities reviewed

- Nutrition
- Sleep
- Mental / psychological techniques
- Cold-water immersion (CWI)
- Laser therapy

## Key findings

- **Strongest evidence: nutrition and sleep** — "definitively shown to benefit volleyball players." These are the two non-negotiable foundations.
- **Promising but uncertain: CWI and laser therapy** — both show signal in the broader sport-science literature, but volleyball-specific evidence is thin and inconsistent.
- **Match-density problem identified** — volleyball features short bouts of high-intensity work with limited inter-match recovery; recovery strategy directly influences both performance and injury risk.
- **Combined-modality research is missing** — most studies test single modalities; the evidence on optimal combinations of recovery strategies for rapid turnaround (e.g., tournament play) is a stated research gap.

## Practical implications for the wiki

1. **Recovery hierarchy: sleep + nutrition first.** These are the highest-evidence interventions and should be the foundation of any recovery program before adding modalities like CWI or contrast therapy.
2. **CWI is reasonable but not mandatory** — coaches who use it have some support, but it's not a settled best practice for volleyball specifically.
3. **Tournament play is the highest-leverage application** — multi-match days are exactly where recovery strategy decides outcomes.
4. **Monitor and individualize** — combined modality stacks should be assessed empirically per athlete since the evidence base is limited.
