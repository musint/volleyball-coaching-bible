# Applying the constraints-led approach to facilitate exploratory learning of the volleyball serve

**Authors:** Brendan Moy, Ian Renshaw, Adam D. Gorman
**Journal:** Journal of Sports Sciences
**Year:** 2024
**Volume/Issue/Pages:** 42(6): 511–518
**DOI:** 10.1080/02640414.2024.2345415
**PMID:** 38676287

## Abstract (synthesized from PubMed access)

The study tested whether adding the **task constraint of a second-serve attempt** would encourage volleyball players to use their first serve to explore their action capabilities. **Forty male high-school students** played two volleyball games — a regulation single-serve game and a modified two-serve game. In the two-serve condition, participants attempted **faster serves** (60.3 km/hr vs. 55.6 km/hr) and **more complex serve techniques** (44.5% jump topspin serves vs. 25.2% under the single-serve regulation). Participants reported the second chance allowed them to feel more confident and relaxed, which facilitated exploration of their serving capability. The authors conclude that selective manipulation of practice constraints (here: serve allowance) encourages motor-skill development through increased exploratory behavior — a direct demonstration of the constraints-led approach (CLA) in volleyball.

## Key claims / ideas
- A **single rule change** (one serve → two serves) shifts player behavior from "play it safe" to "explore harder, more complex serves" — operationalizing CLA in a competitive game format.
- Mean serve speed jumped ~5 km/hr; share of jump-topspin attempts nearly doubled (25% → 45%).
- **Self-reported affect (confidence, relaxation)** mediated the behavior change — pressure relief was a feature, not a bug.
- Direct evidence that "wash drill"-style or "two-serve" rule modifications can be deliberate skill-development tools, not just match-format curiosities.
- Aligns with Renshaw/Gorman/Moy's broader CLA pedagogy literature; also bridges into ecological-dynamics framing.
