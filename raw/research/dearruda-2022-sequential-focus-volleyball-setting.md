# Sequential focus of attention instructions influenced motor performance of volleyball setting despite direction of focus but dependent on motor expertise of the player

**Authors:** Danilo Gomes de Arruda, Boyi Dai, Tucker Readdy, Sean McCrea, Qin Zhu
**Journal:** International Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology
**Year:** 2022 (published online); listed as Vol 22, Issue 1 (2024)
**Volume/Issue:** 22(1)
**DOI:** 10.1080/1612197X.2022.2138495

## Abstract (synthesized from publisher access)

For complex motor skills like volleyball setting, **sequential focus-of-attention (S-FOA) instructions** — multiple cues delivered in sequence — are often given to facilitate skill acquisition and performance, but it remained unknown whether **internal** vs. **external** S-FOA would impact motor performance differently for skilled and novice players. Thirty-two female volleyball players (16 novice, 16 skilled) performed a simulated volleyball setting task without S-FOA (control) and with S-FOA (internal direction or external direction). Movement outcomes (setting accuracy) and inter-joint coordination (flexion-extension across involved joints) were analyzed using linear models and time-series analysis. Results: skilled players outperformed novices in setting accuracy due to a more stable intermediate coordination pattern with **functional inter-joint variability prior to ball contact**. Despite the *direction* of focus, S-FOA **degraded mean setting accuracy in skilled players** without changing their inter-joint coordination pattern, and **failed to influence** novice performance on either accuracy or coordination. S-FOA significantly **reduced inter-joint coordination variability** before and after ball contact for all players — but this reduction was **detrimental for skilled** players (lost their functional variability) and **beneficial for novices** (reduced flailing). The authors recommend reducing the number of attentional cues and exploring conditions where players switch between internal and external cues.

## Key claims / ideas
- Skilled volleyball setters maintain **functional variability of inter-joint coordination** right before ball contact — this variability is part of why they're accurate, not a flaw to coach away.
- Loading skilled setters with **multiple sequential coaching cues (S-FOA)** crushed that variability and **reduced their accuracy** — direction (internal vs. external) didn't matter; cue load alone hurt the skilled.
- **Novices** had no benefit and no harm from S-FOA on accuracy, but their flailing inter-joint coordination got tighter — coaching novices with sequential cues may help reduce noise.
- **Big practical implication:** stack-of-cues coaching that helps a 12U player can actively hurt your varsity setter — at expertise level, prune cues and let inter-joint variability do its job.
- Echoes Wulf's "constrained action hypothesis": conscious cue-following interferes with automated coordination at the expert end of the skill range.
- Connects to broader skilled-vs-novice attentional-focus literature — skilled benefit from external/distal cueing or NO cue more than from layered internal sequences.
