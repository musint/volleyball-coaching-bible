# Sport Practitioners as Sport Ecology Designers: How Ecological Dynamics Has Progressively Changed Perceptions of Skill "Acquisition" in the Sporting Habitat

**Authors:** Carl T. Woods, Ian McKeown, Martyn Rothwell, Duarte Araújo, Sam Robertson, Keith Davids
**Journal:** Frontiers in Psychology, Movement Science and Sport Psychology section
**Year:** 2020
**Publication Date:** April 24, 2020
**DOI:** 10.3389/fpsyg.2020.00654

## Abstract

The paper examines how ecological dynamics has, over two decades, transformed understanding of skill development in sport. Rather than treating skill as an internalized motor memory that the athlete "acquires" and stores, the authors argue that athletes develop **adaptive relationships with competitive environments** through evolving practice ecologies built on the integrated interactions of performer, task, and environment constraints.

Practitioners are reframed as **sport ecology designers** whose role is to shape the informational and task constraints of practice so athletes can self-organize functional movement solutions.

## Key claims

1. **Skill as adaptation, not acquisition.** The term "skill acquisition" misrepresents learning; athletes develop functional relationships with performance environments rather than acquiring reproducible motor memories.
2. **Practitioner role transformation.** Coaches should function as learning-ecology designers who facilitate self-organized athlete–environment interactions rather than prescribe standardized movement templates.
3. **Representative design importance.** Practice tasks must authentically simulate competitive informational constraints so athletes can attune to relevant affordances.
4. **Functional variability.** Movement variability is adaptive in skilled performers and should not be uniformly treated as error.
5. **Self-organization principles.** Athletes exploit self-organizing tendencies through constraint manipulation, enabling emergent problem-solving across varied contexts.
